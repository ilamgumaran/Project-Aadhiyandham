# Pyrolysis Engineering: TLUD Gasifiers for Biochar Production and Clean Combustion

**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

The TLUD (Top-Lit Up-Draft) gasifier is the single most important device in the settlement's technology stack. It is the rare engineered artifact that simultaneously addresses three critical post-collapse needs in a single combustion cycle: (1) permanent soil building through biochar production for Terra Preta, (2) clean, high-temperature cooking heat that replaces the open fire, and (3) optional capture of pyroligneous acid (wood vinegar) for pest control and wood preservation. No other device in the settlement's inventory achieves this density of output per unit of labor and material invested.

The cone-pit method described in Module 02 is adequate for initial biochar production during the first weeks of settlement, when no fabricated equipment exists. However, cone-pit burns lose 40-60% of potential biochar to uncontrolled oxidation, produce dense smoke harmful to respiratory health, and waste all volatile energy as unburned emissions. The TLUD corrects every one of these deficiencies. By separating gasification from combustion through controlled airflow geometry, it captures 70-80% of feedstock energy as either useful cooking heat or embodied biochar carbon. A settlement that transitions from cone-pit burns to TLUD operation within the first 90 days gains a permanent, scalable infrastructure for both soil fertility and thermal energy — the two pillars upon which food sovereignty rests.

This module provides complete construction specifications, operating protocols, failure-mode analysis, and production scheduling for a 50-person settlement. The design requires no welding, no purchased components, and no materials that cannot be scavenged from any post-industrial debris field. Every settlement member should understand the principles; at least two members must achieve operational mastery.

> **DANGER**: TLUD gasifiers operate at temperatures of 400-700 degrees C internally. The outer drum reaches 200-400 degrees C during operation — sufficient to cause severe, deep burns on contact. Operate on cleared ground (3 m bare-earth radius minimum), never indoors or under flammable overhead cover, with a bucket of water and a bucket of sand within arm's reach for emergency quenching. Never add fuel to an operating gasifier — introducing oxygen to the pyrolysis zone can cause a flashback (explosive ignition of accumulated syngas). Refuel ONLY after the previous batch is complete and the char is quenched. Children and animals must be excluded from the operating perimeter at all times.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Chemistry of Pyrolysis

Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of organic matter in the absence — or near-absence — of oxygen. It is not combustion. Combustion requires oxygen and converts carbon to CO2 and water vapor, releasing all stored energy as heat. Pyrolysis, by contrast, uses heat to break molecular bonds in biomass, yielding a solid carbon residue (biochar), combustible gases (syngas), and condensable liquids (bio-oil and pyroligneous acid). The critical distinction: combustion destroys carbon; pyrolysis preserves it.

Biomass is composed of three primary structural polymers, each of which decomposes at a characteristic temperature range:

**Hemicellulose decomposition (200-260 degrees C):** The least thermally stable component. Decomposes first, releasing water vapor, carbon dioxide, acetic acid, and furfural. Hemicellulose constitutes 20-30% of dry wood mass. Its early decomposition is responsible for the initial "steaming" phase when a TLUD is first lit. The acetic acid released during this phase is a primary component of pyroligneous acid.

**Cellulose decomposition (240-350 degrees C):** The main structural polymer of plant cell walls, constituting 40-50% of dry wood mass. Cellulose decomposition produces the bulk of volatile products: carbon monoxide (CO), hydrogen (H2), methane (CH4), complex tars, and condensable organic compounds. This is the most energetically productive phase — the syngas generated here is what provides cooking heat when burned at the secondary air inlet.

**Lignin decomposition (280-500 degrees C):** The slowest and most thermally resistant component, constituting 15-30% of dry wood mass. Lignin decomposition produces phenolic compounds and, critically, forms the stable polycyclic aromatic carbon structure that makes biochar resistant to microbial decomposition for centuries to millennia. Lignin is why biochar is permanent — it is the molecular scaffold that survives.

The products of pyrolysis, by mass fraction of dry feedstock, are approximately:
- Biochar (solid): 20-35% by weight, containing 60-80% of the original fixed carbon
- Syngas (gaseous): CO + H2 + CH4 + CO2 + trace gases, 25-40% by weight
- Bio-oil and tars (condensable liquids): 15-25% by weight
- Pyroligneous acid (aqueous condensate): 5-15% by weight

The syngas fraction is combustible. Burning it provides the heat energy that (a) sustains the pyrolysis reaction itself and (b) delivers useful cooking heat to the pot. This dual function — pyrolysis products fueling their own production while simultaneously cooking food — is the key insight that makes the TLUD a self-sustaining device rather than one that requires external energy input.

### The TLUD Operating Principle

The acronym TLUD describes both the airflow geometry and the ignition pattern of the gasifier. Understanding the name is understanding the device:

**Top-Lit:** The fuel bed is ignited at the top surface, not the bottom. This is counterintuitive — nearly every other fire is lit from below. Top-lighting means the pyrolysis front (the zone of active thermal decomposition) starts at the top of the fuel column and migrates downward over time.

**Up-Draft:** Primary air enters through holes in the bottom of the reactor vessel and flows upward through the fuel column. This air reacts with already-charred material at the bottom of the pyrolysis front, converting hot carbon to syngas (primarily CO and H2) via the reduction reaction: C + H2O -> CO + H2 and C + CO2 -> 2CO. The syngas then rises upward through the uncharred fuel above the pyrolysis front.

The critical design feature is the secondary air inlet: a ring of holes drilled near the top rim of the reactor, above the fuel bed. As syngas rises out of the fuel column, it meets incoming secondary air at these holes. The mixture ignites, producing a clean, high-temperature flame above the fuel bed. This secondary combustion burns off tars, smoke particles, and volatile organics that would otherwise exit as visible smoke and harmful particulate emissions.

The pyrolysis front migrates downward through the fuel column at a rate of approximately 1-3 cm per minute, depending on fuel density and air supply. When the front reaches the bottom of the fuel bed, all biomass has been converted to char. The flame character changes visibly: from a vigorous yellow-blue flame (syngas combustion) to a faint, transparent blue or no visible flame at all. This color change is the operator's primary signal that the batch is complete and quenching must begin immediately.

### Gasification Thermodynamics

The TLUD process is exothermic overall — the energy released by syngas combustion exceeds the energy required to sustain pyrolysis. This self-sustaining energy balance is what makes the device practical. Once lit, no external energy input is needed.

The energy budget for a typical hardwood feedstock batch:

| Energy Fraction | Destination | Percentage of Feedstock Energy |
|----------------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Useful cooking heat | Transferred to pot via convection and radiation | 40-50% |
| Embodied in biochar | Stored as chemical energy in stable carbon | 25-35% |
| Radiation and exhaust losses | Lost to surroundings | 15-25% |

For comparison, an open three-stone fire converts approximately 10-15% of wood energy to useful cooking heat, produces no biochar, and emits the remaining 85-90% as waste heat, smoke, carbon dioxide, and particulate matter. The TLUD captures 3-5 times more useful energy from the same mass of feedstock while simultaneously sequestering 25-35% of the carbon as a permanent soil amendment.

Specific performance figures for a standard 20-liter TLUD:
- Thermal power output: 3-5 kW (sufficient for vigorous boiling of 5-10 liters of water)
- Burn time per batch: 30-90 minutes depending on fuel type, piece size, and packing density
- Biochar yield: 2-3 kg per batch from hardwood feedstock
- Syngas composition (approximate): 20% CO, 15% H2, 3% CH4, 12% CO2, 50% N2

### Feedstock Science

The TLUD is not fuel-agnostic. Performance depends critically on feedstock properties:

**Piece size:** Ideal range is 2-5 cm in the largest dimension. Pieces must be small enough to pack with air gaps (enabling upward gas flow) but large enough to avoid choking the primary air supply. Uniform sizing is more important than absolute size — a batch of uniform 3 cm pieces outperforms a batch of mixed 1-8 cm pieces.

**Moisture content:** Must be below 15% for clean operation. At 15-20% moisture, performance degrades noticeably: more smoke, lower pyrolysis temperature, reduced biochar quality (brown instead of black). Above 20%, the device may fail to sustain pyrolysis — too much energy is consumed evaporating water. Field test for moisture: a piece of wood at less than 15% moisture snaps cleanly with a sharp crack; at greater than 20% it bends and splinters before breaking. Solar-dry all feedstock for a minimum of 2 weeks before use.

**Suitable feedstocks (ranked by biochar quality):**
- Hardwood chunks (oak, hickory, maple): dense, high-CEC biochar with excellent micropore structure
- Nut shells (walnut, coconut, macadamia): very high surface area biochar, excellent for Terra Preta
- Bamboo chunks: good biochar, fast-growing feedstock, excellent renewable supply
- Fruit pits (peach, mango, avocado): dense, high-quality biochar
- Corn cobs: medium-quality biochar, abundant agricultural waste
- Softwood chunks (pine, spruce): lighter, more porous biochar with lower CEC, but still serviceable
- Rice husks, grain chaff: low-density biochar, high silica content, best mixed with other feedstocks

**Feedstocks requiring modification:**
- Sawdust: packs too tightly for adequate airflow. Must be compressed into briquettes (using a simple lever press with a binder of 5-10% clay or starch) or mixed 1:3 with coarser material.
- Straw and grass: too light and prone to channeling. Must be chopped to 3-5 cm lengths and mixed with denser feedstock.
- Green (fresh) wood: moisture content 40-60%. Must be solar-dried for 3-6 weeks before use.

### Historical Context

The principles underlying the TLUD are not new. Humanity has practiced controlled pyrolysis for millennia:

**Traditional Japanese charcoal kilns (sumigama):** For over 1,000 years, Japanese artisans produced bincho-tan (white charcoal) by pyrolyzing oak in sealed earthen kilns at temperatures exceeding 1,000 degrees C. Bincho-tan achieves carbon content above 95% and burns nearly smoke-free. The TLUD applies the same pyrolysis chemistry in a smaller, faster, batch-process format.

**Brazilian earth-mound kilns:** Pre-industrial charcoal production for the Brazilian iron smelting industry used earth-covered mounds of stacked wood, ignited from the top and allowed to smolder for days. Yields were low (10-15% char by weight) and emissions severe, but the scale was enormous — millions of tonnes of charcoal per year.

**Modern TLUD development:** Dr. Thomas Reed at the Colorado School of Mines first articulated the TLUD principle in the mid-1980s. Paul Anderson (known as "Dr. TLUD") refined and popularized dozens of practical designs from the 2000s onward, focusing on developing-world cooking applications. The Aprovecho Research Center in Oregon conducted extensive controlled testing of TLUD designs for thermal efficiency and emissions, establishing the performance benchmarks cited in this module.

## Core Principles

1. **The Dual-Output Principle.** A TLUD is not primarily a cooking device, nor primarily a biochar maker. It is both, simultaneously, in every batch. Any design modification that improves cooking heat at the expense of biochar quality — or vice versa — misses the fundamental purpose. The settlement requires both clean cooking energy and biochar for Terra Preta. The TLUD delivers both from a single fuel load.

2. **The Airflow Geometry Rule.** Primary air (bottom) must be sufficient to sustain gasification but not so excessive that it combusts the char. Secondary air (top) must be sufficient to burn all syngas cleanly, eliminating smoke. The governing ratio: total cross-sectional area of secondary air holes should equal 1.5-2.0 times the total area of primary air holes. Too little secondary air produces smoke. Too much primary air burns char to ash.

3. **The Uniform Fuel Rule.** TLUD performance depends critically on uniform fuel piece size. Mixed sizes create uneven packing density: small pieces fill gaps between large pieces, choking airflow in some zones while leaving open channels in others. The result is channeling (uneven burn), incomplete pyrolysis, and mixed-quality output. Sort feedstock by size before loading. Reject undersized fines and oversized chunks.

4. **The Quench Imperative.** When the batch is complete — signaled by the flame turning from yellow-blue to faint blue or transparent — the biochar must be quenched within 2-5 minutes. If left in the open reactor, secondary air will begin oxidizing the biochar itself, converting it progressively to ash. Every minute of delay destroys product. The fastest and safest quench method is dumping the char into a water bucket. The alternative is clamping an airtight lid over the reactor to exclude oxygen.

5. **The Moisture Threshold.** No operational parameter matters more than feedstock moisture content. Above 15%, pyrolysis temperature drops, volatile yield decreases, smoke production increases, and biochar quality degrades from black to brown. Above 25%, the device may not sustain gasification at all. Test every batch before loading. Invest in covered solar-drying racks as permanent settlement infrastructure.

## Practical Implementation

### The Two-Drum TLUD: Detailed Construction

**Materials and Specifications:**

| Component | Specification | Purpose |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| Inner drum (reactor) | 20 L metal pail or cut-down 200 L drum, 25-30 cm diameter x 35-40 cm tall | Contains the fuel column and defines the pyrolysis zone |
| Outer drum (jacket) | Slightly larger drum or fabricated cylinder, 35-40 cm diameter | Insulation shell and structural support |
| Primary air holes | 20-30 holes, 8-10 mm diameter, drilled in the bottom plate of the inner drum | Air supply for gasification reaction at base of fuel bed |
| Secondary air holes | 15-20 holes, 10-12 mm diameter, drilled in a ring 3-5 cm below the top rim of the inner drum | Air supply for clean syngas combustion above fuel bed |
| Insulation fill | Wood ash, dry sand, or crusite packed between inner and outer drums | Minimizes radial heat loss, maintains pyrolysis temperature |
| Pot support | 3 metal tabs (bent inward from rim) or flat stones | Holds cooking vessel with correct gap above flame |
| Grate (optional) | Wire mesh or perforated metal plate at bottom of inner drum, above primary air holes | Supports fuel column, allows ash to fall clear of air inlet |

**Construction Procedure:**

Step 1 — Prepare the inner drum. Using a drill, punch, or sharpened nail and hammer, create 20-30 evenly spaced holes (8-10 mm) in the bottom plate of the inner drum. Then create 15-20 holes (10-12 mm) in a single ring around the circumference of the inner drum, positioned 3-5 cm below the top rim. Deburr all holes to prevent injury.

Step 2 — Prepare the outer drum. Cut the outer drum to match the inner drum height plus 5 cm. Drill ventilation holes in the bottom of the outer drum, aligned with the inner drum's primary air holes, to allow air ingress from below.

Step 3 — Create spacers. Place 3-4 flat stones, metal offcuts, or bent metal tabs in the bottom of the outer drum. These spacers support the inner drum and maintain a uniform 3-5 cm insulation gap between the two drums on all sides, including below.

Step 4 — Assemble. Set the inner drum on the spacers inside the outer drum, centering it carefully. Pack the annular gap between drums tightly with wood ash (preferred — lightest, best insulator) or dry sand.

Step 5 — Fashion pot support. Bend three metal tabs inward from the top rim of the inner drum, spaced 120 degrees apart. The tabs should protrude 3-4 cm inward, creating a stable tripod for the cooking vessel. The gap between the pot bottom and the drum rim must be approximately 1-2 cm — sufficient to allow exhaust gases to escape but not so large that heat is wasted.

Step 6 — Install grate (optional). If available, place a wire mesh or perforated metal circle inside the inner drum, resting 2-3 cm above the bottom plate. This grate supports the fuel column and allows fine ash to fall away from the primary air holes, preventing blockage during operation.

### Operating Protocol

**Step 1 — Load.** Fill the inner drum with uniform, dry fuel pieces (2-5 cm, less than 15% moisture). Leave 5 cm of headspace between the top of the fuel bed and the secondary air holes. Do not pack tightly — the fuel should settle under its own weight with visible air gaps.

**Step 2 — Light.** Place a small quantity of kindling (crumpled paper, dry bark shreds, or a few small wood shavings) directly on top of the fuel bed. Ignite the kindling. The fire starts at the top surface only.

**Step 3 — Initial smoke phase (2-4 minutes).** As the top fuel layer heats through the hemicellulose decomposition range (200-260 degrees C), water vapor and light volatiles are released. Visible smoke is normal during this phase. Do not attempt to place a pot yet.

**Step 4 — Transition to clean burn (minute 3-5).** As the top layer reaches cellulose decomposition temperatures (240-350 degrees C), syngas production begins in earnest. The rising syngas meets secondary air at the top ring of holes and ignites. The flame transitions from smoky orange to a cleaner yellow-blue. Smoke diminishes to near-zero.

**Step 5 — Stable cooking phase (minutes 5-60+).** The gasifier is now in steady-state operation. The flame above the fuel bed is vigorous, concentrated, and nearly smokeless. Place the cooking pot on the supports. Heat output is 3-5 kW — sufficient for vigorous boiling. The pyrolysis front migrates downward at 1-3 cm per minute. Do not disturb the fuel bed. Do not add fuel. Do not lift the pot rapidly (sudden air ingress can cause flashback).

**Step 6 — Completion indicators.** When the pyrolysis front reaches the bottom of the fuel bed, all biomass has been converted to char. Observable signals: (a) flame color shifts from yellow-blue to faint blue or transparent, (b) heat output drops noticeably, (c) a faint "tinkling" sound may be heard as char pieces settle and crack.

**Step 7 — Quench immediately.** Remove the pot. Using heavy gloves or tongs, lift the inner drum and dump its contents into a water bucket. The char will hiss and steam — this is normal. Alternatively, if water is scarce, clamp a tight-fitting metal lid over the inner drum to exclude oxygen and allow the char to cool over 1-2 hours. Do NOT leave the char in the open reactor. The window between batch completion and char-to-ash combustion is approximately 2-5 minutes.

**Step 8 — Collect and dry biochar.** Retrieve quenched char from the water bucket. Spread on a drying rack in sun for 1-2 days. The dried biochar is ready for crushing, charging, and incorporation into Terra Preta beds (see Module 02).

### Wood Vinegar (Pyroligneous Acid) Capture

Pyroligneous acid is the aqueous condensate of pyrolysis exhaust gases. It is a valuable secondary product that requires only a simple condensation apparatus attached to the TLUD exhaust.

**Condensation apparatus construction:**
1. Attach a chimney pipe (scavenged metal pipe, 5-8 cm diameter, 1.5-2 m long) to the top of the TLUD, directing exhaust gases into the pipe rather than directly to the cooking pot. Note: when capturing wood vinegar, the TLUD operates as a biochar-only device — cooking heat is sacrificed.
2. Angle the pipe downward at 10-15 degrees from horizontal.
3. Cool the pipe externally: wrap with wet rags (re-wet periodically), run a trickle of water over the exterior, or bury the pipe in damp earth.
4. Place a collection vessel (glass or ceramic — not metal, as pyroligneous acid is mildly corrosive) at the low end of the pipe.

**Product characteristics:** The collected liquid is dark brown to amber, with a strong smoky-acidic odor. Approximate composition: 80% water, 10% acetic acid, 3-5% methanol, 2-3% acetone and other ketones, 2-5% phenols and tars.

**Applications by dilution ratio:**

| Dilution (vinegar:water) | Application | Notes |
|--------------------------|-------------|-------|
| 1:200 | Foliar spray, mild plant growth stimulant | Apply every 2 weeks during growing season |
| 1:50 | Soil drench, suppresses fungal pathogens | Apply to seedbed before planting |
| 1:20 | Insect repellent for garden perimeters | Spray on border soil and mulch |
| Undiluted | Wood preservative for fence posts, structural timber | Paint or soak application |

**Safety note:** Undiluted pyroligneous acid is mildly corrosive and irritating to skin, eyes, and mucous membranes. Handle with gloves. Store in sealed containers away from food preparation areas. The methanol content makes it mildly toxic if ingested — label clearly and store out of reach of children.

### Biochar Quality Assessment

Laboratory analysis of biochar (surface area, cation exchange capacity, carbon content) is unavailable post-collapse. The following field tests provide reliable quality assessment:

**Color test:** Break a piece of biochar and examine the fresh fracture surface. Correct result: uniformly deep black throughout. Brown or dark brown indicates incomplete pyrolysis (center of pieces did not reach sufficient temperature). Grey or white indicates over-oxidation (char burned to ash). Correct the process accordingly.

**Sound test:** Strike two pieces of biochar together or drop a piece onto a hard surface. Correct result: a sharp, high-pitched "clink" or "ring" similar to ceramic. A dull thud indicates under-cooked, partially pyrolyzed material with residual volatile content.

**Porosity test:** Examine the fracture surface with magnification if available (a simple water-drop lens suffices). Correct result: visible network of micropores — tiny holes arrayed in the cellular structure of the original wood. These micropores are the functional surfaces where water, nutrients, and microorganisms are retained in Terra Preta soil.

**Float test:** Drop a small piece of biochar into a container of water. Correct result: the piece initially floats (micropores are filled with air, providing buoyancy), then slowly sinks over 30-120 seconds as water infiltrates the pore network. Ash sinks immediately. Undercooked char may not float at all (pores are filled with residual tars).

**Crush test:** Crush a piece between thumb and forefinger. Correct result: the piece fractures into angular fragments with sharp edges. If the piece crumbles to fine powder, ash contamination is high — the quench was too slow or primary air was excessive.

### Production Schedule for a 50-Person Settlement

The biochar requirement for soil building drives the production schedule:

- Target: 0.5 hectares of new Terra Preta beds per year (sufficient to feed 50 people within 3-5 years of continuous soil building)
- Application rate: 15 tonnes of biochar per hectare (the established Terra Preta benchmark)
- Annual biochar requirement: 7.5 tonnes
- Yield per 20 L TLUD batch: 2-3 kg of biochar (average 2.5 kg)
- Required batches per year: 3,000
- Required batches per day: approximately 8-10

This implies 4-5 TLUD units operating simultaneously, each running 2 batches per day. Two dedicated operators can manage 5 units through a full day's production cycle (loading, lighting, monitoring, quenching, reloading).

The cooking heat produced as a byproduct of 8-10 daily batches (approximately 30-50 kWh of thermal energy) is more than sufficient to provide all cooking energy for 50 people. The settlement's cooking fuel problem and soil-building problem are solved by the same device running the same daily schedule. This convergence of needs is the strategic rationale for prioritizing TLUD construction and operator training above almost all other infrastructure tasks.

## Common Failure Modes

**1. Persistent Smoke Production (Failed Secondary Combustion).** Symptom: visible smoke continues beyond the initial 3-4 minute startup phase. Causes: secondary air holes too small or partially blocked by soot; fuel moisture above 15%; insufficient secondary-to-primary air ratio. Corrective actions: clean secondary air holes with a wire; verify fuel is dry (snap test); enlarge secondary air holes to achieve the 1.5-2.0x area ratio relative to primary holes.

**2. Channeling (Uneven Burn).** Symptom: one side of the fuel bed chars completely while the opposite side remains raw. The pyrolysis front does not descend uniformly. Causes: uneven fuel packing density (large pieces on one side, small on the other); mixed fuel sizes creating preferential airflow paths; reactor not level. Corrective actions: sort fuel rigorously by size; pack the reactor evenly, shaking gently to settle; ensure the operating surface is level.

**3. Excessive Ash / Low Biochar Yield.** Symptom: the batch produces mostly grey-white ash with little black biochar. Causes: quenching was delayed beyond the 2-5 minute window after batch completion; primary air supply is too generous, combusting char rather than merely gasifying it. Corrective actions: quench immediately upon flame color change; reduce the number or size of primary air holes; station the quench bucket within arm's reach before lighting.

**4. Flashback (Explosive Ignition).** Symptom: a sudden explosive flare or "whump" when the pot is lifted, fuel is added, or the reactor is disturbed during operation. Cause: accumulated syngas above the fuel bed is suddenly mixed with excess oxygen from an external source. This is the most dangerous failure mode. Prevention: never add fuel during a burn cycle; never lift the pot rapidly; never blow into the reactor; approach from upwind.

**5. Drum Warping and Perforation.** Symptom: the inner drum distorts, develops cracks, or burns through after repeated use. Cause: thermal stress on thin sheet metal subjected to repeated 400-700 degree C cycles. Mitigation: use the thickest available steel (1.5 mm minimum); accept that inner drums are consumable items requiring replacement every 50-100 batches; fabricate spares in advance; the outer drum lasts much longer due to insulation protection.

**6. Tar Accumulation and Clogging.** Symptom: secondary air holes become progressively blocked with hardened tar deposits over multiple batches. Heat output and combustion quality decline. Cause: incomplete secondary combustion allows tar vapors to condense on cooler metal surfaces. Corrective action: clean all holes with a stiff wire after every 5-10 batches; ensure secondary air supply is adequate to combust all volatiles.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

**Pyrolysis** — Thermal decomposition of organic material in the absence or near-absence of oxygen, producing solid char, combustible gases, and condensable liquids.

**TLUD (Top-Lit Up-Draft)** — A gasifier design in which fuel is ignited at the top and primary air enters from the bottom, creating a downward-migrating pyrolysis front with clean secondary combustion above the fuel bed.

**Syngas (Synthesis Gas)** — The combustible gas mixture produced by pyrolysis, primarily carbon monoxide (CO) and hydrogen (H2), with lesser amounts of methane (CH4).

**Producer Gas** — Synonym for syngas in the context of small-scale gasification. The term emphasizes that the gas is "produced" from solid fuel.

**Primary Air** — Air supplied to the base of the TLUD reactor, driving the gasification reaction that converts char to syngas.

**Secondary Air** — Air supplied near the top of the reactor, mixing with rising syngas to achieve clean combustion above the fuel bed.

**Flame Cap** — The visible combustion zone above the fuel bed where syngas burns upon meeting secondary air. A well-functioning flame cap is the hallmark of correct TLUD operation.

**Gasification** — The thermochemical conversion of a solid fuel into a combustible gas through partial oxidation, distinct from full combustion.

**Pyroligneous Acid (Wood Vinegar)** — The aqueous condensate of pyrolysis exhaust, containing acetic acid, methanol, phenols, and water. Used diluted as a pesticide, fungicide, and plant growth stimulant.

**Feedstock** — The biomass fuel loaded into the gasifier. In TLUD context, refers specifically to uniform, dry, sized pieces of wood, shells, cobs, or other lignocellulosic material.

**Quenching** — The rapid cooling of hot biochar by immersion in water or exclusion of oxygen, halting further oxidation and preserving the char product.

**Channeling** — A failure mode in which the pyrolysis front advances unevenly through the fuel bed due to non-uniform packing, creating preferential airflow paths.

**Flashback** — Explosive ignition of accumulated syngas caused by sudden introduction of excess oxygen to the reactor, typically when the pot is lifted or fuel is added during operation.

**Bincho-tan** — Traditional Japanese white charcoal produced by high-temperature pyrolysis of oak in sealed kilns, achieving carbon content above 95%. Represents the historical apex of artisanal charcoal production.
