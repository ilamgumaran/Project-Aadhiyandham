**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> **Caution — the trap that has no second chance:** Most settling failures announce themselves early — a bad water source, a broken tool, a fever. The seasonal-timing failure is different: it is invisible for months and then absolute. A group that arrives at a beautiful site in autumn, plants nothing because the season is over, and lives on its carried rations feels *fine* through October. It starves in February, with the ground frozen and the nearest food a hundred kilometres behind it. There is no recovery move in February. The entire decision was made months earlier, at departure, by people who did not run the arithmetic in this module. **When you arrive is a bigger determinant of whether you live than where you arrive.**

## 1. Why This Is Important

Every other food module answers *how to produce and store food.* This one answers a question that sits above all of them and that the manual, until now, left scattered across three places: **is there a continuous chain of calories from the day you arrive to the day your stored harvest carries you through your first gap season — and if not, what do you do about it before you leave?**

The chain has three links, and a settlement dies if any one breaks:

1.  **The hungry gap** — the weeks between when your carried rations run out and your first crops are harvestable ([Emergency Nutrition](06_Emergency_Nutrition.md) owns this link).
2.  **The first harvest** — which, on new ground with unpracticed hands, yields far less than the seed packet promises.
3.  **The first gap season** — the winter or dry season your first harvest must have stored *enough surplus* to survive ([Refugia Pantry Physics](08_Refugia_Pantry_Physics.md) owns this link).

Arrival timing determines whether these links can connect at all. Arrive at the start of the growing season and you have a full season to bridge the gap, grow a harvest, and bank a reserve. Arrive halfway through and you get a partial harvest and a fatal reserve shortfall. Arrive at its end and you have *no* season and must have carried an entire winter's food on your back — which, for any group larger than a couple, is physically impossible. History is unambiguous here: the Jamestown "starving time" and countless colonial and utopian-community first winters killed by exactly this arithmetic, not by hostility or disease. **This module is the arithmetic, written down once, so the departure decision is made with open eyes.**

## 2. Expected Outcome

Before departure: a **first-year food ledger** that proves (or disproves) a continuous caloric chain from arrival to the second growing season, using honest yield assumptions; a chosen **arrival window** matched to the site's climate; and, if the chain does not close, a decided response — carry more, cache ahead, delay to spring, or choose a longer-season site. After arrival: a season-by-season establishment schedule that keeps the chain intact.

## 3. The Arrival Window: When, Not Just Where

The governing rule is simple and nearly absolute:

> **Arrive at the beginning of the growing season, not its middle and never its end.** You need a full productive season on the ground to bridge the hungry gap, grow a first harvest, and store a reserve before the gap season closes in.

What "the beginning of the growing season" means depends on the climate, and this is the arrival-window table the settlement must consult per site (climate types cross-reference the [Climate Adaptation Thresholds](../../Outcome_1_Locating_Refugia/02_The_Site/10_Climate_Adaptation_Thresholds.md) and [Candidate Atlas](../../Outcome_1_Locating_Refugia/03_Candidate_Atlas/)):

| Climate type | Growing season | Gap season | Arrive at… | Latest safe arrival | If you'd arrive in the gap season |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Temperate continental** (4-season) | spring–autumn, ~150–200 frost-free days | winter, 120–180 days | **the last spring frost** | early summer (enough season left for a 90-day staple + storage) | **overwinter elsewhere; travel in spring** |
| **Mediterranean / dry-summer** | the cool, wet season (autumn–spring) | the hot dry summer | **the first autumn rains** (plant winter crops) | mid-autumn | delay to autumn rains |
| **Humid subtropical** | long, ~200–280 days; often two plantings | a short cool winter | **early spring** | mid-summer (a second planting is still possible) | more forgiving, but still avoid deep winter |
| **Tropical wet-and-dry** (savanna/monsoon) | the wet season | the dry season | **the onset of the rains** | early in the wet season | delay to the rains; the dry season grows little |
| **Tropical wet** (equatorial) | near year-round | minimal, but variable | **any time**, but plant immediately | — | the most forgiving; still store against variability |
| **High-latitude / short-season** (<90–110 frost-free days) | a short, sharp summer | a long, hard winter | **the last spring frost — to the week** | late spring (no slack) | **do not attempt a late arrival; often unviable** |

Two things this table makes concrete. First, "spring" is not universal — in a Mediterranean or monsoon climate the growing season opens with the *rains*, and arriving in the hot dry summer there is the same death trap as arriving in a temperate autumn. Second, the shorter the season, the less forgiving the timing: a high-latitude site punishes a two-week-late arrival with a failed reserve, which is why such sites, however beautiful, are the least tolerant of a late journey.

## 4. The First-Year Food Ledger (the arithmetic)

The ledger proves the caloric chain. Run it in kilocalories, before departure, for your actual party. The numbers below are drawn from [Emergency Nutrition](06_Emergency_Nutrition.md) and [Refugia Pantry Physics](08_Refugia_Pantry_Physics.md); use your own where you have them.

```text
   THE FIRST-YEAR CALORIC CHAIN (must be unbroken)
   ===============================================
   ARRIVE                FIRST HARVEST            NEXT GROWING SEASON
   (growing-season start) (60-120 days later)      (~1 year later)
     |                        |                          |
     |<--- HUNGRY GAP ------->|<--- build the reserve -->|<-- reserve
     |   rations + forage +   |   surplus from a         |   carries
     |   fast crops           |   DISCOUNTED harvest     |   the gap
     |                        |                          |   season
   [Link 1: bridge]        [Link 2: harvest]        [Link 3: reserve]
```

**Link 1 — Bridge the hungry gap (arrival → first harvest).**
*   Carried rations for a 50-person group typically cover **30–45 days** at a maintenance ration; establishment labor is heavy, pushing real need toward **~3,000 kcal/person/day** (150,000 kcal/day for 50).
*   Staple crops take **60–120 days** to first harvest; fast crops shorten part of it (radish 25–30 d, bush beans 50–60 d, buckwheat 60–75 d) but cannot feed the group alone.
*   The gap — ration exhaustion to first harvest — is **30–70 days**, roughly **8 million kcal for 50 people**, and *foraging alone cannot close it* (10 foragers yield 5,000–50,000 kcal/day against a 150,000 kcal/day need).
*   **Bridge deficit = (gap days × people × kcal/day) − (foraging + fishing + fast-crop yield).** Whatever remains must be carried in or cached ahead.

**Link 2 — The first harvest, honestly discounted.**
*   This is the number the manual previously left silent: **a first harvest on new, unimproved ground, in an unfamiliar microclimate, worked by hands new to the land, historically yields 30–60% of the seed-catalogue "expected" figure.** Soil not yet built with [biochar/Terra Preta](02_Terra_Preta_and_Biochar.md), pests not yet understood, frost dates not yet known, and labor split with construction all compound.
*   **Plan Link 2 at a 0.3–0.6 multiplier of expected yield.** Planning at full yield is the single most common way the ledger lies to you.

**Link 3 — The first gap-season reserve.**
*   The reserve target is **population × gap-days × 2,500 kcal/day × 1.25 safety** — for 50 people over a 180-day winter, **≈28 million kcal** ([Refugia Pantry Physics](08_Refugia_Pantry_Physics.md)); a family of four over 180 days needs ≈2.25 million kcal.
*   The reserve must be *stored surplus from the discounted first harvest,* plus any carried/traded food. If the discounted harvest cannot fill it, the shortfall is what you must carry in, cache, or acquire by trade — or the settlement starves in the first gap season.

**The go / no-go test.** If, at honest yield, the chain closes — rations + forage + fast crops bridge to a discounted harvest that both feeds the group and banks the full first-winter reserve — commit. **If it does not close, the plan is not "work harder"; it is one of the four responses in Section 6.** Deciding this at the kitchen table before departure is the difference between a hard first year and a fatal one.

## 5. The First-Year Establishment Calendar

Keyed to arrival at the growing-season start (temperate example; shift to the rains for Mediterranean/monsoon climates):

```text
   PHASE 0  ARRIVAL WEEK      water safe, shelter for the vulnerable,
   (days 1-7)                 latrine, perimeter (see Arrival Protocol);
                              PLANT FAST CROPS within 3 days (radish,
                              beans, buckwheat) - before construction
                              eats all the labor

   PHASE 1  ESTABLISH          main staple beds planted; foraging teams
   (weeks 2-6)                 mapping the land from day 1; fishing/
                               trapping lines set; ration discipline
                               under the Ration Master

   PHASE 2  THE HUNGRY GAP     rations thinning, fast crops arriving
   (weeks 4-10)               (day 40-60), foraging peaking; the
                               dangerous window - guard the ration store,
                               keep everyone above 1,800 kcal/day

   PHASE 3  FIRST HARVEST      staples come in; EVERYTHING surplus goes
   (months 3-5)               to the reserve, not to relief eating -
                               the reserve is next winter's survival,
                               not this month's comfort

   PHASE 4  STORE & BUILD      preserve, cellar, and pest-proof the
   (months 5-8)               reserve to its 28M-kcal target; build
                               the [pantry/root cellar]; save the [seed]

   PHASE 5  THE FIRST GAP      live on the reserve; do NOT dip below the
   (months 8-13)              line that leaves seed and spring-planting
                               strength intact; plan year two from year
                               one's real yield data
```

The hinge of the whole calendar is **Phase 3 discipline:** the psychological pull, after a hungry gap, is to *eat* the first harvest in relief. The settlement that does this starves in Phase 5. The first harvest is not a meal; it is the seed of the reserve. This must be understood and enforced before the harvest comes in, because in the moment, hunger argues the other way.

## 6. When the Ledger Does Not Close

If the arithmetic says the chain breaks, there are exactly four honest responses. "Push through and hope" is not among them.

1.  **Delay to the next growing season.** The strongest option. Overwinter where food already exists (the origin, a waystation, an allied settlement) and travel so you *arrive at the growing-season start.* A season's patience buys a full productive year; impatience buys a fatal winter.
2.  **Carry or cache more.** If the shortfall is modest, close it with carried reserves or supplies [cached ahead](../../Outcome_1_Locating_Refugia/01_The_Journey/12_Journey_Scenarios_and_Contingencies/04_Low_Profile_Travel_and_Encounter_Protocols.md) by an advance party — feasible for small groups and small gaps, impossible for a large group facing a whole winter.
3.  **Choose a longer-season or tropical site.** If timing cannot be fixed, change the climate. A humid-subtropical or tropical site with a near-year-round season removes the knife-edge that a high-latitude site imposes (weigh against the [Candidate Atlas](../../Outcome_1_Locating_Refugia/03_Candidate_Atlas/) trade-offs).
4.  **Split the move.** Send an advance party at the growing-season start to plant, build, and bank a reserve; the main group follows once the chain is proven and the larder exists — turning one impossible arrival into two survivable ones.

## 7. Common Failure Modes

*   **Autumn arrival on carried food.** The signature killer: arriving after the growing season, living on rations, starving when they run out mid-winter. **[Tier L] Consequence:** total group starvation in the first gap season, with no recovery move available once winter has closed in — the decision was locked at departure. **Prevention:** the arrival-window table; overwinter elsewhere rather than arrive into the gap.
*   **Planning at full yield.** Building the ledger on seed-catalogue numbers, so the "sufficient" first harvest comes in at 40% and the reserve is 60% short. **[Tier L] Consequence:** a reserve shortfall discovered in autumn, too late to grow more — a hungry, weakened, sometimes fatal first winter. **Prevention:** the 0.3–0.6 first-year multiplier, always.
*   **Eating the first harvest.** Relief-eating the harvest that was meant to become the winter reserve. **[Tier L] Consequence:** full bellies in month 4, empty stores in month 10. **Prevention:** Phase-3 discipline decided and understood *before* the harvest, because hunger will argue against it in the moment.
*   **Wrong "spring" for the climate.** Arriving in a Mediterranean or monsoon summer thinking it is the growing season. **[Tier C] Consequence:** a planted crop that withers in the dry heat; the season was the wet one you missed. **Prevention:** read the growing season as the *rains* where the climate is rain-defined, not the calendar month.
*   **No fast crops in week one.** Skipping the radish/bean/buckwheat planting because construction is urgent. **[Tier C] Consequence:** the hungry gap runs its full length with no day-40 relief, deepening the ration crisis. **Prevention:** plant fast crops within the first 3 days; the labor cost is trivial against the caloric return.

## 8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Arrival window:** The season-specific span during which a party can arrive and still bank a first-winter reserve; opens at the growing season's start.
*   **First-year yield discount:** The historical 30–60%-of-expected first-harvest reality on new ground; the multiplier that keeps the ledger honest.
*   **The caloric chain:** The unbroken sequence — carried rations → fast crops/foraging → discounted first harvest → stored reserve → next growing season — that must connect for a settlement to survive its first year.
*   **The hungry gap:** The 30–70 day window between ration exhaustion and first harvest; bridged, not skipped.
*   **The overwinter-in-place default:** The rule that a party facing a gap-season arrival waits and travels the next spring rather than arriving into starvation.
*   **The reserve line:** The floor below which the gap-season store must not fall, preserving seed stock and the strength to plant the next season.

## 9. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 9.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Preparation:** Before departure, build the first-year food ledger (Section 4) at honest yield; consult the arrival-window table for your site's climate; confirm the chain closes or choose a Section 6 response.
*   **Execution:** Time the journey to arrive at the growing-season start. On arrival, plant fast crops in the first 3 days; run the establishment calendar; enforce Phase-3 reserve discipline.
*   **Review:** Record year one's *real* yields and frost dates — they replace the catalogue numbers and the estimates for every year after, and they are the settlement's most valuable first-year data.

### 9.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier
*   **[Tier L — Law] Arrive into the gap season without a full carried reserve** → starvation in the first winter, no recovery once the season closes. *Measure the chain before departure; do not "feel" it.*
*   **[Tier L — Law] Fill the reserve at full expected yield** → autumn shortfall, hungry/fatal winter. *Discount to 0.3–0.6; the discount is not pessimism, it is the historical mean.*
*   **[Tier C — Craft] Read "growing season" as a calendar month rather than the local rains/frost** → mistimed planting. *Calibrate to your site's actual frost/rain dates, recorded annually.*

### 9.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** The person who plans the journey and the person who plans the food must build the ledger *together* — the seasonal-timing trap is precisely the failure that occurs when travel logistics and food logistics are planned by different people who never multiplied the two together. Teach the consequence (a February with no food) so the arrival date is never chosen for convenience.
