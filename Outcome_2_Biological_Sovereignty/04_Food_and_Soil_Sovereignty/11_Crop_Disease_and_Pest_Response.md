**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> **Caution — the famine that grows in a healthy-looking field:** A crop disease can go from a few spotted leaves to a destroyed harvest in a fortnight, and the settlement whose winter [reserve](08_Refugia_Pantry_Physics.md) depended on that harvest starves months later. The most infamous case is not ancient history: the Irish potato famine killed a million people because a whole population planted a single potato variety and a water-mould found it. The lesson is exact and it governs this module: **the defense against crop catastrophe is built before the disease arrives, into the diversity of what you plant — not into a cure you apply after.** There is no fungicide in this manual. There is only prevention, early recognition, and ruthless containment.

## 1. Why This Is Important

The [food modules](01_Rationale_and_Importance.md) build the settlement's caloric survival on crops, and crops have enemies that arrive on the wind, in the soil, on tools, and in the seed itself: fungal and water-mould blights, rusts, mildews, wilts, viral diseases, and insect pests that can defoliate a field or bore through a granary. In an industrial system these are met with sprays and replacement seed shipped overnight. The settlement has neither. Its harvest can fail in the field as completely as it can fail from bad timing ([First-Year Food Security](10_First_Year_Food_Security_and_Arrival_Timing.md)) or bad storage ([Food Toxins](09_Food_Toxins_and_Storage_Safety.md)) — and unlike those, a field disease can take *next year's seed* with it, turning one bad harvest into permanent loss of a crop variety.

The settlement's advantage is that pre-industrial and traditional agriculture defeated these enemies for ten thousand years without chemicals — through *diversity, rotation, sanitation, and selection.* This module is that toolkit, plus the discipline to recognize trouble early and contain it before it spreads.

## 2. Expected Outcome

Fields planted for resilience (diverse crops and varieties, rotated, spaced); a grower's eye trained to catch disease and pest outbreaks in their first days; a containment doctrine that removes and destroys infected material before it spreads or contaminates the seed line; and a pre-agreed decision for when to sacrifice a crop to protect the settlement's seed stock and next season.

## 3. Prevention Is the Whole Game: The Structural Defenses

Because there is no cure to apply, nearly all of crop protection is *design*, done before planting:

*   **Diversity — the master defense.** A disease or pest is usually specialized to one crop or even one variety. Plant **many crops and many varieties**, interplanted rather than in vast single-crop blocks, and no single disease can take everything. This is the direct antidote to the [monoculture trap](01_Rationale_and_Importance.md) that caused the potato famine, and it is why [heirloom seed diversity](03_Heirloom_Seed_Banking.md) is survival infrastructure, not variety for its own sake. **The single most important number: never let one crop or variety exceed ~30% of the food base.**
*   **Rotation.** Many soil-borne diseases and pests build up where the same crop grows in the same ground year after year. Rotating crop families through beds over a multi-year cycle breaks their build-up — the medieval three-field system and every traditional rotation exist for exactly this. Following a heavy feeder with a legume also rebuilds the [soil](02_Terra_Preta_and_Biochar.md).
*   **Resistant varieties, selected over time.** Save seed preferentially from the plants that stayed healthy while their neighbors sickened ([Heirloom Seed Banking](03_Heirloom_Seed_Banking.md)). Over generations this breeds locally-adapted disease resistance into the settlement's own landraces — the slow, permanent, free defense that industrial agriculture abandoned.
*   **Spacing and airflow.** Fungal diseases love still, humid, crowded foliage. Adequate spacing and airflow keep leaves drier and slow fungal spread — a free preventive that also raises yield.
*   **Ecological balance.** A field embedded in a diverse [ecosystem](../06_Ecological_Harmony/01_Rationale_and_Importance.md) — with birds, predatory insects, and habitat for them — suffers far less from pest explosions than a sterile one, because the pests' own predators keep them in check. Encouraging this balance is pest control that works while you sleep.

## 4. Early Recognition: Walk the Fields

Disease and pest damage are cheap to stop early and impossible to stop late. The discipline is simple: **walk the fields regularly and look closely.**

*   **Fungal/mould diseases** (blights, rusts, mildews): spots, blotches, powdery or rusty coatings, wilting despite moist soil, rotting stems or tubers, a fast spread in wet weather. Water-mould blights (potato/tomato) can collapse a crop in days in warm damp conditions — these are the emergencies.
*   **Viral diseases:** mottled, streaked, or distorted leaves, stunting, poor yield with no visible organism. Often spread by insects and by tools; there is no cure — infected plants are removed to protect the rest.
*   **Insect pests:** chewed or skeletonized leaves, boreholes, sudden defoliation, visible infestations, damaged roots. Some (locusts, armyworm) can strip a field in a day and demand immediate hand-response.
*   **The habit:** a slow walk through the beds every few days, turning leaves, checking stems and soil line, catches trouble while it is still a few plants. The grower who only looks at harvest time finds the disease when it is everything.

## 5. Containment: Cut It Out Before It Spreads

When disease or a pest outbreak appears, act fast and decisively — the instinct to "wait and see" lets it spread:

*   **Roguing — remove and destroy.** Pull the diseased plants (and a margin around them) and **destroy them by fire or deep burial away from the fields** — never compost diseased material into the [beds](02_Terra_Preta_and_Biochar.md), never leave it lying (both just re-seed the disease). This is the single most effective field response and it is pure discipline: sacrificing a few plants now to save the field.
*   **Tool and hand hygiene.** Many diseases (especially viruses) ride from plant to plant on hands and cutting tools. Clean tools between plants when disease is present; handle healthy plants before sick ones.
*   **Do not save seed from diseased plants — or the field.** Some diseases carry *in the seed* (they are "seed-borne"), so seed from an infected crop plants the disease into next year. When disease has hit, take seed only from clearly healthy, unaffected plants (or an unaffected variety/bed), and lean on the [three-copy reserve](03_Heirloom_Seed_Banking.md) of clean seed you kept from a healthy year. **This is how a field disease is prevented from becoming a permanent variety loss.**
*   **Isolate the outbreak.** Work infected beds last; keep the tools, boots, and hands that touched them away from clean beds until cleaned. The same [quarantine logic](../05_Health_and_the_Body/11_Epidemic_and_Quarantine_Protocol.md) that protects people from epidemics protects fields from crop epidemics.
*   **Physical pest response.** Hand-picking, barriers, traps, encouraging predators, and — for a locust/armyworm-scale event — mass hand-response and protecting the most critical beds first. Diatomaceous earth and ash help against some insects; the [Ecological Harmony](../06_Ecological_Harmony/01_Rationale_and_Importance.md) module carries the coexistence detail.

## 6. When to Sacrifice a Crop

Sometimes a disease is winning and the honest question is not "how do I save this crop?" but "how do I stop this from taking everything?" The pre-agreed decision:

```text
   THE CROP-SACRIFICE DECISION
   ===========================
   Disease/pest spreading despite roguing?
        |
        |-- Is the SEED LINE still protected?   -> priority #1: secure
        |   (clean reserve seed + healthy plants)   clean seed FIRST,
        |                                            from unaffected
        |                                            plants/varieties/beds
        |
        |-- Can other crops/varieties be saved  -> sacrifice the losing
        |   by removing the losing one?             crop entirely; destroy
        |                                            it; protect the rest
        |
        +-- Is the winter RESERVE now short?    -> shift to the
            (harvest will miss target)              [First-Year Food
                                                     Security] responses:
                                                     fast crops, foraging,
                                                     ration, trade
```

The governing priority is the same as everywhere in the food system: **the seed line outranks the harvest.** A lost harvest is one hungry year the reserve and the network can bridge; a lost seed variety is forever. When a disease forces the choice, secure clean seed first, sacrifice the diseased crop without sentiment, and fall back on the [food-security](10_First_Year_Food_Security_and_Arrival_Timing.md) bridges for the calories.

## 7. Common Failure Modes

*   **Monoculture.** Planting one crop or one variety across the food base for yield or simplicity. **[Tier L] Consequence:** a single disease takes the entire harvest — the potato-famine failure. **Prevention:** diversity; no crop/variety over ~30% of the food base.
*   **Composting diseased material.** Returning rogued plants to the beds. **[Tier L] Consequence:** the disease overwinters and re-infects next year, sometimes worse. **Prevention:** burn or deep-bury infected material away from fields, always.
*   **Saving seed from a sick crop.** Taking next year's seed from an infected field. **[Tier L] Consequence:** seed-borne disease plants the epidemic into next season and can permanently ruin a variety. **Prevention:** seed only from healthy plants; keep the clean three-copy reserve.
*   **Waiting to see.** Letting a spreading disease run while hoping it stops. **[Tier C] Consequence:** a few-plant problem becomes a field loss in a wet week. **Prevention:** walk the fields regularly; rogue at first sight.
*   **Fighting a lost crop while the seed line dies.** Pouring effort into saving a doomed harvest and losing the clean seed with it. **[Tier L] Consequence:** permanent variety loss on top of the failed harvest. **Prevention:** the sacrifice decision — secure clean seed first, then let the crop go.

## 8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Landrace resistance:** Locally-adapted disease resistance bred slowly by saving seed from the plants that stayed healthy.
*   **Monoculture trap:** Reliance on a single crop/variety that lets one disease destroy the whole harvest; the potato-famine failure.
*   **Roguing:** Removing and destroying diseased plants (and a margin) to stop spread; the primary field response.
*   **Rotation:** Moving crop families through beds over years to break the build-up of soil-borne disease and pests.
*   **Seed-borne disease:** A disease carried within the seed, so that saving seed from infected plants plants the disease into next year.
*   **The seed line:** The settlement's ongoing supply of clean, viable seed; it outranks any single harvest because its loss is permanent.

## 9. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 9.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Preparation:** Plant diverse crops and varieties in rotation, well-spaced, in an ecologically balanced field; keep the clean three-copy seed reserve.
*   **Execution:** Walk the fields every few days; rogue and destroy at first sign; clean tools; take seed only from healthy plants; select for resistance each year.
*   **Review:** After any outbreak, log what hit which crop and how it was contained; adjust next year's varieties and rotation from that data.

### 9.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier
*   **[Tier L — Law] Monoculture / compost diseased material / save sick seed** → whole-harvest loss and permanent variety loss. *Diversity, destruction of infected material, and clean-seed discipline are non-negotiable.*
*   **[Tier L — Law] Fight the crop and lose the seed line** → forever loss. *Secure clean seed before saving any doomed harvest.*
*   **[Tier C — Craft] Exact rotation cycle, spacing, and when to rogue vs. watch** → calibrate to your crops, climate, and observed pressure; when unsure, act early.

### 9.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** The grower's eye — recognizing the first spots of blight or the first bore-holes — is a *trained* perception that must be taught plant by plant, and the reasoning behind diversity and roguing must pass down whole; a generation that forgets *why* it interplants and rotates drifts back toward the monoculture that starves it.
