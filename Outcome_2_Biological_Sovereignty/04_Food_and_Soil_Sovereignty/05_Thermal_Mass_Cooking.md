## 2. Zero-Tech Thermodynamics: The Rocket Stove

An open fire loses the vast majority of its heat to the surrounding air. A "Rocket Stove" uses an insulated, J-shaped combustion chamber to force all heat and unburned exhaust gases up a central chimney, creating a massive, clean-burning thermal updraft using only small twigs instead of whole logs.

### 2.1. Step-by-Step Construction (The J-Tube):
1. **The Core Trench:** Dig a small trench in the ground or use stacked mud-bricks to form a horizontal tunnel (the feed tube) about 6 inches wide and 12 inches long.
2. **The Riser (Chimney):** At the end of the feed tube, build a vertical chimney (the riser) out of high-clay mud/bricks, exactly three times the height of the horizontal feed tube (e.g., 36 inches tall). This ratio is critical for the "rocket" draft effect.
3. **Insulation (Crucial Step):** The vertical chimney must be heavily insulated. Pack a thick layer of wood ash, pumice, or dry clay mixed with straw *around* the outside of the chimney. This prevents heat from escaping sideways; it forces the internal temperature above 1000°F (540°C), incinerating the smoke itself.
4. **The Skirt:** Place a metal grate or a few stones on top of the chimney to hold your cooking pot. The gap between the pot and the chimney must be exactly equal to the cross-sectional area of the chimney to allow the exhaust to escape without choking the fire.
5. **Firing:** Feed small, dry twigs into the horizontal tube. The intense draft will pull the flames horizontally into the chimney. You can boil 5 gallons of water using only a handful of twigs.

## 3. The Cob Earth Oven (Baking via Thermal Mass)

While a Rocket Stove is for direct boiling, baking requires retained, radiant heat.

### 3.1. Step-by-Step Construction:
1. **The Plinth:** Build a solid, waist-high foundation of stone and gravel.
2. **The Insulation Layer:** Lay down a 4-inch layer of empty glass bottles (if scavenged) packed in clay, or a thick layer of pure wood ash. This stops the heat from sinking down into the foundation.
3. **The Hearth Floor:** Lay smooth, flat fire-bricks or solid sandstone tightly together over the insulation layer.
4. **The Sand Form:** Pile wet sand on the hearth floor into the shape of a perfect dome. Cover the sand dome with wet newspaper.
5. **The Thermal Mass (The Cob):** Mix 1 part clay, 3 parts sharp sand, and water. Pack this dense, heavy mud (Cob) 4 inches thick directly over the newspaper-covered sand dome. Leave a door opening at the front.
6. **The Insulation Coat:** Mix clay with a massive amount of straw. Pack this fuzzy, highly insulated mud 4-6 inches thick over the first Cob layer. 
7. **Excavation:** Once the mud has dried leather-hard, reach through the door and scoop all the sand and newspaper out, leaving a hollow mud cavern.
8. **Firing:** Build a fire *inside* the oven. The inner layer of dense Cob absorbs the heat (Thermal Mass), while the outer layer of straw-mud traps it inside. Once the fire burns down, sweep the ashes out, put your bread/food inside, and seal the door with a wooden plank. The radiant heat will bake bread for hours.

## 5. 🚀 Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 5.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Phase 1: The Tin-Can Prototype:** Do not build a massive clay rocket stove first. Take a large #10 catering tin can, cut a hole in the side, and insert a smaller soup can horizontally. Cut the bottom off another soup can and place it vertically inside to form the J-Tube. Fill the space between the inner cans and the outer #10 can with dry ash. Test its ability to boil water with just twigs.
*   **Phase 2: The Oven Plinth:** Before committing to the massive labor of mixing Cob mud, source and build the stone foundation (the Plinth) for your earth oven. It must be perfectly level and solid, as the final oven will weigh several hundred pounds.

### 5.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes
*   **The Stove Smokes Heavily:** A Rocket Stove should produce almost zero visible smoke once hot. If it smokes, either the chimney is not insulated enough (heat is bleeding out), or the gap under the cooking pot is too narrow, choking the airflow.
*   **Bread Burns on the Bottom:** If food burns instantly when placed in the Earth Oven, the fire was too hot, or you did not let the thermal mass "equalize" (letting the surface heat sink deeper into the cob) for 15 minutes after sweeping the ashes out.
