# Heirloom Seed Banking: Genetic Sovereignty and Adaptive Breeding

**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Of every asset a post-collapse settlement possesses, none is more irreplaceable than its seed stock. A forge can be rebuilt from river stone and salvaged steel. A shelter can be re-raised in a week. Medical knowledge, metallurgy, and mathematics can be re-derived from first principles by a literate community. But a lost crop variety — a landrace of drought-tolerant flint corn, a cold-adapted potato, a short-season dry bean — is gone forever. There is no workshop that can manufacture a genome. There is no amount of labor that can re-evolve 10,000 years of agricultural selection in a single human lifetime.

This asymmetry makes the seed bank the single most valuable physical object in the settlement. It is worth more than any tool, any building, any weapon. A community with good seed and bare earth can feed itself within one growing season. A community with every conceivable tool and no viable seed will starve. The logic is absolute.

Heirloom varieties — open-pollinated cultivars that breed true from saved seed — are the only path to genetic self-sufficiency. Modern F1 hybrids, however productive in their first generation, cannot be reproduced without their proprietary parent lines. They are, by design, a single-use technology. In a world without seed companies, supply chains, or intellectual property enforcement, the only seeds that matter are the ones that make more of themselves. This module provides the theoretical foundation and practical protocols to build, maintain, and improve a permanent seed bank capable of sustaining a settlement for generations.

> **SAFE**: Seed saving and storage involve no inherent hazards. Note: some crop plants have toxic parts (potato greens contain solanine, raw kidney beans contain phytohaemagglutinin). These are food safety concerns, not seed-saving concerns, and are addressed in [Module 06: Emergency Nutrition](06_Emergency_Nutrition.md).

## Theoretical Foundation

### Open-Pollinated vs. Hybrid: Why This Distinction Is Existential

The distinction between open-pollinated (OP) and F1 hybrid seed is not a preference — it is a survival-critical binary. Understanding why requires a brief excursion into genetics.

**Open-pollinated varieties** are produced by natural pollination — wind, insects, or self-fertilization. The parent plants are genetically diverse but stabilized: they share the same general characteristics (fruit size, maturity date, disease resistance) while maintaining enough internal variation to adapt over time. When you save seed from an OP tomato and plant it next year, the offspring are recognizably the same variety. They "breed true." This is the foundation of 10,000 years of agriculture — every crop your ancestors grew before 1930 was open-pollinated.

**F1 hybrid varieties** are produced by crossing two specific inbred parent lines (Line A x Line B). The first-generation (F1) offspring exhibit "hybrid vigor" (heterosis) — often 15-30% higher yield, greater uniformity, and enhanced disease resistance compared to either parent. This is why hybrids dominate modern commercial agriculture. However, the F2 generation — seeds saved from F1 plants — segregates unpredictably. Mendel's laws guarantee it: the carefully assembled gene combinations shatter. Some F2 plants revert toward Parent A, others toward Parent B, others display novel (and usually undesirable) recombinations. Yield drops 20-50%. Uniformity collapses. The variety, as a coherent entity, ceases to exist.

Hybrids dominate modern agriculture for two reasons: they genuinely produce higher yields under optimal conditions with optimal inputs (fertilizer, irrigation, pesticides), and they force farmers to purchase new seed every season — a powerful commercial incentive. Post-collapse, both reasons evaporate. There are no optimal inputs, and there are no seed companies. A settlement that plants F1 hybrid seed has exactly one harvest before its seed stock becomes genetically incoherent.

Heirloom varieties may yield 10-30% less than hybrids under industrial conditions. Under post-collapse conditions — marginal soil, no synthetic fertilizer, variable weather, hand labor — the gap narrows considerably, and landraces often outperform hybrids because they were selected for resilience rather than peak performance. More importantly, they are **genetically sovereign**: the settlement controls its own biological future, indefinitely.

### Pollination Biology and Isolation Distances

Cross-pollination between different varieties of the same species produces offspring that do not breed true. Understanding pollination mechanisms is essential to maintaining varietal purity.

**Self-pollinating crops** (tomato, pepper, bean, lettuce, pea, wheat, barley, rice): flowers pollinate themselves before or shortly after opening. The anthers shed pollen directly onto the stigma within the same flower. Cross-pollination rate under field conditions: typically less than 2-5%. Isolation distance needed: 3-10 meters — sufficient to prevent occasional mechanical transfer by foraging insects. These are the easiest crops for seed saving.

**Insect-pollinated crops** (squash, cucumber, melon, onion, carrot, brassicas): bees and other pollinators carry pollen between plants as they forage. If two varieties of the same species flower simultaneously within bee foraging range (typically 1-3 km), cross-pollination is near-certain. Isolation distance required: 500 m to 1.6 km depending on species and local pollinator density. Squash species (Cucurbita pepo, C. maxima, C. moschata) will cross within the same species but NOT between species — this is a critical distinction for planning.

**Wind-pollinated crops** (corn, beets, spinach, chard, amaranth): pollen is released in vast quantities and carried by air currents. Corn pollen has been detected 5+ km from the source plant, though effective pollination range is typically 300 m to 1 km. Isolation distance for corn: minimum 300 m, ideally 1 km or more. Beets, chard, and spinach (all Beta vulgaris or Chenopodiaceae) require 1-3 km isolation.

**Alternatives to spatial isolation:**

- **Temporal isolation**: stagger planting dates so that two varieties of the same crop do not flower simultaneously. A 2-3 week gap in flowering is usually sufficient. This requires knowledge of each variety's days-to-maturity.
- **Hand pollination with bagging**: for crops like squash, tape female flowers shut the evening before they open, hand-transfer pollen from the desired male flower in the morning, then re-tape. Laborious but effective for maintaining multiple varieties in a small area.
- **Physical barriers**: row covers or caging with fine mesh can exclude pollinators from specific plants. Useful for small-scale seed production.

### Minimum Viable Population for Seed Saving

Just as human populations require genetic diversity to avoid inbreeding depression (minimum viable population approximately 500 individuals; see Outcome 1 Section 01.05), crop populations need genetic diversity to maintain vigor across generations.

The minimum number of plants from which to save seed depends on the pollination mechanism:

| Pollination Type | Minimum Plants for Seed | Recommended | Consequence of Fewer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Self-pollinating (bean, tomato, pea, lettuce) | 5 | 10-25 | Slow decline over 10-20 generations |
| Insect-pollinated (squash, onion, carrot) | 25 | 50-100 | Noticeable decline in 5-10 generations |
| Wind-pollinated (corn, beet, amaranth) | 80 | 200+ | Rapid inbreeding depression in 3-5 generations |

Corn is exceptionally sensitive. A population of fewer than 200 plants for seed saving will show measurable inbreeding depression — reduced ear size, poor tip fill, decreased disease resistance — within 3-5 generations. This is because corn is an obligate outcrosser with strong inbreeding depression genetics. If the settlement cannot plant 200+ corn plants, it must exchange seed with allied settlements every 3-5 years to refresh genetic diversity.

### Seed Viability and Storage Science

Seeds are living embryos in a state of metabolic suspension. They respire, consume stored energy, and slowly die. The rate of decline is governed by two primary variables: moisture content and temperature.

**Harrington's Rule of Thumb**: For each 1% decrease in seed moisture content (within the range of 5-14%), storage life approximately doubles. For each 5 degrees C decrease in storage temperature (within the range of 0-50 degrees C), storage life approximately doubles. These effects are multiplicative.

**The "100 Rule"**: A practical shorthand — seed moisture percentage plus storage temperature in degrees Fahrenheit should sum to less than 100 for viable long-term storage. Example: 8% moisture + 60 degrees F (15.5 degrees C) = 68, well under 100.

**Viability by species** (approximate years under good storage conditions: 8% moisture, 10-15 degrees C, airtight container):

| Viability Class | Crops | Storage Life |
|---|---|---|
| Short-lived (1-2 years) | Onion, parsnip, sweet corn, parsley | Replace annually |
| Medium (3-5 years) | Bean, pea, carrot, pepper, lettuce, spinach | Replace every 3 years |
| Long-lived (5-8 years) | Tomato, squash, cucumber, melon, beet, radish | Replace every 5 years |
| Very long-lived (8-12 years) | Brassicas (cabbage, kale, broccoli, turnip), eggplant | Replace every 8 years |
| Exceptional (15+ years) | Wheat, barley, some legumes under optimal conditions | Test annually after year 10 |

**Desiccant options for post-collapse conditions:**

- **Silica gel** (ideal): absorbs moisture to approximately 40% of its own weight. Can be regenerated by heating to 120 degrees C for 2 hours. If available from salvage, prioritize for seed storage.
- **Dry rice** (adequate): place 1 tablespoon of oven-dried rice per jar. Replace every 6 months.
- **Powdered milk** (adequate): wrap 2 tablespoons in cloth, place in jar. Replace every 6 months.
- **Wood ash** (emergency): highly hygroscopic, absorbs moisture effectively, but fine particles may coat seeds. Wrap in cloth before placing in jar.

**Container options:**
- Glass jars with rubber gasket lids (best) — airtight and rodent-proof
- Sealed metal tins with soldered lids — airtight and rodent-proof
- Vacuum-sealed bags inside a rigid container — airtight but not rodent-proof alone
- Wax-sealed clay pots — traditional method, effective if seal is complete

### Selection Pressure: Breeding Better Crops Each Year

Every time you save seed, you are applying selection pressure. The question is not whether you select — it is whether you select intentionally or accidentally.

**Positive selection (intentional):** Identify the best-performing plants in each crop — earliest to mature, highest yield, most disease-resistant, best flavor, most vigorous — and save seed exclusively from those individuals. Mark these plants clearly (a colored string or stake) BEFORE the general harvest begins, so they are never accidentally eaten. Allow their fruit to fully ripen on the plant; seeds from immature fruit have lower viability and vigor. Over 5-10 generations of consistent positive selection, the variety adapts to the settlement's specific microclimate, soil, and growing conditions. This is how every landrace in human history was created.

**Negative selection (accidental):** The community eats the best-looking, earliest-ripening produce and saves seed from whatever remains at the end — the late, the small, the overlooked. Over 5-10 generations, this selects for lateness, small size, and poor performance. The variety degrades. This is the default outcome when seed saving is treated casually rather than as a discipline.

The difference between these two approaches, compounded over a decade, is the difference between a settlement with improving crop performance and one with declining yields. Selection is the settlement's only breeding tool. Use it deliberately.

## Core Principles

1. **The 10% Seed Tithe.** Save seed from a minimum of 10% of each crop's planting — ideally 20% for cross-pollinating species. This seed stock is sacred. It is never eaten in a hungry month, never traded carelessly, never stored in a secondary location as an afterthought. It is the biological inheritance of the next generation. A season without food is survivable. A season without seed is terminal.

2. **The Three-Copy Rule.** Maintain three copies of every seed variety: (a) the active planting stock, stored near the growing area for convenience; (b) a sealed reserve stored in the coolest, driest location available (root cellar, buried cache, north-facing stone chamber); (c) a trade copy, smaller in quantity, for exchange with allied settlements. If any copy is lost to rodents, flooding, or human error, rebuild it immediately from the surviving copies.

3. **The Isolation Imperative.** Cross-pollinating crops must be isolated by the appropriate distance or timing. One season of uncontrolled cross-pollination between two squash varieties, or two corn varieties, can contaminate a seed line permanently. The resulting seeds will not breed true, and the original variety cannot be recovered from the cross. Isolation is not optional — it is the price of varietal integrity.

4. **The Selection Discipline.** Always save seed from the best plants, marked before the general harvest. Never save seed from the last fruit picked, the sickly plant, the convenient volunteer, or the runt at the end of the row. Every act of seed saving is an act of breeding. Breed deliberately.

5. **The Viability Testing Rule.** Before each planting season, test the germination rate of every stored seed lot: place 10 seeds on a damp cloth in a warm location (20-25 degrees C), keep moist, and count sprouts after 7-10 days. If germination falls below 70%, plant the entire remaining stock that season — before it dies completely — and prioritize saving fresh seed from the resulting crop. A seed lot with 50% germination is not "half good"; it is an emergency requiring immediate action.

## Practical Implementation

### The Winnowing Basket: Construction and Use

The winnowing basket is the seed saver's primary tool for cleaning dry-seeded crops — separating grain from chaff by exploiting the difference in weight and aerodynamic drag.

**Materials:**
- Flexible rods: thin willow, hazel, split bamboo, or any pliable wood approximately 3-5 mm diameter
- Binder: natural fiber twine, split rattan, or thin strips of bark

**Construction:**
1. Soak rods in water for 24 hours to increase pliability.
2. Weave a shallow, wide-mouthed tray approximately 50 cm in diameter and 5-8 cm deep.
3. The weave should be tight in the center (to retain small seeds) but slightly more open near the rim (to allow chaff to escape when tossed).
4. Finish the rim by wrapping a thicker rod around the edge and lashing it securely — this provides structural rigidity and a comfortable grip.

**Use by crop type:**
- **Grains (wheat, barley, amaranth, quinoa):** Thresh seed heads by rubbing between hands or beating with a stick on a clean tarp. Transfer to the winnowing basket. On a day with steady light breeze (5-10 km/h), toss seeds 30-50 cm into the air repeatedly. Heavy seeds fall back into the basket; light chaff blows away. Repeat 10-15 times until seeds are clean.
- **Dry beans and peas:** After threshing pods, winnow to remove pod fragments. Beans are heavy enough that even moderate wind works well.
- **Small seeds (lettuce, carrot, brassicas):** Require gentle winnowing — toss only 10-15 cm in very light breeze. Alternatively, use a series of screens with decreasing mesh sizes to sieve out debris.

### Seed Saving by Crop Type

**Dry-seed crops (beans, peas, corn, grains, sunflower, amaranth):**
1. Allow pods, ears, or seed heads to dry completely on the plant. They should rattle when shaken; pods should shatter when bent.
2. Harvest the entire seed head or pull entire plants and hang upside down in a dry, ventilated location for 1-2 weeks if field drying is incomplete.
3. Thresh: beat with a flail or stick on a clean surface, or rub between hands (for small lots).
4. Winnow using the basket described above to remove chaff and debris.
5. Spread cleaned seeds in a single layer on a screen or cloth in shade with good air circulation for 1-2 additional weeks to reach approximately 8% moisture (seeds should crack when bitten, not dent).
6. Store in airtight containers with desiccant. Label with variety, date, and source.

**Wet-seed crops (tomato, cucumber, melon, squash):**
1. Allow fruit to over-ripen on the plant — past the eating stage. Tomatoes should be soft and beginning to wrinkle. Cucumbers should be large, yellow, and starting to soften. Squash should have a hard, woody stem.
2. Cut fruit open and scoop seeds with surrounding gel into a container.
3. **Fermentation (tomatoes and cucumbers):** Add equal volume of water to the seed-gel mixture. Cover loosely and let sit at room temperature (20-25 degrees C) for 2-3 days. Stir once daily. A white mold will form on the surface — this is normal. The fermentation destroys germination-inhibiting compounds on the seed coat and kills several seed-borne bacterial and fungal diseases. When the viable seeds sink to the bottom and the pulp floats, fermentation is complete.
4. Pour off floating pulp and non-viable seeds. Rinse remaining seeds in clean water through a fine strainer, 3-4 times.
5. Spread clean seeds on a non-stick surface (glass, ceramic plate, wax paper — NOT paper towel, which seeds will bond to) in shade. Dry for 1-2 weeks, stirring daily to prevent clumping.
6. Store in airtight containers with desiccant.

**Note for squash and melon:** Fermentation is not needed. Simply rinse seeds clean of pulp, dry, and store.

**Root crops (carrot, beet, turnip, parsnip, onion) — BIENNIAL CYCLE:**
1. **Year 1:** Grow the root crop to full maturity. In autumn, before the ground freezes (or before the dry season in tropical climates), carefully harvest the best roots — selecting for size, shape, uniformity, and absence of disease.
2. **Winter storage:** Store selected roots in a root cellar or underground cache at 2-10 degrees C and high humidity (85-95%). Pack in damp sand or sawdust to prevent desiccation. Inspect monthly; remove any rotting specimens immediately.
3. **Year 2:** In spring, replant the stored roots at the same depth they originally grew, spacing 30-45 cm apart. They will bolt (send up a flower stalk), bloom, and set seed over the following 2-3 months.
4. Harvest seed heads when they turn brown and dry. Cut entire stalks and hang upside down over a tarp to catch shattering seeds.
5. Thresh and winnow. Carrot and parsnip seeds have small barbs — rub between gloved hands to separate.
6. This two-year cycle is the most challenging aspect of seed saving. It requires advance planning, adequate root storage, and the discipline to sacrifice good eating roots for next year's seed. Failure to complete this cycle means losing the variety.

**Tuber crops (potato, Jerusalem artichoke, garlic):**
These crops are propagated vegetatively — clonally — not from true seed. Save the best tubers or cloves from each harvest and replant them. This is genetically simpler than seed saving but carries a risk: diseases accumulate in clonal lines (especially potato viruses). Every 5-10 years, grow potatoes from true botanical seed (the small fruits that form on potato plants after flowering) to reset disease load, or exchange tubers with distant settlements.

### The Seed Vault: Storage Construction

The seed vault need not be elaborate, but it must satisfy four requirements:

**Cool:** Every 5 degrees C reduction in storage temperature doubles seed life. The coolest available location is therefore ideal: a root cellar (typically 8-12 degrees C), a north-facing stone chamber, an underground cache below the frost line, or a shaded area with thermal mass. Even partial cooling — storing seeds against a stone foundation wall rather than in a warm cabin — provides measurable benefit.

**Dry:** Target less than 40% relative humidity at the storage point. Achieve this by sealing all containers airtight with desiccant inside. In humid climates, double-containment is advisable: sealed jars inside a sealed larger container, both with desiccant.

**Dark:** Light degrades some seed coats over time and can trigger premature metabolic activity. Store containers in opaque boxes or in a dark room.

**Rodent-proof:** Mice and rats will find and consume unprotected seed stores with absolute reliability. Glass jars, metal tins, and heavy ceramic crocks are rodent-proof. Cloth bags, paper envelopes, and plastic bags are not. Assume any container that a rodent can chew through, a rodent will chew through.

**Organization:** Label every container with: variety name, year saved, source location, number of plants seed was saved from, and most recent germination test result. An unlabeled jar of seed is useless — you cannot plan isolation distances, track viability, or make breeding decisions without records.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Cross-Pollination Contamination.** Two squash varieties planted within bee range cross-pollinate undetected. Saved seeds produce inedible or unrecognizable fruit the following year. The original variety is lost. **Prevention:** Maintain species-appropriate isolation distances. When in doubt, grow only one variety of each cross-pollinating species. Test questionable seed by growing a small trial plot before committing the full planting.

2. **Seed Death in Storage.** Seeds stored in a warm, humid location (a cabin shelf, an unsealed container) lose viability within 1-3 years instead of the expected 5-10. The settlement discovers the loss only at planting time, when germination fails. **Prevention:** Follow Harrington's Rule. Use desiccant in every container. Store in the coolest available location. Test germination of every seed lot every winter — never assume viability.

3. **Genetic Erosion Through Inbreeding.** A settlement saves corn seed from 20-30 plants year after year. By generation 5, ears are small, tip fill is poor, and disease susceptibility has increased. The population has lost allelic diversity below the minimum viable threshold. **Prevention:** Maintain minimum population sizes (see table above). For corn, 200 plants is the floor. Exchange seed with allied settlements every 3-5 years to introduce new alleles.

4. **Accidental Negative Selection.** The community eats the largest, earliest, best-flavored produce all season and saves seed from whatever is left — the late, the small, the tough. After 5-10 years, the variety has shifted toward those undesirable traits. **Prevention:** Mark seed-saving plants BEFORE the general harvest. Those plants are off-limits for food. Selection must be active, not passive.

5. **Biennial Seed Loss.** The settlement grows excellent carrots and beets in Year 1 but fails to store roots over winter for Year 2 seed production — either because root cellar storage failed, or because hungry people ate the seed stock. The variety is lost. **Prevention:** Plan biennial seed crops one full year in advance. Allocate root cellar space specifically for seed roots. Physically segregate seed stock from food stock. Treat biennial seed roots as inventory that belongs to next year, not this year.

6. **Single-Copy Loss.** The settlement's only jar of a particular variety is destroyed by rodents, flooding, a dropped jar, or simple misplacement. **Prevention:** The Three-Copy Rule (Principle 2). No variety should ever exist in fewer than two physical copies in two separate locations.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Open-Pollinated (OP):** A variety that reproduces true-to-type from saved seed, maintained by natural pollination and human selection.
- **F1 Hybrid:** The first-generation offspring of a cross between two inbred parent lines, exhibiting hybrid vigor but unable to breed true in subsequent generations.
- **Heirloom:** An open-pollinated variety with a documented history of cultivation, typically predating the hybrid era (pre-1950). Often associated with specific regions, cultures, or families.
- **Genetic Sovereignty:** The condition in which a community controls its own crop genetics without dependence on external seed sources.
- **Isolation Distance:** The minimum spatial separation between varieties of the same species required to prevent uncontrolled cross-pollination.
- **Cross-Pollination:** The transfer of pollen from one plant to a genetically distinct plant of the same species, producing offspring with mixed genetics.
- **Self-Pollination:** The transfer of pollen within the same flower or between flowers on the same plant, producing offspring genetically similar to the parent.
- **Winnowing:** The process of separating grain from chaff by tossing into moving air, exploiting the difference in weight.
- **Threshing:** The mechanical separation of seeds from their enclosing structures (pods, husks, seed heads) by beating, rubbing, or impact.
- **Desiccant:** A hygroscopic substance used to absorb moisture from the air within a sealed container, reducing humidity and extending seed life.
- **Germination Rate:** The percentage of seeds in a sample that successfully sprout under favorable conditions, a measure of seed lot viability.
- **Seed Viability:** The capacity of a seed to germinate and produce a normal seedling, which declines over time as a function of storage conditions.
- **Biennial:** A plant that completes its life cycle over two growing seasons — vegetative growth in Year 1, flowering and seed production in Year 2.
- **Vavilov Center:** One of the regions of the world identified by Nikolai Vavilov as a center of origin for cultivated plants, where the greatest genetic diversity of crop species is found.
- **Genetic Erosion:** The loss of allelic diversity within a crop population due to small population size, drift, or directional selection, resulting in reduced adaptability and vigor.
