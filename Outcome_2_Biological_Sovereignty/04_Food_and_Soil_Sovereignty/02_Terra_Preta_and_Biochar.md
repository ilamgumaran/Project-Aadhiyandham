# Terra Preta and Biochar: Building Permanent Soil Fertility

**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Terra Preta do Indio — the "dark earth of the Indians" — is the single most successful soil-building technology in recorded human history. Across the Amazon basin, soils created by pre-Columbian civilizations between 1000 BC and 1500 AD still outperform the surrounding oxisols by factors of two to three in crop yield. These soils were not maintained after European contact caused population collapse in the 1500s. They have been untended for five centuries, and they are still fertile. No other soil amendment technology on Earth can make that claim.

The active ingredient is biochar: biomass that has been thermally decomposed in the absence of oxygen (pyrolysis) to produce a stable carbon matrix with extraordinary physical properties. One gram of well-made biochar has an internal surface area of 200 to 400 square metres — roughly the floor area of a two-storey house. That surface hosts microbial colonies, holds water against gravity, and retains dissolved nutrients through cation exchange. When biochar is properly "charged" with nutrients and microbes and then mixed into soil, the result is a permanent improvement in soil fertility that persists for centuries to millennia.

This module teaches the complete Terra Preta replication workflow using zero-technology pyrolysis methods: feedstock selection, cone-pit and trench-kiln production, the charging protocol, and Terra Preta bed construction. The methods require no equipment beyond hand tools, fire, and water. A two-person team working two days per week can produce approximately eight tonnes of biochar per year — enough to convert half a hectare of degraded land into permanently fertile soil annually.

> **DANGER**: Biochar production involves open fire and the management of extremely hot materials (400-700 C). All pyrolysis operations must be conducted in a cleared area (minimum 3 m radius of bare soil), with water or sand for quenching available, and with at least two operators present. Burns from pyrolysis are severe — molten biochar adheres to skin. Never reach into an active cone pit. Never quench biochar by leaning over the pit. Pour water from the rim. See [07. Pyrolysis Engineering and TLUD Gasifiers](07_Pyrolysis_Engineering_and_TLUD_Gasifiers.md) for full safety protocols.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Chemistry of Pyrolysis

Pyrolysis is the thermal decomposition of organic matter in the absence or near-absence of oxygen. Unlike combustion — which converts carbon to CO2 and ash — pyrolysis converts solid biomass into three products: solid biochar (stable carbon), combustible gases (syngas), and condensable liquids (bio-oil and wood vinegar). The ratio of these products depends on temperature, heating rate, and residence time.

The temperature ranges and their products are as follows:

- **200-300 C (torrefaction)**: Water and volatile organic compounds are driven off. The biomass turns brown and becomes brittle. This is incomplete pyrolysis; the product is "brown char" with low carbon stability and low surface area. It is not true biochar and will decompose in soil within years.
- **300-500 C (primary pyrolysis)**: Cellulose and lignin decompose. The biomass turns black as aromatic carbon structures form. This is the zone that produces true biochar — a material that is chemically and biologically stable. The majority of syngas and bio-oil is released in this range.
- **500-700 C (high-temperature pyrolysis)**: Carbon structures become increasingly ordered and graphitic. Surface area reaches its maximum (300-400 m2/g). Volatile content drops. The biochar is maximally stable but has lower initial cation exchange capacity because surface functional groups (carboxyl, hydroxyl) are destroyed at these temperatures.
- **Above 700 C (gasification)**: Carbon itself begins to react with CO2 and H2O through the Boudouard and water-gas reactions. Biochar yield drops sharply as the solid carbon is converted to gas. This is undesirable for biochar production but is the principle behind gasification for energy.

The optimal temperature range for Terra Preta biochar is 400-600 C. This balances carbon stability (half-life of 1,000+ years) with retained surface functional groups that provide immediate cation exchange capacity. At these temperatures, approximately 20-35% of dry biomass weight is converted to biochar. The remaining 65-80% is released as combustible gases (CO, H2, CH4 — collectively called syngas) and condensable vapours. In a well-managed flame-cap kiln, these gases burn above the char bed, providing the heat to drive continued pyrolysis — the process is partially self-sustaining.

### The Physics of Biochar's Soil Benefits

Biochar improves soil through five distinct physical and chemical mechanisms:

**Surface area.** Biochar produced at 400-600 C has an internal surface area of 200-400 m2/g, comparable to commercial activated carbon. One kilogram of biochar provides the total surface area of 50 to 100 football fields (25-50 hectares). This surface is colonised by bacteria, archaea, and fungi. A single gram of biochar-amended soil can harbour 10 billion microbial cells — roughly double the population density of unamended soil. The micropores (less than 2 micrometres) provide refuge where bacteria are protected from predation by protozoa and nematodes, allowing populations to stabilise.

**Cation exchange capacity (CEC).** CEC measures a material's ability to adsorb and release positively charged nutrient ions (ammonium NH4+, potassium K+, calcium Ca2+, magnesium Mg2+). Fresh biochar has a CEC of 5-20 cmol(+)/kg — modest but not negligible. Over 6 to 12 months in soil, oxidation of the biochar surface creates carboxyl and phenolic groups that dramatically increase CEC to 40-150 cmol(+)/kg. For reference: sandy soil has a CEC of 3-5, clay soil 15-40, and humus 100-300 cmol(+)/kg. Biochar permanently elevates the nutrient-holding capacity of any soil it is added to.

**Water retention.** The microporous structure of biochar holds water against gravitational drainage. Sandy soils amended with 10% biochar by volume retain 18-25% more plant-available water than unamended controls (Glaser et al., 2002). In practical terms, this can extend the interval between required irrigation or rainfall events by 2-4 days during the growing season — a significant margin in dryland agriculture.

**pH buffering.** Most biochar is alkaline, with a pH of 7-10 depending on feedstock and production temperature. Applied to acidic soils (pH below 5.5 — common in tropical and temperate forest regions), biochar raises pH toward the neutral range where most nutrients are maximally plant-available. The buffering effect persists for years because the alkalinity is bound in the mineral ash fraction and the carbonate groups of the biochar itself.

**Carbon stability.** The aromatic carbon lattice of properly produced biochar resists microbial decomposition. Measured and modelled half-lives range from 500 to 5,000 years depending on production temperature, soil conditions, and climate (Lehmann et al., 2006). At the conservative end of that range, biochar applied today will still be functional in the year 2500. This is effectively permanent carbon sequestration — and permanent soil improvement.

### The Archaeological Evidence: Amazonian Terra Preta

Terra Preta do Indio is found across the Amazon basin, covering an estimated 10% of the total Amazon forest area — approximately 50,000 square kilometres. These soils were created by pre-Columbian civilizations between approximately 1000 BC and 1500 AD through the deliberate and sustained addition of charcoal, pottery shards, bone fragments, fish bones, and organic waste to the naturally infertile oxisols of the Amazon.

The resulting soils are 1-2 metres deep with up to 70 times the carbon content of surrounding oxisols (Lehmann et al., 2003). They are visually distinctive: deep black, with a granular structure quite unlike the orange-red clay of the native soil. Archaeological evidence (pottery styles, radiocarbon dating of the charcoal layers) indicates that Terra Preta was built incrementally over centuries, with each generation adding to the soil capital of previous generations.

These soils remain fertile after 500 years of zero maintenance. The biochar is still functional, the microbial communities still diverse, the CEC still elevated. Terra Preta supports 2-3 times the crop yield of adjacent non-amended soils in controlled trials (Glaser, 2007). Population density estimates based on Terra Preta extent suggest 5-10 million people were supported by intensive agriculture on what was previously assumed to be "virgin" rainforest.

The lesson is unambiguous: biochar-based soil amendment is the only proven technology for creating permanently fertile soil from infertile substrates using locally available materials and no external inputs.

### Biochar Feedstock Selection

Not all biomass produces equal biochar. Feedstock density, lignin content, and mineral composition determine the quality of the final product.

- **Hardwood (oak, hickory, maple, eucalyptus)**: Dense, high-carbon biochar with excellent mechanical structure. Retains its porous architecture in soil for centuries. This is the preferred feedstock for Terra Preta construction.
- **Softwood (pine, spruce, cedar)**: Produces lighter biochar with higher microporosity but lower bulk density. Acceptable for soil amendment but less durable under mechanical stress (tillage, foot traffic).
- **Agricultural residue (corn stalks, rice husks, straw, nut shells)**: Produces lightweight biochar with higher ash content. Lower carbon density per unit volume but excellent for bulk amendment of sandy soils. Rice husk biochar in particular has high silica content, which benefits cereal crops.
- **Bone**: Produces bone char — a calcium phosphate matrix with 15-20% available phosphorus. Not a true carbon biochar but an excellent phosphorus source. Mix bone char at 5-10% by weight with standard wood biochar for a balanced amendment.
- **Bamboo**: Excellent feedstock where available. High silica content, dense structure, rapid regrowth. Produces biochar comparable to hardwood.
- **Materials to avoid**: Treated or painted wood (toxic heavy metals persist in biochar), plastic-contaminated waste (produces toxic polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons), high-salt materials (damages soil structure), diseased plant material (some pathogens survive low-temperature pyrolysis).

## Core Principles

**1. The Charge-Before-Use Rule.** Raw fresh biochar is hydrophobic and nutrient-poor. Its vast surface area is empty — and it will fill that surface area by adsorbing whatever nutrients are already present in your soil. Applied directly to productive soil without charging, fresh biochar temporarily REDUCES fertility by stripping dissolved nitrogen, potassium, and phosphorus from the soil solution. Biochar must be "charged" (pre-loaded with nutrients and microbes) by soaking in compost, urine, or compost tea for a minimum of two weeks before soil application. This is non-negotiable.

**2. The 10% Volume Rule.** The optimal biochar application rate for most soils is 5-10% by volume in the top 15-20 cm. This translates to approximately 10-20 tonnes per hectare (dry weight). Higher rates provide diminishing returns and may cause nitrogen immobilisation if the biochar is not fully charged. Lower rates (2-5%) still provide measurable benefit but take longer to reach Terra Preta-level fertility.

**3. The Permanence Principle.** Biochar in soil is effectively permanent. Unlike compost — which decomposes in 1-3 years and must be continuously reapplied — biochar is a one-time capital investment in soil infrastructure. Once applied at the target rate, that soil has been permanently improved. All subsequent fertility inputs (compost, urine, manure) will be retained more efficiently by the biochar matrix.

**4. The Temperature-Quality Tradeoff.** Higher pyrolysis temperature (500-600 C) produces more stable biochar with higher surface area but lower initial CEC and nutrient content (surface functional groups are destroyed). Lower temperature (350-450 C) produces biochar with higher initial CEC but lower long-term stability. For Terra Preta construction, 400-500 C represents the optimal balance. In practical terms with flame-cap kilns: a vigorous, sustained burn with visible blue-purple pyrolysis flames indicates the 400-500 C range.

**5. The Crush-and-Mix Rule.** Biochar must be crushed to fragments of 2-10 mm before soil application. Larger pieces create air pockets that dry out root zones and are too coarse for root hairs to interface with. Smaller pieces (dust) pack too tightly, reducing drainage and aeration. Crush biochar while it is still warm from quenching — warm biochar is brittle and fractures easily along its natural pore structure. Cold biochar is harder and more laborious to crush.

## Practical Implementation

### Method 1: The Cone Pit (Simplest, No Equipment)

The cone pit is the simplest biochar production method. It requires only a shovel, a source of biomass, and water for quenching. The conical shape and the "flame cap" principle ensure that pyrolysis occurs in the lower zone while combustion of pyrolysis gases occurs only at the top surface, protecting the char below from oxygen.

**Pit construction:**
1. Select a site with minimum 3 m cleared radius of bare soil or mineral ground, away from structures and overhanging branches.
2. Dig a cone-shaped pit: 1 m diameter at the top rim, tapering to a point at 1 m depth. The cone shape is critical — it minimises the surface area exposed to air relative to the volume of char being produced.
3. Smooth the pit walls. Remove roots that protrude into the pit (they will burn and create air channels).

**The burn sequence:**
1. Build a small kindling fire at the bottom of the pit. Let it establish fully (10-15 minutes).
2. Add the first layer of biomass: 5-10 cm of dry wood pieces (wrist-thickness or smaller). Arrange loosely to allow air flow.
3. Wait for the layer to ignite fully. Flames should cover the entire surface — this is the "flame cap." The flame cap consumes oxygen and pyrolysis gases at the surface while the material below is oxygen-starved and pyrolysing.
4. When the flames begin to die down and the layer shows glowing red-orange coals, add the next 5-10 cm layer of biomass immediately. Do not wait for the coals to ash over.
5. Repeat: each new layer ignites from the coals below, re-establishes the flame cap, and the previous layer continues to char beneath it.
6. Monitor flame colour: yellow-orange flames indicate combustion of solid biomass (normal during ignition of each new layer). Blue-purple flames indicate combustion of pyrolysis gases (the desired steady state). If flames disappear entirely, the fire is oxygen-starved — add a thinner layer or wait.
7. Continue layering until the pit is full of glowing char to within 10-15 cm of the rim.

**Quenching:**
1. Flood the pit with water. Pour from the rim — never lean over the pit. The quench will produce a violent steam eruption.
2. Add water until all hissing and steaming stops and the char is fully submerged.
3. Allow to cool for 1 hour minimum before handling.
4. Do NOT let the char cool in air without quenching. Exposure to air while hot will convert char to ash through continued oxidation. This is the single most common cause of biochar production failure.

```
            THE CONE PIT KILN (Cross-Section)

            ~~ ~~ ~~ ~~ ~~ ~~ ~~         Smoke and steam
              \  FLAME CAP   /            (pyrolysis gases
               \ ~ ~ ~ ~ ~ /              burning at surface)
                \  fresh  /
                 \biomass/    <--- 5-10 cm layer just added
                  \_ _ _/
                  |     |
                  |CHAR |     <--- Previous layers:
                  |     |         black, glowing, 400-500 C
                  | _ _ |         (oxygen-starved = pyrolysis)
                  |/   \|
                  | HOT |     <--- Earliest layers:
                  |CHAR |         fully charred, cooling
                   \   /
                    \ /
                     V

            |<- 1 m ->|      Depth: 1 m
            |         |
       - - -+---------+- - -  Ground level
            |         |
            Bare soil perimeter: 3 m minimum
```

**Yield and timing:**
- A 1 m cone pit produces approximately 50-80 kg of biochar per batch (depending on feedstock density and moisture content).
- Total burn time: 3-6 hours for a full pit.
- Two operators can manage 2 batches per day if biomass is pre-staged.
- Weekly output (2 days of production): approximately 200-320 kg.

### Method 2: The Trench Kiln (Higher Volume)

The trench kiln applies the same flame-cap principle in a linear format, allowing the processing of longer pieces of wood without cross-cutting. It is the preferred method when clearing land for settlement, as felled trees and branches can be processed directly with minimal preparation.

**Trench construction:**
1. Dig a trench 0.5 m wide, 0.5 m deep, and 2-3 m long. Orient the long axis perpendicular to the prevailing wind to minimise wind-driven oxygen intrusion.
2. Walls should be vertical and smooth.

**Burn protocol:**
1. Light a kindling fire along the full length of the trench bottom.
2. Lay long pieces of wood (up to 2-3 m) across the trench width, resting on the trench edges. As the bottom layer chars, the pieces sag into the trench.
3. Maintain the flame cap by adding new layers of wood as each previous layer chars.
4. Quench by flooding when the trench is full of glowing char.

**Yield:** 100-150 kg per batch from a 3 m trench. Burn time: 4-8 hours.

**Advantages over the cone pit:** Processes longer pieces without cross-cutting, higher throughput per session, easier to quench (water flows along the trench). **Disadvantages:** Requires level ground, harder to dig in rocky soil, and the linear shape is more exposed to wind-driven oxygen intrusion.

### Method 3: The TLUD Gasifier (Most Efficient)

The Top-Lit Up-Draft (TLUD) gasifier is a dual-output device that produces biochar and cooking heat simultaneously from a single fuel load. The pyrolysis gases are burned in a secondary combustion zone above the fuel bed, providing a clean, hot flame suitable for cooking while the solid residue becomes biochar.

This method is covered in detail in [07. Pyrolysis Engineering and TLUD Gasifiers](07_Pyrolysis_Engineering_and_TLUD_Gasifiers.md). For Terra Preta construction, the TLUD is most valuable as a daily biochar accumulator: each cooking session produces 0.5-1.5 kg of biochar as a byproduct. Over a year, a household cooking exclusively on a TLUD gasifier can accumulate 200-500 kg of biochar — a meaningful contribution to soil building.

### The Soil Sieve

Before mixing biochar into soil, both the biochar and the base soil must be processed to a consistent particle size. A soil sieve removes rocks, roots, and debris from subsoil, and can also be used to grade crushed biochar.

**Construction:**
1. Build a square frame from four timber planks, approximately 60 cm on each side.
2. Stretch 6 mm (1/4 inch) hardware cloth or heavy-duty wire mesh across the bottom of the frame. Staple or nail securely to all four sides.
3. Attach handles to two opposite sides (longer planks or separate wood grips).

**Use:** Place the sieve over a wheelbarrow or tarp. Shovel material onto the mesh and shake with a rocking motion. Fine material passes through; oversized pieces remain on top. Sieve the crushed biochar first (discard or re-crush pieces larger than 10 mm), then sieve the base soil to remove rocks and roots before mixing.

### The Charging Protocol

Charging is the process of pre-loading biochar with nutrients and microbial inoculant. This step converts raw biochar from a nutrient sink into a nutrient source.

1. **Crush** the quenched biochar to 2-10 mm fragments. Spread on a hard surface and crush with a wooden mallet, flat stone, or by stomping. Sieve to remove dust (below 1 mm) and oversized pieces (above 10 mm).
2. **Prepare the charging vessel.** Any watertight container works: a barrel, a pit lined with clay or tarp, or a large trough. The vessel must hold the biochar submerged in liquid.
3. **Combine biochar and compost.** Mix one volume of crushed biochar with one volume of mature compost. Humanure compost that has completed its 12-month thermophilic cycle (see [05. Humanure Integration](../03_Water_and_Sanitation/05_Humanure_Integration.md)) is ideal, as it is rich in both nutrients and diverse microbial communities. Standard garden compost is also suitable.
4. **Add diluted urine** as a nitrogen and phosphorus source. Dilute urine 1:3 with water, then add enough to wet the biochar-compost mixture to the consistency of a wrung-out sponge. Urine supplies ammonium nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, and trace minerals. Approximately 5-10 litres of diluted urine per 50 kg of biochar.
5. **Mix thoroughly.** The biochar should be uniformly coated with compost and evenly moist throughout. No dry pockets.
6. **Cover and incubate.** Cover with a tarp or lid to retain moisture and warmth. Let sit for a minimum of 2 weeks in warm weather (above 20 C ambient) or 4 weeks in cool weather (below 15 C). Turn the pile once per week to ensure even colonisation.
7. **Quality check.** Properly charged biochar should: (a) smell earthy, like forest soil — not ammonia or putrid; (b) absorb water readily when a handful is dropped into a bucket of water — it should sink within 30 seconds, not float; (c) show visible white fungal mycelium threads when a handful is broken apart. If the biochar still floats, extend the charging period by one week.

### Terra Preta Bed Construction

This protocol creates a permanent raised bed with Terra Preta-level fertility. Once constructed, the bed requires only annual compost top-dressing and mulch renewal — the biochar component is permanent.

1. **Mark the bed.** Dimensions: 1.2 m wide (reachable from both sides without stepping on the bed) by desired length. Leave 0.5 m paths between beds.
2. **Double-dig to 40 cm depth.** Remove the top 20 cm of soil and set aside. Loosen the next 20 cm with a fork without inverting it. If adopting a no-till approach, sheet-mulch over existing vegetation with cardboard and compost instead of digging.
3. **Mix charged biochar into the top soil.** Combine the reserved top 20 cm of soil with charged biochar at a rate of 10% by volume. For a bed 1.2 m wide and 3 m long, this is approximately 70 litres of charged biochar (roughly 35-50 kg depending on density). Mix thoroughly — no pockets of pure biochar or pure soil.
4. **Return the amended soil** to the bed. The bed surface will be slightly raised (5-10 cm) above path level due to the added biochar volume.
5. **Top-dress with 5 cm of mature compost.** This provides immediately available nutrients for the first planting.
6. **Mulch with 10 cm of straw, leaves, or wood chips.** Mulch suppresses weeds, retains moisture, moderates soil temperature, and feeds soil organisms as it decomposes.
7. **First planting: nitrogen-fixing cover crop.** Sow clover, vetch, field peas, or a mix of nitrogen-fixing legumes. These plants form symbiotic relationships with rhizobia bacteria, converting atmospheric N2 into plant-available ammonium. The nitrogen is deposited in the soil via root exudates and, when the cover crop is terminated, via decomposition of the root mass. This further charges the biochar in situ.
8. **After one season of cover cropping** (3-4 months in warm climates, 6-8 months in cool climates), terminate the cover crop by cutting at ground level (leave roots in place to decompose). The bed is now ready for food production. Expected yield improvement over unamended soil: 50-200% in the first year, increasing over subsequent years as the biochar continues to age and its CEC rises.

### Biochar Production Schedule for a 50-Person Settlement

- **Total target**: 5 hectares of Terra Preta beds at 15 tonnes biochar per hectare = 75 tonnes of biochar. This is a multi-year capital project, not a single-season effort.
- **Year 1**: Focus on 0.5 hectares of demonstration and high-priority kitchen garden beds. Requirement: approximately 7.5 tonnes.
- **Production rate using cone pits**: 80 kg per batch, 2 batches per day, 2 production days per week = 320 kg/week = approximately 8 tonnes in a 6-month production season (excluding winter or wet season when pyrolysis is impractical).
- **Labour**: 2 dedicated operators, 2 days per week. Additional labour for biomass collection and transport (2-4 people for half a day per production day).
- **Years 2-5**: Scale production to 15-20 tonnes per year using a combination of cone pits, trench kilns, and TLUD gasifier accumulation. By Year 5, the full 5 hectares is amended and the settlement has permanent food sovereignty infrastructure.

## Common Failure Modes

**1. Ash instead of char.** The biomass burns to white or grey ash instead of retaining its black, porous structure. Cause: excessive oxygen exposure — the flame cap was not maintained, or the fire was stirred/disturbed during the burn, allowing air to penetrate the char bed. Prevention: add biomass in thin, even layers (5-10 cm); maintain a continuous visible flame at the surface; never stir or poke the fire during production; quench promptly when the pit is full. Diagnostic: good biochar is jet black, retains the cellular structure of the original wood (visible grain and pores), and breaks with a clean snap. Ash is grey-white, powdery, and has no structural integrity.

**2. Hydrophobic biochar repelling water.** After amendment, the bed surface sheds water or develops dry spots. Cause: biochar was applied without adequate charging. Fresh biochar surfaces are coated with hydrophobic tars and oils from pyrolysis. Prevention: always charge for the full 2-4 week period before soil application. Recovery: heavily water the amended bed (saturate it), apply 5-10 cm of compost as a top-dress, and mulch. The compost microbes will gradually colonise the biochar surfaces over 2-3 months, resolving the hydrophobicity.

**3. Nitrogen lock-up (chlorosis).** Crops show uniform yellowing of older leaves (nitrogen deficiency) within 2-4 weeks of planting in biochar-amended soil. Cause: uncharged or undercharged biochar is adsorbing soil nitrogen, making it unavailable to plants. Prevention: charge biochar with nitrogen-rich materials (urine, manure, compost) before application. Recovery: apply diluted urine (1:10 with water) directly to the soil surface at a rate of 5 litres per square metre, or apply compost tea weekly until symptoms resolve.

**4. Incomplete pyrolysis.** The product is brown or reddish-brown rather than deep black. It crumbles to brown dust rather than fracturing into shiny black fragments with visible pore structure. Cause: pyrolysis temperature did not reach 300 C — the burn was too cool or too brief. Prevention: maintain a vigorous flame cap throughout the burn, allow each layer to fully char (all visible flames from that layer should transition from yellow to blue-purple) before adding the next layer. Do not rush the process by adding too much biomass at once, which smothers the fire.

**5. Fire escape during pyrolysis.** The fire spreads to surrounding vegetation or structures. Cause: insufficient cleared perimeter, wind-driven ember transport. Prevention: maintain a 3 m bare-soil perimeter; conduct pyrolysis only in low-wind conditions (below 15 km/h); stage water and sand at the pit rim; assign one operator as dedicated fire watch. If fire escapes: flood the cone pit immediately (sacrificing the batch), then address the escaped fire.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Pyrolysis**: Thermal decomposition of organic matter in the absence or near-absence of oxygen. Produces solid biochar, combustible gases (syngas), and condensable liquids (bio-oil). Distinguished from combustion, which requires oxygen and produces CO2 and ash.

- **Biochar**: The solid carbon residue of pyrolysis. A porous, stable, aromatic carbon matrix with high surface area (200-400 m2/g) used as a soil amendment. Distinguished from charcoal (which is produced for fuel) by its intended use in soil.

- **Terra Preta**: Portuguese for "dark earth." Specifically refers to the anthropogenic (human-created) dark soils of the Amazon basin, enriched with biochar, pottery fragments, bone, and organic matter by pre-Columbian civilizations. Used generically to refer to any soil intentionally built using biochar-based amendment.

- **Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC)**: A measure of a soil's or material's ability to adsorb and release positively charged nutrient ions (cations). Expressed in centimoles of charge per kilogram, cmol(+)/kg. Higher CEC means the soil can store more nutrients and release them gradually to plant roots, reducing leaching losses.

- **Flame cap**: The layer of active combustion at the top surface of a cone pit or trench kiln. The flame cap consumes oxygen and pyrolysis gases, preventing oxygen from reaching the char bed below. Maintaining a continuous flame cap is the essential technique for producing biochar rather than ash in open-air kilns.

- **Syngas**: Synthesis gas — a mixture of carbon monoxide (CO), hydrogen (H2), and methane (CH4) produced during pyrolysis. Combustible; burns with a blue-purple flame. In TLUD gasifiers, syngas is captured and burned for cooking heat.

- **Charging (inoculation)**: The process of pre-loading raw biochar with nutrients and microbial communities before soil application. Typically achieved by soaking biochar in a compost-urine mixture for 2-4 weeks. Charged biochar is hydrophilic, nutrient-rich, and biologically active.

- **Mycorrhizal fungi**: Symbiotic fungi that colonise plant roots and extend their hyphal networks into the soil matrix, dramatically increasing the volume of soil from which a plant can extract water and nutrients. Biochar micropores provide protected habitat for mycorrhizal spores and hyphae.

- **Humus**: The stable, dark, amorphous organic fraction of soil that has been fully decomposed and humified. Humus has very high CEC (100-300 cmol(+)/kg) and is the benchmark against which biochar's soil benefits are compared. Unlike humus, biochar does not decompose.

- **Feedstock**: The raw biomass input for pyrolysis. Feedstock characteristics (species, density, moisture content, mineral content) determine biochar quality.

- **Torrefaction**: Low-temperature thermal treatment (200-300 C) that drives off moisture and volatile organics without achieving full pyrolysis. The product is "torrefied biomass" — energy-dense but not chemically stable in soil.

- **Carbon sequestration**: The long-term removal of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and its storage in a stable form. Biochar production converts atmospheric carbon (fixed by plants through photosynthesis) into a form with a half-life of 500-5,000 years, making it one of the most effective and durable carbon sequestration technologies available.

- **Oxisol**: A highly weathered tropical soil characterised by deep red-orange colour (iron and aluminium oxides), extremely low CEC, and very low natural fertility. The dominant soil type in the Amazon basin and the substrate from which Terra Preta was built.
