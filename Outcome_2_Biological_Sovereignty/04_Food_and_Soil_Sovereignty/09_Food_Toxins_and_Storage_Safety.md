**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> **Caution — the food that passes inspection and still kills:** The preservation and pantry modules teach you to *make* food safe. This module teaches you to recognize when stored food has *become* deadly in ways your senses miss. Botulinum toxin is odorless and tasteless. Aflatoxin on mold has no flavor and survives cooking. Ergot hides as a few dark grains in a good harvest. The lethal error is the reasonable one: *"it looks alright, it smells alright, we can't afford to waste it."* Some food poisons cannot be cooked, washed, or picked out — they can only be prevented, and the contaminated batch discarded. Knowing which is which is the difference between a hungry winter and a dead one.

## 1. Why This Is Important

A settlement's food stores are its survival, and the instinct to never waste them is correct almost always — except for a short list of contaminants where eating the questionable food is far more dangerous than the hunger of discarding it. Historically, these are catastrophic precisely because they defeat normal caution:

*   **Ergotism** ("St. Anthony's Fire") burned through medieval Europe repeatedly — whole villages convulsing, hallucinating, or losing gangrenous limbs — from a fungus that looks like a few dark, oversized grains in the rye.
*   **Aflatoxin** from mold on stored grain and nuts is one of the most potent liver carcinogens known and a cause of acute mass poisoning; it is invisible in taste and survives every cooking method.
*   **Botulism** from improperly preserved anaerobic foods produces the most lethal toxin by weight known to biology, with no warning to the senses.
*   **Nitrate accumulation** in certain over-fertilized or drought-stressed leafy greens and in stored/cooked-and-held vegetables converts to toxic nitrite.
*   **Mushroom and plant misidentification** kills foragers who trusted a resemblance.

Every one of these is *preventable* with knowledge and discarded-batch discipline, and *unsurvivable* through the "waste not" instinct. This module makes the poisons legible.

## 2. Expected Outcome

A settlement that inspects stored grain, nuts, and preserves on a schedule; recognizes the specific visual and situational signatures of ergot, dangerous mold, botulism-risk preserves, and nitrate-risk greens; and has an ironclad, taught, non-negotiable rule: **contaminated batches from this list are destroyed, never eaten, never fed to livestock casually, never "cooked to be safe."**

## 3. Ergot: The Fungus in the Grain

**The danger.** *Claviceps purpurea* infects rye and other cereals (wheat, barley, triticale, some grasses), replacing kernels with hard, dark purple-black, curved bodies called **sclerotia** — noticeably larger and darker than healthy grain. They contain potent alkaloids (the chemical family that includes LSD and vasoconstrictors). Two forms of poisoning:
*   **Gangrenous ergotism:** blood vessels constrict; fingers, toes, and limbs go numb, then cold, then blacken and die. The medieval "fire."
*   **Convulsive ergotism:** violent seizures, hallucinations, psychosis.
Ergot alkaloids are also powerfully **abortifacient** — devastating in a settlement dependent on every pregnancy.

**Detection and threshold.** Inspect grain by eye and hand before milling: **any dark, hard, curved, oversized bodies among the kernels mean the batch is ergot-contaminated.** There is no safe percentage for a staple you eat daily. Cooking and baking do **not** destroy ergot alkaloids.

**Prevention & response:**
*   Ergot thrives in cool, wet flowering seasons and on grasses at field edges — a wet year is a high-ergot year; inspect harder.
*   Sort visually; ergot bodies float in brine (denser removal method) — soaking suspect grain in strong salt water floats off some sclerotia, but *visual rejection of the batch* is the real defense for heavily infected grain.
*   Do not save seed from an ergot-infected field without rigorous cleaning; the fungus overwinters in the soil and sclerotia.
*   **A batch with more than trace contamination is discarded.** This is a Tier-L / Law call.

## 4. Aflatoxin and Storage Mold: The Invisible Carcinogen

**The danger.** Molds of the *Aspergillus* family grow on grain, maize, peanuts/groundnuts, tree nuts, and dried foods stored warm and damp, producing **aflatoxin** — acutely, it causes liver failure and death (mass poisonings have killed hundreds at a time); chronically, it is a leading cause of liver cancer. Other storage molds produce their own toxins (ochratoxin, fumonisins). Crucially: **the toxin is tasteless, and it persists after the mold is killed by cooking.** Removing visible mold does not remove the toxin that has diffused into the food.

**Detection:** musty, "cellar" smell; visible mold (green, grey, yellow-green, black); caking, discoloration, or a warm spot in a grain store (mold generates heat — a warm patch in cool grain is an alarm). Because the toxin outruns the visible mold, **visible mold in a batch condemns more than the moldy portion.**

**Prevention — this is almost entirely a storage-conditions problem:**
*   Store grain and nuts **dry** (moisture below the [preservation snap-test threshold](04_Long_Term_Food_Preservation.md)) and **cool**, in the [two-chamber, ventilated pantry](08_Refugia_Pantry_Physics.md). Damp + warm + time = aflatoxin.
*   Dry crops fully *before* storage; a batch stored one point too wet molds from the inside.
*   Inspect stores monthly; a warm or musty spot means open, assess, and likely condemn that batch.
*   **Discard moldy grain and nuts; do not salvage by washing or cooking; do not feed heavily-molded grain to livestock** (aflatoxin passes into milk and accumulates in the animal).

## 5. Botulism: The Toxin With No Warning

**The danger.** *Clostridium botulinum* grows only without oxygen (sealed jars, submerged ferments, sealed meats, oil infusions) and produces a neurotoxin that causes descending paralysis — drooping eyelids, difficulty swallowing and speaking, then paralysis of the breathing muscles. Without a ventilator it is frequently fatal. **The toxin is odorless and tasteless; the food often looks and smells normal.** This is the poison that most defeats sensory caution.

**The Law-tier defenses** (detailed in [Long-Term Food Preservation](04_Long_Term_Food_Preservation.md)):
*   **Acidity: pH < 4.6.** Botulinum cannot grow below this. A proper lacto-ferment (2–3% salt, 7–14 days) reaches pH 3.5–4.0 and is safe *by acidity*. Low-acid foods (most vegetables, meat, fish) sealed anaerobically **without** reaching this pH are the classic botulism source.
*   **Water activity or salt/cure** for anaerobic meats — follow the cure ratios exactly.
*   **When in doubt, boil the food itself hard before eating** — unlike the spores, the *toxin* (not the organism) is destroyed by a genuine rolling boil for several minutes. This is the one salvage step, and it applies to the toxin only; treat it as a last line, not a license to store carelessly.

**The rule:** any anaerobically-stored low-acid food that was not properly acidified, cured, or heat-processed, and any bulging, gassy, or off jar, is **discarded without tasting.** Do not taste to check. Tasting is how people die.

## 6. Nitrate/Nitrite in Greens and Held Food

**The danger.** Leafy greens (spinach, chard, beets, lettuce, amaranth) heavily fertilized with fresh manure, or drought/cold-stressed, accumulate nitrate. Two risks: (1) in infants, high-nitrate purées cause the same **methemoglobinemia** as [contaminated water](../03_Water_and_Sanitation/07_Water_Contaminant_Testing_and_Remediation.md); (2) cooked vegetables held warm for many hours let bacteria convert nitrate to toxic **nitrite**.

**Prevention:**
*   Do not make infant food from heavily-manured or stressed greens; use mature, moderately-fertilized produce.
*   Do not hold cooked vegetables warm for extended periods — eat fresh or cool promptly ([hay-box food-safety timing](05_Thermal_Mass_Cooking.md) applies).
*   Compost and age manure before it touches leafy-crop beds (the [charging/curing discipline](../03_Water_and_Sanitation/05_Humanure_Integration.md)); fresh-manure market gardens are the classic high-nitrate source.

## 7. Forage & Mushroom Toxins (brief — see Emergency Nutrition)

The [Emergency Nutrition](06_Emergency_Nutrition.md) module carries the full foraging-toxin treatment. The Law-tier reminders that belong in every food-safety review:
*   **Mushrooms:** the deadly species (death cap, destroying angel) resemble edibles and kill the liver with a **delayed** onset — you feel fine for 6–24 hours, then it is often too late. **No mushroom is eaten on resemblance alone; only on certain, taught identification.**
*   **Acorns and tannin-rich staples:** leach before eating (cold 1–3 days or 3–5 hot changes).
*   **Rabbit starvation:** lean meat without fat poisons over weeks; store and eat fat deliberately.
*   **The Universal Edibility Test** exists for genuine unknowns but is slow and imperfect; it is a last resort, not a routine.

## 9. Common Failure Modes

*   **The "waste not" override.** Eating visibly moldy grain or a questionable preserve because discarding feels wasteful. *Consequence:* aflatoxin exposure or botulism — a far costlier "waste." *Prevention:* teach the four un-fixable toxins as absolute discard rules, separate from all other food thrift.
*   **Tasting to check.** Sampling a suspect jar "just a little." *Consequence:* botulism needs almost no dose. *Prevention:* the no-taste rule, taught as law.
*   **Storing one point too wet.** Grain that felt "dry enough" molds from within over months. *Consequence:* a whole silo lost to aflatoxin, discovered as a warm musty spot. *Prevention:* the snap-test before storage; monthly inspection for warm spots.
*   **Cooking to "make it safe."** Believing heat fixes ergot, aflatoxin, or a bad batch. *Consequence:* these survive cooking. *Prevention:* the Section 8 table, posted at the pantry.
*   **Fresh-manure infant greens.** *Consequence:* nitrate methemoglobinemia. *Prevention:* aged compost only for leaf beds; mature produce for infant food.

## 10. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Aflatoxin:** A tasteless, heat-stable liver toxin/carcinogen from *Aspergillus* molds on damp-stored grain and nuts.
*   **Botulinum toxin:** The odorless, tasteless neurotoxin from *C. botulinum* in improperly preserved anaerobic food; prevented by pH < 4.6, cure, or heat process.
*   **Condemn / discard batch:** The disciplined destruction of contaminated food from the un-fixable list — a survival act, not waste.
*   **Delayed-onset poisoning:** The pattern (death-cap mushroom, aflatoxin) where the victim feels fine long after the lethal dose, defeating "I ate it and I'm fine" reasoning.
*   **Methemoglobinemia:** Nitrite-driven loss of blood oxygen-carrying capacity; a risk from high-nitrate infant greens as well as water.
*   **Sclerotia (ergot):** Hard, dark, curved fungal bodies replacing grain kernels; the visible sign of ergot contamination. Not destroyed by cooking.

## 11. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 11.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Preparation:** Post the Section 8 "Can I fix this batch?" table at the pantry and mill. Dry every crop to the snap test before storage.
*   **Execution:** Inspect grain before milling (ergot) and stores monthly (mold/warm spots). Acidify/cure/process every preserve to its Law-tier threshold. Age manure before leaf beds.
*   **Review:** Any contaminated batch from the un-fixable list is destroyed and the *cause* logged (too wet? wet year? bad seal?) so the next batch is prevented.

### 11.2. Consequence of Deviation
*   **[Tier L] Mill ergot-contaminated rye** → gangrene/convulsions/abortion across everyone who eats the bread. *Recovery:* none once eaten; visual rejection is the only defense.
*   **[Tier L] Salvage moldy grain by cooking** → aflatoxin exposure → liver failure or cancer. *Recovery:* none; discard.
*   **[Tier L] Taste a suspect preserve** → botulism → paralysis, likely death. *Recovery:* boiling the *food* destroys the toxin, but doubt = discard.

### 11.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** Every person who mills, stores, or preserves must know the four un-fixable toxins and their signatures. This is the knowledge that, missing for one generation, produces a village of St. Anthony's Fire.
