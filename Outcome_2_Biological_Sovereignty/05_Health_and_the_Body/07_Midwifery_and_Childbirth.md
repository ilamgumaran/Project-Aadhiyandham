# Generational Midwifery and Neonatal Care: The First Year of Life

**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Childbirth is the single most dangerous event in a woman's life in any pre-industrial society. In pre-modern Europe, maternal mortality ran between 1% and 1.5% per birth — roughly 1 death for every 65 to 100 deliveries. Over a reproductive lifetime of six to ten births, the cumulative risk of maternal death was 6% to 15%. A woman entering her childbearing years faced odds comparable to a soldier entering a series of battles. Neonatal mortality was even worse: 20% to 30% of infants died in their first year, overwhelmingly from three preventable causes — infection, hypothermia, and feeding failure. These are not abstract historical statistics. They are the baseline that any post-collapse settlement will revert to unless it possesses specific, actionable knowledge.

That knowledge exists. The physiology of normal labor is well understood. The three obstetric emergencies that account for the vast majority of maternal deaths — postpartum hemorrhage, obstructed labor, and neonatal asphyxia — have been studied extensively and have established management protocols that do not require electricity, pharmaceuticals, or surgical equipment. The evidence-based practices that protect newborn survival — thermal care, early breastfeeding, microbiome transfer — require nothing more than warm blankets, a mother's body, and a trained attendant who knows what to watch for.

This module provides that knowledge. It is not a replacement for professional obstetric care. In any scenario where a hospital, a surgeon, or a trained obstetrician is available, that care should be sought without hesitation. But in a world where professional care does not exist, the difference between a community that possesses this knowledge and one that does not is the difference between a 1% and a 10% maternal mortality rate — the difference between a settlement that grows and one that dies within a generation.

> **DANGER**: Childbirth can become a life-threatening emergency within minutes. Postpartum hemorrhage can cause death in 15-30 minutes. Obstructed labor can cause uterine rupture. Neonatal asphyxia causes brain damage within 4-6 minutes. The procedures in this module — uterine massage, manual placenta removal, neonatal resuscitation — are emergency interventions that carry their own risks. They should be practiced on training models and learned through apprenticeship with experienced birth attendants BEFORE being applied in a real birth. Every settlement must have a minimum of TWO trained midwives — one primary, one backup — available at all times.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Physiology of Normal Labor

Labor is divided into three stages, each with distinct physiology and distinct risks. Understanding these stages allows the midwife to assess whether labor is progressing normally or whether intervention is required.

**First Stage: Cervical Dilation (0 to 10 cm)**

The cervix — the muscular opening at the base of the uterus — must thin (efface) and open (dilate) from a closed position to a full 10 centimeters to allow the baby's head to pass. This stage has two distinct phases.

*Latent phase (0 to ~6 cm):* Characterized by irregular, relatively mild contractions spaced 5 to 15 minutes apart, each lasting 30 to 45 seconds. This phase is slow and unpredictable. For a first birth, it may last 12 to 20 hours. For subsequent births, it is typically shorter — 6 to 12 hours. The mother should eat, drink, rest, and remain mobile during this phase. There is nothing to do but wait.

*Active phase (~6 to 10 cm):* Contractions intensify dramatically — every 2 to 3 minutes, lasting 60 to 90 seconds. This phase typically lasts 1 to 3 hours. The mother will no longer be able to talk through contractions. She may shake, vomit, or become emotionally distressed. This is normal. The transition from late active phase to full dilation is the most intense part of labor and often the point at which the mother says she cannot continue. The midwife's role at this point is reassurance and physical support — not intervention.

**Second Stage: Delivery of the Baby**

From full cervical dilation to the birth of the infant. The mother will feel an involuntary, overwhelming urge to push — this is the Ferguson reflex, triggered by the baby's head pressing on stretch receptors in the pelvic floor. The urge is unmistakable and should not be resisted once full dilation is confirmed. This stage typically lasts 30 minutes to 2 hours for a first birth, and may be as short as 5 to 20 minutes for subsequent births. The midwife should support the mother in an upright position and let her push instinctively. Directed pushing ("hold your breath and push to a count of ten") is less effective than spontaneous pushing and increases the risk of perineal tearing.

**Third Stage: Delivery of the Placenta**

From the birth of the baby to the delivery of the placenta. This stage typically takes 5 to 30 minutes. Active management — gentle, steady traction on the umbilical cord during a uterine contraction, combined with counter-pressure on the uterus above the pubic bone (the Brandt-Andrews maneuver) and immediate uterine massage — reduces the risk of postpartum hemorrhage by approximately 60% compared to passive management (simply waiting). The placenta should be examined after delivery to confirm it is complete. A retained fragment of placenta is a common cause of continued bleeding and late postpartum hemorrhage.

**The Role of Oxytocin**

Endogenous oxytocin is the master hormone of labor. Released from the posterior pituitary gland, it drives uterine contractions in a positive feedback loop: contractions push the baby's head against the cervix, cervical stretching triggers more oxytocin release (the Ferguson reflex), which drives stronger contractions. This loop is exquisitely sensitive to the mother's emotional state. Stress hormones — adrenaline and cortisol — directly inhibit oxytocin release. A frightened, cold, or observed mother will have weaker, less frequent contractions and a longer, more difficult labor. This is not a psychological nicety. It is endocrinology. Privacy, warmth, dim lighting, quiet voices, and the presence of only trusted, familiar people are physiological necessities for normal labor progression. Every additional observer, every bright light, every loud conversation slows labor.

**The Pelvis and Cardinal Movements**

The fetal head must navigate through the bony pelvis, which presents three planes of different shape and diameter: the pelvic inlet (widest transversely), the midpelvis (widest anteroposteriorly), and the pelvic outlet (widest anteroposteriorly). To pass through these changing diameters, the baby performs a series of rotations known as the cardinal movements: engagement (the head enters the pelvic inlet), descent, flexion (the chin tucks to the chest, presenting the smallest diameter of the head), internal rotation (the head rotates 90 degrees to align with the anteroposterior diameter of the midpelvis), extension (the head extends as it passes under the pubic bone — this is crowning), external rotation (the head rotates back to align with the shoulders), and expulsion (delivery of the body). These rotations happen automatically when the mother is upright and mobile — squatting, kneeling, walking, swaying. They are impeded when she is lying flat on her back, which also compresses the vena cava (reducing blood flow to the uterus) and narrows the pelvic outlet by up to 30%.

### The Three Obstetric Emergencies

These three emergencies account for the vast majority of preventable maternal and neonatal deaths. Every trained midwife must be able to recognize and manage all three.

**1. Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH)**

Postpartum hemorrhage is defined as blood loss exceeding 500 mL after vaginal delivery. It is the leading cause of maternal death worldwide and can kill within 15 to 30 minutes if uncontrolled. In 80% of cases, the cause is uterine atony — the uterus fails to contract firmly after delivery of the placenta, and the open blood vessels at the placental site bleed freely. Other causes include retained placental fragments (10% of cases), genital tract lacerations from tearing during delivery (5%), and coagulopathy or clotting disorders (5%).

The critical management sequence is:

- (1) Vigorous bimanual uterine massage — cup the uterus through the abdomen with both hands and rub firmly until it feels like a hard grapefruit.
- (2) Empty the bladder — a full bladder displaces the uterus and prevents contraction, so the mother must urinate or be catheterized if available.
- (3) Nipple stimulation — put the baby to the breast immediately, as suckling triggers oxytocin release which drives uterine contraction.
- (4) If bleeding continues despite these measures, escalate to bimanual uterine compression — one hand on the abdomen pressing down on the uterine fundus, the other hand (gloved, cleaned with boiled water) inserted into the vagina as a fist, compressing the uterus between the two hands. This is painful and invasive but it is a lifesaving measure when external massage fails.

**2. Obstructed Labor**

Obstructed labor occurs when the baby cannot pass through the birth canal despite adequate contractions. Causes include cephalopelvic disproportion (the baby's head is too large for the mother's pelvis — more common in very young mothers whose pelvises have not fully matured and in cases of fetal macrosomia), malpresentation (the baby is in a position other than head-down, vertex presentation — breech, transverse, brow, or face presentation), and cervical arrest (the cervix stops dilating despite strong contractions).

Warning signs include:

- A prolonged second stage with no descent (more than 3 hours for a first birth, more than 2 hours for subsequent births, with adequate contractions and maternal effort).
- Visible maternal exhaustion and distress disproportionate to the stage of labor.
- Most critically: Bandl's ring — a visible or palpable horizontal constriction across the abdomen, marking the boundary between the thinning lower uterine segment and the thickening upper segment. Bandl's ring is an emergency sign of impending uterine rupture. If it is visible, the uterus is on the verge of tearing open.

In a zero-technology environment, the response options are limited. Repositioning the mother — hands and knees, deep squat, asymmetric positions (one knee up, one knee down) — can resolve some minor malpresentations and allow the baby to rotate through the pelvis. Breech delivery is possible with specific maneuvers (described in Emergency Protocols below) but carries significantly higher risk. If the obstruction is truly mechanical — a head that will not fit through the pelvis — this is the honest limit of zero-technology obstetrics. Without cesarean section capability, true cephalopelvic disproportion is a fatal condition for the baby and potentially for the mother. The midwife must recognize this situation early and be honest about it.

**3. Neonatal Asphyxia**

Neonatal asphyxia — failure of the newborn to breathe spontaneously at birth — accounts for approximately 25% of neonatal deaths worldwide. Causes include prolonged or obstructed labor (the baby is deprived of oxygen during a difficult delivery), umbilical cord compression or prolapse, and meconium aspiration (the baby inhales its own stool, which can block the airways).

The "Golden Minute" protocol: the newborn must be stimulated to breathe within 60 seconds of birth. Step one: dry the baby vigorously with a clean, warm cloth — the tactile stimulation of drying is often sufficient to trigger the first breath. Step two: position the head in slight extension (the "sniffing position" — as if smelling a flower) to open the airway. Step three: clear the mouth and nose — use a suction bulb if available, or wipe with a clean cloth wrapped around a finger; if neither is available, mouth-to-mouth-and-nose suction (the attendant's mouth over the baby's mouth and nose, gentle suction, spit out secretions). Step four: if the baby is still not breathing after 30 seconds of stimulation, begin mouth-to-mouth-and-nose ventilation — the attendant places their mouth over the baby's mouth and nose simultaneously and gives small, gentle puffs, just enough to make the chest rise visibly. One puff every 3 seconds (20 breaths per minute). The puffs must be gentle — a newborn's lungs hold only 20-30 mL of air. Overinflation can cause pneumothorax (lung rupture). Brain damage begins at approximately 4 minutes without oxygen. Death at approximately 10 minutes.

### Neonatal Physiology: The First 28 Days

**Thermoregulation**

Neonates lose heat four times faster than adults. Their surface-area-to-mass ratio is enormous, their subcutaneous fat layer is thin, and their thermoregulatory mechanisms are immature. A wet newborn in a room at 22 degrees Celsius will lose core temperature at approximately 0.1 degrees per minute — enough to become hypothermic within 10 to 15 minutes. Hypothermia (core temperature below 36.5 degrees Celsius) causes peripheral vasoconstriction, metabolic acidosis, hypoglycemia, and respiratory distress. It is the leading preventable cause of neonatal death in low-resource settings. The defense against hypothermia is the thermal chain — an unbroken sequence of warm interventions from the moment of birth (described in Core Principles below).

**The Immune System and Microbiome Transfer**

The neonatal immune system is naive — it has never encountered pathogens and has no memory. Protection in the first months of life comes from three sources. First, maternal IgG antibodies transferred across the placenta during the third trimester provide passive immunity against pathogens the mother has previously encountered. This transfer is most efficient in the final weeks of pregnancy, which is one reason premature infants are so vulnerable to infection. Second, secretory IgA antibodies in breast milk coat the infant's gut and respiratory mucosa, providing ongoing passive protection that adapts to the local pathogen environment — the mother's immune system detects local pathogens and produces specific IgA antibodies that are delivered to the infant through milk. Third, the infant's own microbiome, acquired during and after birth, provides colonization resistance — beneficial bacteria occupy niches that would otherwise be colonized by pathogens.

The sequence of microbiome acquisition is critical and follows a specific order:

- *Vaginal birth* coats the infant in Lactobacillus and Prevotella species from the birth canal — these are the first colonizers of the infant gut.
- *Immediate skin-to-skin contact* transfers the mother's skin flora — Staphylococcus, Corynebacterium, and other commensal species.
- *Breastfeeding* provides human milk oligosaccharides (HMOs) — complex sugars that are indigestible by the infant but serve as selective prebiotics for Bifidobacterium and other beneficial gut bacteria.

This sequence — vaginal birth, then skin-to-skin, then breastfeeding — is the infant's immune system boot sequence. Disrupting any step (unnecessary separation, delayed breastfeeding, supplementation with animal milk) degrades the microbial colonization pattern and increases susceptibility to infection.

**Neonatal Jaundice**

Approximately 60% of full-term newborns develop visible jaundice — a yellowing of the skin and whites of the eyes — by day 2 to 3 of life. In most cases this is physiological jaundice: the newborn's immature liver is processing the breakdown products of fetal hemoglobin, which is being replaced by adult hemoglobin. Bilirubin accumulates temporarily. This is normal and self-resolving within 1 to 2 weeks. Indirect sunlight exposure (placing the baby near a window in natural light, or outside in shade — never in direct sunlight) accelerates bilirubin breakdown through photoisomerization. Adequate breastfeeding (8-12 times per day) promotes bilirubin excretion through stool.

Pathological jaundice is dangerous and requires recognition. Warning signs:

- Jaundice appearing within the first 24 hours of life (this is never physiological).
- Jaundice that is deep yellow or orange and extends to the palms and soles.
- Jaundice accompanied by lethargy, poor feeding, or high-pitched cry.
- Jaundice that persists beyond 2 weeks.

Pathological jaundice may indicate blood group incompatibility (the mother's antibodies are destroying the infant's red blood cells), infection, or liver disease. In a zero-technology environment, the only available interventions are aggressive breastfeeding and maximum sunlight exposure — but the midwife must recognize that severe pathological jaundice (kernicterus) causes permanent brain damage and death, and that this is another honest limit of field obstetrics.

## Core Principles

1. **The Oxytocin Principle**: Labor progresses when the mother feels safe, warm, private, and supported. Every intervention, every observer, every interruption reduces oxytocin release and slows labor. The midwife's primary role during normal labor is to protect the birth environment, not to manage the birth. Dim lights. Quiet voices. Warm room. Familiar people only. The midwife who creates an environment of calm does more for the progress of labor than any procedure.

2. **The Gravity Principle**: Upright positions — squatting, kneeling, standing, hands-and-knees — increase the diameter of the pelvic outlet by 20% to 30% compared to the supine (lying on back) position. The mother should never be placed on her back unless there is a specific medical reason (such as the need for bimanual uterine compression during hemorrhage). Gravity assists fetal descent. Maternal movement assists fetal rotation through the cardinal movements. A laboring woman who is walking, swaying, or squatting is doing exactly what her body needs to do.

3. **The Hands-Off Principle**: In a normal birth, the midwife does not need to "deliver" the baby. The mother delivers. The midwife watches, supports the perineum during crowning, catches the baby as it emerges, and monitors for complications. Approximately 85% to 90% of all births proceed normally without any intervention whatsoever. The midwife's skill is not in performing procedures — it is in recognizing the 10% to 15% of births that require action, and acting decisively when they do.

4. **The Thermal Chain**: From the moment of birth, the newborn must be kept warm through an unbroken chain of thermal interventions. Dry the baby immediately and thoroughly with a warm cloth (wet skin loses heat through evaporation at a devastating rate). Place the baby directly on the mother's bare chest (skin-to-skin contact regulates neonatal temperature more effectively than any external heat source). Cover both mother and baby with warm, dry blankets. Delay the first bath for a minimum of 24 hours. Keep the birth room warm — 25 to 28 degrees Celsius minimum. Breaking any link in this chain leads to hypothermia, and hypothermia kills neonates.

5. **The Breast-First Principle**: Breastfeeding should begin within 1 hour of birth. Colostrum — the thick, yellow first milk produced in the first 2 to 3 days — contains concentrated secretory IgA antibodies, lactoferrin, white blood cells, and growth factors. It is the infant's first immunization. Breastfeeding also stimulates oxytocin release in the mother, driving uterine contraction and reducing postpartum bleeding. Early and frequent breastfeeding — a minimum of 8 to 12 times per day in the first week — establishes the milk supply through the supply-and-demand feedback loop. The more the infant suckles, the more milk the mother produces.

## Practical Implementation

### The Birth Kit

Every settlement must maintain a prepared birth kit, checked and restocked after each use. The following items should be assembled and stored in a clean, dry, sealed container.

| Item | Specification | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Clean cloths (minimum 6) | Boiled for 20 minutes, dried in direct sunlight | Drying baby, perineal support, absorbing blood |
| String ties (minimum 4) | Boiled cotton or hemp cord, 20 cm each | Tying umbilical cord in two places |
| Cutting blade | Flame-sterilized sharp knife or razor, dedicated to this purpose only | Cutting cord between ties |
| Suction bulb | Scavenged rubber bulb syringe, boiled before use | Clearing infant airway |
| Warm blankets (minimum 3) | Clean, dry wool or heavy cotton | Thermal chain for mother and infant |
| Basin | Clean vessel, capacity 2-3 liters | Warm water for compresses |
| Clean mat or surface | Boiled cloth or hide, laid over clean straw | Birth surface |
| Clove oil | Cold-pressed from dried flower buds | Perineal massage, mild analgesic |
| Raspberry leaf tea | Dried leaves, prepared as strong infusion | Uterine tonic (third trimester and postpartum) |
| Measuring tape or marked cord | Calibrated in centimeters | Fundal height measurement |
| Pinard horn (improvised) | Carved wooden or clay cone, 20 cm length | Listening to fetal heart tones |

### Prenatal Care Protocol

Prenatal assessment should begin as soon as pregnancy is confirmed and continue monthly from the fourth month, increasing to weekly in the final month.

**Monthly Assessment (Month 4 Onward):**
Fundal height measurement — using a measuring tape or a marked cord, measure from the upper edge of the pubic bone to the top of the uterine fundus (the firm, rounded mass palpable through the abdomen). From approximately 20 weeks to 36 weeks, the fundal height in centimeters roughly equals the gestational age in weeks. A fundal height measurement that is more than 3 centimeters smaller than expected may indicate growth restriction; more than 3 centimeters larger may indicate multiple pregnancy or excess amniotic fluid. Fetal heart rate — from approximately 20 weeks, the fetal heartbeat can be heard by pressing an ear firmly against the mother's lower abdomen, or more effectively using an improvised Pinard horn (a cone-shaped listening device carved from wood or molded from clay). Normal fetal heart rate is 110 to 160 beats per minute. Count for a full 60 seconds. A rate below 110 or above 160 requires increased monitoring.

**Nutritional Requirements:**
The pregnant woman requires approximately 2,500 calories per day (300 above baseline), with particular emphasis on the following micronutrients. Folate — essential for neural tube closure, which occurs at 21 to 28 days after conception (often before the woman knows she is pregnant); sources include dark leafy greens (amaranth, spinach, kale), liver, and legumes; all women of childbearing age should consume folate-rich foods daily as a matter of routine. Iron — maternal blood volume increases 40% to 50% during pregnancy, requiring substantial iron intake to prevent anemia; heme iron from liver and red meat is most bioavailable; cooking in cast-iron vessels significantly increases the iron content of acidic foods; non-heme iron from plants is enhanced by concurrent vitamin C intake (citrus, fermented vegetables). Calcium — required for fetal skeletal development; if dairy is unavailable, calcium must be sourced from eggshell powder dissolved in vinegar (one teaspoon provides approximately 800 mg calcium), bone broth boiled for 24 to 48 hours, or small fish consumed whole with bones. Protein — minimum 70 grams per day from any available animal or combined plant sources.

**Exercise:**
Daily walking (30 to 60 minutes), deep squatting practice (the Garland pose / Malasana — spending 5 to 10 minutes daily in a deep, supported squat stretches the perineum and opens the pelvic outlet), and pelvic floor exercises (Kegel contractions — contract the muscles used to stop urine flow, hold for 10 seconds, release, repeat 10 times, three times daily) prepare the body for delivery.

**Warning Signs Requiring Immediate Attention:**

- Vaginal bleeding at any stage of pregnancy.
- Severe headache accompanied by visual disturbances (seeing spots, blurred vision) and swelling of the face or hands — these are signs of preeclampsia, a dangerous hypertensive condition that can progress to seizures (eclampsia) and death.
- Decreased or absent fetal movement after 28 weeks (the mother should feel the baby move at least 10 times in a 2-hour period when resting; fewer movements may indicate fetal distress).
- Fever above 38 degrees Celsius (may indicate infection).
- Sudden gush or persistent leak of clear fluid from the vagina (rupture of membranes — if labor does not begin within 24 hours, infection risk increases dramatically).
- Severe abdominal pain that does not correspond to contractions (may indicate placental abruption).

### Normal Birth Protocol

The following is a step-by-step protocol for the attending midwife during an uncomplicated vaginal delivery.

**Step 1 — Recognize Onset of Labor.** True labor is characterized by contractions that are regular, progressively increasing in frequency and intensity, and do not stop with rest or position change. Other signs include "bloody show" (a plug of blood-tinged mucus from the cervix) and rupture of membranes (a gush or trickle of clear amniotic fluid). False labor (Braxton-Hicks contractions) is irregular, does not intensify over time, and stops with rest.

**Step 2 — Prepare the Birth Space.** Warm the room to 25 to 28 degrees Celsius. Dim the lighting. Lay out the birth kit within arm's reach. Prepare warm water for compresses. Ensure privacy — only the mother, the primary midwife, the backup midwife, and one support person (partner or family member chosen by the mother) should be present.

**Step 3 — Support Through the First Stage.** Encourage the mother to eat light, easily digestible food and drink water or herbal tea frequently. Encourage movement — walking, swaying, rocking on hands and knees. Offer back massage or counter-pressure on the sacrum during contractions if the mother finds it helpful. Do not perform cervical checks unless specifically needed to assess progress — each vaginal examination increases the risk of introducing infection.

**Step 4 — Assess Full Dilation.** If a cervical check is performed: wash hands thoroughly with boiled water and soap, insert two fingers gently into the vagina, and palpate the cervix. At full dilation, the cervix will no longer be palpable around the baby's head — only the smooth, round surface of the head will be felt. In practice, full dilation can often be assessed without vaginal examination: the mother develops an involuntary urge to push, she may grunt or bear down with contractions, and a visible line of purple discoloration may appear in the natal cleft (the "purple line" — a reliable indicator of dilation).

**Step 5 — Support Pushing in an Upright Position.** Deep squat (supported by a partner behind or by gripping a rope suspended from above), kneeling, or hands-and-knees are all effective positions. The mother should push when she feels the urge, resting between contractions. Do not direct her to push harder or longer than her body demands.

**Step 6 — Perineal Support as the Head Crowns.** As the largest diameter of the baby's head distends the perineum, apply a warm, damp cloth to the perineum with gentle counter-pressure. This slows the delivery of the head and reduces the risk of perineal tearing. Instruct the mother to stop pushing and pant or blow during crowning — the head should be delivered slowly, over several contractions.

**Step 7 — Deliver the Head.** As the head emerges, support it gently with one hand. Feel around the baby's neck for a nuchal cord (umbilical cord wrapped around the neck). If the cord is loose, slip it over the baby's head. If it is tight and cannot be slipped, clamp or tie the cord in two places and cut between them before delivering the body.

**Step 8 — Deliver the Shoulders and Body.** With the next contraction, the baby will rotate externally (the head turns to one side to align with the shoulders). Guide the head gently downward to deliver the anterior (top) shoulder under the pubic bone, then upward to deliver the posterior (bottom) shoulder. The body follows easily. Catch the baby — do not pull. The baby will be slippery.

**Step 9 — Immediate Skin-to-Skin.** Place the baby directly on the mother's bare chest, face down. Dry the baby vigorously with a warm cloth while on the mother's chest. Remove the wet cloth and cover both mother and baby with warm, dry blankets. Assess breathing — the baby should cry or breathe within 60 seconds. If not, begin the neonatal resuscitation protocol immediately.

**Step 10 — Delayed Cord Clamping.** Wait until the umbilical cord stops pulsing — typically 3 to 5 minutes after birth. This allows up to 30% of the infant's total blood volume, along with critical iron reserves and stem cells, to transfer from the placenta. When the cord is flat, white, and pulseless, tie it firmly in two places — at 3 centimeters and 6 centimeters from the baby's abdomen — using boiled string. Cut between the ties with a flame-sterilized blade.

**Step 11 — Deliver the Placenta.** During the next uterine contraction (usually within 5 to 15 minutes), apply gentle, steady traction on the cord with one hand while pressing upward on the uterus above the pubic bone with the other hand (Brandt-Andrews maneuver). Do not pull forcefully — this risks uterine inversion, a catastrophic emergency. When the placenta is delivered, examine it carefully. It should be complete — a roughly circular disc, 15 to 20 centimeters in diameter, with intact membranes. If fragments appear to be missing, the risk of hemorrhage and infection is high.

**Step 12 — Assess for Bleeding.** After the placenta is delivered, palpate the uterus through the abdomen. It should feel like a firm, grapefruit-sized ball below the navel. If it is soft and boggy, begin uterine massage immediately. Estimate blood loss by weighing blood-soaked cloths (1 gram equals approximately 1 mL of blood). If estimated blood loss exceeds 500 mL, begin the PPH protocol.

**Step 13 — First Breastfeed Within One Hour.** The baby placed skin-to-skin on the mother's chest will typically begin rooting (turning the head, opening the mouth, making sucking motions) within 30 to 60 minutes. Guide the baby to the breast if needed. Ensure a proper latch: the baby's mouth should be wide open, covering most of the areola (not just the nipple), with the lower lip flanged outward and the chin pressed into the breast.

### Emergency Protocols

**Postpartum Hemorrhage Protocol**

Escalation sequence — each step is taken if the previous step has not controlled bleeding within 2 to 3 minutes:

Level 1. Vigorous bimanual uterine massage. Cup the uterine fundus through the abdomen with both hands. Rub firmly and continuously with a kneading motion. The goal is to stimulate the uterine muscle to contract. Continue for a minimum of 15 seconds before assessing effect. Simultaneously, put the baby to the breast for nipple stimulation.

Level 2. Empty the bladder. A full bladder displaces the uterus and physically prevents it from contracting effectively. The mother must urinate. If she cannot, apply gentle suprapubic pressure. Catheterization if equipment is available.

Level 3. Bimanual uterine compression. If external massage is insufficient, the attendant (hands washed with boiled water and soap) inserts one hand into the vagina, forms a fist, and presses it against the anterior wall of the uterus. The other hand presses down on the uterine fundus through the abdomen, compressing the uterus between the two hands. This is painful and should be explained to the mother. Maintain compression for a minimum of 5 minutes before reassessing.

Level 4. If bleeding is from a retained placental fragment, manual removal may be necessary. With a cleaned hand, follow the cord through the cervix, identify the plane between the placenta and the uterine wall, and gently sweep the fragment free. This carries a risk of uterine perforation and hemorrhage and should only be attempted when the alternative is the mother bleeding to death.

Level 5. Aortic compression. If all other measures fail and the mother is losing consciousness, locate the aorta (the pulsating vessel in the midline of the abdomen, above the navel) and compress it firmly against the spine with a closed fist. This reduces blood flow to the uterus and may buy time. This is a last resort.

Throughout the entire protocol: keep the mother warm (hypothermia worsens coagulopathy), elevate her legs, give fluids by mouth if she is conscious, and do not leave her unattended.

**Breech Birth Protocol**

A breech presentation (buttocks or feet presenting first instead of the head) occurs in approximately 3% to 4% of term pregnancies. In a zero-technology environment, external cephalic version (manually turning the baby through the abdominal wall) may be attempted between 36 and 37 weeks, but success rates are only 50% to 60% and the procedure carries risks of placental abruption and cord compression.

If a breech baby is in labor and delivery is imminent, a vaginal breech delivery may be the only option. The critical principle is "hands off the breech." The body must deliver spontaneously to the level of the umbilicus — do not pull, do not assist, do not touch. Pulling on a breech baby causes the arms to extend above the head and the head to deflect, making delivery of the after-coming head much more difficult and dangerous.

Once the baby has delivered to the umbilicus spontaneously: gently sweep down each arm by inserting two fingers along the baby's back, over the shoulder, and down to the elbow, flexing the arm across the chest. Once both arms are delivered, the head must be delivered. The Mauriceau-Smellie-Veit maneuver: the attendant places the baby face-down over their forearm, with two fingers of that hand placed on the baby's cheekbones (malar bones) to maintain flexion of the head. The other hand is placed on the baby's back with two fingers hooked over the shoulders. Gentle downward traction is applied until the hairline is visible at the perineum, then the body is rotated upward in an arc to deliver the face and head over the perineum.

This is a high-risk delivery. The after-coming head may become trapped. If this occurs and the head cannot be delivered within 3 to 4 minutes, the baby will suffer brain damage or death from asphyxia. This protocol should be practiced on models (a doll and a pelvic model, or a cloth simulation) until the maneuvers are automatic.

**Neonatal Resuscitation Protocol**

Time zero: the baby is born. Begin the count.

0-30 seconds: Dry the baby vigorously with a warm cloth. Rub the back, flick the soles of the feet. Position the head in slight extension (neutral or "sniffing" position — do not hyperextend). Most babies will begin breathing with stimulation alone.

30-60 seconds: If the baby is not breathing or is gasping, clear the airway. Suction the mouth first, then the nose (suctioning the nose first can trigger a gasp that aspirates oral secretions). Use a bulb syringe if available. If not, wipe the mouth with a clean cloth around a finger, then use mouth-to-mouth-and-nose suction (place your mouth over the baby's mouth and nose, apply gentle suction, spit out secretions).

60 seconds (the Golden Minute): If the baby is still not breathing, begin positive pressure ventilation. Place your mouth over the baby's mouth and nose. Give a small, gentle puff — just enough to see the chest rise. If the chest does not rise, reposition the head (the airway may be blocked by poor positioning) and try again. Give one puff every 3 seconds (20 breaths per minute). Continue for 60 seconds, then reassess.

2-3 minutes: If the baby is still not breathing spontaneously but the heart rate is palpable (feel for the pulse at the base of the umbilical cord — it should be above 60 beats per minute), continue ventilation. If the heart rate is below 60 despite adequate ventilation, begin chest compressions: two thumbs on the lower third of the sternum, fingers encircling the chest, compress one-third of the chest depth, 3 compressions followed by 1 ventilation breath (3:1 ratio), 90 compressions and 30 breaths per minute.

4-6 minutes: If there is still no response — no spontaneous breathing, no heart rate — brain damage is occurring. Continue resuscitation efforts for a minimum of 10 minutes before considering cessation. Some babies with prolonged asphyxia do recover, though the risk of permanent neurological damage increases with every minute.

### Postnatal Care

**The Fourth Trimester**

The first 30 to 40 days after birth are the most dangerous period for both mother and infant. The mother is vulnerable to postpartum hemorrhage (which can occur as a delayed event up to 6 weeks after delivery if retained placental tissue is present), puerperal infection (infection of the uterus via the open cervix — fever, foul-smelling lochia, and abdominal tenderness are warning signs requiring immediate antibiotic treatment if available), and postpartum mood disorders. The mother must be relieved of all physical labor for a minimum of 30 days. The community provides all meals, water, firewood, and childcare for older children. This is not indulgence — it is a survival protocol. A mother who hemorrhages on day 10 because she was carrying water is a preventable death.

**Breastfeeding Support**

Proper latch: the baby's mouth should be wide open, covering most of the areola, not just the nipple. The lower lip should be flanged outward. The baby's chin should be pressed into the breast, with the nose free. A clicking sound during feeding indicates a poor latch that should be corrected (break the suction with a clean finger in the corner of the mouth and reposition). Breastfeeding should not be painful after the first few seconds of latch — persistent pain indicates incorrect positioning.

Engorgement (painful, swollen breasts when the milk comes in, typically day 3 to 5): apply warm compresses before feeding (to promote milk flow), cold compresses after feeding (to reduce swelling). Frequent feeding and complete breast emptying are the best treatment.

Mastitis (breast infection — a hot, red, painful area on one breast, often with fever): continue breastfeeding from the affected breast (this does not harm the baby and is essential for treatment). Apply warm compresses. Increase fluid intake. If available, treat with antibiotics (see cross-reference to Module 03).

If the mother cannot breastfeed (maternal death, severe illness, or true lactation failure after exhausting all support measures): the preferred substitute is goat milk, diluted 2 parts goat milk to 1 part boiled water, with a small amount of sugar or honey (for infants over 12 months only — honey is dangerous for infants under 12 months due to botulism risk; for younger infants use sugar only). Feed by small open cup held to the infant's lips — never by bottle, which is impossible to sterilize in field conditions and causes nipple confusion that may prevent a return to breastfeeding if the mother recovers.

**Galactagogues (Milk-Promoting Substances)**

If milk supply is inadequate despite frequent feeding: fenugreek seed tea (steep 1 tablespoon of seeds in hot water for 10 minutes, drink 3 times daily), fennel seed tea (same preparation), oats (porridge or gruel daily). See Module 06 for preparation details and additional herbal galactagogues.

**Newborn Danger Signs**

The following signs in a newborn require immediate attention:

- Fever (body hot to touch, especially the abdomen) or hypothermia (body cold to touch despite warming measures).
- Poor feeding — the baby who was feeding well and stops, or who cannot sustain sucking.
- Lethargy — the baby who is unusually sleepy and difficult to rouse for feeds.
- Rapid breathing (more than 60 breaths per minute) or chest indrawing (the skin between the ribs sucks in with each breath).
- Jaundice appearing within the first 24 hours.
- Umbilical redness, swelling, or foul-smelling discharge (signs of omphalitis — umbilical infection, which can become rapidly fatal sepsis).
- Convulsions.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Premature Intervention in Normal Labor.** Anxiety, impatience, or lack of experience causes the midwife to intervene in a labor that is progressing normally — artificial rupture of membranes, directing the mother to push before full dilation, pulling on the baby during delivery. Each of these interventions increases the risk of complications. Prevention: patience. Labor takes time. First births commonly take 12 to 24 hours from onset to delivery. The midwife intervenes only for specific indications (signs of fetal distress, prolonged second stage without progress, hemorrhage). Watching and waiting is not passive — it is the primary skill of midwifery.

2. **Delayed PPH Response.** The midwife does not recognize excessive bleeding in time because blood is absorbed by cloths and bedding, and the total volume is underestimated. A woman who is lying on absorbent material may lose 1,000 mL of blood before anyone realizes it. Prevention: weigh all blood-soaked cloths and materials on a scale if available (1 gram equals approximately 1 mL of blood). If no scale is available, a fully soaked standard-sized cloth (30 x 30 cm) holds approximately 100 mL. If estimated blood loss reaches 500 mL, begin PPH protocol immediately — do not wait to see if it stops on its own.

3. **Neonatal Hypothermia from Routine Bathing.** The baby is bathed immediately after birth, as is the custom in many cultures, causing rapid evaporative and conductive heat loss. A healthy newborn can become dangerously hypothermic within 10 minutes of a bath in a room below 25 degrees. Prevention: delay the first bath for a minimum of 24 hours. The vernix caseosa (the white, waxy coating on the baby's skin at birth) is protective — it has antimicrobial properties and helps insulate the skin. Do not wash it off. If the baby must be cleaned, use a warm, damp cloth only.

4. **Breastfeeding Failure Leading to Neonatal Starvation.** Breastfeeding difficulties — poor latch, insufficient milk supply, nipple pain — cause the mother to give up on breastfeeding in the first few days. Without an adequate substitute, the infant develops dehydration and hypoglycemia by day 3 to 4. Prevention: skilled breastfeeding support from the first hour. A second trained woman (not the midwife, who is managing the birth and immediate postpartum) should be designated as the breastfeeding support person. Latch assessment at every feed in the first 48 hours. If breastfeeding is truly failing despite support, begin cup feeding with animal milk substitute immediately — do not wait until the infant shows signs of dehydration.

5. **Obstructed Labor Not Recognized.** The midwife does not recognize the signs of true obstruction — no descent despite hours of pushing, development of Bandl's ring, maternal exhaustion — and continues to encourage pushing. Prolonged obstruction risks uterine rupture (catastrophic hemorrhage, near-certain maternal death without surgery) and fetal death from asphyxia. Prevention: if there is no descent of the presenting part after 2 to 3 hours of active pushing with good contractions, acknowledge the situation. Attempt repositioning (hands and knees, deep squat, lateral position). If repositioning does not produce progress within 30 to 60 minutes, this is likely true mechanical obstruction. The midwife must communicate honestly with the mother and family about the situation and the limits of what can be done.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

* **Oxytocin** — the hormone that drives uterine contractions during labor and the milk ejection reflex during breastfeeding; released in response to cervical stretching, nipple stimulation, and emotional safety
* **Ferguson reflex** — the positive feedback loop in which pressure of the fetal head on the cervix triggers oxytocin release, which drives stronger contractions, which increase pressure on the cervix
* **Cervical dilation** — the progressive opening of the cervix from 0 to 10 centimeters during the first stage of labor
* **Effacement** — the thinning and shortening of the cervix that occurs alongside dilation
* **Cardinal movements** — the sequence of rotations (engagement, descent, flexion, internal rotation, extension, external rotation, expulsion) the fetal head performs to navigate through the bony pelvis
* **Uterine atony** — failure of the uterine muscle to contract after delivery, causing the open blood vessels at the placental site to bleed; the most common cause of postpartum hemorrhage
* **Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH)** — blood loss exceeding 500 mL after vaginal delivery; a life-threatening emergency
* **Bandl's ring** — a visible or palpable constriction ring across the abdomen during obstructed labor, marking the boundary between the thinning lower uterine segment and the contracting upper segment; a warning sign of impending uterine rupture
* **Colostrum** — the thick, yellow first milk produced in the first 2 to 3 days after birth; rich in secretory IgA antibodies, lactoferrin, and immune cells
* **Secretory IgA** — an antibody class present in breast milk that coats the infant's mucosal surfaces and provides passive immune protection against local pathogens
* **Microbiome transfer** — the process by which the infant acquires its initial microbial colonization from the mother through vaginal birth, skin-to-skin contact, and breastfeeding
* **Kangaroo care** — prolonged skin-to-skin contact between mother (or caregiver) and infant, which regulates temperature, heart rate, breathing, and promotes breastfeeding
* **Galactagogue** — a substance that promotes or increases breast milk production; examples include fenugreek, fennel, and oats
* **Nuchal cord** — an umbilical cord wrapped around the baby's neck; occurs in approximately 20% to 30% of deliveries and is usually manageable by slipping the cord over the head
* **Fundal height** — the distance from the pubic bone to the top of the uterine fundus, measured in centimeters; correlates approximately with gestational age in weeks from 20 to 36 weeks
* **Pinard horn** — a cone-shaped listening device placed against the mother's abdomen to auscultate fetal heart tones; can be improvised from carved wood or molded clay
