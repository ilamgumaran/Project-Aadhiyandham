**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> **Caution — small does not mean unimportant:** The [big-animal module](02_Livestock_and_Mechanical_Animals.md) covers cattle and oxen — capital-intensive, slow to breed, years to a return. Poultry and rabbits are the opposite and the perfect complement: they breed fast, convert scraps and forage to protein in weeks, need little space, and give a new settlement a resilient protein supply long before a cattle herd could exist. Two traps recur, though, and both are lethal in their way: the *night predator* that empties an unprotected coop or hutch in one visit, and the *lean-meat diet* — rabbit especially — that starves people of fat even as they eat their fill. Respect both and small stock become the settlement's most reliable everyday protein.

## 1. Why This Is Important

A settlement's protein security has two timescales. Draft animals and cattle are the long game — traction, milk, large meat — but they take years to build and are a heavy loss if they die. **Small stock are the fast game:** a hen lays through the season, a rabbit doe can produce many kilos of offspring meat a year, and a flock or colony rebuilds from a single breeding pair in months. They convert what would be waste — kitchen scraps, garden trimmings, forage, insects — into eggs and meat, and they do it in a footprint a courtyard can hold. For the first hungry years especially, small stock bridge the gap between arrival and a mature animal system, and they never stop being valuable.

They also do *work* beyond protein: poultry control pests, till and manure the ground, and (geese and guinea fowl) act as living alarms; rabbits produce fur and a garden-ready manure. In a [closed-loop settlement](01_Rationale_and_Importance.md) they are small, efficient engines that turn waste into food and fertility.

## 2. Expected Outcome

A self-sustaining flock of poultry (eggs, meat, pest control, manure) and a rabbit colony (efficient meat, fur, manure), both housed predator-proof, fed largely on the settlement's waste and forage, breeding without external input, and integrated so their manure feeds the garden and their appetites reduce its pests and scraps — with the fat-pairing and night-security disciplines that keep them a benefit and not a disaster.

## 3. Poultry — The Everyday Producer

Chickens are the default; ducks (wetter ground, more forage), geese (grazers and guardians), and guinea fowl (tick and pest control, superb alarms) each add something.

*   **What they give:** **eggs** — near-daily complete protein through the laying season, the settlement's steadiest animal food; **meat** — surplus and spent birds; **services** — they eat insects, ticks, and weed seeds, scratch and till the soil, and their rich manure is a potent (hot) fertilizer that must be [composted before use](../04_Food_and_Soil_Sovereignty/02_Terra_Preta_and_Biochar.md); and **security** — geese and guinea fowl raise a loud alarm at intruders (a living tripwire — see [passive defense](../../Outcome_3_Perimeter_Defense/07_Passive_Defense/02_Passive_Perimeter_Defense.md)).
*   **Housing:** a **predator-proof coop for the night** is non-negotiable — solid walls or tight fencing dug in at the base against diggers, closed at dusk. Inside: roosts (birds sleep off the ground), nest boxes (clean, dim, for laying), ventilation without drafts, and dry bedding. Days can be free-range or in a run; free-ranging maximizes forage and pest control but raises daytime predator and crop-raid exposure.
*   **Feeding:** scraps, forage, insects, and grain; free-ranging birds feed themselves substantially in season. Laying hens need enough protein and calcium (crushed shell or bone) to keep laying and not thin their shells.
*   **Breeding — self-sustaining is the goal:** keep a rooster and let broody hens hatch, or manage a simple incubation; select breeders for hardiness, foraging ability, and steady laying, not fragility. A flock that reproduces itself is protein sovereignty; one dependent on outside chicks is not.
*   **Health:** most poultry disease is a crowding-and-filth disease — clean dry bedding, space, and clean water prevent the majority. [Quarantine new birds 14 days](02_Livestock_and_Mechanical_Animals.md), keep species reasonably separate, and remove sick birds promptly. During any [epidemic](../05_Health_and_the_Body/11_Epidemic_and_Quarantine_Protocol.md) (avian disease can be serious), tighten biosecurity.

## 4. Rabbits — The Efficient Meat Machine

Rabbits are the most efficient small meat animal a settlement can keep, with one important nutritional caveat.

*   **Why they excel:** they breed prolifically (short gestation, large frequent litters), convert *forage and greens* — not grain — to meat efficiently, need little space, and are **quiet** (a real security asset over noisy stock — see [low-profile](../../Outcome_1_Locating_Refugia/01_The_Journey/12_Journey_Scenarios_and_Contingencies/04_Low_Profile_Travel_and_Encounter_Protocols.md) living). A few does and a buck can supply a family's meat.
*   **The fat trap — this is the important one.** Rabbit meat is extremely *lean*. A diet of rabbit (or any lean meat) *alone* causes **"rabbit starvation"** — protein poisoning from too much lean protein without fat: nausea, weakness, and decline even while eating to fullness (see [Emergency Nutrition](../04_Food_and_Soil_Sovereignty/06_Emergency_Nutrition.md)). **Rabbit must be eaten with a fat source** — rendered animal fat, poultry fat, nuts, or oils. Plan the fat, not just the protein.
*   **Housing:** hutches or a colony, protected from predators (the same night-security rule as poultry) **and from heat** — rabbits tolerate cold well but *die in heat*; they need shade, ventilation, and cool water in summer, and this kills more domestic rabbits than predators do. Keep them dry (wet, dirty conditions breed disease).
*   **Breeding:** does and a buck; the cycle is fast, so a small colony turns over quickly — manage numbers to your forage and space, and select for hardiness and mothering.
*   **Byproducts:** **fur/pelts** for warm clothing and lining (see [Textiles and Leather](../../Outcome_5_Decadal_Resilience/09_The_Mechanics/05_Textiles_and_Leather.md)), and manure that — unusually — is "cold" and can often go **straight to the garden** without composting, a real convenience.

## 5. Integration: Small Engines in the Loop

Small stock shine because they *close loops*: they eat the settlement's scraps, weeds, and pests; they produce eggs and meat; their manure fertilizes the [garden](../04_Food_and_Soil_Sovereignty/02_Terra_Preta_and_Biochar.md); poultry till and de-pest beds (a chicken tractor over a fallow bed clears and manures it); and their fur and feathers clothe and insulate. Designed into the settlement rather than bolted on, they raise total food output while *reducing* waste and pest pressure — the ecological-harmony ideal at small scale.

## 6. Common Failure Modes

*   **The unlocked coop/hutch at night.** Leaving small stock unprotected after dark. **[Tier L] Consequence:** a single fox, weasel, dog, or raccoon can kill an entire flock or colony in one night — the classic, devastating loss. **Prevention:** predator-proof housing, dug in against diggers, closed every dusk without fail (see [livestock guardian systems](../../Outcome_3_Perimeter_Defense/07b_Animal_Coexistence_Protocols/04_Livestock_Guardian_Systems.md)).
*   **Rabbit-only diet.** Relying on lean rabbit as the main meat without fat. **[Tier L] Consequence:** rabbit starvation — protein poisoning, weakness, decline. **Prevention:** always pair lean meat with a deliberate fat source.
*   **Rabbits in the heat.** No shade or cooling in summer. **[Tier L] Consequence:** heat kills the colony. **Prevention:** shade, ventilation, cool water; site hutches out of afternoon sun.
*   **Crowding and filth.** Overstocked, dirty, damp housing. **[Tier C→L] Consequence:** disease sweeps the flock/colony. **Prevention:** space, clean dry bedding, clean water, prompt removal of the sick, 14-day quarantine of newcomers.
*   **Dependence on outside stock.** Keeping animals that don't reproduce for you. **[Tier C] Consequence:** the protein supply is only as secure as the supply of replacements. **Prevention:** breed your own; select self-sufficient, hardy lines.

## 7. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Broody hen:** A hen that will sit on and hatch eggs; the settlement's free incubator.
*   **Chicken tractor:** A movable coop/run that lets poultry clear, de-pest, and manure a bed in sequence.
*   **Cold manure:** Rabbit (and some other) manure mild enough to use in the garden without composting first.
*   **Doe / buck / kit:** Female / male / young rabbit.
*   **Point-of-lay / broiler:** A hen entering egg production / a bird raised for meat.
*   **Rabbit starvation:** Protein poisoning from lean meat without fat; the reason rabbit is always eaten with fat.

## 8. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 8.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Preparation:** Build predator-proof housing *first*; secure hardy breeding stock; line up scrap/forage feed and bedding.
*   **Execution:** Establish a laying flock with a rooster and a rabbit colony with does and a buck; feed on waste and forage; integrate manure and pest-control into the garden; always pair lean meat with fat.
*   **Review:** Manage numbers to forage and space; select breeders for hardiness and productivity each generation; tighten biosecurity at any disease sign.

### 8.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier
*   **[Tier L — Law] Unsecured night housing** → a predator empties the flock/colony in one visit. *Lock up every dusk; dig housing in against diggers.*
*   **[Tier L — Law] Rabbit/lean-meat diet without fat** → rabbit starvation. *Always eat lean meat with a fat source.*
*   **[Tier L — Law] Rabbits without summer shade/cooling** → heat death of the colony. *Shade, air, water.*
*   **[Tier C — Craft] Stocking density, feed, breeding rate** → calibrate to forage, space, and climate.

### 8.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** Small-stock keeping is easy to learn and easy to do carelessly; the night-lockup and the fat-pairing are simple rules whose lapse is catastrophic, so teach the *consequences*, not just the chores, to everyone who tends the animals.
