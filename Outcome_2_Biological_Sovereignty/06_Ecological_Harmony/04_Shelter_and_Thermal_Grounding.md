# Shelter and Thermal Grounding: Building with the Earth's Thermal Rhythms

**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Shelter is the second most critical survival need after water. Exposure kills faster than starvation — hypothermia can be fatal in 3-4 hours in wet, windy conditions when the body's core temperature drops below 30C, and hyperthermia becomes dangerous above 40C core temperature. In a post-grid settlement, the margin between survivable and lethal conditions is not bridged by a thermostat. It is bridged by the physics of the building itself. Every design decision — wall thickness, window orientation, roof pitch, foundation depth — is a thermal engineering decision, whether the builder recognizes it or not.

In a settlement without electricity, the shelter must provide thermal regulation through entirely passive means: thermal mass to absorb and release heat, insulation to slow heat transfer, solar orientation to capture or reject solar energy by season, natural ventilation to drive cooling airflow, and earth coupling to tap the stable subsurface temperature. These are the same physical principles that governed human shelter for 10,000 years before the industrial era, and they will govern it again when the grid fails permanently.

This module covers the building physics — heat transfer by conduction, convection, and radiation — and the traditional building techniques that exploit these physics to create comfortable, durable shelters from local materials alone. The settlement's shelters must last 20-50 years, withstand local weather extremes (wind, snow load, heavy rain, temperature swings of -15C to 40C), and maintain interior temperatures of 15-25C year-round without any external energy input. That is a demanding engineering specification. It is achievable with stone, timber, earth, and straw — but only if the builder understands why these materials work.

> **CAUTION**: Construction involves working at height (roofing), with heavy materials (stone, timber), and with cutting tools (axes, saws, chisels). Falls from roof height are a leading cause of construction injury and death. Use scaffolding or ladders secured at the base and braced against lateral movement. Never work alone on roofing — a second person must be present and capable of rendering aid. Wear eye protection when splitting stone, as fragments travel at high velocity. Timber felling requires a clear escape path at 45 degrees to the planned fall direction — see Common Failure Modes for specific hazards.

## Theoretical Foundation

### Building Physics: Heat Transfer

All thermal comfort in a shelter reduces to controlling three modes of heat transfer. Understanding these modes is the difference between a warm, dry dwelling and a cold, damp box that slowly kills its occupants through chronic exposure.

**Conduction** is heat flow through solid materials from the warm side to the cool side. The rate is governed by thermal conductivity, denoted k and measured in W/m-K. Every building material has a characteristic k value:

- Stone (granite, basalt): k = 1.5-3.0 W/m-K — conducts heat rapidly
- Earth (rammed, cob): k = 0.5-1.0 W/m-K — moderate conductor
- Wood (across grain): k = 0.12-0.17 W/m-K — poor conductor, decent insulator
- Straw (baled): k = 0.05-0.07 W/m-K — excellent insulator
- Still air: k = 0.025 W/m-K — the best insulator available in nature

Lower k means better insulation. Stone conducts heat 30-60 times faster than straw. A stone wall without insulation bleeds heat to the exterior in winter and admits heat in summer. But stone's high conductivity also means it absorbs and stores heat readily — the basis of thermal mass design.

**Convection** is heat transfer by the movement of air. Warm air is less dense than cool air, so it rises — this buoyancy drives natural convective loops inside any heated space. In a shelter with a fire, warm air rises to the ceiling, spreads laterally, cools against the walls and roof, descends, and returns to the fire.

Controlled ventilation exploits convection for summer cooling by allowing hot air to exit through high openings while cool air enters through low openings. Uncontrolled air leakage — gaps in walls, poorly sealed doors, cracks around window frames — causes massive winter heat loss as warm interior air escapes and is replaced by cold exterior air.

**Radiation** is the emission of infrared energy from all warm surfaces. A campfire heats the person sitting beside it primarily by radiation, not by warming the air between them. Dark, rough surfaces absorb and emit radiation more effectively than light, smooth surfaces. A dark stone floor absorbs solar radiation during the day and re-emits it as infrared heat at night. A whitewashed wall reflects solar radiation in summer, keeping the interior cooler.

**R-value (thermal resistance)** quantifies how well a wall assembly resists heat flow. R = thickness (in meters) divided by thermal conductivity (k). Higher R means better insulation.

Practical R-values for common wall assemblies:

- 30 cm stone wall: R = 0.30 / 2.0 = 0.15 m2-K/W (very poor insulation)
- 30 cm cob wall: R = 0.30 / 0.7 = 0.43 m2-K/W (poor insulation, good thermal mass)
- 30 cm straw bale wall: R = 0.30 / 0.06 = 5.0 m2-K/W (excellent insulation)
- 45 cm straw bale wall: R = 0.45 / 0.06 = 7.5 m2-K/W (comparable to modern insulated wall)

**Thermal mass** is the capacity of a dense material (stone, earth, water) to absorb heat slowly and release it slowly. A stone wall heated by afternoon sun releases that stored heat into the interior through the night — functioning as a thermal battery.

The key metric is volumetric heat capacity: stone stores approximately 2.0 MJ/m3-K, earth about 1.5 MJ/m3-K, wood about 1.0 MJ/m3-K, and straw about 0.15 MJ/m3-K. Dense materials store vastly more heat per unit volume.

**The diurnal temperature cycle** is the daily swing between daytime highs and nighttime lows. In most temperate climates, this swing ranges from 10-25C. A building with sufficient thermal mass can average these extremes, maintaining a more stable interior temperature than the exterior.

A 40 cm rammed earth wall has a thermal lag of approximately 10-12 hours — meaning the heat absorbed from afternoon sun reaches the interior surface around midnight, precisely when heating is most needed.

### Passive Solar Design

Solar gain through south-facing openings (in the Northern Hemisphere) is the simplest and most powerful passive heating strategy available. In winter, the sun travels a low arc across the southern sky, with a noon altitude of only 20-30 degrees at the solstice (at 40 degrees North latitude). In summer, the same sun passes nearly overhead at 60-70 degrees altitude. This seasonal variation allows a south-facing window with a properly sized roof overhang to admit maximum solar energy in winter while blocking it in summer.

**The overhang calculation:** overhang depth = window height multiplied by tan(90 degrees minus summer solar altitude). For 40 degrees North latitude, where summer noon solar altitude reaches approximately 73 degrees, the overhang depth should be roughly 0.6 times the window height. A window 1.0 m tall requires an overhang of 0.6 m to achieve full summer shading.

**The thermal mass floor** — dark-colored stone, compacted earth, or clay tile — placed where winter sunlight falls through south-facing openings absorbs solar energy during daylight and re-radiates it as infrared heat through the evening and night. A well-designed thermal mass floor provides 4-8 hours of passive heating after sunset, bridging the gap between the last daylight and the coldest pre-dawn hours.

**Cross-ventilation** is the summer counterpart to passive solar heating. Openings on opposite walls create a pressure differential when wind blows against the building, driving air through the interior.

The Venturi effect enhances this cooling: where the airflow path constricts — through a narrow window, between two buildings, or through a gap between geological features like two hills or a break in a dense tree line — air velocity increases and pressure drops. Building just behind such a topographic funnel compresses and accelerates the summer breeze, creating a passive cooling system. The velocity increase is proportional to the ratio of the wider opening to the narrower passage.

### The Thermal Belt

Cold air drainage is a nightly phenomenon driven by radiational cooling. After sunset, exposed ground surfaces radiate infrared energy to the sky and cool rapidly. The air in contact with these surfaces cools, becomes denser, and flows downhill under gravity — a katabatic wind. This cold air pools in valley floors, topographic bowls, and depressions, creating temperature inversions.

Valley floors can be 5-10C colder than mid-slope positions on clear, calm nights. In extreme cases with snow cover and clear skies, the differential can reach 15C.

The thermal belt occupies the zone between the cold valley floor and the windy, exposed ridge top — typically one-third to two-thirds of the way up the slope. This band receives the benefits of cold air drainage (the cold air drains past it, downhill) without suffering the wind exposure of the ridge. On a south-facing slope, the thermal belt also receives maximum winter solar radiation.

Historical validation is extensive and global. Mediterranean hill towns perch on mid-slope terraces. Appalachian homesteads occupy the "bench" above the creek bottom. Japanese mountain villages cluster at mid-elevation between valley rice paddies and forested peaks. Andean communities build on slopes well above the valley floor where frost settles. These patterns were not accidents — they were accumulated survival knowledge of hundreds of generations.

### Structural Principles for Durability

**The moisture gradient** determines the lifespan of every timber structure. Wood rots when moisture content exceeds 20% for extended periods, because fungal decay organisms require moisture, oxygen, and moderate temperature (5-35C) to colonize. The critical detail: structural timber must never touch wet soil directly. Capillary action wicks groundwater upward through any porous material in contact with soil, maintaining permanently elevated moisture levels. Stone foundations, gravel pads, or ventilated underfloor spaces break this capillary path.

**Rot-resistant species** provide an additional margin of safety. Certain species contain natural tannins, resins, or tyloses (cellular plugs) that resist fungal colonization:

- **Black locust** (Robinia pseudoacacia): 25+ years of ground-contact life, the most durable temperate hardwood. Heartwood is dense (770 kg/m3), extremely hard, and naturally resistant to both rot and insects.
- **White oak** (Quercus alba): 15-25 years ground contact. Tyloses block the vessels, preventing water penetration. Historically used for ship keels and foundation sills.
- **Eastern red cedar** (Juniperus virginiana): 15-20 years ground contact. Aromatic oils repel insects and inhibit fungal growth.
- **American chestnut** (Castanea dentata): 20+ years ground contact. High tannin content. Where available (standing dead, salvage), it remains superb.

Never use pine, poplar, birch, or other sapwood-heavy species for posts or sills near the ground. They rot in 2-5 years even with good drainage. Harvest structural poles in deep winter when sap is down in the roots — the wood dries harder, lighter, and with fewer cracks.

**The clink test** provides a field method for assessing stone quality. Strike two candidate stones together sharply. A dull thud indicates soft stone — sandstone, shale, or heavily weathered material that will crumble under structural load and disintegrate with freeze-thaw cycling. A sharp, high-pitched clink indicates hard, dense stone — granite, basalt, quartzite, or dense limestone — suitable for foundations, walls, and paving.

**Timber joinery** creates structural connections without metal fasteners:

- **Mortise-and-tenon:** a rectangular peg (tenon) fits tightly into a matching hole (mortise). The strongest joint for connecting beams to posts. Secured with a wooden treenail.
- **Dovetail:** interlocking fan-shaped projections resist pulling apart. Used for corner joints in log construction and for connecting tie beams to wall plates.
- **Lap joint:** two timbers each notched to half depth and overlapped, creating a flush connection. Simpler than mortise-and-tenon but adequate for non-critical connections.

These joints have been proven over 5,000+ years — Egyptian, Chinese, Japanese, and European timber framing traditions all independently developed them.

**Roof pitch and material** are interdependent. Each roofing material requires a minimum pitch to shed water:

- Thatch (reed, straw): 45-50 degrees minimum. Steep pitch forces water to run off before soaking inward. Well-laid thatch lasts 20-30 years.
- Wood shingles (cedar, oak): 30-40 degrees. Overlapping split wood sheds water at lower angles.
- Sod/turf: 20-30 degrees. Heavy (200-400 kg/m2, requires robust structure), but excellent insulation (R 2-4). Requires waterproof membrane beneath (historically birch bark).
- Slate or flat stone: 25-35 degrees. Extremely durable (100+ years) but adds significant dead load.

## Core Principles

1. **The Thermal Mass + Insulation Principle.** The ideal shelter combines thermal mass on the interior (stone, earth, or water containers that store heat) with insulation on the exterior (straw, earth berm, or air gap that prevents heat loss). Thermal mass alone — a stone building without insulation — is cold in winter because the mass conducts heat to the cold exterior as fast as it absorbs heat from the fire. Insulation alone — straw bale without interior mass — warms quickly but loses stored heat quickly. The combination provides stable temperatures with minimal heating input: the insulation traps the heat, and the thermal mass stores and slowly releases it.

2. **The South-Facing Principle** (Northern Hemisphere; reverse for Southern). Orient the long axis of every building east-west, with the primary window wall facing south. This maximizes winter solar gain through the low-angle winter sun while allowing summer shading with roof overhang. A south-facing building in a temperate climate can meet 30-50% of its winter heating needs from passive solar gain alone — a massive reduction in firewood consumption.

3. **The Moisture Exclusion Principle.** More shelters fail from moisture than from structural overload. A dry timber building lasts 50-100 years; a wet one rots in 5-10. Three critical moisture barriers: (a) stone or gravel foundation lifting all timber above ground moisture, (b) adequate roof overhang — minimum 30 cm beyond the wall face, 45 cm preferred — preventing rain splash, and (c) roof waterproofing maintained in good repair with immediate patching of any breach.

4. **The Ventilation Control Principle.** A shelter must close tightly in winter (retaining heat) and open fully in summer (expelling heat). Adjustable openings — solid wood shutters, doors on opposite walls, high vent openings near the roof peak, low intake vents near the floor — give occupants control. A sealed shelter with a fire can reach lethal carbon monoxide levels; a shelter that cannot seal becomes unheatable. Both extremes kill.

5. **The Local Materials Principle.** Build with what grows or lies within 1 km of the site. The transport cost of imported materials is prohibitive for a non-motorized settlement: moving one ton of stone 10 km by hand or animal cart represents a full day of team labor. Study local vernacular architecture — traditional builders solved the same problems with the same materials over centuries of refinement. Their solutions are tested by time and adapted to local conditions in ways no generic plan can match.

## Practical Implementation

### Micro-Site Selection: The Perfect Spot

Before gathering a single branch, the builder must identify the exact location that will naturally moderate temperature, shed water, and resist flooding.

**Flood avoidance (the 100-year rule).** Never build on a flat valley floor or immediately adjacent to a water source. Walk up from the riverbank until you find the highest line of dead twigs and water-washed debris tangled in tree roots — this is the historic flood line, the physical evidence of past high-water events. The shelter must be built at least 6 m (20 feet) vertical elevation above this debris line, on a gentle slope of 2-5% grade that sheds surface water without eroding.

**The thermal belt position.** As detailed in the Theoretical Foundation, build at mid-slope — one-third to two-thirds of the way up a south-facing slope. This captures maximum low-angle winter sun, stays above the cold air that pools in the valley at night, and avoids the wind exposure of the ridge. Before committing to a site, spend at least one calm, clear night at the candidate location and physically feel the temperature difference compared to the valley floor. The difference — often 5-10C — will confirm or deny the thermal belt position.

**The Venturi breeze corridor.** Identify two large geological features (hills, rock outcroppings) or a break in a dense tree line that naturally funnels prevailing summer wind. Building just behind this constriction accelerates the summer breeze through the Venturi effect, providing passive cooling without any constructed mechanism.

### The Timber-Frame Roundhouse (Primary Recommendation for First Shelter)

The roundhouse is recommended as the settlement's first permanent shelter for sound structural reasons. A circular plan encloses the maximum floor area per unit of wall material (highest area-to-perimeter ratio of any plane figure). It distributes wind load evenly around the curved wall, with no flat faces for wind to push against. It eliminates corners — both structural weak points and locations where heat loss concentrates.

**Dimensions:** 5-6 m internal diameter provides 20-28 m2 of floor space, adequate for 4-6 occupants. A 6 m roundhouse has approximately 19 m of wall perimeter. The same area in a square requires 21.2 m of wall — 12% more material for identical space.

**Construction sequence:**

1. **Site preparation.** Clear vegetation from the footprint plus 2 m beyond. Level the ground within 5 cm variation. Dig a drainage channel (15 cm deep, 15 cm wide) around the perimeter at 1 m beyond the wall line, draining downhill.

2. **Stone ring foundation.** Excavate a ring trench 30-40 cm deep and 30 cm wide. Pack tightly with hard stones selected by the clink test. The top must project at least 15 cm above surrounding grade. Level the top course carefully — the entire structure sits on this ring.

3. **Wall posts.** Set 8-12 timber posts (15-20 cm diameter, rot-resistant species — black locust preferred) into the stone ring at equal spacing. Posts stand 1.8-2.2 m above foundation, bottom 30 cm embedded in stone and held plumb by packing. Brace temporarily with diagonal stakes.

4. **Wall infill: wattle-and-daub.** Weave flexible rods (hazel, willow, or any straight pliable species) horizontally between posts. Plaster both sides with daub — clay subsoil, chopped straw, and water mixed to thick mortar consistency. Apply in 2-3 cm layers, drying between coats. Total thickness 15-20 cm gives R approximately 1.5-2.0. For colder climates, add an exterior coat of clay-straw slip to increase insulation.

5. **Ring beam.** Install a horizontal timber (wall plate) connecting all post tops, distributing roof load evenly. Join segments with half-lap joints secured by wooden pegs.

6. **Roof structure.** Two options: (a) central king post with radiating rafters, or (b) reciprocal frame — a self-supporting spiral where each rafter rests on the one before it, requiring no central post. The reciprocal frame is structurally elegant but requires precise carpentry. Either must achieve minimum 45 degree pitch for thatch.

7. **Thatch roofing.** Lay bundles of reed (Phragmites australis), cattail, or long-stemmed straw from eave to ridge. Begin at the bottom course and work upward. Each course overlaps the one below by at least 80%, so only 20% of each bundle is exposed to weather. Minimum thickness: 30 cm at the eave, 20 cm at the ridge. Tie to rafters with twisted straw rope or willow withies. Cap the ridge with a thick saddle-shaped bundle.

8. **Floor.** Compact earth by wetting and tamping in layers. For a durable surface, apply lime-earth finish: 1 part slaked lime to 3 parts sandy earth, 3-5 cm layer, tamped smooth. Install a flagstone hearth (minimum 1 m square, 1.5 m preferred) at center for fire, stones bedded in sand.

9. **Smoke hole.** Leave a central opening (0.5-0.8 m diameter) at the roof peak. Install a movable rain hood — a small conical thatch cap suspended above the opening — that sheds rain while allowing smoke to escape around its edges.

10. **Door.** Face south (Northern Hemisphere) to admit winter sun. Dimensions: 1.8 m high, 0.8 m wide minimum. Frame with stout timber using mortise-and-tenon joinery. Fit a solid plank door or woven panel. Install a threshold stone to prevent water entry.

### The Earth-Sheltered Variant (Extreme Cold Climates)

For settlements with prolonged severe winters (below -15C for extended periods) or strong prevailing winds, dig into a south-facing hillside to create a semi-subterranean dwelling. Earth temperature at 2 m depth is relatively constant year-round — typically 10-15C in temperate zones, varying by only 2-4 degrees across seasons. This earth coupling provides massive thermal mass and insulation simultaneously.

The critical challenge is waterproofing. Groundwater seepage and condensation will destroy an earth-sheltered dwelling that is not properly drained and sealed.

Essential measures:

- Drainage trench at the base of all earth-contact walls, filled with gravel, leading to a daylight outlet downhill. This trench must be maintained — inspect the outlet annually and clear any blockage.
- Waterproof membrane behind earth walls: historically birch bark layered in overlapping sheets (each layer overlapping the one below by at least 15 cm), or puddled clay applied 10-15 cm thick and allowed to dry completely before backfilling.
- Ventilation system maintaining slight air movement to prevent condensation, even in winter. A low intake vent on the south-facing open wall and a high exhaust vent near the back of the shelter creates a gentle convective loop that carries moisture-laden air out.

The south-facing front wall should be fully open to solar gain, with a generous window area and thermal mass floor to capture winter sun. The remaining three sides and the roof are earth-bermed, providing R-values of 3-6 depending on soil type and depth. The combination of earth coupling (stable 10-15C base temperature) and passive solar gain through the south wall can maintain interior temperatures of 15-20C even when exterior temperatures drop to -20C — with only modest supplemental heating from a rocket mass heater or small wood stove.

### The Rocket Mass Heater (Cross-Reference Module 04.05)

The rocket mass heater is the most efficient zero-technology space heating system. It burns small-diameter wood (sticks of 2-5 cm diameter, not split logs) at very high temperature — exceeding 600C in the insulated combustion chamber — achieving near-complete combustion of wood gases that would escape as wasted smoke in an open fire.

Hot exhaust is routed through a long horizontal duct buried inside a massive thermal mass bench (cob-covered stone or earth), extracting more than 90% of heat energy before the cool exhaust exits through a short chimney.

A well-built rocket mass heater can heat a roundhouse through the night on a single 30-minute burn of small sticks — fuel gathered in minutes from the forest floor. The thermal mass bench absorbs heat during the burn and releases it over 8-12 hours.

Construction requires:

- J-tube combustion chamber (vertical feed tube connected to horizontal burn tunnel)
- Insulated heat riser (vertical chimney above combustion zone, insulated with wood ash or perlite to maintain extreme temperature)
- Horizontal exhaust duct running through the thermal mass bench
- Short chimney at far end providing draft

### Pest Deterrence

A shelter overrun with biting insects undermines health and sleep within days. Management begins with construction choices and continues with daily practice.

**The smudge fire.** Before sleeping, burn a small bundle of dried mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris), sage (Salvia species), or pine needles inside the shelter with ventilation adequate to prevent smoke accumulation at breathing height. Thick, aromatic smoke masks the carbon dioxide and body odor signals that guide mosquitoes, and particulates interfere with insect respiration. The smudge should smolder (thick white smoke) rather than burn with open flame.

**Botanical perimeter planting.** Plant lemon balm (Melissa officinalis), catnip (Nepeta cataria), marigolds (Tagetes species), and wild garlic (Allium vineale) around the foundation. These plants release pyrethrins, nepetalactone, and volatile sulfur compounds that repel insects. Crushed leaves of wild mint or cedar rubbed onto doorframes and window sills disrupt the pheromone trails of ants and spiders.

**Physical barriers.** Fit all door and window openings with screens — tightly woven cloth (1-2 mm mesh) or split bamboo panels — that admit air but exclude mosquitoes. Treat earthen floors with lime wash (1 part slaked lime to 5 parts water), creating an alkaline surface hostile to fleas and mites. Maintain a cleared perimeter of 3-5 m with short grass and raked leaf litter to reduce tick habitat. Guinea fowl, where available, are voracious tick predators.

**The raised sleeping platform.** Build a platform of lashed saplings at least 45 cm (18 inches) above the floor. This elevation places the sleeper above ground-dwelling snakes, scorpions, centipedes, and rodents that travel the floor at night. The platform also improves airflow around the body and keeps bedding away from floor-level dampness.

### Seasonal Maintenance Calendar

A shelter is not a one-time construction project. It is a living system that requires regular inspection and maintenance to achieve its 20-50 year design life.

**Spring (after snowmelt/rainy season):**
- Inspect thatch for thinning, displacement, or animal damage. Patch immediately.
- Check all post bases for signs of rot (probe with knife blade).
- Clear drainage channels around the foundation perimeter.
- Reapply lime wash to earthen floors.
- Inspect and re-daub any cracks in wattle-and-daub walls (freeze-thaw cycles open cracks that admit drafts and rain).

**Summer:**
- Open all ventilation — remove winter door seals, open high vents, prop shutters.
- Harvest and dry thatch material (reed, straw) for autumn repairs.
- Replace any botanical pest-repellent plantings that did not survive winter.
- Check insect screens for holes; repair or replace.

**Autumn (before first frost):**
- Complete all thatch repairs before wet season.
- Seal gaps around doors and window frames with clay-straw caulking.
- Stock firewood supply (minimum 3 cords for winter heating via rocket mass heater, more for open fire).
- Inspect chimney or smoke hole for blockage (bird nests, accumulated creosote).

**Winter:**
- Monitor interior humidity — condensation on interior walls indicates insufficient ventilation. Open a small vent even in cold weather.
- Clear snow accumulation from thatch if depth exceeds 30 cm (snow load can collapse marginal roof structures).
- Maintain fire discipline: never leave a fire unattended, ensure smoke hole is functional before lighting any fire.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Foundation Moisture Failure.** Timber on soil wicks moisture through capillary action, maintaining wood content above 20%. Fungal colonization begins within months; structural rot progresses over 2-5 years, ending in sudden collapse (exterior may appear sound while interior is consumed). Prevention: always use stone or gravel foundation with minimum 15 cm clearance. Inspect post bases annually by probing with a knife — if the blade penetrates more than 3 mm, rot has begun and the post must be replaced.

2. **Thatch Roof Failure.** Pitch below 45 degrees allows rainwater to soak inward rather than shedding. Failure is progressive: water saturates inner layers, drips onto structure, initiates rot in rafters and wall plates. Prevention: minimum 45 degrees, 50 preferred. Inspect annually — when thickness falls below 15 cm at any point, re-thatch that section. Maintained thatch lasts 20-30 years; neglected thatch fails in 5-10.

3. **Carbon Monoxide Poisoning.** Any indoor fire produces CO, an odorless gas that binds hemoglobin 200 times more effectively than oxygen. In enclosed shelters, lethal concentrations build in 1-2 hours. Symptoms: headache, drowsiness, confusion, unconsciousness, death — sleeping occupants may never wake. Prevention: always maintain functional smoke hole or chimney. Never seal all ventilation while a fire burns. If any occupant reports headache or unusual drowsiness, evacuate immediately.

4. **Cold Air Pooling.** Shelter in valley floor or depression collects pooling cold air nightly. Interior temperatures drop well below mid-slope ambient, increasing fuel requirements by 30-50%. Prevention: build in the thermal belt, never in valley bottoms. Before committing to a site, spend one clear calm night on location and compare temperature with a mid-slope position.

5. **Wind Exposure Failure.** Ridge or hilltop sites suffer wind speeds 2-3 times mid-slope levels, stripping thatch, collapsing panels, and causing massive heat loss through infiltration. Prevention: build in the thermal belt with windward protection from terrain or forest. If insufficient, establish a windbreak hedge (holly, yew, spruce, juniper) 10-15 m upwind, minimum 3 m tall at maturity, reducing wind speed 50-70% for 10-15 times the hedge height downwind.

6. **Timber Felling Injury.** A falling tree follows its lean and hinge cut, but wind, hung branches, and internal rot can redirect the fall. Prevention: assess natural lean, check for dead branches overhead (widow-makers), clear two escape paths at 45 degrees behind the planned fall. Notch cut on the fall side, one-third of trunk diameter deep. Back cut 2-5 cm above the notch floor, leaving a hinge of uncut wood. When the tree moves, walk (do not run) along the escape path. Never stand directly behind a falling tree — the butt can kick backward violently.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Thermal conductivity (k)**: Rate at which a material conducts heat, in W/m-K. Lower values indicate better insulation.
- **R-value**: Thermal resistance of a wall or roof assembly. R = thickness / k. Higher values mean better insulation.
- **Thermal mass**: Capacity of a dense material to absorb, store, and slowly release heat. Stone, earth, and water have high thermal mass.
- **Passive solar**: Design that captures solar energy for heating without mechanical systems, using orientation, windows, and thermal mass.
- **Diurnal temperature cycle**: Daily oscillation between daytime high and nighttime low, typically 10-25C in temperate climates.
- **Thermal belt**: Mid-slope zone between cold valley floor and windy ridge, offering the most moderate microclimate for building.
- **Katabatic wind**: Cold dense air flowing downhill at night under gravity after radiational cooling, pooling in low areas.
- **Venturi effect**: Acceleration of airflow through a constriction with corresponding pressure drop. Used in passive cooling.
- **Wattle-and-daub**: Wall construction using woven rods (wattle) plastered with clay-straw mixture (daub).
- **Mortise-and-tenon**: Timber joint where a projecting tenon fits a matching mortise cavity, secured with a wooden peg.
- **Cob**: Building material of clay subsoil, straw, sand, and water, mixed and built up in courses to form walls.
- **Thatch**: Roof covering of dried plant stems (reed, straw, cattail) in overlapping bundles to shed rain.
- **Rocket mass heater**: High-efficiency combustion system burning small wood at extreme temperature, storing heat in a thermal mass bench.
- **Earth-sheltered**: Building partially or fully embedded in earth, exploiting stable subsurface temperatures.
