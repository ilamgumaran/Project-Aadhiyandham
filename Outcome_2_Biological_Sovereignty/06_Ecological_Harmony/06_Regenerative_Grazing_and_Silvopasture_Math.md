# Regenerative Grazing and Silvopasture Math

**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

## 1. Why This Is Important (The Rationale)
Livestock in a refugia are not just food sources; they are **Biological Nutrient Processors**. If animals graze a single area for too long, they destroy the soil structure and kill the forage. If managed through **Rotational Grazing**, their impact triggers rapid plant growth and sequesters massive amounts of carbon. This study provides the mathematical formulas to calculate stocking density and paddock rotation cycles.

---

## 2. Calculating Carrying Capacity (The AUM)

### 2.1. The Animal Unit (AU)
To standardize calculations, we use the **Animal Unit (AU)**, defined as one 450kg (1,000 lb) cow.
*   **1 Cow = 1.0 AU**
*   **1 Mule/Horse = 1.2 AU**
*   **1 Pig = 0.2 AU**
*   **5 Sheep/Goats = 1.0 AU**

### 2.2. The Animal Unit Month (AUM)
`AUM` is the amount of forage required by 1 AU for one month (approx. 360kg of dry matter).
*   **Formula:** `Total AU * Months of Grazing = Required AUM`.
*   **The Land Check:** If your land produces 2 AUM per hectare, and you have 5 AU (5 cows), you need `(5 AU / 2 AUM) = 2.5 Hectares` of forage per month.

## 3. Paddock Rotation Geometry

To maximize soil building, animals must "Impact and Move."

### 3.1. The 30-Day Recovery Rule
Plants require rest to rebuild root mass. 
*   **Protocol:** Divide your total pasture into a minimum of **8 Paddocks**.
*   **Rotation:** Graze each paddock for 3-4 days, then leave it empty for at least 30 days.

### 3.2. Stocking Density vs. Carrying Capacity
*   **Carrying Capacity:** How many animals the whole land can support over a year.
*   **Stocking Density:** How many animals are in one specific paddock *right now*.
*   **Aadhiyandham Strategy:** High Stocking Density for short durations. This forces animals to graze evenly and trample manure/urine into the soil (High Impact), followed by Long Rest.

---

## 4. Silvopasture Geometry: Trees and Forage

Silvopasture is the intentional integration of trees and grazing.

### 4.1. The "Wide Row" Layout
To allow enough sunlight for forage while providing shade for animals:
*   **Tree Spacing:** Plant rows of fodder trees (Mulberry, Black Locust, Willow) every **10-15 meters**.
*   **Orientation:** Align rows East-to-West to maximize sunlight penetration to the grass between the rows.

---

## 5. 🔬 Scientific Validation: The Grazing Pulse
Research into **Holistic Planned Grazing** (Savory, 1983) confirms that the "Pulse" of intense grazing followed by long rest mimics the behavior of wild herds and their predators. This process increases soil organic matter by up to 1% per year, which significantly increases the water-holding capacity of the refugia.

## 6. 6.Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 6.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Phase 1: Forage Survey:** Measure the average height of your grass in the dry season. If it is <10cm, your land is already overgrazed.
*   **Phase 2: Fencing Geometry:** Use simple movable "Electric" (or physical rope/bamboo) fences to create temporary paddocks.
*   **Phase 3: The Move:** Move the animals as soon as they have eaten 50% of the available forage. "Take half, leave half."

### 6.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes
*   **Parasite Load:** If animals graze too close to the ground (<5cm), they ingest parasite eggs. Always move animals while forage is still "tall."
*   **Soil Compaction:** In wet weather, move animals faster to prevent "pugging" (destructive hoof-holes in mud).

### 6.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **The Shepherd's Ledger:** Every move must be logged. Tracking the days of rest for each paddock is the primary metric for increasing the settlement's total carrying capacity over decades.
