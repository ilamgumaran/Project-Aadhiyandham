# Regenerative Grazing and Silvopasture Math

**Alignment:** Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Livestock in a refugia are not merely food sources; they are **Biological Nutrient Processors** that move fertility across the landscape on four legs. The single decision that determines whether a herd builds soil or destroys it is not *how many* animals you keep but *how long* they stay in one place. Graze a single area for too long and the animals strip the forage faster than it can regrow, crush the soil structure, and convert living pasture into bare, eroding dirt. Manage that same herd through disciplined **Rotational Grazing** — a brief, intense pulse of grazing followed by a long rest — and the identical animals trigger rapid plant regrowth, press seed into mineral soil, and sequester carbon year after year. The companion module 02 covers the husbandry, species selection, and general rotation philosophy; this module is the **quantitative engine room**. It provides the arithmetic that converts a vague intention ("rotate the animals") into a defensible plan: how many animals the land can feed, how many paddocks to cut, how many days of rest each one needs, and how to lay out trees so that grass and shade can coexist.

The discipline here is measurement. A settlement that grazes by intuition will discover its mistake only after the carrying capacity has already collapsed — and on the timescale of a refugia, that recovery takes decades. A settlement that grazes by the numbers in this module catches the error in the ledger before it appears in the soil. Every figure below is a tool for staying on the right side of that line.

> **CAUTION**: Overgrazing is silent until it is catastrophic. The pasture looks "fine" right up to the season it does not recover. Trust the recovery clock and the forage ruler, not the appearance of green. If grass is not returning to full height before the herd cycles back, you are already overstocked — reduce the herd *before* the bare patches appear, not after.

> **CAUTION**: Animals grazed below 5 cm ingest concentrated parasite larvae from the soil surface and damage the grass crown that powers regrowth. The "take half, leave half" rule is not aesthetic; it is the boundary between a self-renewing pasture and a degrading one.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Grazing Pulse and the Root-Reserve Cycle

When a grass plant is bitten, it does not regrow from sunlight alone. It draws first on carbohydrate reserves stored in the root crown to push out new leaf, and only once that leaf is photosynthesizing again does it begin to *repay* and rebuild those root reserves. This is the central mechanism of all grazing science. If an animal returns and bites the plant a second time *before* the reserves are replenished, the root system is forced to spend down a balance it has not yet refilled. Repeat that premature bite three or four times and the plant exhausts its reserves entirely and dies. Bare soil appears, weeds colonize it, erosion begins, and carrying capacity collapses. Critically, this failure is driven by **time, not numbers** — it happens at high stocking density and low stocking density alike. The variable that matters is whether the plant gets enough *rest* between bites.

This insight is the foundation of **Holistic Planned Grazing** (Allan Savory, formalized 1983). Savory observed that the great grasslands of the world co-evolved with large herds of grazing herbivores kept tightly bunched by predators. The herd grazed an area intensely for a short time — trampling old growth flat, breaking soil crusts with hooves, pressing seed into the ground, and depositing dense manure and urine — and then *moved on*, driven by the predators and the search for fresh forage, not returning for months. The grass thrived under this rhythm of **intense impact followed by long rest**. It collapses under the opposite rhythm — the gentle, continuous nibbling of a small number of animals left in one place indefinitely, which never lets any plant complete its recovery cycle. Managed grazing deliberately recreates the herd-and-predator pulse using fences and a calendar in place of wolves.

In **brittle** (arid and semi-arid) environments the effect is even more counterintuitive: periodic, concentrated animal impact *increases* grass establishment compared to complete rest, because undisturbed dryland soil forms a biological crust that prevents seed germination. The hooves that would compact a wet temperate pasture are exactly what a dry one needs to break the crust and plant the seed. Savory's research indicates that this pulse can increase soil organic matter by up to **1% per year**, and because organic matter holds many times its own weight in water, that single percentage point sharply raises the water-holding capacity of the entire refugia — drought insurance written directly into the ground.

### Standardizing the Herd: The Animal Unit

Different animals eat different amounts, so before any land calculation is possible the herd must be reduced to a common currency. That currency is the **Animal Unit (AU)**, defined as one mature 450 kg (1,000 lb) cow. Every other animal is expressed as a fraction or multiple of that reference cow according to how much forage it consumes.

| Animal | Animal Units (AU) |
|--------|-------------------|
| 1 Cow | 1.0 AU |
| 1 Mule / Horse | 1.2 AU |
| 1 Pig | 0.2 AU |
| 5 Sheep / Goats | 1.0 AU (0.2 AU each) |

Once the whole herd is converted to AU, the land arithmetic becomes a single consistent calculation regardless of which species are actually present. A mixed herd of cattle, a mule, pigs, and goats collapses into one number — total AU — and that number is what the land must feed.

### Forage Demand: The Animal Unit Month

The demand side of the equation is measured in the **Animal Unit Month (AUM)** — the quantity of forage one AU requires for one month, approximately **360 kg of dry matter**. This converts a herd plus a span of time directly into a forage requirement:

```
Required AUM = Total AU × Months of Grazing
```

The supply side is the land's productivity, also measured in AUM — how many animal-unit-months of forage a hectare yields. Dividing demand by supply gives the area required:

> **The Land Check** — If your land produces **2 AUM per hectare**, and you run **5 AU** (e.g. five cows), then each month you need `5 AU ÷ 2 AUM/ha = 2.5 hectares` of forage. Over a full grazing year the same arithmetic, multiplied by the number of grazing months, tells you the *total* area the herd requires and therefore whether your land can carry it at all.

### Silvopasture: The Light Budget Between the Rows

Silvopasture is the intentional integration of trees and grazing on the same ground, and it is governed by a competition that is fundamentally about **light**. Trees and forage both want the sun; place the trees too close and their canopies shade out the grass beneath, starving the very forage the animals depend on. The quantitative solution is the **wide-row layout**. Rows of fodder trees — mulberry, black locust, willow — are planted at intervals of **10-15 meters**, leaving a broad sunlit alley of grass between them. The rows are oriented **East-to-West**, which keeps the strip of shade cast by each row narrow and confined close to the trunks through the middle of the day, so the maximum amount of sunlight reaches the grass in the alleys rather than being swept across them. The trees pay for the space they occupy with shade for the animals, fodder from their own leaves, and deep roots that pump subsoil minerals up into reach — but only if the geometry is right.

## Core Principles

1. **Time, not numbers, destroys pasture.** The cause of overgrazing is insufficient *rest* between grazings, not high animal counts. A plant bitten before its root reserves recover will die after three or four cycles regardless of how many animals did the biting. Manage the calendar, not just the headcount.

2. **Impact hard, then rest long.** The Aadhiyandham strategy is high stocking density for a short duration — forcing animals to graze evenly and trample manure and urine into the soil (High Impact) — followed by a long recovery. This is the grazing pulse that mimics wild herds and their predators, and it builds soil where continuous grazing depletes it.

3. **Convert everything to Animal Units first.** No land calculation is valid until the entire mixed herd has been reduced to total AU using the standard equivalences (1 cow = 1.0, mule/horse = 1.2, pig = 0.2, sheep/goat = 0.2 each). The AU is the common currency of carrying capacity.

4. **Match demand (AUM) to supply (AUM/ha) explicitly.** Required forage is `Total AU × Months of Grazing` in AUM; land area required is that demand divided by the land's AUM-per-hectare yield. Run the Land Check before committing animals to ground.

5. **Distinguish carrying capacity from stocking density.** Carrying capacity is what the *whole* land sustains over a *year*; stocking density is how many animals are in *one paddock right now*. The strategy deliberately runs high stocking density in the moment while staying within annual carrying capacity overall.

6. **Honor the 30-day recovery rule.** Divide the pasture into a minimum of **8 paddocks**, graze each for only 3-4 days, then leave it empty for at least **30 days** so plant root mass can fully rebuild before the herd returns.

7. **Take half, leave half.** Move the herd as soon as it has eaten **50%** of the available forage. Never graze below 8-10 cm (and never below 5 cm), preserving the leaf area that powers regrowth and keeping animals' mouths above the parasite-larvae zone.

8. **Lay trees out for the light, not the convenience.** Fodder-tree rows at 10-15 m spacing, oriented East-West, keep the alley grass in full sun. Geometry, not goodwill, is what lets trees and forage share ground.

9. **The ledger is the carrying-capacity instrument.** Every move and every rest-day count must be logged. The record of days-of-rest per paddock is the primary metric by which the settlement raises its total carrying capacity over decades.

## Practical Implementation

### Step 1 — Forage Survey (establish the baseline)

Before any animals are committed, measure the average height of standing grass in the **dry season** (the lean part of the year, when forage is scarcest and therefore the binding constraint). If the dry-season grass height is **below 10 cm**, the land is *already* overgrazed or inherently low-yielding and must be rested or destocked before a grazing plan can be built on it. Estimate the land's productivity in **AUM per hectare** from this survey; a poor temperate pasture may yield on the order of 2 AUM/ha, and richer ground proportionally more.

### Step 2 — Convert the Herd and Run the Land Check

Reduce the intended herd to total Animal Units using the AU table. Then run the Land Check both monthly and annually:

```
Monthly area = Total AU ÷ (land AUM per hectare)
Annual demand (AUM) = Total AU × Months of Grazing
```

If the annual demand in AUM exceeds what the available hectares can supply, **reduce the herd now**. It is always safer to understock: spare carrying capacity becomes a drought-year reserve, while overstocking has no recovery on a useful timescale.

```text
       CARRYING-CAPACITY CALCULATION PIPELINE
  ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
   STEP 1   Count the herd by species
            cows · mule · pigs · sheep/goats
              │
              ▼  apply AU table (1.0 / 1.2 / 0.2 / 0.2)
   STEP 2   Total Animal Units  ──────────►  TOTAL AU
              │
              ▼  × Months of Grazing
   STEP 3   Required forage  ─────────────►  AUM demand
              │
              ▼  ÷ land yield (AUM per hectare)
   STEP 4   Hectares required  ───────────►  AREA NEEDED
              │
              ▼  compare to land available
   STEP 5   AREA NEEDED ≤ land?  ✔ proceed
            AREA NEEDED >  land?  ✘ destock now
  ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  Worked example: 5 AU ÷ 2 AUM/ha = 2.5 ha needed / month
  RULE: Convert to AU before you ever divide by the land.
```

### Step 3 — Cut the Paddocks (fencing geometry)

Divide the total pasture into a **minimum of 8 paddocks**. Use simple, movable temporary fencing — electric tape where available, or physical rope and bamboo where it is not — so the paddock boundaries can flex as forage and season demand. Permanent perimeters can be living hedgerow (see module 02 for living-fence establishment); the *internal* divisions that drive the rotation should stay light and movable.

### Step 4 — Run the Rotation (the 30-day clock)

Graze each paddock for **3-4 days** at high stocking density, then leave it empty for **at least 30 days**. With 8 paddocks at 3-4 days each, the herd takes roughly 24-32 days to make one full circuit — which is exactly what delivers the ~30-day rest to the paddock it just left. This is the arithmetic reason for the eight-paddock minimum: fewer paddocks means a shorter circuit and a rest period too short for the root reserves to recover.

### Step 5 — Know When to Move ("take half, leave half")

Within each paddock, move the animals **the moment they have eaten 50% of the available forage**. Do not graze to the ground. Entering grass should be tall (well above the survey baseline) and exiting grass should never drop below 8-10 cm — and absolutely never below 5 cm, where parasite larvae concentrate and the grass crown is damaged.

```text
       STOCKING DENSITY  vs  CARRYING CAPACITY
  ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                 │ STOCKING DENSITY      │ CARRYING CAPACITY
   ──────────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────
   Question      │ how many animals in   │ how many the whole
                 │ THIS paddock NOW?     │ land sustains/YEAR
   ──────────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────
   Timescale     │ moment (3-4 days)     │ annual average
   ──────────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────
   Strategy      │ HIGH (intense pulse)  │ stay within it
                 │ ████████████          │ ████████
   ──────────────┼───────────────────────┼───────────────────
   Effect        │ even graze, trample   │ no degradation;
                 │ manure into soil      │ spare = drought
                 │                       │ reserve
  ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
  RULE: Spike the density in the moment; never the yearly load.
```

Where trees are integrated, plant rows of fodder trees (mulberry, black locust, willow) every **10-15 m**, oriented **East-West**, leaving wide sunlit grass alleys between. The animals graze the alley forage on the rotation schedule above and gain shade from the rows; the trees gain manure deposited directly in their root zone.

### Step 7 — Keep the Shepherd's Ledger

Every move must be logged. For each paddock, record the date the herd entered, the date it left, and — most importantly — the running count of **days of rest** since it was last grazed. This ledger is not bookkeeping for its own sake; the days-of-rest record is the *primary instrument* by which the settlement measures whether its land is improving and steadily raises total carrying capacity across decades.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Overgrazing through insufficient rest.** Too many animals on too little land, or rest periods cut short, so plants are re-bitten before root reserves recover. After 3-4 such cycles the grass dies, bare soil appears, weeds and erosion follow, and carrying capacity collapses — a degradation measured in decades to reverse. *Prevention:* Honor the 30-day recovery rule and the 8-paddock minimum; monitor that grass returns to full height before the herd cycles back, and destock at the first sign that it is not, rather than waiting for visible bare patches.

2. **Too few paddocks shorten the rest below recovery.** Running the rotation across only three or four paddocks makes the circuit too short, so each paddock is re-grazed before 30 days of rest have elapsed — overgrazing arrives even though the headcount looks reasonable. *Prevention:* Cut a minimum of 8 paddocks so that 8 × 3-4 days yields a ~30-day circuit. The geometry, not the herd size, guarantees the rest.

3. **Grazing too close (parasite load).** When animals graze below ~5 cm they ingest parasite eggs and larvae concentrated near the soil surface, and they strip the grass crown that powers regrowth. *Prevention:* Apply "take half, leave half" — move animals at 50% forage utilization while the sward is still tall, never letting exit height drop below 8-10 cm.

4. **Soil compaction and pugging in wet weather.** On saturated ground, hooves punch destructive holes ("pugging") that ruin soil structure and forage. *Prevention:* In wet weather, move animals through paddocks faster, shortening occupation to limit the damage; reserve high-impact bunching for firmer ground.

5. **Silvopasture rows too tight or wrongly oriented.** Tree rows planted closer than 10-15 m, or running North-South, throw shade that sweeps across the alleys and starves the forage of light, collapsing grass yield beneath the canopy. *Prevention:* Hold the 10-15 m row spacing and the East-West orientation so the shade stays narrow and close to the trunks and the alley grass keeps full sun.

6. **Grazing by appearance instead of by ledger.** Relying on how green the pasture looks rather than tracked rest-days masks degradation until it is irreversible, because pasture can appear adequate the very season before it fails. *Prevention:* Keep the Shepherd's Ledger and act on the days-of-rest figures, not on a visual impression.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Biological Nutrient Processor**: A framing of livestock as the engine that converts inedible biomass into mobile, deposited fertility and ecosystem impact, rather than as mere consumers of feed.
- **Animal Unit (AU)**: The standard reference for forage demand, defined as one mature 450 kg (1,000 lb) cow = 1.0 AU; all other species are expressed relative to it (mule/horse 1.2, pig 0.2, sheep/goat 0.2 each).
- **Animal Unit Month (AUM)**: The amount of forage one AU requires for one month, approximately 360 kg of dry matter; used to measure both herd demand and land yield (AUM per hectare).
- **Carrying capacity**: The number of animals the whole land can support over a full year without degradation; an annual-average property of the land.
- **Stocking density**: The number of animals in one specific paddock at a given moment; deliberately run high for short pulses while staying within annual carrying capacity.
- **Rotational grazing**: Moving the herd between paddocks on a planned schedule so each paddock receives a short, intense graze followed by a long rest.
- **Grazing pulse**: The rhythm of intense, concentrated impact followed by long recovery that mimics wild herds driven by predators; the core of Holistic Planned Grazing.
- **30-day recovery rule**: The protocol of leaving each paddock empty for at least 30 days after grazing so plant root mass fully rebuilds before the next graze.
- **High Impact**: Concentrating animals at high density for a short time so they graze evenly and trample manure and urine into the soil, breaking crusts and pressing in seed.
- **Take half, leave half**: The move-trigger rule — relocate the herd once 50% of available forage is eaten, preserving leaf area for regrowth.
- **Silvopasture**: The intentional integration of trees and grazing on the same land.
- **Wide-row layout**: Fodder-tree rows spaced 10-15 m apart and oriented East-West so alley forage retains full sunlight.
- **Pugging**: Destructive hoof holes punched into saturated soil, ruining structure and forage.
- **The Shepherd's Ledger**: The logbook of every paddock move and rest-day count; the primary metric for raising settlement carrying capacity over decades.
