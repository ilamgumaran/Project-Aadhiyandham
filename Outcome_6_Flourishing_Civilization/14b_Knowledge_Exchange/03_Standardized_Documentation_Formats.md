# Standardized Documentation Formats: A Common Language for Technical Knowledge

**Alignment:** Outcome 6 -- A Flourishing Civilization

A traveling scholar arrives at a settlement and hands over a document describing a new clay-pipe water filter. The document is in a language the host community partially understands, but the measurements are in units they do not use, the diagram style is unfamiliar, and the plant names refer to local flora at the originating settlement that does not grow here. The document is technically accurate but practically useless.

This is not a hypothetical problem. It is exactly what happened repeatedly in the ancient world until civilizations developed standardized formats. The Mesopotamians solved it with cuneiform on standardized clay tablets with fixed measurement systems. The Chinese solved it with standardized character sets and the Imperial examination system that ensured a common technical vocabulary. The Islamic Golden Age solved it with Arabic as a shared scholarly language and rigorous conventions for citing sources and describing experimental procedures.

Small post-collapse settlements cannot impose a single language. They can, however, agree on a shared documentation format that makes technical documents legible regardless of the reader's native tongue. This module defines that format: a system of visual conventions, measurement standards, and structural templates that allow any literate person in the network to extract actionable knowledge from any document produced within the network. Every technical document produced for inter-settlement exchange follows a common template with standardized sections, measurement units, diagram conventions, and a visual vocabulary that transcends linguistic barriers. A person who has never seen the originating settlement's language can still understand the core procedure by reading the diagrams, measurements, and structured layout. In the first year, the standardized format feels burdensome -- an extra layer of work on top of already-demanding settlement life. By the third year, new documents are written in the standard format from the start, because everyone has internalized the template. By the fifth year, a visiting scholar from a distant settlement can hand over a document that a local farmer, who has never met the author and does not speak their language, can follow well enough to try a new composting technique. By the tenth year, the accumulated library of standardized documents across the network constitutes a genuine technical encyclopedia -- decentralized, redundant, and accessible to anyone trained in the format. This is the foundation of a civilization that learns faster than it forgets, which is the fundamental requirement for long-term flourishing.

> **SAFE**: Documentation practices carry no physical risk. All activities involve writing, drawing, committee discussion, and document review. No hazardous materials or procedures are described in this module.


## Theoretical Foundation

### Information Design and the ISOTYPE Legacy

The International System of Typographic Picture Education (ISOTYPE), developed by Otto Neurath in the 1920s and 1930s, demonstrated that pictographic systems can convey statistical and technical information across language barriers. The system was successfully used in public health campaigns in countries with low literacy rates, proving that a limited set of clearly distinct symbols, each representing one concept and combined according to simple spatial rules, could communicate complex quantitative relationships to audiences with no shared written language.

The pictographic supplement system in this module draws directly on these principles. Where Neurath used standardized human figures and geometric shapes to represent population statistics and economic data, we use a comparable vocabulary of material symbols, action symbols, and connectors to represent technical procedures.

The critical lesson from ISOTYPE is restraint: the symbol set must remain small enough to memorize (approximately 80-120 symbols) and each symbol must be visually distinct enough to recognize at arm's length. Expanding the set beyond what people can hold in working memory defeats the purpose of visual standardization. Neurath's team found through extensive field testing that symbols work best when they depict a single concrete object or action rather than an abstract concept. A wavy line for water is immediately legible; a complex composite symbol for "water purification system" is not. This principle guides the entire pictographic supplement: keep each symbol atomic, and let spatial arrangement and sequence convey the relationships between symbols.

### Historical Standards from Mesopotamia to Medieval Manuscripts

From approximately 3000 BCE, Mesopotamian scribes used standardized units and notation for recording quantities of grain, land area, and labor time. These metrological standards were taught in scribal schools and enforced across the Sumerian and later Babylonian empires, enabling administrative documents to be understood across hundreds of city-states speaking different languages. The key insight is that Mesopotamian standardization was not top-down decree alone; it was maintained through a network of schools that trained every scribe in the same conventions, creating a self-reinforcing system of shared practice.

European medieval manuscripts developed their own elaborate conventions for layout: rubrics (red headings marking new sections), marginal glosses (annotations explaining difficult terms), paragraph marks called pilcrows, and colophons (source attributions at the end recording the scribe, date, and patron). These conventions allowed a reader familiar with the format to navigate a text written in an unfamiliar dialect or even a different language entirely. A Benedictine monk in England could extract useful information from a manuscript produced in a Cistercian monastery in France, not because he spoke French fluently, but because he knew where to find the title, the chapter divisions, the source references, and the corrections.

This module's standard section sequence serves the same navigational function. A reader trained in the format knows that the materials list always comes second, the procedure always comes third, and the cautions always come fifth, regardless of what language those sections are written in.

### Body-Referenced Measurement and Anthropology

Anthropological research confirms that every pre-industrial society developed measurement systems referenced to the human body: cubits, fathoms, hand-breadths, finger-widths, paces, and arm-lengths. These systems are inherently reproducible without instruments, though they vary between individuals by roughly 10-15 percent. For the precision levels needed in most settlement-scale construction and agriculture, this margin is acceptable. A wall that is 20 hand-spans long when built by one mason and 21 hand-spans long when measured by another is functionally the same wall.

Body-referenced units also carry a pedagogical advantage: anyone can immediately understand what a "hand-span" means by spreading their own hand, whereas an abstract unit like "centimeter" requires either a manufactured ruler or a chain of definitions anchoring it to some physical reference. By choosing body-referenced units as the network standard, we ensure that any person at any settlement can verify and reproduce measurements without specialized equipment.

Where greater precision is needed -- metalworking tolerances, for instance, or medicinal dosing -- the system accommodates calibration rods: physical artifacts of fixed length distributed to settlements as reference standards. The body-referenced unit remains the everyday language of measurement, while the calibration rod serves as the legal standard for disputes or high-precision work. Calibration rods should be checked against each other whenever scholars travel between settlements, ensuring that the network's length standard does not silently diverge over time.

### The Vienna Circle and the Limits of Unified Science

The philosophical project of creating a universal scientific language, led by Neurath, Carnap, and others in the Vienna Circle, demonstrated both the power and the limits of standardization. The Vienna Circle sought to reduce all scientific knowledge to a single logical framework with a shared vocabulary. The project ultimately failed because human knowledge is too diverse and context-dependent for a single language to capture.

The lesson for this module is precise and practical: standardize the format and measurement system, but do not attempt to standardize the language itself. Let communities keep their linguistic identity while sharing a common technical scaffold. The document header, section sequence, pictographic supplements, and unit system are the scaffold. The prose within the sections remains in whatever language the originating community speaks. This approach preserves cultural autonomy while enabling technical cooperation -- a balance that imperial standardization projects historically failed to achieve. The Roman Empire, for instance, imposed Latin as the language of administration and law, which worked while the empire lasted but left post-Roman communities unable to read their own archives once Latin literacy declined. A format-based standard avoids this fragility because the conventions are visual and structural rather than linguistic -- they survive even if the originating committee's language falls out of use.


## Core Principles

1. **Format over language.** A shared structure and visual vocabulary can bridge linguistic gaps that translation alone cannot, because structure is learned once and applied to every document, while translation must be performed anew each time.

2. **Body-referenced measurement.** Every unit in the standard system can be reproduced by any human being without instruments, ensuring that measurements remain meaningful even when calibration tools are lost or unavailable.

3. **Visual redundancy.** Every piece of critical information appears in at least two forms -- written text and a pictographic or diagrammatic representation -- so that a reader who cannot parse one form can extract the information from the other.

4. **Navigational consistency.** A fixed section sequence means that a reader who knows the format can locate specific information (materials, procedures, cautions) in any document without reading the entire text, just as a reader familiar with a dictionary knows to look alphabetically.

5. **Living standard with controlled change.** The documentation format evolves through consensus at inter-settlement meetings, not through unilateral modification. Any settlement may propose new symbols or revised conventions, but changes are adopted only when the network agrees, preventing format drift that would undermine interoperability.


## Practical Implementation

### The Universal Document Header

Every inter-settlement document begins with a standardized header block that conveys essential metadata without requiring language fluency. The header contains six elements:

- **Title line**: Written in the originating settlement's language and also in a simplified pictographic representation.
- **Origin mark**: The settlement's unique sigil (the same one used on credential tokens), stamped or drawn in the upper-left corner.
- **Date**: Expressed as a season-and-year pair using the shared calendar system. Format: season symbol followed by the year count from network founding. Spring is an upward triangle, summer is a circle, autumn is a downward triangle, and winter is a square.
- **Domain marker**: A standardized icon indicating the knowledge domain. Seven icons are defined: a seed or sprout symbol for agriculture, three wavy lines for water systems, a right angle or corner bracket for construction, a cross within a circle for medicine and health, a flame symbol for metallurgy, a circle of dots for governance and social matters, and a zigzag line for energy and fuel.
- **Difficulty indicator**: One to four filled circles, where one means any community member can execute the procedure and four means specialist training is required.
- **Version mark**: A simple tally indicating how many times the document has been revised.

### The Standard Section Sequence

After the header, every technical document follows a fixed section order. A reader who knows the sequence can navigate any document even without reading the text:

1. **Purpose Statement** (marked with a horizontal line above and below): One to three sentences explaining what the document teaches.
2. **Materials List** (marked with a table grid): Items needed in a two-column table with item name including pictographic supplement and quantity in standard units.
3. **Procedure** (marked with numbered steps each beginning with a bold action verb): Step-by-step assembly or execution instructions.
4. **Diagram Section** (marked with a box border): Visual illustrations following the diagram conventions defined below.
5. **Cautions** (marked with a triangle-exclamation symbol): What can go wrong and how to prevent it.
6. **Verification** (marked with a checkmark symbol): How to test that the procedure was performed correctly.
7. **Source Attribution** (marked with a chain-link symbol): Which settlement originated this knowledge and when.

### The Pictographic Supplement System

Language-independent communication requires a shared visual vocabulary. The network agrees on a set of approximately 80-120 pictographic symbols covering the most common technical nouns and verbs, organized into five categories:

**Natural materials**: water (wavy line), fire (triangle with wavy top), stone (irregular polygon), wood (rectangle with grain lines), clay (amorphous shape with dots), sand (field of small dots).

**Processed materials**: rope (braided line), cloth (crosshatch), metal (shaded rectangle), charcoal (black filled shape), ash (gray filled shape), lime (white circle).

**Actions**: cut (scissors shape), heat (flame plus arrow), mix (two arrows converging into one), pour (tilted container with flow line), press (downward arrow between two lines), wait or dry (hourglass or sun symbol).

**Measurements**: length (double-headed arrow), volume (cup shape), weight (balance shape), temperature (thermometer shape with fill levels for cold, warm, hot, and very hot), time (sun position for dawn, noon, and dusk).

**Connectors**: "then" (right arrow), "at the same time" (parallel arrows), "if this then that" (branching arrow), "repeat" (circular arrow).

These symbols are compiled into a master reference sheet that every settlement copies and keeps posted in their archive. The reference sheet should be organized by category with each symbol drawn at a size large enough to be recognized from across a workbench. New symbols can be proposed at inter-settlement meetings and added by consensus, but the threshold for adding new symbols should be high -- a proposed symbol must fill a gap that existing symbols cannot cover through combination, and it must pass the arm's-length distinguishability test described above.

### Diagram Conventions

Technical diagrams are the most language-independent element of any document. To ensure they are unambiguous, all diagrams follow six conventions:

**Orientation**: All diagrams are drawn as if the viewer is looking at the object from the south, meaning north is "away" from the viewer. This prevents the confusion that arises when different communities use different default viewing angles.

**Scale bar**: Every diagram includes a horizontal bar at the bottom labeled with the standard length unit -- the hand-span of approximately 20 cm, further subdivided into finger-widths of approximately 2 cm. This allows reconstruction even if the reader has no ruler.

**Cross-section marking**: A dashed line across a diagram indicates where a cross-section view is taken. The cross-section is drawn adjacent to the main diagram with a matching letter or number label.

**Flow direction**: Arrows indicate the direction of water flow, air flow, or material movement. Thick arrows indicate primary flow; thin arrows indicate secondary flows.

**Hatching conventions**: Different fill patterns indicate different materials -- diagonal lines for wood, dots for sand and soil, crosshatch for stone, solid black for metal, wavy lines for water.

**Assembly sequence**: When a diagram shows a multi-step assembly, each step is drawn in a separate panel numbered left-to-right and top-to-bottom, since reading order is universal for numbered sequences even across script directions.

Every diagram should also include a small legend in the bottom-right corner repeating the hatching key for the materials shown in that particular diagram. While trained readers will eventually memorize the conventions, the legend ensures that newcomers to the format can interpret any diagram without consulting a separate reference.

### The Standard Unit System

Settlements will inevitably develop local measurement habits. For inter-settlement documents, the network agrees on a single set of body-referenced units that any human can reproduce without instruments:

**Length**: The hand-span (tip of thumb to tip of little finger, spread wide) as the base unit at approximately 20 cm. Subdivided into finger-widths at approximately 2 cm. Larger distances measured in paces (one full stride, approximately 75 cm) and bowshots (approximately 200 paces, roughly 150 m).

**Volume**: The cupped-hand at approximately 100 mL as the base unit. The head-pot (the volume of a clay vessel that fits comfortably on a human head) as the standard large unit at approximately 10 liters.

**Weight**: The stone (a specific type of river-smoothed stone of standardized size, approximately 1 kg, kept as a reference at each settlement) with subdivisions by halving.

**Temperature**: Described by reference to observable phenomena -- cool enough to touch comfortably, too hot to hold but water does not boil, water boils, metal glows dull red, metal glows bright orange.

**Time**: Described by sun position (dawn, mid-morning, noon, mid-afternoon, dusk) or by duration references such as "the time it takes to walk one bowshot," approximately 3 minutes. For processes requiring more precise timing, water clocks or sand timers can supplement sun-position references, but documents should always include the sun-position equivalent so that settlements without timekeeping devices can still follow the procedure.

### Document Production Workflow

The workflow for producing a standardized document has four stages:

1. The originating specialist writes a draft in their own language and format.
2. The settlement's documentation committee translates the draft into the standardized format: adds the header, restructures into the standard section sequence, replaces local units with network standard units, adds pictographic supplements, and draws diagrams per the conventions.
3. A second reviewer (ideally someone less expert in the topic) attempts to follow the document without additional explanation. Any point of confusion is flagged and revised.
4. The finalized document is copied onto durable paper in clean handwriting and added to the settlement's archive and the next scholar's payload. If a document describes a procedure that multiple settlements are likely to need, additional copies should be prepared in advance so that traveling scholars can distribute them without delay.

### Implementation Timeline

**Week 1** -- Appoint a documentation committee of two to five literate individuals with good handwriting and at least one with drawing ability. This is not a permanent role; rotate membership annually.

**Weeks 2-3** -- Study this module and practice drawing the pictographic symbols until they are consistent and recognizable. Produce a reference sheet of all symbols and post it in the archive.

**Week 4** -- Convert one existing document -- the most important technical document the settlement already possesses -- into the standardized format as a first test case.

**Week 5** -- Conduct peer review by having someone not on the committee attempt to follow the reformatted document with no additional explanation, noting every point of confusion.

**Weeks 6-8** -- Incorporate lessons from peer review, revise, and convert a second document. By this point the committee has internalized the format.

**Months 3-6** -- Systematically convert all critical settlement documents to the standard format, prioritizing documents likely to be exchanged with other settlements: water filtration, food preservation, seed selection, and basic medicine. By the end of this period, the settlement should have a core library of at least ten to fifteen standardized documents ready for inter-settlement exchange.

**Year 1 and beyond** -- Maintain the standard by reviewing all new documents against the format before they enter the archive. Participate in annual inter-settlement format review meetings to adopt any agreed-upon changes to the symbol set, unit definitions, or section structure. Train new committee members as existing members rotate out.


## Common Failure Modes

The following table lists the most frequently observed problems when settlements first adopt the standardized format, along with their root causes and tested remedies.

| Failure | Cause | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| Pictographic symbols are ambiguous | Symbols too similar to each other or too abstract | Revise the symbol set so each symbol is distinguishable at arm's length; test with children, because if a 10-year-old cannot tell two symbols apart they are too similar |
| Documents are too long | Overwriting or trying to include every detail | Limit each document to one procedure or one system; split complex topics into multiple linked documents |
| Measurements do not match between settlements | Different-sized hands producing different hand-spans | Distribute a calibration rod of fixed length to each settlement; the rod is the true standard, the hand-span is a convenient approximation |
| Diagrams are interpreted differently | Orientation or hatching conventions not followed | Enforce the convention rigidly and include a small legend on every diagram showing the hatching key and orientation marker |
| Committee burns out from tedious work | Reformatting treated as low-status obligation | Rotate committee membership; treat documentation as a valued craft, not a chore; publicly credit the documentarians |
| Format drift across settlements | Isolated communities modifying conventions independently | Schedule annual format review at inter-settlement meetings; circulate exemplar documents as benchmarks |


## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Pictographic supplement** -- A visual symbol drawn alongside written text to convey meaning independent of language; not a full writing system but a targeted set of technical icons.

- **Domain marker** -- A standardized icon on a document header indicating which field of knowledge the document addresses.

- **Hand-span** -- The base unit of length in the network's standard measurement system, approximately 20 cm, defined as the distance from thumb tip to little finger tip with the hand spread.

- **Finger-width** -- The subdivision of the hand-span, approximately 2 cm, used for finer measurements in diagrams and material specifications.

- **Head-pot** -- The standard large volume unit, approximately 10 liters, defined as the capacity of a clay vessel sized to be carried on a human head.

- **Cupped-hand** -- The base unit of volume, approximately 100 mL, defined as the amount of liquid held in a loosely cupped hand.

- **Hatching convention** -- A system of fill patterns in technical diagrams where each pattern indicates a specific material: diagonal lines for wood, dots for sand, crosshatch for stone, solid black for metal, wavy lines for water.

- **Source attribution** -- A mandatory section of every inter-settlement document recording which community originated the knowledge and when it was first documented.

- **Calibration rod** -- A physical length standard distributed to each settlement to anchor body-referenced measurements to a fixed reference.

- **Rubric** -- In medieval manuscript tradition, a heading written in red ink to mark a new section; in this system, any visual marker that delineates section boundaries.

- **Pilcrow** -- A paragraph mark used in medieval manuscripts to indicate structural divisions, analogous to the section markers in the standard document format.

- **Colophon** -- A scribal note at the end of a document recording its origin, date, and author; the ancestor of the source attribution section.

- **ISOTYPE** -- International System of Typographic Picture Education, the pictographic communication system developed by Otto Neurath that informs the design of the pictographic supplement.

- **Metrological standard** -- An agreed-upon reference for measurement units maintained across multiple communities; in Mesopotamia, standardized weights and measures enforced through scribal training.

- **Documentation committee** -- The two-to-five person group in each settlement responsible for converting local technical knowledge into the standardized inter-settlement format.
