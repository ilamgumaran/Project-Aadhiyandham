# Topographical Resilience: Land Selection Guide

## 1. Why This Is Important
The topography of your site is your primary defense against a changing climate. High-tech defenses (like sea walls or giant fans) require constant energy and maintenance. Natural topographical features—elevation, ridges, and valleys—provide "passive protection" that never fails. By choosing the right shape of land, you reduce the physical and energetic cost of survival to a minimum.

## 2. Expected Outcome
A settlement location that is naturally immune to sea-level rise, flood spikes, and the most destructive forces of hurricane-speed winds, while simultaneously maximizing natural warmth.

## 3. Topographical Indicators (What to Look For)

| Feature | Target Specification | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Elevation** | 100m - 300m above sea level | Immune to 100m sea-level rise; low enough for high oxygen and easier agriculture. |
| **Wind Buffer** | High ridge to the North/West | Blocks prevailing winter winds and breaks the force of storm surges. |
| **Slope** | 2% to 10% grade | Perfect for gravity-fed water drainage without causing severe erosion. |
| **The "Bowl"** | Protected Valley/Plateau | Creates a stable microclimate; cold air drains away, warm air stays. |

## 4. Land Assessment Instructions (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Elevation Mapping
Using the provided GPS coordinates and a physical or salvaged topographic map:
1.  Identify the 100m contour line.
2.  Mark all potential building sites *above* this line.
3.  **The High Ground Rule:** Never place critical infrastructure (food storage, nurseries) in the lowest point of a valley, even if it's 100m+ ASL, due to localized flood risks.

### Step 2: The Wind-Shadow Test
Observe the local vegetation and topography:
1.  Identify the direction of prevailing storm winds (usually from the ocean or large plains).
2.  Look for "Leeward" slopes—the side of the mountain facing away from the wind.
3.  **Observation:** Are the trees on the ridge stunted or leaning? If so, the wind energy is high. Move lower into the "shadow" of that ridge until the vegetation appears upright and calm.

### Step 3: Identifying the Thermal Belt
Thermal belts are horizontal zones on mountain slopes that stay warmer than the valley floor (where cold air pools) and the mountain peaks.
1.  **Night Observation:** During a clear night, monitor the temperature at three elevations: Valley Floor, Mid-Slope, and Ridge Top.
2.  **The Discovery:** The "Thermal Belt" is the mid-slope zone where the temperature remains consistently higher than the floor.
3.  **Action:** This is where you must place your primary agriculture and housing.

### Step 4: Hydrological Runoff Assessment
1.  Observe the land during or after a heavy rain.
2.  Identify where water "pools" vs. where it "flows."
3.  **Outcome:** Map your "Gravity Drain" paths. Ensure no building sites are in the path of natural water-shedding gullies.

## 6. Maintenance of the Land
*   **Terracing:** On slopes over 5%, build stone or timber terraces to prevent soil loss (Module 02).
*   **Windbreaks:** Plant dense, multi-layered "Living Walls" of trees on the edges of your thermal belt to further dissipate wind energy.
