# Precision Metallurgy: Crucible Steel (Wootz) Guide

## 1. Why This Is Important
While wrought iron (Module 03) is excellent for structural work, it is too soft to hold a surgical or industrial edge. High-carbon steel is required for the "precision layer" of civilization—scalpels, fine chisels, and mechanical gears. By mastering the crucible method, we can create the world's highest quality steel using only local wrought iron and organic carbon.

## 2. Expected Outcome
A 1-2kg ingot of high-carbon "pattern-welded" steel with extreme hardness and significant toughness.

---

## 3. How-To: The Co-Fusion Process (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Preparing the Charge
1.  **Iron:** Chop wrought iron (from the Bloomery) into small 2cm chunks.
2.  **Carbon:** Source organic carbon (green leaves or bamboo) or high-carbon cast iron scrap.
3.  **The Mix:** 98.5% Iron to 1.5% Carbon by weight.

### Step 2: Sealing the Crucible
1.  Place the mixture into a high-alumina clay crucible.
2.  **The Lid:** Cap the crucible with a clay lid and seal it hermetically with "fire-clay" daub.
3.  **Rationale:** The seal prevents oxygen from entering, ensuring the carbon is absorbed into the iron rather than burning away.

### Step 3: The Melt
1.  Place the sealed crucible in a high-temp forge.
2.  Maintain a white-hot temperature (approx. 1450°C) for 4–6 hours.
3.  **Observation:** Use a long rod to "jiggle" the crucible. If it feels liquid, the fusion is complete.

### Step 4: The Slow Cool (The Pattern)
**CRITICAL:** Do not remove the crucible from the forge. 
1.  Stop the air blast.
2.  Allow the crucible to cool *inside* the furnace over 2–3 days.
3.  **Rationale:** Slow cooling allows large carbide crystals to grow, creating the legendary "Damascus" watered pattern and superior edge retention.

## 5. Maintenance Guide
*   **Forging:** The resulting ingot is extremely hard. It must be forged at a "blood red" heat. If forged too hot, the carbide pattern will dissolve.
*   **Etching:** To reveal the watered pattern, dip the finished tool in a mild acid (vinegar or lemon juice).
