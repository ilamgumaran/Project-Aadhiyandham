# Material Synthesis and Recycling: Iron Smelting, Steel Making, and Metal Recovery

**Alignment:** Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

Iron is the foundation material of all tool-making. Without iron, a community cannot make axes, saws, knives, plows, nails, fasteners, or machine components. Every other manufacturing capability — woodworking, construction, agriculture, textile production — depends on iron tools. A settlement that loses the ability to produce and maintain iron tools is a settlement in civilizational collapse. This module describes how to ensure iron is always available.

The community has two iron sources: scavenged modern steel, which will be abundant in the early years but degrades through rust, fatigue, and breakage; and primary smelting from ore, which is renewable and indefinite but demands skill, infrastructure, and enormous quantities of charcoal. This module covers both pathways. The recycling pathway — forge-working scavenged steel to reshape, repair, and extend its useful life — is the immediate priority. The primary pathway — bloomery smelting of local ore to produce new iron from raw minerals — is the long-term foundation. Both must be developed in parallel. Recycling buys time while smelting capability matures. Neglecting either pathway creates a critical vulnerability.

Historical precedent eliminates any doubt about feasibility. Bloomery smelting was independently invented on every inhabited continent and operated continuously for over three thousand years. Japanese tatara furnaces, African direct-reduction traditions, European bloomeries, and Indian wootz crucible steel all produced functional iron and steel at village scale without industrial infrastructure, without electricity, without fossil fuels. The chemistry is well understood, the equipment is simple, and the skills can be learned through deliberate practice. Nothing in this module is speculative. Every technique described here has been validated by centuries of use and confirmed by modern experimental archaeology.

> **DANGER**: Iron smelting involves temperatures exceeding 1200 degrees C, molten slag, toxic carbon monoxide gas, and the handling of incandescent metal. This is the highest-risk manufacturing activity in the settlement. All operators must understand and accept these risks before participating.
>
> - **Eyes:** Light at 1200 degrees C can permanently damage your retinas. Never stare directly into the tuyere or the furnace opening. Use tinted glass or smoked mica eye protection if available. At minimum, avert your gaze when adding charges or tapping slag.
> - **Skin:** Molten slag is liquid silicate glass at 1200 degrees C or higher. A single splash causes deep, disabling burns. Wear thick leather aprons, heavy leather gloves, and face shields during all tapping and extraction operations.
> - **Carbon Monoxide:** The bloomery produces carbon monoxide (CO) as its primary reducing agent. CO is colorless and odorless. NEVER operate a bloomery in an enclosed space. Position the furnace so that prevailing wind carries exhaust AWAY from operators. Symptoms of CO exposure: headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion. If anyone shows these symptoms, STOP the smelt immediately and evacuate all personnel to fresh air. CO poisoning can be fatal.
> - **Buddy System:** Never smelt alone. If an operator faints from heat, CO exposure, or exhaustion, they need immediate rescue. Minimum crew: two operators and one safety observer at all times during a smelt.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Chemistry of Iron Reduction

Iron does not occur as pure metal in nature. It exists as iron oxide — iron atoms chemically bonded to oxygen atoms in mineral form. The three most common iron ore minerals are hematite (Fe2O3), magnetite (Fe3O4), and limonite or bog iron (FeOOH). The purpose of smelting is to break these chemical bonds and separate the iron from the oxygen.

The core reduction reaction is: Fe2O3 + 3CO produces 2Fe + 3CO2. Carbon monoxide from charcoal combustion strips oxygen atoms from the ore, leaving metallic iron behind. The CO itself is produced by incomplete combustion of charcoal in the reducing zone of the furnace, where air supply is limited: 2C + O2 produces 2CO.

This is why the air blast must be carefully controlled. Too much air burns charcoal completely to CO2, useless for reduction. The right amount produces CO needed to strip oxygen from the ore. The smelter controls this balance through bellows rate and tuyere diameter.

Temperature requirements are critical. The ore must reach 1100-1300 degrees C for reduction to proceed efficiently. Below 1100 degrees C, the reaction is too slow to produce a useful bloom in a reasonable time. Above 1400 degrees C, the iron melts and absorbs excess carbon from the surrounding charcoal, producing cast iron rather than wrought iron — a fundamentally different material with different properties.

The bloom forms as a spongy mass of iron particles that weld together in the heat but never fully melt. This is the critical distinction: a bloomery produces SOLID iron, not liquid iron. The iron consolidates through hammering (shingling), not through casting.

### Iron, Steel, and Cast Iron

Understanding the three forms of ferrous metal is essential for any settlement metalworker. Each has distinct properties determined by its carbon content.

**Wrought iron** contains 0.02-0.08 percent carbon. It is soft, highly forgeable, weldable, and relatively corrosion-resistant. Wrought iron is the direct product of a bloomery smelt. It is suitable for nails, chains, hinges, structural ironwork, and agricultural implements — anything that must bend without breaking but does not need to hold a sharp cutting edge.

**Steel** contains 0.3-1.5 percent carbon. It is hard, holds an edge, can be spring-tempered, and is tougher than wrought iron under impact. Steel is made by carburizing wrought iron — heating it in a sealed pack of charcoal at 900-1000 degrees C for 4-12 hours so that carbon atoms diffuse into the iron crystal structure. Steel is essential for cutting tools: axes, knives, chisels, saws, files, and drill bits.

**Cast iron** contains 2-4 percent carbon. It is brittle but castable, melting at approximately 1150 degrees C compared to wrought iron's melting point of approximately 1530 degrees C. Cast iron is produced when bloomery temperature is too high or when iron sits too long in the charcoal bed absorbing excess carbon. Though often treated as a failure product, cast iron is useful for pots, stove plates, anvil bases, and any component that can be cast in a mold and does not need to flex or absorb impact.

**Heat treatment** transforms steel's properties through two sequential processes:

- **Quenching** — rapid cooling in water or oil — hardens steel by locking its crystal structure in a strained state. The steel becomes very hard but also very brittle.
- **Tempering** — reheating quenched steel to 200-300 degrees C — restores toughness while maintaining much of the hardness gained from quenching.

The color of the oxide layer that forms during tempering indicates the temperature reached: straw yellow at 220 degrees C gives maximum hardness for cutting edges; brown at 260 degrees C gives moderate toughness for general-purpose tools; blue at 300 degrees C gives maximum springiness for springs and saw blades. This color scale is the blacksmith's thermometer — learn it and use it.

### Ore Identification and Sourcing

**Bog iron** (limonite/goethite, FeOOH) is found in wetlands, bogs, stream beds, and poorly drained meadows as orange-brown deposits, crusts, or nodules. It is formed by iron-oxidizing bacteria that concentrate dissolved iron from groundwater. Bog iron is the most accessible ore source for most temperate settlements and is renewable — a depleted deposit regenerates in 15-30 years as bacterial activity continues.

**Magnetite sand** (Fe3O4) is black, heavy sand found in river and beach deposits. It is strongly attracted to a magnet — the simplest field test for iron ore. Magnetite is higher grade than bog iron but requires washing and concentration before smelting.

**Hematite** (Fe2O3) is a red-brown mineral found in geological formations. It has the highest iron content of common ores (approximately 70 percent Fe by weight) but typically requires mining from bedrock. It is unlikely to be the first ore source for a newly established settlement.

**Laterite** is iron-rich tropical and subtropical soil. Its characteristic red-orange color indicates high iron oxide content. Lower grade (20-40 percent Fe) but extremely abundant in tropical regions and can be smelted directly in some cases.

**Field identification:** carry a magnet at all times when surveying. Any soil, rock, or sand that attracts a magnet contains iron minerals worth investigating. For the streak test, rub the sample firmly on a rough white surface such as unglazed ceramic or the back of a porcelain tile. Red-brown streak indicates hematite. Yellow-brown streak indicates limonite or bog iron. Black streak indicates magnetite.

### The Recycling Pathway: Working Scavenged Steel

Modern steel in its various grades — mild, stainless, tool, and spring — can be reshaped in a forge at 800-1000 degrees C using charcoal fuel and bellows-driven air. This is far simpler than smelting and should be the community's first metalworking activity. A forge can be operational within days, whereas a bloomery requires months of preparation.

Identify steel type by the spark test: grind the steel against a stone or abrasive wheel and observe the sparks carefully.

- Mild steel produces long orange sparks with few forks
- High-carbon steel produces bright white sparks with many forks and bursts
- Stainless steel produces short, dull orange sparks with very few forks
- Cast iron produces short red sparks that die quickly

This test allows the smith to sort scavenged steel by carbon content without laboratory equipment.

Forge welding allows two pieces of steel to be permanently joined by heating to yellow-white heat (1200 degrees C and above) and hammering together. Flux — borax or fine sand — must be applied to the joint surfaces to prevent oxidation, which would block the weld. The technique requires practice: both pieces must reach welding temperature simultaneously, and the hammer blows must be firm and rapid before the metal cools below the welding range. Critical sources for scavenged steel include the following:

- Leaf springs from vehicles — excellent high-carbon tool steel for axes and knives
- Rebar — mild steel suitable for structural ironwork, nails, and hooks
- Railroad spikes — medium carbon, good for general-purpose tools and hardware
- Files — high-carbon tool steel, ideal for chisels and cutting edges
- Ball bearings — chrome steel, excellent for small cutting tools and punches
- Coil springs — high-carbon steel suitable for saw blades and spring mechanisms

A systematic salvage effort in the early years can extend the community's tool supply by 10-30 years while bloomery capability develops. Prioritize collecting and sorting steel by type during the first year. Metal rusting in the open can be preserved by storing under cover with a light coating of grease or oil.

### Historical Smelting Traditions

**African iron smelting** represents the oldest confirmed iron smelting tradition in the world. The Nok culture of central Nigeria was producing iron by approximately 900 BCE, and some Central African sites may be older still. African bloomeries achieved temperatures sufficient for steel production, and some traditions used natural draft from tall furnace shafts (up to 2 meters or more) rather than bellows, demonstrating that forced air is not strictly necessary if the furnace design compensates with height and chimney effect.

**Japanese tatara** smelting was a semi-continuous process using iron sand (magnetite). A single 72-hour tatara smelt consumed approximately 13 tonnes each of iron sand and charcoal, producing 2-3 tonnes of mixed iron and steel. The resulting tamahagane steel was broken apart after cooling and selectively sorted by carbon content — high-carbon pieces for cutting edges, low-carbon pieces for blade cores and structural elements — demonstrating sophisticated quality control at pre-industrial scale. The tatara tradition confirms that large-scale iron production requires no equipment beyond what a pre-industrial community can build.

**European bloomery tradition** was widespread from the Iron Age (approximately 800 BCE) through the eighteenth century in rural areas. Village-scale operations with one to three smelters supported communities of 100-500 people. The transition to blast furnaces occurred only in regions with access to water power for large bellows — bloomeries remained viable wherever water power was unavailable. Many rural European communities operated bloomeries continuously for centuries, passing skills from master to apprentice across generations.

**Indian wootz steel** was produced by sealing wrought iron with carbon sources (wood chips, leaves, rice husks) in small clay crucibles and heating to near-melting temperature (approximately 1400 degrees C). The resulting high-carbon steel, traded across the medieval world as Damascus steel, demonstrates that advanced metallurgy is achievable at cottage-industry scale with no equipment more complex than a clay pot and a charcoal fire. The crucible steel technique should be attempted once basic bloomery and forge skills are established.

## Core Principles

1. **The Dual Pathway Principle** — develop BOTH recycling (forge-working scavenged steel) and primary smelting (bloomery) simultaneously. Recycling extends the tool supply while primary smelting capability matures. Neither alone is sufficient. Recycling without smelting is a countdown to zero. Smelting without recycling wastes irreplaceable modern steel while the community is still learning.

2. **The Charcoal Budget Principle** — iron smelting consumes enormous quantities of charcoal: 10-15 kg of charcoal per kg of iron produced. The community's charcoal supply is a finite resource unless coppice woodland is actively managed. Calculate the annual charcoal budget before committing to a smelting schedule. A single smelt producing a 5 kg bloom requires 50-75 kg of charcoal, which in turn requires 200-300 kg of dry hardwood. See Outcome 2, Section 04.07 for sustainable charcoal production through coppice management.

3. **The Quality Progression Principle** — the first bloom will be poor quality: small, full of slag inclusions, difficult to forge. This is normal and expected. Quality improves with experience as the smelters learn to read the furnace — adjusting air blast, charge ratios, and timing. Expect 5-10 smelts before producing consistent, workable iron. DO NOT abandon bloomery development because early results are disappointing.

4. **The Tool Steel Priority Principle** — not all iron is equal. Wrought iron from the bloomery is suitable for structural use but too soft for cutting tools. The community must also produce STEEL through carburization for axes, knives, saws, and chisels. Carburization capability should be developed within the first year of successful bloomery operation. Without steel, the settlement cannot maintain its woodworking, agricultural, or construction tool inventory.

5. **The Safety Discipline Principle** — iron smelting is the most dangerous manufacturing process in the settlement. Strict safety protocols — eye protection, leather aprons, buddy system, CO monitoring, no-go zones around the furnace during operation — must be established BEFORE the first smelt and enforced without exception. A single serious burn or CO poisoning incident can disable a critical community member for weeks or permanently.

## Practical Implementation

### Phase 1: Establish the Forge (Months 1-6)

Build a simple charcoal forge for recycling scavenged steel. Required materials: a hearth constructed from firebrick or refractory clay, bellows (leather bag type or rotary blower), an anvil (a large flat rock or a heavy piece of scrap steel with a flat face), a hammer of at least 1 kg, and tongs long enough to keep hands away from the fire.

The forge must reach 800-1000 degrees C (visible orange to yellow heat) — sufficient for shaping and forge welding but not for smelting. Begin with simple practice tasks: straightening bent tools, drawing nails from wire stock, reshaping rebar into hooks and fasteners. Build skill progressively before attempting complex forgework such as forge welding or tool making.

Train at least two community members in basic forge operation so that the capability survives the loss of any single individual. Document the location and type of all scavenged steel stockpiles.

### Phase 2: Ore Survey and Preparation (Months 3-12)

Survey the settlement's surrounding area within a 10 km radius for iron ore sources using the magnet test described above. Prioritize bog iron deposits in wetlands, magnetite sands in stream beds, and any exposed red or orange mineral formations. Collect and stockpile promising ore — a minimum of 50 kg is needed for the first test smelt.

Roast the ore before smelting: build a large wood fire, place the raw ore on top, and maintain heat for 4-6 hours. Roasting drives out chemically bound moisture and converts some mineral phases to more easily reduced forms. Bog iron turns from yellow-brown to dark red or black as goethite converts to hematite.

After roasting, quench the hot ore in water to induce fracturing, then crush to pea-sized fragments (5-15 mm diameter) using a hammer and flat stone. Fragments too large will not reduce fully; fragments too fine clog airflow. Pea-sized is the target.

### Phase 3: Build and Operate the Bloomery (Months 6-18)

**Furnace construction:** Build a cylindrical shaft furnace with 25-30 cm internal diameter and 80-120 cm height. Walls should be 5-8 cm thick, constructed from refractory clay mixed with sand at a 50:50 ratio (or with crushed quartz for improved thermal shock resistance). Dry the furnace thoroughly for a minimum of two weeks before first use — slow drying prevents cracking. Install the tuyere hole at 10-15 cm above the furnace base, angled 15-20 degrees downward so that the air blast is directed toward the center of the hearth. Leave a tap hole at base level for draining liquid slag during the smelt.

**Charging:** Alternate layers of charcoal and crushed roasted ore. Begin with a charcoal-to-ore ratio of 2:1 by weight. Adjust based on results — more charcoal raises temperature and improves reduction but increases the risk of producing cast iron; less charcoal conserves fuel but may result in incomplete reduction. Each charge should be approximately 1-2 kg of ore and 2-4 kg of charcoal.

**The smelt:** Preheat the furnace by filling with charcoal only and lighting from the top. Run bellows for one hour to establish operating temperature before adding the first ore charge. Maintain continuous bellows operation at a minimum of 100 liters per minute air blast through the tuyere for 6-10 hours. This requires two operators working in 30-minute shifts — bellows operation is physically exhausting and cannot be sustained by one person.

Add fresh charges of charcoal and ore from the top as the burden descends. The furnace should never be allowed to cool during charging. Tap liquid slag from the base every 45-60 minutes by clearing the tap hole with a long wooden rod. The slag should flow freely as a glowing orange-yellow liquid — if it is viscous and dark, the temperature is too low.

**Extraction:** When the smelt is complete, allow the furnace to cool to dark red heat. Break open the front wall or lift the bloom from the top using heavy tongs. Immediately shingle the bloom: hammer it vigorously on a flat stone or iron anvil to squeeze out trapped slag while the iron is still at welding heat. This consolidation step is critical — an unshingled bloom is porous and weak. Work quickly: once the bloom cools below orange heat, it can no longer be effectively shingled and must be reheated in the forge.

### Phase 4: Carburization — Making Steel (Month 12+)

Pack wrought iron bars tightly in a sealed clay vessel (crucible) surrounded by fine charcoal powder. Seal all openings with clay to exclude air. Place the crucible in a forge or kiln maintained at 900-1000 degrees C for 4-12 hours. Longer times produce deeper carbon penetration.

The carbon from the charcoal diffuses into the iron surface, converting the outer layer to steel with 0.3-0.8 percent carbon content over a wrought iron core. The resulting bar has a hard steel exterior over a tough wrought iron core.

For uniform steel throughout the cross-section, fold and forge-weld the bar repeatedly — the technique used in Japanese sword-making — distributing the carbon evenly through the metal. The folding process works as follows:

- Heat the bar to yellow-white welding temperature
- Apply flux (borax or fine sand) to the surface
- Cut or nick the bar at its midpoint and fold it over onto itself
- Hammer the folded bar flat, welding the layers together
- Repeat: each fold doubles the number of layers

Four to six folds (producing 16-64 layers) is sufficient for usable tool steel. Each fold requires heating to welding temperature and applying flux before hammering, so this process consumes significant charcoal and time. The result is steel of consistent quality suitable for the settlement's most demanding cutting tools.

## Common Failure Modes

**Furnace Cracking** — thermal shock or inadequate drying causes the furnace wall to crack during the smelt, losing heat and potentially causing a structural collapse that spills hot charcoal and slag. Prevention: dry the furnace for a minimum of two weeks before first use, ideally in warm dry weather. Preheat slowly over several hours rather than bringing to full temperature immediately. Mix sand or crushed quartz into the clay body to reduce shrinkage cracking. Expect to rebuild the furnace every 3-5 smelts as thermal cycling degrades the structure. Keep spare refractory clay prepared for patching minor cracks between smelts.

**No Bloom Produced** — after 6-8 hours of smelting, only slag is found at the furnace base with no consolidated iron. This is the most common failure for beginners. Causes: temperature too low due to insufficient air blast, ore too low-grade to yield a useful bloom, or charcoal-to-ore ratio incorrect. Prevention: ensure bellows deliver a minimum of 100 liters per minute sustained airflow. Increase the charcoal ratio. Test ore grade before committing to a full smelt — strong magnetic attraction indicates higher iron content and better prospects. The first smelt is primarily a learning exercise; do not invest the entire ore stockpile in a single attempt.

**Cast Iron Instead of Wrought Iron** — the bloom is hard and brittle, shattering when struck with a hammer rather than deforming. Cause: furnace temperature was too high or the iron sat in contact with charcoal for too long, absorbing excess carbon beyond the steel range into the cast iron range. Prevention: reduce air blast intensity toward the end of the smelt. Extract the bloom promptly once it has reached adequate size. Reduce the charcoal-to-ore ratio. Note that cast iron, while not forgeable, is still useful — it can be remelted at approximately 1150 degrees C and cast into molds for pots, plates, weights, and structural components.

**Carbon Monoxide Poisoning** — operators experience headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, or collapse during the smelt. CO is the primary reducing agent in the bloomery and is produced in large quantities throughout the process. Prevention: ALWAYS operate the bloomery outdoors or in a structure open on at least three sides, with the exhaust carried downwind of all personnel. Monitor all team members for symptoms continuously. Maintain the buddy system without exception — never allow a single person to remain near the furnace alone. If anyone shows symptoms, STOP the smelt immediately and evacuate everyone to fresh air upwind of the furnace.

**Slag Burns** — molten slag at 1200 degrees C or higher splashes onto skin during tapping, causing severe deep burns that heal slowly and risk infection. Prevention: wear a thick leather apron, heavy leather gloves, and a face shield during all tapping operations. Stand to the SIDE of the tap hole, never directly in front. Clear the tap channel with a long rod before opening the tap hole to prevent pressure buildup and splash-back. Keep a bucket of dry sand (not water) nearby to cover spilled slag — water on molten slag causes steam explosions.

**Charcoal Depletion** — smelting operations consume charcoal faster than the community can produce it, leading to progressive deforestation of the surrounding woodland and eventual inability to smelt at all. Prevention: calculate the annual charcoal budget before establishing a smelting schedule. A single 5 kg bloom requires 50-75 kg of charcoal, which requires 200-300 kg of dry hardwood. Establish coppice woodland management from Year 1 of settlement. Limit smelting frequency to what the woodland can sustain on a renewable basis — typically 4-8 smelts per year for a settlement-scale coppice of 5-10 hectares.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Reduction** — the chemical process inside the furnace where carbon monoxide from the charcoal strips oxygen from the iron ore, leaving behind metallic iron. The reverse of rusting (oxidation).

- **Refractory** — materials, particularly specific clays mixed with sand or quartz, that can withstand extremely high temperatures without melting, deforming, or cracking. Essential for furnace construction.

- **Shingling** — the process of hammering a hot iron bloom immediately after extraction to squeeze out trapped liquid slag and consolidate the spongy iron mass into a solid, workable bar.

- **Slag** — the glassy waste material that separates from the iron during smelting. Composed primarily of iron silicate, it contains impurities from the ore, flux, and furnace wall material. Liquid above approximately 1100 degrees C.

- **Tuyere** — (pronounced *tweer*) the pipe or channel that carries the air blast from the bellows into the furnace interior. Positioned 10-15 cm above the base and angled downward. The most thermally stressed component of the furnace.

- **Bloom** — the spongy mass of iron and slag produced by a bloomery smelt. Not yet usable metal — must be shingled (hammered) to consolidate into wrought iron.

- **Bog iron** — iron hydroxide (FeOOH) deposited in wetlands and stream beds by iron-oxidizing bacteria. The most accessible ore source for temperate settlements. Regenerates in 15-30 years after harvesting.

- **Carburization** — the process of adding carbon to wrought iron by heating it in sealed contact with charcoal powder at 900-1000 degrees C, converting the surface layer to steel.

- **Forge welding** — joining two pieces of iron or steel by heating both to yellow-white heat (1200 degrees C and above) and hammering them together. Requires flux (borax or fine sand) applied to the joint surfaces to prevent oxidation.

- **Quenching** — rapid cooling of hot steel in water or oil to harden it by locking the crystal structure in a strained configuration. Must be followed by tempering to restore toughness.

- **Tempering** — reheating quenched steel to 200-300 degrees C to reduce brittleness while retaining most of the hardness gained from quenching. The oxide color on the surface indicates temperature reached.

- **Spark test** — method of identifying steel type and approximate carbon content by observing the color, length, and forking pattern of sparks produced when the steel is ground against an abrasive surface.

- **Flux** — a substance (borax, fine sand, or wood ash) applied to metal surfaces before forge welding to dissolve oxide scale and ensure a clean metallurgical bond between the pieces.

- **Charge** — a measured quantity of fuel and ore added to the furnace as a single batch during a smelt. Multiple charges are added sequentially as the burden descends.

- **Draft** — the flow of air through the furnace, either natural (created by the chimney effect of a tall shaft) or forced (driven by bellows or a blower). Controls combustion rate and furnace temperature.

## Maintenance and Long-Term Operations

The bloomery furnace is a consumable structure. Expect to rebuild or extensively repair it every 3-5 smelts. Keep a stockpile of prepared refractory clay (mixed with sand at 50:50) available at all times so that repairs can be made immediately after each smelt while the furnace condition can be assessed.

The tuyere is the most thermally stressed component and will need replacement most frequently. Prepare spare tuyeres in advance — a ceramic or clay pipe of 3-5 cm internal diameter, dried and pre-fired before installation.

The first usable bloom should be used to make improved metalworking tools: a better hammer, a pair of tongs, a hardy (a cutting tool that fits into the anvil), and a punch. Each generation of tools improves the quality and efficiency of subsequent work. This bootstrapping cycle — using iron to make better tools for making iron — is foundational.

Maintain a written log of each smelt recording the following:

- Ore source, type, and quantity charged
- Charcoal quantity consumed
- Smelt duration and bellows shift schedule
- Slag color, viscosity, and total volume
- Bloom weight, density, and forgeability assessment

This record allows the team to diagnose failures and systematically improve technique over successive smelts.
