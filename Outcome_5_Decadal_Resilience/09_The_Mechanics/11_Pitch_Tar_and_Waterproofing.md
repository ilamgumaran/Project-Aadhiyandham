**Alignment:** Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

> **Caution — water is patient, and it wins:** Everything the settlement builds that must keep water out — a boat, a barrel, a roof, a rope, a wooden joint, a leather boot — fails eventually to moisture unless it is sealed, and the failure is slow and quiet until it is sudden and total. Pitch and tar are the settlement's answer, made from its own trees, and the making involves fire and hot, sticky, flammable material that burns skin badly. Handle the hot tar with the same respect as the [forge and the lye](07_Foundational_Chemistry.md); a splash of hot pitch is a serious burn.

## 1. Why This Is Important

A great deal of what a settlement makes is wood, plant fiber, and hide — all of which rot, swell, and fail when wet. Without a waterproofing material, the settlement cannot make a boat that floats for long, a barrel or bucket that holds water, a rope that survives the rain, a roof that does not leak into the timbers, or a preserved wooden structure. Historically the answer, for millennia and across cultures, was **tar and pitch** — the thick, black, water-repellent, wood-preserving substance cooked out of resinous wood and bark. It waterproofed the ships that crossed oceans, sealed the barrels that stored the food and drink, and preserved the timber and rope of whole civilizations. It is one of those quiet materials whose absence quietly caps what a settlement can build and keep.

The settlement can make it from its own resinous pine or birch, with fire and simple earthworks, and this module is how — plus the related waterproofing agents (wax, fat, oil) and where each is used.

## 2. Expected Outcome

A reliable supply of wood tar and pitch from local trees, and the knowledge of how to apply it and its companion sealers (wax, oil, fat) to waterproof and preserve the boats, containers, cordage, roofing, and wooden structures the settlement depends on.

## 3. Making Tar and Pitch

Tar is made by **destructive distillation** — heating resinous wood or bark in a low-oxygen environment so that, instead of burning to ash, it "sweats" out its tar, which is collected.

*   **The tar kiln / pit method.** The traditional approach: pack resinous pinewood (especially resin-rich stumps and roots, "fatwood") or birch bark tightly into a sloped, sealed pit or an earthen/clay kiln with a channel at the bottom leading out to a collection vessel. Fire it from above or around so it heats *without* free-burning; the heat drives the tar out of the wood, and it runs down the sloped floor and out the channel into the collector. Days of accumulated craft-knowledge went into doing this well, but the principle is simple: **heat the resinous wood hot, starved of air, and catch what runs out.**
*   **Birch-bark tar — the small-batch version.** Birch bark yields an excellent tar and can be made in small amounts by heating rolled bark in a sealed container (a "double-pot" retort — bark in an upper vessel, its neck into a lower collecting vessel, the whole thing heated) so the tar drips down into the collector. This is the settlement's easy entry point and makes a fine adhesive and sealant.
*   **Pitch from tar.** *Tar* is the raw liquid; **pitch** is tar boiled down thicker (and sometimes blended) until it sets harder — the stiffer, more durable sealant for hulls and heavy use. Boil tar to concentrate it to the consistency you need; blend with a little fat, wax, or oil to adjust how hard or flexible it sets.

## 4. Applying It — and the Companion Sealers

Different jobs want different sealers; pitch is the heavy-duty one, but it has partners:

*   **Boats and barrels — pitch.** Pay hot pitch (often over caulking of fiber, "oakum") into the seams and over the wood of a [boat](../../Outcome_1_Locating_Refugia/01_The_Journey/10_Maritime_and_Riverine_Sovereignty/01_Rationale_and_Importance.md) hull or a water barrel; it fills gaps and repels water. This is what made wooden ships and casks watertight.
*   **Wood preservation — tar.** Brushed onto exposed timber, posts, and shingles, tar penetrates and preserves against rot and insects — the reason tarred wood and "tarred" fences and roofs lasted. It protects the [structural timber and roofing](../../Outcome_2_Biological_Sovereignty/06_Ecological_Harmony/04_Shelter_and_Thermal_Grounding.md) the shelter depends on.
*   **Cordage and rigging — tar.** Tarred rope resists rot and water enormously better than bare fiber, which is why working [rope](09_Cordage_and_Rope.md) exposed to weather was traditionally tarred. Essential for anything wet and load-bearing.
*   **Wax and fat — the gentler sealers.** [Beeswax](../../Outcome_2_Biological_Sovereignty/06_Ecological_Harmony/10_Beekeeping.md) and rendered fat/oil waterproof what pitch is too harsh or too hot for: sealing [pottery](08_Ceramic_Synthesis.md) and food vessels (wax is food-safe where tar is not), dressing [leather and boots](05_Textiles_and_Leather.md) (fat/oil/wax keep leather supple and water-resistant), waxing cloth for rain gear, and finishing fine woodwork. **Use wax/fat, not tar, on anything that touches food or skin.**
*   **Oil finishes.** Drying plant oils (and boiled oils) harden into a water-resistant finish on wood and are the gentler preserve for tools and furniture.

## 5. Common Failure Modes

*   **Hot-pitch burns.** Careless handling of hot tar/pitch. **[Tier L] Consequence:** severe, sticky burns that cling to skin and keep burning. **Prevention:** treat hot pitch like molten metal — cover skin, pour carefully, keep water away from the hot pot (it spatters), work with the [fire-safety](../../Outcome_2_Biological_Sovereignty/06_Ecological_Harmony/07_Fire_Safety_and_the_Hearth.md) discipline; the material is also flammable.
*   **Tar on food or skin surfaces.** Using wood tar to seal food vessels or dress skin. **[Tier C→L] Consequence:** tar's compounds are not food-safe and can be irritant/toxic. **Prevention:** wax/fat for anything touching food or skin; tar for boats, timber, and rope.
*   **Sealing over wet or dirty wood.** Applying pitch/tar to damp or unclean surfaces. **[Tier C] Consequence:** it doesn't bond, water gets trapped underneath, and the wood rots faster behind the seal. **Prevention:** apply to clean, dry surfaces in dry weather.
*   **No re-sealing.** Treating waterproofing as one-and-done. **[Tier C] Consequence:** seals wear, crack, and wash off; the leak returns unnoticed until damage is done. **Prevention:** inspect and re-pitch/re-tar/re-wax on a maintenance cycle, especially boats and roofs.
*   **Running out of resinous wood.** Over-harvesting the resin-rich pine/birch. **[Tier C] Consequence:** the tar supply fails. **Prevention:** manage the resinous-wood source; save stumps and fatwood, which are richest.

## 6. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Destructive distillation:** Heating wood/bark in low oxygen so it releases tar instead of burning; the basis of tar-making.
*   **Fatwood:** Resin-saturated pine (stumps, roots, heartwood); the richest tar feedstock.
*   **Oakum:** Caulking fiber driven into seams before pitch, to fill and hold the seal (boats, barrels).
*   **Paying (a seam):** Applying hot pitch into a caulked seam to waterproof it.
*   **Retort:** A sealed vessel in which bark/wood is heated to drive off and collect tar (the birch-bark double-pot).
*   **Tar vs. pitch:** Tar is the raw liquid distillate; pitch is tar boiled down thicker and harder for heavy sealing.

## 7. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 7.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Preparation:** Gather resinous pinewood/fatwood or birch bark; build a tar pit/kiln or a small retort; ready a collection vessel.
*   **Execution:** Distill tar by heating starved of air; boil some down to pitch; apply pitch to boats/barrels, tar to timber and rope, and wax/fat to food vessels, leather, and skin-contact items.
*   **Review:** Inspect and re-seal on a maintenance cycle; manage the resinous-wood supply.

### 7.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier
*   **[Tier L — Law] Careless hot pitch** → severe clinging burns; flammable-material fire. *Handle like molten metal; keep water clear of the hot pot.*
*   **[Tier L — Law] Tar on food/skin surfaces** → toxic/irritant exposure. *Wax/fat for food and skin; tar for boats, wood, and rope.*
*   **[Tier C — Craft] Tar-making technique, pitch consistency, re-seal interval** → calibrate to your wood, your use, and your weather; learn the burn by doing.

### 7.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** Tar-burning is a skilled, somewhat dangerous craft easily lost; keep it two-deep, and make sure the safety rules (hot-pitch handling, tar-not-on-food) are common knowledge, since the people applying sealers are not always the ones who made them.
