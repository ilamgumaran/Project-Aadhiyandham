# Ceramic Synthesis and Pottery: Vessels of Civilization

**Alignment:** Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

When scavenged plastic buckets photodegrade and metal pots rust through, a community without the ability to create its own containers faces a slow-motion crisis. You cannot perform foundational chemistry — lye extraction, lime slaking, tannin concentration — without vessels that tolerate heat and caustic liquids. You cannot store grain, ferment food, or carry water without watertight containers that resist impact, rot, and vermin. Ceramics are not decorative art in a post-collapse context; they are survival infrastructure on the same tier as shelter, fire, and clean water.

The transformation of soft, wet clay into hard, permanent ceramic is one of the most remarkable chemical reactions available to a community with no industrial inputs. At its core, the process is dehydration and sintering: water molecules bonded between microscopic alumino-silicate plates are driven off by heat, and above 600 degrees Celsius the silica particles begin to fuse at their contact points, creating an irreversible stone-like matrix. This is not drying — it is a one-way chemical transformation. A fired pot can never return to clay. The physics governing this transformation — crystal phase changes, thermal expansion, sintering kinetics — are precise and unforgiving, but learnable from first principles with no instruments beyond your hands and a fire.

Pottery is arguably the most universal technology in human history. Every agricultural civilization on every inhabited continent independently invented ceramics within one to two generations of permanent settlement. The Jomon people of Japan were firing pots 16,500 years before the present. Potters at Catalhoyuk in Anatolia were producing standardized vessel forms by 7500 BCE. African, Chinese, Mesoamerican, and South Asian traditions each arrived at the same core techniques through independent discovery. This universality is not coincidence — it reflects the fact that suitable clay is abundant in most environments, the fuel requirement is modest, and the resulting product is irreplaceable. If every past civilization needed ceramics, yours will too.

> **CAUTION**: Pit and kiln firing involves sustained temperatures of 600-1100 degrees Celsius. Burns, fire spread, and exploding vessels from trapped moisture are serious hazards. Fire in cleared areas only, with water/sand available. Allow 24+ hours cooling before handling fired ware.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Science of Clay

Clay is not simply "fine dirt." It is a specific family of hydrated alumino-silicate minerals with a distinctive plate-like crystal structure that gives rise to the property called plasticity — the ability to be shaped when wet and hold that shape when dry. The most common clay mineral is kaolinite, with the chemical formula Al2Si2O5(OH)4. Under electron microscopy, kaolinite crystals appear as hexagonal plates roughly 0.1-2 micrometers across and less than 0.1 micrometers thick.

When water is introduced between these plates, it forms thin films that allow the plates to slide past one another while surface tension holds them together. This is why wet clay feels smooth and can be shaped: you are sliding millions of microscopic plates across one another on a lubricating film of water.

Different clay types have different working properties. Ball clay (high plasticity, fine particle size, fires white to cream) is excellent for throwing but shrinks heavily — 10-15% from wet to fired. Fire clay (coarse particle size, high alumina content, refractory) tolerates high temperatures but is difficult to shape. Common earthenware clay (moderate plasticity, high iron content, fires red to brown) is the most abundant and the easiest to work, firing successfully at the lowest temperatures (600-900 degrees Celsius). In a post-collapse context, you will almost certainly be working with earthenware clay found in river banks, road cuts, and exposed subsoil.

The most dangerous moment in firing is the quartz inversion at 573 degrees Celsius. At this exact temperature, the crystalline silica (quartz) present in all clay bodies undergoes a sudden crystallographic phase change from alpha-quartz to beta-quartz. The crystal lattice rearranges, and the material expands by approximately 2% in volume — instantaneously.

If the vessel is heated unevenly, or if the temperature ramps too quickly through the 500-600 degree Celsius window, different parts of the vessel expand at different rates and the pot cracks or shatters explosively. The same danger exists in reverse during cooling: beta-quartz snaps back to alpha-quartz at 573 degrees Celsius with a 2% contraction. This is why slow, even heating and slow cooling are not optional refinements — they are the difference between a vessel and a pile of shards.

Beyond the quartz inversion, the degree of final heating determines the fundamental character of the finished ware:

| Ware Type   | Temperature Range | Porosity    | Description                                      |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| Earthenware | 600-900 C         | 10-25%      | Porous, permeable without glaze, adequate for dry storage and cooking |
| Stoneware   | 1200-1300 C       | 2-5%        | Partially vitrified, nearly waterproof, ring when struck |
| Porcelain   | 1300-1400 C       | 0-1%        | Fully vitrified, translucent when thin, requires specialized clay |

Earthenware is the only category achievable with pit firing and simple kilns. Stoneware requires an updraft or crossdraft kiln capable of sustained high temperatures. Porcelain requires both specialized kaolin-rich clay and kiln technology beyond Year 5 capabilities. Plan your vessel program around earthenware for the first several years.

Thermal shock — the stress induced by rapid temperature change — is governed by the coefficient of thermal expansion of the fired body. Vessels with high quartz content expand more per degree of temperature change and are more susceptible to cracking when placed on a fire or filled with boiling liquid. Adding temper materials with low thermal expansion (such as crusite grog or shell) reduces the overall expansion coefficient and produces cookware that tolerates direct flame contact.

### Chemistry of Glazes and Slips

A slip is simply clay mixed with enough water to form a pourable liquid, applied as a coating to a vessel before firing. Slip smooths the surface, seals minor cracks, and can change the color of the finished ware if a different clay is used. An engobe is a slip with added flux or colorant — iron oxide for red-brown, manganese for black-purple — applied for decorative effect.

True glazes are glass coatings fused to the ceramic surface during firing. All glazes require three components in balanced proportion:

- **Flux** (CaO, K2O, Na2O) — lowers the melting point of silica
- **Alumina** (Al2O3) — stiffens the molten glaze so it does not run off the pot
- **Silica** (SiO2) — forms the glass network itself

This is the glaze chemistry triangle, and understanding it allows you to formulate glazes from natural materials without any manufactured chemicals.

Wood ash is the most accessible glaze flux in a post-collapse setting. Hardwood ash contains 30-40% calcium oxide (CaO) and potassium oxide (K2O) by weight — both powerful fluxes. A simple wood ash glaze can be formulated from ash and clay alone.

Salt glazing, used historically in Europe and America, involves throwing common salt (NaCl) into a kiln at 1200 degrees Celsius or above; the sodium vapor reacts with the silica in the clay body itself to form a thin, orange-peel-textured glass coating directly on the vessel surface. This technique requires kiln temperatures beyond basic pit firing.

Lead glazes must be avoided entirely. While lead oxide is an exceptionally effective low-temperature flux (producing glossy glazes at only 800 degrees Celsius), lead is a cumulative neurotoxin that leaches from glazed surfaces into acidic foods and beverages. Historical lead-glazed pottery caused widespread chronic poisoning. There is no safe way to use lead glaze on food or drink vessels. Do not use it.

Temper is non-plastic material added to clay to control shrinkage and improve thermal shock resistance. Common temper materials include:

- **Sand (quartz):** readily available, reduces shrinkage, but increases quartz inversion sensitivity
- **Grog (crushed fired clay, also called crusite):** the ideal temper — matches the thermal expansion of the clay body, reduces shrinkage by 2-5%, and improves thermal shock resistance. Crush failed pots to produce grog.
- **Shell (crushed freshwater mussel or snail):** calcium carbonate decomposes during firing to calcium oxide, acting as both temper and mild flux
- **Organic fiber (grass, straw, dung):** burns out during firing, leaving voids that arrest crack propagation. Used in cooking vessels across Africa and Polynesia for millennia.

A standard earthenware body for cooking vessels: 70-80% processed clay, 20-30% grog or sand temper by volume. For storage vessels where thermal shock is not a concern, temper can be reduced to 10-15%.

### Historical Precedent

The oldest confirmed pottery in the archaeological record comes from the Jomon culture of Japan, dated to approximately 16,500 years before the present — predating agriculture by thousands of years. These early vessels were used to process shellfish, nuts, and other wild foods, demonstrating that ceramics do not require agricultural settlement as a prerequisite.

By 7500 BCE, the settled community at Catalhoyuk in Anatolia was producing standardized pottery forms integrated into daily food processing and ritual life. Chinese potters developed high-fired stoneware by 1400 BCE, celadon glazes by 200 BCE, and true porcelain by 600 CE — a technological trajectory spanning two millennia of continuous refinement.

African traditions independently developed sophisticated firing techniques including the reduction-fired blackware of Nigeria and the large-vessel coil-building traditions of East Africa. Pre-Columbian potters in Mesoamerica and the Andes produced polychrome painted ware, stirrup-spout vessels, and ceramic musical instruments — all without the potter's wheel.

The universal pattern is unmistakable: wherever humans settle permanently, ceramics appear within one to two generations. The convergence of technique across isolated cultures — coil building, pit firing, slip decoration — reflects the constraints of the underlying physics. There are only so many ways to heat clay, and every culture discovers them.

This history carries a practical lesson. A single individual with access to clay, water, fuel, and fire can produce functional vessels within weeks of beginning to learn. No other material technology — metalworking, glassmaking, textile weaving — has such a low barrier to entry with such a high functional return.

## Core Principles

1. **Clay Characterization First.** Never build a vessel from untested clay. Perform the ribbon test, shrinkage test, and a test firing of small tiles before committing hours to forming a large pot. Clay that looks identical can behave completely differently under heat.

2. **Slow and Even.** Thermal gradient is the enemy. Every phase of the process — drying, heating through quartz inversion, peak firing, and cooling — must proceed slowly and uniformly. Rushing any stage produces cracks, warping, or explosive failure. Budget 2-4 weeks for drying and 24+ hours for a firing-and-cooling cycle.

3. **Temper Everything.** Pure clay shrinks 8-15% from wet to fired. Adding 20-30% temper (grog, sand, or shell) reduces shrinkage to 3-6%, dramatically lowering the risk of cracking during drying and firing. Every clay body you prepare should include temper.

4. **Form Follows Function.** A water storage jar needs thick walls, a narrow mouth to reduce evaporation, and a rounded base for strength. A cooking pot needs thin walls for heat transfer, wide mouth for access, temper for thermal shock resistance, and a flat base for stability on a hearth. Design each vessel for its intended use.

5. **Fire Together.** Firing is the most fuel-intensive step. A single pit firing consumes roughly 15 kg of hardwood regardless of whether it contains one pot or ten. Always accumulate enough bone-dry ware to fill a firing — batch processing saves fuel, labor, and time.

## Practical Implementation

### Clay Prospecting and Processing

Your first task is finding workable clay. Look below the topsoil layer — the dark, organic-rich surface — in river banks, road cuts, exposed ravines, construction excavations, and any place where subsoil is visible. Clay deposits appear as dense, smooth, often gray or red-brown material that feels slippery when wet and cracks into angular blocks when dry.

Perform three field tests on every candidate deposit:

**The Snake Bend Test.** Wet a handful of subsoil, work it until pliable, and roll it into a snake roughly 1 cm in diameter. Bend the snake into a ring. If it bends without cracking, the soil has high clay content and is worth processing. If it cracks or crumbles, the clay content is too low — move on.

**The Ribbon Test.** Squeeze a walnut-sized lump of wet clay between thumb and forefinger, pushing it out into a flat ribbon. Measure the length before it breaks under its own weight. A ribbon of 5 cm or longer indicates high plasticity suitable for vessel forming. Less than 2 cm indicates too much silt or sand for pottery without blending.

**The Shrinkage Test.** Form a flat tile approximately 1 cm thick. While still wet, scratch two parallel lines exactly 10 cm apart into the surface. Allow the tile to dry completely, then measure the distance between the lines. Repeat after test-firing. Total shrinkage from wet to fired should be 5-10% for a workable body. Above 12% indicates you need more temper.

**The Specific Gravity Test.** Weigh a small, dry clay sample, then submerge it in a known volume of water and measure the displacement. Dense clay (specific gravity 2.5-2.7) indicates low organic content and good firing properties. Light material (specific gravity below 2.0) indicates excessive organic matter that will burn out and leave a weak body.

Once you have identified a good deposit, process the raw clay:

1. **Slaking.** Break the raw clay into fist-sized chunks and submerge in a barrel or pit of water. Allow to soak for 24-48 hours, stirring vigorously several times. The clay will break down into a thin, muddy suspension called slip.

2. **Filtering.** Pour the slip through a scavenged window screen, woven fabric, or basket weave into a second container. Rocks, roots, pebbles, and coarse sand are caught by the screen. The pure clay particles, being less than 2 micrometers in size, pass through with the water.

3. **Settling.** Let the filtered slip sit undisturbed for 48-72 hours. Clay particles settle to the bottom, leaving clear water above. Carefully siphon or ladle off the clear water without disturbing the settled clay.

4. **Dewatering.** Scoop the thick clay sludge onto a porous surface — canvas stretched over a frame, a plaster bat, or a dry fired brick. Allow excess water to absorb and evaporate until the clay reaches the consistency of stiff bread dough.

5. **Wedging.** This is the most critical preparation step. Wedging removes trapped air bubbles that will expand explosively during firing. Use the spiral kneading method: push the clay forward with the heel of your palm, fold it back over itself, rotate 45 degrees, and repeat. A minimum of 100 spiral kneads is required. When you slice the clay mass in half with a wire and see no bubbles or voids in the cross-section, wedging is complete.

6. **Tempering.** Add grog (crushed fired pottery ground to sand-grain size) or clean sand at 20-30% by volume. Wedge the temper evenly throughout the body with an additional 30-50 kneads.

### Forming Methods

**Pinch Pot (starter exercise).** Begin here. Take a ball of prepared clay the size of a tennis ball. Press your thumb into the center, stopping 1 cm from the bottom. Pinch the walls outward between thumb and fingers, rotating the ball steadily, until the walls are 6-8 mm thick throughout. This teaches you the feel of the clay — how thin you can push it, how fast it dries, how it responds to pressure. Fire your first pinch pots as test pieces to evaluate your clay body before investing time in larger vessels.

**Coil Building.** This is your primary production method for the first several years. Roll prepared clay into coils (snakes) approximately 2 cm in diameter and as long as you can manage — 30-50 cm is typical. Flatten a ball of clay into a disc 1 cm thick for the base.

Score (scratch with a stick or shell) the edge of the base, moisten with slip, and lay the first coil around the perimeter. Press and blend the coil into the base so no seam remains visible on either the inside or outside surface. Stack successive coils, scoring and slipping each joint, blending inside and outside smooth with your fingers or a wooden rib tool.

Build walls to a uniform thickness of 6-10 mm. For vessels taller than 20 cm, allow the lower section to stiffen for 1-2 hours before adding upper coils to prevent the weight from causing collapse.

**Slab Building.** Roll or pat clay into flat slabs of uniform thickness (6-10 mm) using guide sticks as thickness gauges. Cut slabs to shape with a knife or wire. Join edges by scoring both surfaces, applying slip, pressing together, and reinforcing the interior joint with a thin coil blended smooth. Slab construction is ideal for flat-bottomed containers, rectangular storage boxes, tiles, and kiln furniture (shelves and supports for stacking ware during firing).

**Paddle-and-Anvil.** After rough-forming a vessel by coil or other method, thin and compress the walls by holding a smooth stone (the anvil) against the interior surface and striking the exterior with a flat wooden paddle. This Polynesian and Southeast Asian technique produces thin, dense, strong walls with excellent thermal shock resistance — ideal for cooking pots. The paddling also closes microscopic pores and aligns clay particles, improving the fired density of earthenware.

Note: the potter's wheel is a Year 3-5 technology. It requires a heavy flywheel, a vertical axle, and a bearing — components that depend on metalworking or precision woodworking. Cross-reference the Mechanical Power module (02_Mechanical_Power.md) for bearing and flywheel construction. Until then, coil building and paddle-and-anvil produce every vessel form you need.

### Drying Protocol

More pots are lost to improper drying than to improper firing. The goal is to remove all water — both the free water between clay particles and the pore water adsorbed on particle surfaces — slowly and evenly, so that shrinkage occurs uniformly across the entire vessel.

Dry all formed ware in shade, protected from wind and direct sun. For the first 3 days, loosely cover vessels with a damp cloth to slow surface evaporation and allow the interior moisture to equalize with the surface. After 3 days, remove the cloth but keep the ware in shade. Total drying time: 2-4 weeks depending on wall thickness, humidity, and temperature. In humid climates, drying may take longer; in arid climates, the danger is drying too fast, which makes covering even more important.

Design vessels for uniform wall thickness of 6-10 mm. Thick and thin sections in the same vessel dry at different rates, creating differential shrinkage stresses that produce cracks before the vessel ever sees a fire. Handles, lugs, and rims should be the same thickness as the vessel wall. Attach handles and lugs when the vessel body is leather-hard (firm but still damp) to ensure they shrink at the same rate as the body.

Test for dryness: hold the pot against your cheek. If it feels cool, moisture is still evaporating from the surface — the pot is not ready. A fully bone-dry pot feels room temperature against the skin. Only bone-dry ware goes into the fire. Any residual moisture will flash to steam during heating and blow the pot apart.

For critical vessels, a pre-heating step reduces risk further: place bone-dry ware near (not in) a fire or in direct sun for several hours before loading into the pit or kiln. This drives off any residual adsorbed moisture that the cheek test cannot detect.

### Pit Firing

You do not need a massive brick kiln to fire basic earthenware. A pit fire, the oldest firing technology, produces functional ceramic with nothing but a hole in the ground and fuel.

**Pit Preparation.** Dig a pit approximately 60 cm deep and 90 cm wide in cleared ground, well away from structures, dry grass, and overhead branches. The pit walls help retain heat and shield the fire from wind.

**Fuel Layering.** Line the bottom of the pit with 10 cm of dry sawdust or fine wood shavings. This provides a soft, slow-burning bed that cushions the pots and produces gradual initial heating.

On top of the sawdust bed, lay a 15 cm layer of dried animal dung cakes (cow, horse, or goat). Dung burns slowly and evenly, producing sustained moderate temperatures ideal for ramping through the quartz inversion zone.

**Loading.** Place bone-dry vessels upside-down or on their sides on the dung layer. Pots must not touch each other — leave 3-5 cm gaps on all sides. Nest smaller vessels inside larger ones to maximize capacity.

Surround and cover the pots with more dung cakes, filling all gaps so that no vessel is exposed directly to the heavy fuel above.

**Top Fuel.** Cover the dung layer with hardwood — split pieces 5-8 cm in diameter. This heavy fuel provides the peak temperature. Total fuel load: approximately 15 kg of hardwood plus 5-8 kg of dung for a single firing.

**Firing Sequence.** Light the top of the pile. The fire burns downward through the fuel layers, producing a gradual temperature ramp:

| Phase                  | Temperature   | Duration  | Rate              |
|------------------------|---------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Initial ramp           | 20-500 C      | 3-5 hrs   | ~100 C/hr         |
| Quartz inversion zone  | 500-600 C     | 2-3 hrs   | 50 C/hr (SLOW)    |
| Peak firing            | 700-900 C     | 1-2 hrs   | Hold at peak      |
| Smothering and cooling | 900 to ambient| 24 hrs    | Natural cool       |

**Smothering.** Once the heavy wood has burned to a bed of red-hot coals — visible as a uniform orange-red glow across the pit — bury the entire pit in loose earth, sand, or ash to smother the fire and trap the heat. Do not disturb for a full 24 hours. The slow cooling is as critical as the slow heating: the reverse quartz inversion during cooling at 573 degrees Celsius will crack any vessel that cools too fast.

**Expected Results.** Beginners should expect a 30-50% survival rate for their first several firings. With consistent practice in drying, fuel layering, and loading, survival rates improve to 80% or higher. Crush all broken pieces to produce grog for tempering future clay bodies — nothing is wasted.

### Updraft Kiln Construction

An updraft kiln is a Year 2+ technology that dramatically improves firing consistency, peak temperature, and survival rates compared to pit firing. The basic structure consists of three sections: a firebox where fuel is burned, a ware chamber above it where pots are stacked, and a chimney that draws hot gases upward through the ware.

```
         ___________
        |  CHIMNEY  |    <-- 2m tall, 15cm opening
        |  |     |  |
        |__|     |__|
   _____|___________|_____
  |                       |
  |    WARE CHAMBER       |    <-- 60cm x 60cm x 80cm
  |    (pots on shelves)  |        Fired brick walls 10cm thick
  |                       |
  |_______________________|
  |  |||  GRATE  |||      |    <-- Perforated shelf or bars
  |_______________________|
  |                       |
  |    FIREBOX            |    <-- 40cm x 40cm x 30cm
  |    (stoke fuel here)  |        Stoke hole on one side
  |_______________________|
```

Build the kiln from fired brick laid in clay mortar (unfired mud brick will not survive the temperatures). Wall thickness: minimum 10 cm, ideally 15 cm for insulation. The firebox has an opening on one side for stoking fuel.

A perforated grate (fired brick bars with 2-3 cm gaps) separates the firebox from the ware chamber, allowing hot gases to rise through while supporting the ware. The chimney, 2 m tall with a 15 cm opening, creates the draft that pulls air through the firebox and up through the ware. Taller chimneys produce stronger draft and higher temperatures; a damper (removable brick or clay plate) at the chimney top allows you to control airflow and temperature.

Advantages over pit firing:

- Repeatable temperature control — stoke more or less fuel to raise or lower temperature
- Higher achievable temperatures: 1000-1100 degrees Celsius versus 700-900 for pit firing
- Higher survival rates: 70-90% even for relative beginners
- Ability to use saggers (covered containers that protect ware from direct ash and flame contact)
- Capacity for reduction firing — restricting the air supply converts iron oxide from red (Fe2O3) to black (FeO), producing dark-bodied ware
- Fuel efficiency improves with insulated walls retaining heat between stokings

### Wood Ash Glaze

Earthenware fired below 900 degrees Celsius remains porous — adequate for dry storage but not for long-term liquid storage. Glazing renders vessels waterproof and food-safe using only materials available from your hearth.

1. **Collect ash.** Burn hardwood (oak, maple, ash, hickory) to complete white ash. Softwood ash contains less flux and is inferior. Collect ash from clean fires — no painted wood, no trash.

2. **Process ash.** Sift through the finest mesh available (window screen, then cloth) to remove charcoal fragments. Soak the sifted ash in water for 24 hours and stir, then allow to settle and pour off the water. This leaches out soluble potassium compounds that cause excessive bubbling during firing. Dry the washed ash.

3. **Formulate glaze.** Mix by dry volume: 40% processed wood ash, 40% finely ground dry clay (the same clay used for your vessels), 20% water. Blend thoroughly until smooth and the consistency of heavy cream.

4. **Apply.** Bisque-fire vessels first — a low firing to 600-700 degrees Celsius that hardens the ware enough to handle but leaves it porous enough to absorb glaze. Dip or pour the glaze slurry over the bisque-fired vessel, coating to a thickness of approximately 1 mm. Allow to dry.

5. **Glaze fire.** Fire the glazed ware to 1100 degrees Celsius or above in an updraft kiln. The ash flux melts and reacts with the silica in both the glaze mixture and the clay surface, forming a thin, glassy coating fused to the vessel. The result is a waterproof, food-safe glaze produced entirely from wood ash and clay — no manufactured chemicals required.

## Common Failure Modes

**1. Exploding Vessels**
Cause: Trapped moisture flashing to steam, or air bubbles sealed in the clay wall expanding with heat.
**Prevention:** Ensure all ware is fully bone-dry (2-4 weeks in shade, passes the cheek test). Wedge clay with a minimum of 100 spiral kneads and inspect cut cross-sections for voids before forming.

**2. Quartz Inversion Cracking**
Cause: Heating too rapidly through the 500-600 degrees Celsius zone, causing the sudden 2% expansion of the alpha-to-beta quartz transition to stress the vessel unevenly.
**Prevention:** Use slow-burning fuel (dung, sawdust) as the lower layers in a pit fire to ensure the ramp rate through this zone stays below 50 degrees Celsius per hour. In a kiln, reduce stoking during this phase.

**3. Dunting**
Cause: Cooling too rapidly through 573 degrees Celsius on the way down, causing the reverse beta-to-alpha quartz contraction to crack the vessel. Dunting cracks are typically clean, sharp-edged fractures.
**Prevention:** Do not open the pit or kiln until at least 24 hours after peak firing. Smothering with earth or sealing kiln openings traps residual heat and enforces slow cooling.

**4. Warping**
Cause: Uneven wall thickness causing differential shrinkage during drying or uneven heating during firing. One side of the vessel shrinks or expands more than the other, distorting the form.
**Prevention:** Maintain uniform wall thickness of 6-10 mm during forming. Dry slowly in shade, rotating the vessel periodically. Load the kiln so heat can circulate evenly around all surfaces.

**5. Under-firing**
Cause: The vessel never reached sintering temperature (minimum 600 degrees Celsius for earthenware). The clay particles never fused. The pot dissolves back into mud when wetted.
**Prevention:** Use sufficient hardwood fuel to achieve and sustain peak temperature. Do not smother the fire too early — wait until the entire fuel load has burned to a uniform bed of glowing coals. In a kiln, maintain peak stoking rate for at least 1 hour.

**6. Glaze Crawling and Crazing**
Cause: Thermal expansion mismatch between the glaze and the clay body. If the glaze has a higher coefficient of expansion than the body, it contracts more during cooling and crazes (develops a network of fine cracks). If lower, it may peel or crawl (bead up into droplets during firing).
**Prevention:** Use the same base clay in your glaze formula as in your vessel body — this ensures similar expansion coefficients. Adjust the ash-to-clay ratio: more clay increases the expansion coefficient, more ash decreases it. Test glaze formulas on tiles before applying to finished vessels.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Kaolinite**: The most common clay mineral, Al2Si2O5(OH)4, composed of alternating layers of silica tetrahedra and alumina octahedra that give clay its plate-like particle structure.
- **Plasticity**: The property of wet clay that allows it to be deformed without cracking and to retain its new shape. Caused by water films between plate-like clay particles.
- **Wedging**: A systematic kneading technique (spiral or ram's-head method) used to homogenize the clay body and expel trapped air bubbles before forming.
- **Slip**: Clay mixed with water to a pourable liquid consistency, used as a joining agent (scoring and slipping), a surface coating, or the basis for glaze formulation.
- **Engobe**: A slip with added colorants or flux, applied to the surface of an unfired or bisque-fired vessel for decorative or functional effect.
- **Temper**: Non-plastic material (sand, grog, shell, fiber) added to clay to reduce shrinkage, improve workability, and increase thermal shock resistance in the fired ware.
- **Grog**: Crushed, previously fired ceramic (also called crusite), ground to sand-grain size and used as temper. The ideal temper material because its thermal expansion matches the clay body.
- **Bone Dry**: The state of an unfired vessel from which all free and pore water has evaporated. A bone-dry pot feels room temperature, not cool, against the skin.
- **Quartz Inversion**: The sudden crystallographic phase change of silica (SiO2) from alpha-quartz to beta-quartz at 573 degrees Celsius, accompanied by an approximately 2% volume change that can crack vessels if heating or cooling is too rapid.
- **Sintering**: The process by which clay particles fuse at their points of contact under sustained heat (above 600 degrees Celsius), creating a rigid, permanent ceramic matrix without fully melting.
- **Vitrification**: The progressive formation of a glass phase within the clay body at high temperatures (above 1200 degrees Celsius), filling pores and producing a dense, nearly waterproof ceramic.
- **Bisque**: A preliminary low-temperature firing (600-700 degrees Celsius) that hardens unfired ware enough to handle and makes it porous enough to absorb glaze before the final glaze firing.
- **Kiln**: An enclosed structure designed to contain and control the heat of firing, achieving higher, more uniform, and more repeatable temperatures than open or pit firing.
- **Glaze**: A glass coating applied to a ceramic surface and fused by firing, composed of flux, alumina, and silica in balanced proportion. Renders the vessel waterproof and can be food-safe.
- **Dunting**: Cracking caused by thermal stress during cooling, specifically the contraction at the reverse quartz inversion (573 degrees Celsius) when the vessel cools too rapidly.
