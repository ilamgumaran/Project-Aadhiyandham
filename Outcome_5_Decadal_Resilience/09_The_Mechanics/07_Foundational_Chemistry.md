# Foundational Chemistry: Lye, Soap, Vinegar, and Essential Compounds

**Alignment:** Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

Chemistry is not an advanced luxury reserved for specialists with laboratory equipment. It is survival infrastructure. Soap prevents the fecal-oral disease transmission that kills more people in post-collapse scenarios than violence, exposure, or starvation combined. Vinegar preserves the autumn harvest through winter. Lye processes raw flax fiber into linen. Lime purifies drinking water and binds mortar into stone-hard walls. Tannin converts a raw animal hide — which would rot within days — into leather that lasts decades. Every one of these is a chemical process the settlement must perform reliably from the first year onward.

The good news is that every essential compound can be produced from materials available in any temperate settlement. Hardwood ash yields lye. Animal fat combines with lye to produce soap. Fruit ferments into vinegar. Limestone becomes quicklime and then slaked lime. Tree bark yields tannin. No industrial infrastructure is required, no rare minerals, no electrical power. These processes were performed by every household in pre-industrial societies for millennia. They were not specialist trades — they were common knowledge, as basic as cooking. That knowledge was lost within two generations of industrialization. This module restores it.

This module covers six foundational processes: lye extraction, soap manufacture, vinegar production, lime calcination, tannin extraction, and natural dye processing. Together they provide the chemical foundation for hygiene, food preservation, textile processing, leather production, construction, water purification, and material finishing. Master them, and the settlement commands an entire domain of capability that most modern people assume requires a factory.

> **DANGER**: Lye (KOH/NaOH) causes severe chemical burns to skin and eyes on contact. Quicklime (CaO) reacts violently with water, generating extreme heat sufficient to ignite dry materials. Never mix lye with aluminum — the reaction produces explosive hydrogen gas. Always wear eye protection and heavy gloves when handling any of these substances. Keep vinegar accessible at all times as a lye neutralizer. Work outdoors or in a well-ventilated dedicated space, never in living quarters.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Chemistry of Alkali: Lye from Ash

- Wood ash contains potassium carbonate (K2CO3, commonly called "potash") and smaller amounts of sodium carbonate (Na2CO3, "soda ash"). When water percolates through ash, it dissolves these salts, producing a solution of potassium hydroxide (KOH) — lye. The process is simple dissolution and mild chemical reaction, but the product is powerfully caustic.

- Hardwood ash contains 5-15% potash by weight; softwood ash contains only 2-5%. This is why hardwood ash from oak, hickory, ash, and maple is specified for lye-making. Softwood ash can be used in desperation, but requires far more ash per unit of usable lye, and the resulting solution is often too dilute for reliable saponification.

- Lye concentration is measured by density (specific gravity). The egg float test is a field-expedient densitometer: a fresh egg floats when the solution reaches approximately 1.05-1.10 specific gravity, corresponding to roughly 5-10% KOH concentration — the correct range for soap-making. If the egg sinks, the lye is too weak. If the egg floats high with a large area exposed above the surface, the lye is dangerously concentrated and should be diluted.

- Safety chemistry: KOH is a strong base with a pH of 13-14. It saponifies (dissolves) fats — including the fats in human skin. This is precisely why it causes chemical burns: it is literally making soap out of your flesh on contact. Acid neutralization is the immediate treatment: vinegar (acetic acid) neutralizes lye on contact, converting it to harmless potassium acetate and water. Always keep a bucket of vinegar within arm's reach when working with lye.

### The Chemistry of Saponification

- The core reaction: fat (a triglyceride molecule) + alkali (KOH or NaOH) yields soap (the potassium or sodium salt of fatty acids) + glycerin as a byproduct.

- Chemical equation: C3H5(OOCR)3 + 3KOH produces 3RCOOK + C3H5(OH)3, where R represents the hydrocarbon chain of the fatty acid.

- KOH derived from wood ash produces soft soap — a paste or jelly consistency suitable for general washing. NaOH, obtainable from mineral deposits or by reacting wood ash lye with slaked lime, produces hard bar soap that is easier to store and transport. Both are equally effective at destroying pathogens.

- The "trace" point is the critical milestone in soap-making. It occurs when the saponification reaction has progressed enough that the mixture thickens and a drizzle of soap batter leaves a visible trail on the surface before sinking back in. This indicates that the fat and lye have combined irreversibly — the mixture will become soap regardless of further stirring. Before trace, the mixture can separate back into fat and lye. After trace, the reaction is committed.

- Curing is not optional. Fresh soap still contains unreacted lye distributed throughout the bar, making it caustic to skin. Curing for 4-6 weeks allows complete saponification of remaining free lye and evaporation of excess water, producing a mild, hard, safe bar. Soap used before full curing will cause skin irritation or burns.

- How soap works at the molecular level: the soap molecule has a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and a lipophilic (fat-loving) tail. When soap contacts a pathogen, the lipophilic tails penetrate and disrupt the lipid membrane that forms the outer shell of bacteria and enveloped viruses. The membrane disintegrates. The hydrophilic heads then allow water to wash away the destroyed pathogen fragments. Soap does not merely rinse away germs — it chemically destroys them by tearing apart their structural membranes.

### The Chemistry of Fermentation: Vinegar

- Vinegar is acetic acid (CH3COOH) at 4-8% concentration in water, produced by acetobacter bacteria oxidizing ethanol: C2H5OH + O2 yields CH3COOH + H2O. The process is entirely biological — bacteria do the chemical work — but it requires correct conditions to succeed.

- Two-stage process: (1) yeast converts sugar to ethanol through anaerobic fermentation — this is ordinary alcohol production; (2) acetobacter bacteria convert the ethanol to acetic acid through aerobic fermentation, which requires oxygen exposure. The two stages have opposite oxygen requirements: alcohol fermentation needs minimal oxygen, while vinegar fermentation needs maximum oxygen. Understanding this distinction is essential.

- Any sugar source works as the starting material: fruit (apple cider vinegar, grape wine vinegar), honey (mead vinegar), grain (malt vinegar), or even tree sap. The sugar content determines the final alcohol content, which in turn determines the final acid strength. Fruits with 10-15% sugar produce the best results without requiring dilution.

- Vinegar applications: food preservation through pickling (maintaining pH below 4.6 prevents the growth of Clostridium botulinum and virtually all dangerous food-borne pathogens), cleaning, medical antisepsis (weak but useful for wound irrigation), fiber processing (neutralizing lye after textile treatment), and as a general-purpose chemical reagent throughout the settlement's chemistry operations.

### The Lime Cycle

The lime cycle is a closed chemical loop that has been exploited by builders for over 10,000 years. It proceeds in three stages, and cross-references Module 06 for detailed kiln construction and mortar formulation.

- **Calcination:** CaCO3 heated to 900 degrees Celsius yields CaO + CO2 — limestone becomes quicklime, releasing carbon dioxide. This requires a sustained high-temperature kiln fire maintained for many hours.

- **Slaking:** CaO + H2O yields Ca(OH)2 — quicklime reacts with water to produce slaked lime, releasing dangerous amounts of heat. This reaction is violently exothermic and can cause severe burns, generate scalding steam, or ignite nearby combustible materials. Always add quicklime to water slowly, never the reverse, and stand well back.

- **Carbonation:** Ca(OH)2 + CO2 yields CaCO3 + H2O — slaked lime absorbs carbon dioxide from the air over days, weeks, and months, slowly reverting to calcium carbonate — limestone. This final stage is why lime mortar hardens over time: it is literally turning back into stone. The process is called "curing" and continues for years, with mortar growing stronger the longer it is exposed to air.

Lime applications extend well beyond mortar:

- **Water purification:** adding slaked lime raises pH, killing waterborne pathogens
- **Soil amendment:** lime raises the pH of acidic soils, unlocking nutrient availability for crops
- **Nixtamalization:** treating corn with lime solution releases bound niacin (vitamin B3), preventing pellagra — a potentially fatal deficiency. Mesoamerican cultures practiced nixtamalization for over 3,500 years
- **Whitewash:** lime-water paint creates an antimicrobial wall coating that reflects light and resists mold
- **Hide processing:** lime solution removes hair from animal hides in preparation for tanning
- **Iron smelting:** lime serves as a flux, binding with impurities to form removable slag

### Tannin Chemistry

- Tannins are polyphenolic compounds found in high concentrations in the bark of oak (8-15% tannin by dry weight), chestnut (6-10%), hemlock (8-12%), and in the leaves of sumac (20-30%). They are also present in tea leaves, unripe persimmons, and acorns — anything that produces that characteristic mouth-puckering astringent sensation.

- Mechanism: tannin molecules form multiple hydrogen bonds with collagen fibers in animal hide, cross-linking them into a stable, rot-resistant matrix. Raw hide is collagen that bacteria can easily digest; tanned leather is the same collagen locked into a structure that resists bacterial decomposition for decades. The transformation is permanent and irreversible.

- Tannin concentration: a typical bark-water extract for tanning contains 5-10% tannin by weight. Preparing this extract requires soaking crushed bark in water for weeks, producing a dark brown liquor that serves as the tanning bath. The process is slow — full vegetable tanning takes weeks to months — but requires no equipment beyond containers and patience.

- Additional uses: water purification (tannins precipitate heavy metals and bind some organic contaminants), ink production (iron gall ink, made by combining tannin extract with iron sulfate, was the standard writing ink of Western civilization from the 5th to 19th century), and wood preservation (tannin-treated wood resists rot and insect damage significantly better than untreated wood).

### Natural Dyes and Pigments

Plant-based dyes available in temperate regions:

- Walnut hulls — brown to black
- Onion skins — gold to orange
- Pokeberry — red-purple, though fugitive without strong mordanting
- Indigo plant leaves — blue, requiring a specialized fermentation vat
- Weld — bright yellow, one of the most lightfast natural dyes
- Madder root — red, the most important historical red dye

Mordanting is the critical step that most beginners skip, leading to immediate disappointment. Most natural dyes do not bond directly to plant or animal fiber. A mordant — from the Latin "mordere," to bite — is a chemical applied to the fiber first that creates a molecular bridge between the fiber and the dye molecule. Common mordants include alum (potassium aluminum sulfate, extractable from certain clays), iron (produces darker, "saddened" shades), and tannin itself (which serves as both a mordant and a brown dye). Without proper mordanting, even brilliant dyes wash out within a few wearings.

Mineral pigments complement plant dyes for applications where liquid dye is impractical:

- Ochre (iron oxide) — red, yellow, and brown
- Charcoal — black
- Lime — white
- Copper carbonate (from weathered copper or bronze) — green-blue

These pigments are ground to fine powder and mixed with a binder (egg, oil, or lime water) for use as paint, pottery decoration, or textile printing paste.

## Core Principles

1. **The Ash Economy Principle** — Hardwood ash is the single most valuable "waste" product in the settlement. It is the source of lye for soap, fiber processing, and hide dehairing. It provides potash for glass-making and fertilizer. It contributes to pozzolanic cement formulations. NEVER discard hardwood ash. Collect it after every fire and store it dry in a dedicated weatherproof container. A settlement that throws away its ash is throwing away its chemistry. Assign responsibility for ash collection to a specific person or rotation to ensure none is lost.

2. **The pH Discipline Principle** — Every chemical process in this module revolves around pH control. Lye operates at high pH (13-14): it dissolves fats, removes hair from hides, and breaks down plant fibers. Vinegar operates at low pH (2-3): it preserves food, neutralizes lye spills, and cleans mineral deposits. Lime operates at high pH (12-13): it kills pathogens, processes corn, and hardens into mortar. Understanding pH as a single continuum from acid (0) to base (14) with neutral water at 7 is the most important chemistry concept the settlement can internalize. Every chemical problem is, at its root, a pH problem.

3. **The Safety-First Principle** — Lye, quicklime, and concentrated acids cause chemical injury that can permanently disable within seconds of contact. Establish a dedicated chemistry workspace AWAY from living quarters, food preparation, and children's areas. Stock neutralizers: vinegar for lye/lime exposure, lime water for acid exposure. Eye protection is MANDATORY — a single splash of lye to an unprotected eye causes permanent blindness. Train every adult in chemical safety and first-response procedures.

4. **The Stockpile Principle** — Chemical production is seasonal and batch-dependent. Soap requires rendered fat (available after autumn slaughter) and concentrated lye (requires dry-stored ash). Vinegar requires fruit (available at harvest). Tannin bark is best harvested in spring when sap loosens the bark. Produce in quantity during the appropriate season and stockpile for year-round use. A settlement of 50 people requires approximately 50-100 kg of soap per year (1-2 kg per person) — roughly one batch every two weeks.

5. **The Cascade Principle** — Chemical processes share inputs and outputs in a web of interdependence. Ash yields lye. Lye combines with fat to produce soap. Lye processes flax into linen. Lye dehairs hides. Dehaired hides go into tannin baths to become leather. Vinegar neutralizes lye residue on fiber. Vinegar preserves vegetables grown in ash-amended soil. Design chemistry operations as an integrated cascade where outputs feed inputs, not as isolated activities.

## Practical Implementation

### Lye Extraction

**Ash collection:** Collect only the white, thoroughly burned ash from hardwood trees — oak, hickory, ash, maple. Softwood ash (pine, fir, spruce) lacks sufficient potassium content and should not be used. Store ash dry and protected from rain in a dedicated weatherproof container until you have accumulated enough for a batch.

**Ash volume:** Approximately one barrel (100-120 liters) of tightly packed hardwood ash produces 20-30 liters of usable lye solution. This is enough lye for one large batch of soap.

**The leaching barrel:** Construct the apparatus by drilling a small drainage hole in the bottom of a wooden barrel or large bucket. Place a layer of clean gravel at the bottom, covered by a 5-centimeter layer of dry straw acting as a filter to prevent ash from clogging the drain. Pack the barrel tightly with hardwood ash to within a few inches of the top.

**The wash:** Slowly pour rainwater (naturally soft and mineral-free) over the ash. Do not flood it; pour in batches over 3-5 days, allowing each addition to percolate through before adding more. First runnings are the strongest; later runnings are progressively weaker. Collect all runnings in a single container, then boil down to concentrate if the combined solution is too weak.

**Testing:** The brown liquid that drips from the bottom of the barrel is raw lye. Test its strength with the egg float test: float a fresh, raw egg in its shell in the lye water. If the egg sinks, the lye is too weak — pour it back through a fresh batch of ash or boil it down further. If the egg floats with an area of shell roughly the size of a small coin exposed above the surface, the concentration is correct for soap-making.

**Storage:** Store finished lye in wooden, ceramic, or glass containers. NEVER use aluminum containers — lye reacts with aluminum to produce hydrogen gas, creating an explosion risk. Iron and steel containers are acceptable. Keep lye containers clearly labeled, sealed, and stored in the dedicated chemistry workspace away from children and food.

### Soap Manufacturing

**Lye preparation:** Prepare lye to the correct concentration using the egg float test as described above.

**Rendering tallow:** Separately, render tallow by cutting animal fat (beef, deer, or sheep) into small cubes and boiling them in a pot of water. Pure fat melts and floats to the surface, separating from meat tissue and connective material. Let the pot cool completely until the fat solidifies into a hard white disc on top of the water. Lift off this disc of tallow — this is your purified lipid source. If the tallow is not pure white, re-render it.

**Fat sources and their properties:**

- Tallow (beef or mutton fat) — hard, long-lasting bars with excellent cleaning power
- Lard (pig fat) — softer, milder soap, gentler on skin
- Plant oils (olive, sunflower, linseed) — liquid or very soft soap
- Mixed fats (e.g., 70% tallow, 30% lard) — combines hardness with mildness for a balanced everyday bar

**Ratio:** The approximate proportion is 1 part concentrated lye to 2-3 parts rendered fat by volume, though the exact ratio depends on lye concentration and fat type. The egg float test calibrates the lye side; experience calibrates the fat side. Err on the side of slightly more fat (called "superfatting"), which produces a milder bar with less risk of residual caustic lye.

**Saponification:** Melt the rendered tallow in a heavy cast-iron or steel pot. DO NOT use aluminum — lye reacts violently with aluminum, producing explosive hydrogen gas. Slowly and carefully pour in the concentrated lye water while stirring constantly in one direction with a wooden spoon. The mixture will heat from the exothermic chemical reaction. Keep stirring for 30-60 minutes until the mixture thickens to the consistency of warm pudding — this is "trace," the point at which a drizzle of batter leaves a visible trail on the surface before sinking back. Trace confirms that the fat and lye have combined irreversibly.

**Additives:** At trace, aromatic additives may be stirred in: crushed lavender, dried mint leaves, pine needles, or other fragrant plant material. These provide pleasant scent and mild antimicrobial properties.

**Molding and curing:** Pour the traced mixture into wooden molds lined with cloth for easy removal. Let it sit for 48 hours to harden, then cut into bars using a taut wire or thin knife. Bars MUST cure in a well-ventilated area for 4-6 weeks, turned occasionally to ensure even drying on all sides. The curing process ensures all remaining caustic lye reacts completely with the fat, producing a mild and safe finished bar.

Do not use soap before full curing — it will cause skin irritation or chemical burns from residual unreacted lye.

**Production schedule:** A single batch producing approximately 5 kg of finished soap requires one day of active work plus the curing period. Produce one batch per month to maintain an adequate stockpile for a settlement.

### Vinegar Production

**Step 1 — Make alcohol.** Crush fruit — apples, grapes, berries, or any available sugar-rich fruit — and place in a large container with an equal volume of water. Cover loosely to allow carbon dioxide to escape but prevent contamination by insects or debris. Wild yeast naturally present on fruit skins will begin fermentation within 1-3 days, visible as bubbling on the surface. Maintain temperature between 15-25 degrees Celsius. After 1-2 weeks, bubbling subsides and the liquid tastes dry and alcoholic — this is "hard cider" or fruit wine at approximately 5-8% alcohol.

**Step 2 — Convert to vinegar.** Strain the alcohol into a wide, shallow container — maximum surface area for oxygen exposure is critical, as acetobacter requires oxygen to function. Cover with cloth to exclude insects while allowing full air exchange. Acetobacter bacteria, naturally present on fruit skins and ubiquitous in the environment, will colonize the liquid surface, gradually forming a gelatinous mat called the "mother of vinegar." Over 4-8 weeks at 20-25 degrees Celsius, the bacteria convert ethanol to acetic acid.

**Step 3 — Test and store.** Finished vinegar should taste sharply sour with no residual alcohol flavor. If it smells like alcohol, fermentation is incomplete — allow more time with adequate air exposure. If it smells musty, moldy, or otherwise off, contamination has occurred — discard the batch and start over with clean equipment.

**Storage:** Sealed ceramic or glass containers. Avoid prolonged storage in metal, as acid-metal reactions produce off-flavors. Properly sealed vinegar does not spoil — it IS the preservative.

**Preserve the mother.** Save the gelatinous mother of vinegar culture: it can be transferred to new batches to inoculate them directly, accelerating future production from weeks to days. Keep the mother submerged in finished vinegar when not in use. Guard it as you would a sourdough starter — it is irreplaceable biological infrastructure.

### Tannin Extraction

**Bark collection:** Collect bark from tannin-rich species: oak (8-15% tannin by dry weight), chestnut (6-10%), sumac leaves (20-30%), or hemlock (8-12%). Spring is the optimal harvest time when rising sap loosens bark from the trunk, allowing it to be peeled in large sheets. Bark from recently felled timber is also excellent.

**Preparation:** Crush or chop the bark into small pieces to maximize surface area. The smaller the pieces, the faster and more complete the extraction will be.

**Extraction:** Soak the crushed bark in water in a wooden or ceramic container for 2-4 weeks, stirring occasionally. The water gradually darkens to a rich brown as tannins dissolve into solution.

Strain out the bark solids before use. The spent bark can be composted.

**Use:** The resulting brown liquid is tanning liquor — ready for use in leather production (cross-reference Module 05), wood preservation treatment, or iron gall ink production when combined with iron sulfate.

**Storage:** Tanning liquor can be concentrated by gentle boiling for long-term storage. Keep in sealed containers in a cool location. Fresh liquor is more effective than aged, so time production to coincide with hide availability.

### Natural Dye Processing

**Step 1 — Mordanting.** Mordant fiber or fabric before dyeing. Dissolve alum at a ratio of 10-15% of the dry weight of the fiber in warm water. Soak the fiber in this mordant bath for 1-2 hours, ensuring thorough saturation. Wring out excess mordant solution but do not rinse — proceed directly to the dye bath. Skipping this step is the most common cause of dye failure.

**Step 2 — Dye bath preparation.** Simmer plant material (walnut hulls, onion skins, madder root, or other dye source) in water for 1-2 hours to extract color. The water should take on a deep, rich hue. Strain out the plant material, leaving only the colored liquid.

**Step 3 — Dyeing.** Add the mordanted fiber to the dye bath and simmer for 1-2 hours, stirring gently to ensure even color penetration. Do not boil vigorously, as this can felt wool or damage plant fibers.

**Step 4 — Finishing.** Remove the dyed fiber, rinse in cool water until runoff runs clear, and dry in shade to prevent immediate sun-bleaching.

The mordant-dye bond strengthens as fiber dries and ages. Colors often shift slightly during drying — this is normal. Keep a sample card of each dye-mordant combination for reference.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Weak Lye** — The lye solution is too dilute for saponification. Soap remains liquid, fails to trace, or separates into layers of fat and water.
   - *Cause:* Insufficient ash quality, too much water, or ash that was exposed to rain before leaching.
   - *Prevention:* Always use the egg float test before beginning saponification. If the egg sinks, pass the liquid through a fresh batch of ash or boil it down to concentrate. Never proceed to soap-making with untested lye.

2. **Lye Burns** — Chemical burns from skin or eye contact with lye or quicklime. These substances dissolve tissue on contact and cause permanent damage, especially to eyes.
   - *Cause:* Inadequate protective equipment, careless pouring, or splashing during stirring.
   - *Prevention:* ALWAYS wear eye protection and heavy gloves. Keep a bucket of vinegar within arm's reach as an immediate neutralizer. For skin contact, flush with vinegar then copious water. For eye contact, flush with clean water for a minimum of 15 minutes and seek any available medical attention.

3. **Soap Will Not Trace** — The fat-lye mixture remains thin and liquid after extended stirring, refusing to thicken.
   - *Cause:* Lye too weak, temperature too low, or fat not fully rendered (water remaining in the tallow interferes with the reaction).
   - *Prevention:* Verify lye passes the egg float test, heat the mixture to 40-50 degrees Celsius during stirring, and ensure tallow is pure white and fully rendered. If tallow contains visible meat tissue or is gray or brown, re-render it before use.

4. **Vinegar Fails to Acidify** — The alcohol does not convert to vinegar, remaining alcoholic or developing off-flavors.
   - *Cause:* Ambient temperature below 15 degrees Celsius slows or halts acetobacter activity; insufficient oxygen exposure due to a container that is too narrow or improperly sealed; or alcohol concentration above 10% which inhibits acetobacter growth.
   - *Prevention:* Use wide shallow containers at 20-25 degrees Celsius, verify the cloth cover allows free air exchange, and dilute overly strong alcohol with water before beginning the vinegar stage.

5. **Aluminum Explosion** — Lye contacts an aluminum vessel, producing hydrogen gas that can ignite or explode. This is not a theoretical risk — it is a rapid, violent reaction that begins within seconds of contact.
   - *Cause:* Using salvaged aluminum cookware for lye work, or failing to identify aluminum vessels in the settlement's equipment.
   - *Prevention:* NEVER use aluminum containers, pots, or utensils for any process involving lye. Use only wood, ceramic, glass, stainless steel, or cast iron. Clearly mark all aluminum vessels in the settlement and store them physically separated from the chemistry workspace.

6. **Dye Fading** — Natural dye washes out of fabric within a few uses, leaving the garment pale and blotchy.
   - *Cause:* Insufficient or entirely absent mordanting before dyeing. The dye was deposited on the fiber surface without chemical bonding and simply rinses away.
   - *Prevention:* Always mordant fiber in an alum bath before dyeing. Alum is the most accessible mordant — extractable from certain clay deposits or scavengeable from industrial-era sources. Never skip the mordanting step regardless of how vivid the dye bath appears.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Lye** — An alkaline solution of potassium hydroxide (KOH) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) produced by leaching water through hardwood ash. The essential reagent for soap-making, fiber retting, and hide dehairing.

- **Saponification** — The chemical reaction between a fat (triglyceride) and an alkali (KOH or NaOH) that produces soap (fatty acid salt) and glycerin as a byproduct.

- **Trace** — The point during saponification when the mixture thickens enough that drizzled batter leaves a visible trail on the surface before sinking. Indicates irreversible combination of fat and lye.

- **Tallow** — Rendered animal fat, purified by boiling and cooling to separate pure lipid from meat tissue and water. The primary fat source for soap production.

- **Mother of vinegar** — The gelatinous mat of acetobacter bacteria that forms on the surface of fermenting vinegar. A living culture that can be transferred to inoculate and accelerate new batches.

- **Mordant** — A chemical substance (alum, iron, tannin) applied to fiber before dyeing that creates a molecular bond between the fiber and the dye, making color permanent and wash-resistant.

- **Potash** — Potassium carbonate (K2CO3), the water-soluble alkaline salt extracted from hardwood ash. The precursor to lye and a valuable trade commodity in pre-industrial economies.

- **Quicklime** — Calcium oxide (CaO), produced by calcining limestone at approximately 900 degrees Celsius. Reacts violently and exothermically with water. Handle with extreme caution.

- **Slaked lime** — Calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)2), the product of quicklime reacted with water. Used for mortar, whitewash, water purification, nixtamalization, and hide processing.

- **Nixtamalization** — The process of treating corn with slaked lime solution to release bound niacin (vitamin B3), preventing the deficiency disease pellagra. Practiced by Mesoamerican cultures for over 3,500 years and essential for any settlement relying on corn as a staple grain.

- **Acetobacter** — The genus of aerobic bacteria that converts ethanol to acetic acid in the presence of oxygen. The biological agent responsible for vinegar production.

- **Hydrophilic** — Water-attracting. Describes the polar head of a soap molecule that allows water to carry away disrupted pathogen material.

- **Lipophilic** — Fat-attracting. Describes the nonpolar tail of a soap molecule that penetrates and disrupts the lipid membranes of bacteria and viruses.

- **pH** — The measure of hydrogen ion concentration in a solution. Scale runs from 0 (strong acid) through 7 (neutral water) to 14 (strong base). The single most important measurement concept in settlement chemistry.

- **Iron gall ink** — Permanent black-purple ink produced by combining tannin extract with iron sulfate. The standard writing ink of Western civilization from the 5th to 19th century, noted for its permanence and resistance to fading.
