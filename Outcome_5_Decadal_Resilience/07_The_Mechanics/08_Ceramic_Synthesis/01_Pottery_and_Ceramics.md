## 2. Sourcing and Processing Raw Clay

Earth is not immediately clay; it must be refined.

### 2.1. Step-by-Step Processing:
1. **The Soil Test:** Dig below the topsoil (which contains organic matter). Take a handful of subsoil, wet it, and roll it into a snake. If you can bend the snake into a circle without it cracking, the soil is high in clay.
2. **Slaking (Wet Processing):** Dump the raw, rocky clay into a large barrel of water. Stir it violently until it becomes a thin, muddy soup (called "slip").
3. **Filtering:** Pour the slip through a scavenged window screen or woven fabric into a second barrel. The heavy rocks, roots, and sand will be caught by the screen, leaving pure, microscopic clay particles suspended in the water.
4. **Settling:** Let the second barrel sit completely still for 3 days. The pure clay will settle to the bottom. Carefully siphon off the clear water on top.
5. **Drying & Wedging:** Scoop out the thick clay sludge and lay it on a porous surface (like canvas or a dry brick) to absorb the excess water until it reaches the consistency of dough. Vigorously knead (wedge) the clay to remove all microscopic air bubbles.

## 3. Forming and Zero-Tech Firing (The Pit Kiln)

Unlike brick-making, which is thick and structural, thin-walled pottery is incredibly fragile during the firing process and requires precise thermal management.

### 3.1. The Coil Building Method
1. **The Base:** Flatten a small ball of clay into a disc for the base of the pot.
2. **The Coils:** Roll the remaining clay into long, even "snakes" or coils. 
3. **Stacking:** Score (scratch) the edge of the base, wet it slightly (slip), and lay the first coil around the perimeter. Press and blend the coil into the base so no seam is visible. Stack coil upon coil to build the walls of the vessel, blending the inside and outside smooth.
4. **Bone Dry:** The finished pot must dry slowly in the shade for at least two weeks until it is "bone dry" (feels room temperature, not cool to the touch).

### 3.2. The Pit Fire
You do not need a massive brick kiln to fire basic earthenware.
1. **The Pit:** Dig a shallow hole, 2 feet deep and 3 feet wide.
2. **The Bed:** Line the bottom of the pit with 4 inches of dry sawdust or dried animal dung. This provides a soft, slow-burning bed that prevents the pots from cracking against hard dirt.
3. **Loading:** Place the bone-dry pots into the pit. They must not touch each other. 
4. **The Cover:** Carefully cover the pots completely with dried dung cakes, small twigs, and then larger pieces of hardwood on top.
5. **The Slow Burn:** Light the top of the pile. Let the fire slowly burn *downward* into the pit over several hours. This gradual heating prevents thermal shock. 
6. **The Smother:** Once the heavy wood has burned down to a massive bed of red-hot coals (around 800°C / 1400°F), bury the entire pit in loose dirt to smother the fire and trap the heat. Leave it buried for a full 24 hours. When you dig it up, the clay will have chemically transformed into hard ceramic.

## 4. 🔬 Scientific Validation & Research Context
*   **The Quartz Inversion:** Ceramic science dictates that at exactly 573°C (1063°F), the silica crystals within the clay undergo a sudden, violent expansion (the Quartz Inversion). If the heat of the fire fluctuates rapidly during this precise window, the pot will instantly shatter. The slow, smothered, downward-burning method of a Pit Fire ensures the temperature rises steadily through this critical threshold without spiking.

## 5. 🚀 Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 5.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Phase 1: The Soil Survey:** Do not attempt to make a pot yet. Walk the perimeter of your settlement, specifically checking river banks, exposed ravines, or road cuts. Gather 5 different samples of subsoil and perform the "snake bend test" to identify the richest clay deposit.
*   **Phase 2: The Pinch Pot:** Before trying the coil method, simply take a ball of processed clay, stick your thumb in the center, and pinch the walls outward to form a small, crude bowl. Let it dry completely and attempt a test fire in the coals of your normal cooking hearth to see if the clay survives the heat without exploding.

### 5.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes
*   **Exploding Pots:** If your pot explodes into shrapnel during the fire, it either had a microscopic air bubble trapped in the clay wall (improper wedging), or it was not 100% bone dry. 
*   **Pots Dissolving in Water:** If a fired pot turns back into mud when you pour water into it, the fire never reached the minimum temperature required (around 600°C) to permanently fuse the silica. Use harder wood and do not smother the fire too early.
