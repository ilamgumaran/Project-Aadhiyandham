# 01: Geography, Topography, and Resource Requirements

**Purpose:** To define the absolute physical constraints a location must meet regarding landmass, elevation, layout, and water availability to sustain the target population indefinitely.

---

## 1. Elevation & Coastline Constraints
To ensure long-term resilience against worst-case climate projections over a 300-year timeline (including complete polar ice melt and extreme weather events), coastal and low-lying areas must be strictly filtered out.

* **Minimum Safe Elevation:** > 100 meters (328 feet) above current sea level. This accounts for maximum projected sea-level rise through the year 2300, plus massive extreme storm surge buffers.
* **Coastal Distance:** Minimum 10-20 miles inland. This prevents groundwater contamination from saltwater intrusion as oceans rise, and mitigates the worst impacts of coastal super-storms.

---

## 2. Land Area Requirements (Carrying Capacity)
The amount of land required depends heavily on diet, agricultural methods, and energy needs. A completely closed-loop system requires significantly more land than one reliant on external inputs (like imported fertilizer or feed). 

### The Acreage Formula
To calculate the total land required for the settlement, use the following baseline variables per person. 
*(Note: These are conservative estimates for a low-fossil-fuel, self-sufficient model).*

* $A_f$ (Crop Area): ~0.5 acres per person (intensive vegetable, grain, and root crop production).
* $A_l$ (Livestock/Grazing Area): ~2.0 acres per person (assuming dairy, eggs, and occasional meat via rotational grazing).
* $A_m$ (Materials & Fuel): ~1.5 acres per person (managed timber for building, firewood, coppicing, and craft materials).
* $A_e$ (Ecological Buffer & Expansion): ~1.0 acre per person (wetlands, wildlife corridors, and space for the population to double without crisis).

**Total Area Calculation:**
$$Total\ Area = P \times (A_f + A_l + A_m + A_e)$$
*(Where $P$ is the target population).*

**Example for a baseline population of 50 people:**
$$Total\ Area = 50 \times (0.5 + 2.0 + 1.5 + 1.0) = 250\ acres$$


*Reference: When evaluating topography, ensure the land allows for efficient "zoning" (e.g., intensive daily tasks near the center, wild foraging and timber at the edges).*

---

## 3. Hydrology and Water Needs
Access to independent, naturally replenishing freshwater is the single most critical geographic feature. The location must support drinking, sanitation, and heavy agricultural use.

### Water Demand Baseline
* **Human Consumption & Hygiene:** ~15 gallons per person, per day.
* **Livestock:** ~10-30 gallons per large animal (cow/horse), per day.
* **Agriculture:** Highly variable by climate, but assume ~3,000 to 4,000 gallons per acre, per week during dry growing seasons.

### Required Water Sources (Redundancy is Key)
A viable location must have at least **two** of the following three reliable sources:
1. **Surface Water:** A year-round creek, river, or deep lake (fed by springs or rain, not shrinking glaciers).
2. **Groundwater:** High-yield aquifers accessible via well. (Must check historical well-depth data to ensure the water table is not dropping).
3. **Rainwater Catchment:** Sufficient annual rainfall to capture and store. 

**Rainwater Catchment Formula:**
To calculate how much water a specific roof or catchment surface can generate:
$$Catchment\ (gallons) = Area\ (sq\ ft) \times Rainfall\ (inches) \times 0.623$$


*Reference: Look for properties situated well within a healthy watershed, ideally at the middle-to-upper elevations of a valley to ensure gravity-fed water access while avoiding flash-flood zones at the very bottom.*

---

## 4. Geological Features & Microclimates
Beyond flat space and water, the physical shape of the land dictates survivability and energy efficiency.

* **Solar Aspect:** In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing slopes are critical for maximizing solar energy generation and extending the agricultural growing season. (North-facing in the Southern Hemisphere).
* **Natural Defenses:** Look for topography that creates a natural windbreak against prevailing winter winds (e.g., a ridge or dense forest to the north). 
* **Building Materials:** The geology should ideally provide on-site construction materials to reduce import dependency:
    * Clay-heavy subsoil (for bricks, cob, or pond sealing).
    * Surface rock/stone (for foundations and retaining walls).
    * Healthy, mature timber stands.

## 5. Access, Tenure, and the People Already There

Every criterion above is *physical* — elevation, water, soil, geology. But a site's terrain being ideal says nothing about whether a settlement can lawfully reach it, occupy it, and remain — and this Atlas, honestly, evaluates the land and is largely silent on the law. That silence is a real gap the reader must fill per site:

*   **Ownership and legal path.** Almost every Atlas location sits within a functioning country with borders, land law, and owners. Under the most likely future — society keeps working — settling means *lawful acquisition*: purchase, lease, tenure, or residency, with real costs and legal requirements this Atlas does not cover. A gorgeous site with no lawful path is a daydream while the world still functions.
*   **Jurisdiction and protection status.** Several candidate regions are national parks, reserves, or protected areas where settlement is simply prohibited. Check before investing hope.
*   **Existing occupancy.** Land that looks empty on a terrain map usually is not. In any slow-decay scenario, the people already there remain — with the decisive local advantage — so the realistic paths are lawful coexistence or purchase, or choosing elsewhere; never assumed vacancy, which is how arrivals get into conflict they cannot win.
*   **Borders and transit.** Reaching a foreign site may cross controlled borders; fold that into the [journey plan](../01_The_Journey/00_Before_Departure/01_Rationale_and_Importance.md).

**Use this Atlas as a terrain shortlist, not a destination list.** The physical evaluation narrows the world to places that *could* sustain a settlement; the access-and-tenure question — answered by you, for your circumstances — decides which of those you can lawfully and peacefully make your own.
