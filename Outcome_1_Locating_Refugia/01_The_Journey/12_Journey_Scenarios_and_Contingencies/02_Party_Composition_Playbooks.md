**Alignment:** Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

> **Caution:** The tables below are planning baselines drawn from load-carriage research and long-trail data. Your party's real numbers will differ. Run a loaded rehearsal walk of at least 8 km before departure and replace every estimate here with what you measured. The rehearsal is the module.

## 1. Why This Is Important

The [Emergency Family Transit Protocol](../07_Emergency_Family_Transit_Protocol.md) establishes the physiology of mixed-group movement — the 50% Rule, the Guardian role, child pacing. This playbook extends it sideways: the same journey is a *different expedition* depending on who walks it. A fit solo traveler, four able adults, and a family spanning ages 4 to 74 differ not by degree but by kind — in speed, in vulnerability, in decision-making, and in how they should respond when things go wrong.

Historical movement data makes the differences concrete. Military route-march doctrine plans 20–32 km/day for conditioned adults under ~20 kg loads. Refugee-column studies and pilgrimage records show mixed-age civilian groups sustaining 8–15 km/day — roughly *half* — with rest days forced every 3–5 days. Planning a family journey on adult numbers is the single most common and most dangerous journey-planning error: it produces food budgets that run out and morale collapses timed exactly to the hardest terrain.

## 2. Expected Outcome

A one-page **Party Profile** for your specific group, containing: realistic daily range, total journey time with rest days, load assignment per person, named roles, and a decision protocol everyone accepted *before* departure. Time to produce: one evening of discussion plus one rehearsal walk.

## 3. The Comparative Baseline

| Factor | Solo | 2–6 Able Adults | With Children | With Elders / Infirm |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Planning range/day** | 20–30 km | 15–25 km | 8–15 km | 6–12 km |
| **Rest days** | 1 per 5–6 | 1 per 4–5 | 1 per 3–4 | 1 per 2–3 |
| **Greatest strength** | Speed, invisibility | Load capacity, watch rotation, redundancy | Morale anchor, long-term purpose | Knowledge, judgment, calm |
| **Greatest vulnerability** | Injury = death; no watch during sleep | Authority disputes | Pace ceiling, illness sensitivity, noise | Joint/cardiac limits, thermoregulation, medication dependence |
| **Dominant failure mode** | Overconfidence → injury | Split over route/pace | Pushing adult pace | Concealing decline until crisis |
| **Security posture** | Pure avoidance | Deterrence by numbers | Avoidance + sympathy asset* | Avoidance + negotiation asset* |

\* In most degraded-society encounters recorded historically, visible children and elders *reduce* perceived threat and often invite aid rather than predation — but they also mark the party as unable to flee. See [Low-Profile Travel](04_Low_Profile_Travel_and_Encounter_Protocols.md) for how to use this honestly.

## 4. Playbook A — The Solo Traveler (தனி பயணி)

Solo travel trades every group strength for two assets: speed and invisibility. The playbook maximizes those assets and manages the one unpayable cost — *there is no one to carry you.*

*   **Margin doctrine.** Where a group accepts a risk at 1-in-100 (a steep scree crossing, a swollen ford), the solo traveler must decline it. Alone, a sprained ankle 30 km from water is not an inconvenience; it is the whole story. Rule of thumb: **halve your acceptable risk, double your water margin.**
*   **The sleep problem.** No watch rotation exists. Mitigations, in order of value: (1) camp *concealed* rather than defended — 200 m+ off any path, no fire after dark or a Dakota fire hole only, cold camps in inhabited country; (2) natural alarm perimeter — dry brush, gravel approaches; (3) sleep before midnight, move at first light — most human activity on routes concentrates after mid-morning.
*   **The message chain.** Before departure, leave your route, alternates, and expected arrival window with someone at both ends if any communication exists. You are creating the only rescue trigger you will have.
*   **Load.** 20% of body weight is the ceiling (see [Essential Packing List](../08_Essential_Packing_List.md)); solo travelers should hold to 15% — you cannot redistribute a heavy pack to a partner when a knee begins to fail, and knee failure is the classic solo endgame.
*   **Psychology.** Expect the third-to-fifth day to be the psychological trough (documented consistently in solo long-trail journals): motivation dips as novelty fades and fatigue arrives. Pre-decide your response — a fixed daily walking ritual and a written evening log. Do not make route decisions in the trough.

## 5. Playbook B — Able Adults (2–6)

The strongest configuration — *if* it solves the one problem strength creates: **coordination**.

*   **Roles, assigned by evidence, not status.** Navigator (best map-and-terrain record on the rehearsal walk), Medic (most training, however little), Quartermaster (food/water rationing and kit audit), Rear Guard (most patient observer). Rotate physically exhausting duties; do *not* rotate Navigator and Medic — continuity beats fairness where error compounds.
*   **Decision protocol — decide how you decide, before you must.** The recommended default, proven in mountaineering parties:
    1.  Routine decisions (when to rest, campsite among adequate options): **the role-holder decides**, no discussion required.
    2.  Route and risk decisions: brief structured input — each adult states their read in one minute, *most cautious view is adopted* unless the Navigator can state a concrete reason it is wrong. This "most conservative response" rule is standard avalanche-country doctrine because groups otherwise talk each other into shared risks no individual would take (risky-shift effect).
    3.  Deadlock: pre-name one **tie-breaker** per domain (route → Navigator; medical → Medic; security → Rear Guard). Deadlock without a named tie-breaker is how parties split on day nine.
*   **The split question.** Splitting a healthy party is almost always wrong; the historical record of separated sub-groups is grim. The two legitimate exceptions: (a) a static injured member + fetch-water/help sortie with a fixed return time; (b) a final short-range scout at arrival (see [Arrival Protocol](05_Arrival_Protocol_First_72_Hours.md)). Any split requires: fixed rendezvous point *and* time, plus a fallback rendezvous 24 h later, agreed aloud twice.
*   **Watch rotation.** With 4+ adults, 2-hour night watches cost each person one watch and preserve everyone's sleep architecture; with 2–3, use a concealment-first camp (solo doctrine) and accept partial watches only in genuinely contested country.

## 6. Playbook C — Traveling with Children

The base physiology and psychology live in the [Emergency Family Transit Protocol](../07_Emergency_Family_Transit_Protocol.md) (50% Rule, Discovery Bag, Guardian role). This playbook adds the decision layer:

*   **The pace ceiling is a hard constraint, not a target to negotiate.** Write the day's distance the night before, based on the *child's* previous day. A child who covered 10 km cheerfully yesterday can cover 10 km today; the adult wish for 14 km has no vote. Attempting to "make up time" through a child is how families convert one lost day into four (illness cascade).
*   **Illness triage trigger.** Children dehydrate and hypothermia faster than adults (higher surface-to-mass ratio) and mask exhaustion until sudden collapse. Pre-decided trigger: fever, vomiting, or refusal to eat = **halt day**, full stop, regardless of schedule. One halt day early beats five forced later.
*   **The carry budget.** One adult must always be *unloaded enough to carry a child* (≤10% body-weight pack). In a two-adult family this means the other adult and the travois absorb the difference — plan the load table this way from the start rather than discovering it on day three.
*   **Authority clarity for the children.** Children under stress need one voice. The Guardian speaks; other adults route instructions through the Guardian. Contradictory adult instructions are, per child-development field studies in displacement settings, a leading proximate cause of child panic behaviors (bolting, freezing, night terrors).

## 7. Playbook D — Traveling with Elders and the Infirm

*   **Honest capacity assessment — before departure, with dignity.** The single most dangerous dynamic is an elder concealing decline to avoid burdening the family (documented across evacuation literature). Counter it structurally, not by vigilance: the daily schedule includes a private two-minute check-in between the Medic and each elder — joints, chest, feet, bowels — framed as routine data collection for the whole party, because it is (adults conceal too).
*   **Medication cliff.** Inventory every medication with a hard dependency (cardiac, insulin, thyroid, seizure). For each: days of supply, taper feasibility (some drugs cannot be stopped abruptly — beta-blockers, benzodiazepines, steroids), and the [Temperate Antibiotic Kit](../../../Outcome_2_Biological_Sovereignty/05_Health_and_the_Body/03_Temperate_Antibiotic_Kit.md) has *no substitute for most of them*. This inventory can change the route (shorter), the timing (sooner, while supply lasts), or the plan itself (a nearer refugium). Face that trade explicitly; the alternative is facing it at kilometer 80.
*   **Terrain translation.** Elders' limiting factor is rarely distance — it is *grade and footing*. 12 km on a rail-grade or river-terrace route costs less than 6 km on broken slope. When planning with elders, route flatness outranks route shortness (see [Non-Motorized Transit Routes](../04_Terrestrial_Navigation/04_Non_Motorized_Transit_Routes.md)).
*   **The travois is for cargo — until it is for people.** Rig every travois so it can convert to a person-carry within ten minutes (longer platform lashing points, padding roll kept accessible). A party that can carry a member at 3 km/day has an option; a party that cannot has an ultimatum.
*   **What elders carry.** Not weight — *function*: navigation memory, plant knowledge, watch-sitting (stationary observation suits them), morale, and the settlement's institutional memory. Assign these explicitly. An elder with a named role walks farther than one who believes they are cargo; this is not sentiment, it is the documented link between perceived burden and effort tolerance.

## 8. Building the Party Profile (worked example)

```text
PARTY PROFILE — example: 2 adults, 1 child (9), 1 elder (71)
============================================================
Governing playbooks: C + D (most restrictive governs)
Planning range: 8 km/day  | Rest: 1 day per 3
Route: 118 km river-terrace alternate (flat) over
       94 km ridge direct (steep)  → elder constraint
Journey budget: 15 walking days + 5 rest = 20 days
Food budget: 20 days x 4 persons x 1.2 safety = 96 person-days
ROLES  Navigator: Adult A   Medic+Guardian: Adult B
       Quartermaster: Elder    Scout-of-camp: Child (supervised)
DECISIONS  Routine: role-holder | Risk: most-cautious-wins
       Tie-break: route→A, medical→B, security→Elder
TRIGGERS   Child fever/vomiting → halt day
       Elder chest pain/joint failure → halt + reassess route
       2 days behind schedule → shift to cached-resupply alternate
       (NOT: walk faster)
```

The last line is the heart of the module: **schedule pressure is absorbed by the route and the food margin, never by the bodies.**

## 9. Common Failure Modes

*   **Planning on the strongest member's numbers.** The party moves at its most restrictive playbook. Prevention: the rehearsal walk, timed, fully loaded, with everyone.
*   **Unnamed authority.** "We'll decide together" works until fatigue, then becomes the crack the party splits along. Prevention: the decision protocol in Section 5, written and spoken aloud before departure.
*   **The concealment spiral (elders and adults alike).** Members hide blisters, chest tightness, diarrhea to avoid slowing the group — until the concealed problem halts it entirely. Prevention: routine daily body check-ins for *everyone*, so disclosure is data, not confession.
*   **Making up time.** Two days behind schedule is a route/rationing problem; treating it as a pace problem converts it into a medical one. Prevention: pre-decide the schedule-slip response (alternate route, ration shift) before departure.
*   **Splitting without a double rendezvous.** A single missed rendezvous with no fallback is how parties lose members permanently. Prevention: every split names two rendezvous (point + time, then fallback point + time), spoken back by both sub-groups.

## 10. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Double Rendezvous:** The two-stage meeting plan (primary and 24-hour fallback) required before any party split.
*   **Medication Cliff:** The date on which a hard-dependency medication runs out, after which the carrier's capability changes category.
*   **Most-Conservative-Response Rule:** Group decision doctrine adopting the most cautious member's read on risk decisions unless concretely refuted; the antidote to risky-shift.
*   **Pace Ceiling:** The hard daily-distance limit set by the most restrictive member; a constraint, not a negotiating position.
*   **Party Profile:** The one-page pre-departure document fixing pace, roles, loads, triggers, and decision protocol for a specific group.
*   **Risky Shift:** The documented tendency of groups to accept risks larger than any individual member would accept alone.
