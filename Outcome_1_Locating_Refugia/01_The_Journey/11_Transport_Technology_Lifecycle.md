# Transport Technology Lifecycle: What Breaks, When, and What Replaces It

**Alignment:** Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

The existing Journey framework assumes motorized transport is already gone and jumps directly to foot travel with travois. But collapse is not binary — it is a gradient. Vehicles do not stop working on the same day. Fuel depletes unevenly. Electric vehicles outlast gasoline ones in specific ways but fail catastrophically in others. A community that understands the **degradation sequence** of each transport technology can extract months or years of additional mobility from systems that others abandon prematurely.

Equally, a community that clings to a vehicle past its useful life wastes time maintaining a dead machine when its members should be walking. The discipline this module teaches is therefore neither optimism nor fatalism but timing: knowing exactly what transport mode to ride right now given what still functions, knowing the threshold at which to abandon it, and knowing what mode inherits the journey in the next phase. The output is a single decision framework that answers three questions at any moment during or after collapse: (a) what transport technology to use **right now** given what still works, (b) **when** to abandon it, and (c) **what replaces it** in the next phase.

> **CAUTION**: Do not waste weeks attempting to repair a modern engine that requires a part you cannot fabricate — electronic sensors, fuel injectors, or moulded rubber hoses. Every day spent reviving a dead machine is a day not spent walking toward the refugium. Set the abandonment threshold in advance and honour it.

> **DANGER**: An electric vehicle below 20% charge with no charging source is a trap. Do NOT drain a lithium traction battery to zero — a fully depleted lithium battery may never recover even if power returns later. Abandon the EV while it still has reserve, and strip it for parts.

## Theoretical Foundation

Transport after industrial collapse is governed by a single first principle: **a machine survives exactly as long as its rarest consumable.** A gasoline engine is a marvel of metallurgy, but it is hostage to a refined hydrocarbon that begins oxidising the day it leaves the refinery. The American Petroleum Institute documents that unstabilised gasoline degrades significantly within 3-6 months as its volatile light fractions evaporate and the remaining compounds oxidise into gum and varnish that coat injectors and carburetor jets until fuel can no longer atomise. Diesel resists this longer — 6-12 months — but trades chemical oxidation for biological spoilage: water condensation in the tank feeds algae and bacteria that clog filters and ultimately seize the injection pump. The engine block may be flawless and still the vehicle is dead, because the fuel that animates it has rotted.

The bicycle escapes this trap entirely, and the reason is energetic. Wilson and Schmidt, in *Bicycling Science* (MIT Press, 3rd ed.), establish that a human on a bicycle is **3-5 times more energy-efficient per unit distance than the same human walking**, which makes the bicycle the most efficient human-powered transport mechanism ever measured. It owes this to two facts: rolling friction is far cheaper than the repeated lifting of the body's centre of mass that walking demands, and the drivetrain converts large slow leg movements into fast wheel rotation with minimal loss. Crucially, every part of that drivetrain — bearings, chain, brakes, spokes — is mechanically simple enough to repair in the field with hand tools, and a steel frame can be re-welded at a forge. The bicycle has no rare consumable except rubber, and rubber can be patched, wrapped, or improvised.

Animals occupy a different category altogether: they are the only transport technology that **manufactures its own fuel and reproduces its own replacements.** A horse, mule, or donkey converts grass — a resource that grows back — into locomotion, and a breeding pair produces more transport units without a factory. This is why animal transport is the permanent replacement rather than a stopgap, a logic developed in full in the livestock module of Outcome 2. Beneath all of these sits the irreducible floor: the human body itself, dragging a travois. The travois never runs out of fuel beyond the food its puller already eats, and it functions on terrain — mud, scree, dense forest — where no wheel can roll.

Fire-making redundancy belongs to this same theory of rare consumables, because a journey's cooking, water purification, and cold-weather survival all collapse if the single ignition source is lost. The principle of three independent methods spans the entire history of human pyrotechnology: the modern ferrocerium rod (a mischmetal alloy producing sparks near 3,000 degrees C), the ancient flint-and-steel pairing that demands no industrial product but a hard edge and a stone, and the bow drill, which manufactures fire from wood alone. Experimental archaeology (Hough, 1926; Wescott, 1999) confirms that bow-drill fire is achievable in 30-60 seconds by practised individuals across many wood species, with cottonwood, willow, and cedar yielding the most reliable coal. The redundancy is not paranoia; it is the same lesson the fuel timeline teaches — never let mobility, warmth, or safety hang on a single consumable that you cannot remake.

## Core Principles

1. **Collapse is a gradient, not a switch.** Transport technologies fail in sequence, not simultaneously. The traveller who maps that sequence extracts months of additional mobility from each mode before retiring it.

2. **A machine lives only as long as its rarest consumable.** Identify the single resource each transport mode cannot survive without — refined fuel for engines, grid electricity for EVs, rubber for bicycles, grass for animals — and you can predict its death date.

3. **Abandon on a pre-set threshold, never on hope.** Decide the exact failure condition that triggers abandonment *before* the journey, and honour it. The cost of clinging to a dead machine is measured in days of lost walking.

4. **Strip before you walk.** A dead vehicle is a parts depot. Every abandoned machine yields high-carbon steel, copper, glass, magnets, and acid that have post-collapse value. Never walk away from salvage you can carry or cache.

5. **Efficiency ranks the modes.** Bicycle (3-5x walking efficiency, 50-80 km/day) beats foot (15-25 km/day) beats nothing. Choose the most efficient mode that still works for the terrain in front of you.

6. **Acquire the next mode before you need it.** Animals must be secured during Phase 0 or early Phase 1, while roads are passable and barter still functions. By the time you need them, no one will trade them.

7. **The travois is the floor that never fails.** It requires no fuel beyond food, no maintenance beyond replacing worn poles, and works on any terrain. It is the permanent baseline beneath every other mode.

8. **Redundancy where failure is fatal.** Fire-making, like transport, must never depend on a single method. Carry three independent ignition systems and be able to use at least two.

## Practical Implementation

The lifecycle unfolds as a sequence of five overlapping phases. Each has an entry condition, a set of priority actions, and a defined abandonment threshold that hands the journey to the next mode.

### Phase 0: Pre-Collapse — The Acquisition Window (Months -6 to 0)

Everything still works but supply chains are failing. This is the only window in which transport assets can be bought, fuelled, and stockpiled at pre-collapse prices.

**Priority actions:**

- **Fuel:** Top off all vehicles. Fill every available container with gasoline. Add fuel stabiliser if available — it extends shelf life from 3 months to 12-18 months. Diesel lasts longer than gasoline without stabiliser (6-12 months versus 3-6 months).
- **Bicycles:** Acquire the most durable bicycles available. Steel-frame touring bikes with wide tyres are ideal. Avoid carbon fibre (catastrophic failure without warning) and suspension bikes (complex, unrepairable). Stock spare inner tubes, tyre patches, brake pads, and chain links.
- **Maps:** Acquire every paper topographical map within your planned route radius. Laminate if possible. Digital maps are worthless once power grids fail.
- **Animal contacts:** Identify local horse, mule, and donkey owners. Establish relationships before collapse — post-collapse, animals become the most valuable asset in existence.

### Phase 1: Motorized Transport (Months 0-6)

Drive while fuel lasts. Do not waste fuel on scouting trips; drive directly toward the destination or as far as fuel allows.

**What still works and for how long:**

| Vehicle Type | Fuel Shelf Life | Failure Mode | Realistic Range |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Gasoline car/truck** | 3-6 months (unstabilized) | Fuel degrades → gum deposits → injector/carburetor clog | 300-500 miles on a full tank |
| **Diesel truck** | 6-12 months | Fuel grows algae/bacteria → filter clog → injection pump failure | 500-800 miles on a full tank |
| **Electric vehicle (EV)** | Battery holds charge 1-3 months sitting idle | No charging infrastructure → battery depletes → permanently dead | 150-300 miles on a full charge |
| **Hybrid** | Gasoline component: same as above; battery extends range 20-40% | Same as gasoline but marginally longer | 400-700 miles |
| **Motorcycle/scooter** | Same fuel constraints | Lighter = more efficient; but carries less cargo | 200-400 miles; 1-2 people only |

**The EV Trap:** Electric vehicles are silent, powerful, and require zero fuel — but they are **absolutely dependent on the electrical grid**. Once the grid fails, an EV is a 4,000-pound paperweight. There is no field-expedient way to charge an EV. Solar panels sufficient to charge an EV battery (60-100 kWh) would require 20-30 residential panels and 3-5 days of full sun. This is not portable.

**Abandonment thresholds:**

- **Fuel vehicles:** Abandon when fuel runs out OR when the engine develops a problem requiring a part you cannot fabricate (electronic sensors, fuel injectors, rubber hoses). Strip the vehicle for useful parts and walk.
- **EVs:** Abandon when the battery drops below 20% charge with no charging source. Do NOT drain it to zero.

**What to salvage from abandoned vehicles:**

|:---|:---|

| Leaf springs | Knife/tool blade stock (high-carbon steel) |
| Battery (lead-acid) | Acid for leather tanning; lead for casting; battery case for storage |
| Wiring (copper) | Snare wire, lashing, electrical conductor for future use |
| Tires | Cut into sole rubber for sandals/shoe repair; burn for emergency signal (toxic — last resort) |
| Glass (windshield/windows) | Greenhouse panels, water distillation, lens grinding |
| Seat foam | Insulation padding, sleeping mat |
| Fan belts / hoses | Cordage, strapping, tourniquet material |
| Alternator magnets | Permanent magnets for future generator construction |

### Phase 2: Bicycle Transport (Months 3-36)

The bicycle is the most efficient human-powered machine ever invented. A human on a bicycle is 3-5x more efficient than walking; a fit cyclist covers 50-80 km/day versus 15-25 km/day on foot.

**Why bicycles outlast vehicles:**

- No fuel requirement.
- Mechanical simplicity (bearings, chain, brakes — all field-repairable).
- Tyre patches and a pump can extend tyre life for years.
- Steel frames can be welded at a forge (see The Mechanics modules).
- Cargo capacity with trailer: 50-100 kg.

**Bicycle failure timeline:**

| Component | Expected Lifespan (heavy use) | Field Repair? | Failure Consequence |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Tires/tubes | 1,000-3,000 miles | Yes (patches, solid inner tubes from cut hose) | Flat = walk until patched |
| Chain | 2,000-5,000 miles | Yes (spare links, chain tool) | Broken = no drivetrain; becomes a scooter |
| Brake pads | 500-2,000 miles | Yes (carved hardwood or leather substitute) | No brakes = dangerous on hills |
| Bearings | 5,000-15,000 miles | Difficult (requires grease, correct size) | Grinding = increasing drag; eventual seizure |
| Cables | 3,000-10,000 miles | Moderate (paracord substitute for shift cables; braided wire for brake) | No shifting = single-speed; no brakes = foot braking |
| Spokes | 5,000+ miles individually | Yes (spare spokes + spoke wrench) | 2-3 broken spokes = wobbly wheel; 5+ = wheel collapse |
| Frame | Indefinite if steel; 2-5 years if aluminum (fatigue cracks) | Steel: weld at forge. Aluminum: No field repair. | Frame failure = total loss |

**Abandonment thresholds:**

- When tyres are destroyed beyond patching AND no replacement rubber exists. A tyreless bicycle can still roll on dirt with wrapped-cloth rims, but painfully slowly and with rapid rim wear.
- When bearings seize and cannot be repacked.
- When the frame cracks (aluminium) or rusts through (steel, after years of exposure).

**The bicycle-to-cart conversion:** A broken bicycle's wheels, axle, and chain can be repurposed into a human-pulled cart that rolls on bearings — far more efficient than a travois on flat terrain.

### Phase 3: Animal Transport (Months 6-Indefinite)

This is the permanent replacement. Animals reproduce. They fuel themselves on grass. They navigate terrain no vehicle can handle. See the livestock module in Outcome 2 (cross-referenced below) for selection, handling, harness, and husbandry detail.

**Acquisition window:** Animals must be secured during Phase 0 or early Phase 1, while roads are still passable and barter is still functional. By Phase 2, anyone with animals will not trade them for anything less than irreplaceable skills or critical supplies.

### Phase 4: Foot + Travois (Permanent Baseline)

If no animals can be acquired, the travois system described in the existing Journey modules becomes the permanent transport mode. The travois is the floor — it always works, requires no maintenance beyond replacing worn poles, and functions on any terrain.

### The Master Decision Matrix

At any point during or after collapse, walk the matrix top-down and ride the most efficient mode that still works:

```text
Do you have fuel + working motor vehicle?
├── YES → Drive. Do not waste fuel on scouting trips. Drive to destination.
│         Strip vehicle when fuel is exhausted or engine fails.
└── NO
    Do you have a working bicycle + navigable route?
    ├── YES → Ride. 3-5x faster than walking. Carry max cargo in trailer.
    │         Switch to foot when tires/bearings fail beyond repair.
    └── NO
        Do you have a pack animal?
        ├── YES → Animal carries cargo. You walk alongside.
        │         See the livestock module for management.
        └── NO
            Build a travois. Walk. 15 km/day.
            This is the permanent baseline. It works.
```

### Fire-Making Redundancy (The Critical Backup)

The existing modules assume ferrocerium rods for fire. If these are lost, the journey's cooking, water purification, and cold-weather shelter systems all fail. Every traveller must carry **three independent fire-making methods:**

1. **Ferrocerium rod** (primary) — produces 3,000 degrees C sparks, works when wet, lasts 10,000+ strikes.
2. **Flint and steel** (secondary) — a piece of high-carbon steel (the back of a knife blade, a file, or a vehicle leaf spring) struck against natural flint, chert, or quartz produces sparks that ignite char cloth.
   - **Making char cloth:** Place small squares of cotton or linen fabric in a sealed tin with one small hole. Place the tin in a fire. Smoke will vent from the hole. When smoke stops, remove from heat and seal the hole. The fabric is now charred carbon that catches a spark instantly.
3. **Bow drill** (tertiary) — friction fire from wood alone. Requires practice but uses only materials found on the ground.
   - **Components:** A hardwood fireboard (cedar, willow, cottonwood), a hardwood spindle (same species), a bow (any curved stick with cordage), and a hand socket (hard stone or shell with a depression).
   - **Technique:** Place the spindle in a notch in the fireboard. Wrap the bow string once around the spindle. Saw the bow back and forth rapidly. Friction creates a coal in the notch. Transfer the coal to a tinder bundle (dry grass, bark fibre) and blow gently to flame.
   - **The reality:** Bow-drill fire-making takes 30-60 seconds for an experienced practitioner and 10-30 minutes for a beginner. **Practice before the journey, not during it.**

```text
       FIRE-MAKING REDUNDANCY LADDER (three independent tiers)

  TIER 1  Ferrocerium rod   ●───► 3,000°C sparks, works wet,
          (PRIMARY)               10,000+ strikes
                │ if lost
                ▼
  TIER 2  Flint & steel     ◐───► high-carbon edge + stone,
          (SECONDARY)             ignites char cloth
                │ if lost
                ▼
  TIER 3  Bow drill         ○───► wood only; 30-60 s expert,
          (TERTIARY)              10-30 min beginner
                │ if all fail
                ▼
  FLOOR   Carry an ember    ✦───► fire bundle smoulders 6-12 hr
RULE: Two methods minimum per adult — fire lost in cold kills in hours.
```

### Step-by-Step Action Plan

- **Now (Pre-Collapse):** Acquire steel-frame bicycles, spare tubes, patch kits, and a chain tool for every travelling adult. Acquire paper topographical maps. Practice flint-and-steel and bow-drill fire-making until reliable (minimum 10 successful fires each method).
- **Phase 0:** Top off all fuel. Stabilise if possible. Load vehicles with maximum supplies and drive to the destination or as far as fuel allows.
- **Phase 1:** When the vehicle fails, strip it (see salvage table). Transfer cargo to bicycle trailers or travois. Continue on bicycle if the route permits.
- **Phase 2+:** When bicycles fail, transition to animal transport (if acquired) or foot + travois.

### Community and Decadal Flourishing

Every adult in the travelling group must be able to make fire with at least two of the three methods, and every adult must be able to perform basic bicycle repair — patch a tyre, reconnect a chain. These are non-negotiable transit skills. Distribute them deliberately through apprenticeship before departure, because a single failure point in mobility or fire endangers the whole party.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Over-investing in a dead engine.** A group spends two weeks trying to revive a modern vehicle whose fuel injectors or electronic sensors cannot be fabricated, burning daylight and morale while the refugium grows no closer. **Prevention:** Set the abandonment threshold in advance — when the failure is a part you cannot make, strip and walk the same day.

2. **The EV deep-discharge trap.** A traveller drains an electric vehicle's traction battery to zero hoping the grid will return, and the lithium pack is permanently destroyed, eliminating any later chance of recovery or salvage power. **Prevention:** Abandon the EV at 20% charge with no charging source; never run it to empty.

3. **Degraded fuel killing a sound engine.** Gasoline left unstabilised more than 3-6 months oxidises into varnish that clogs injectors and jets; the engine cranks but will not run, and the traveller assumes mechanical failure. **Prevention:** Stabilise fuel in Phase 0 (extends life to 12-18 months), and when fuel smells off, drain the tank, filter through cloth to remove varnish particles, and cut with any fresh fuel before condemning the engine.

4. **Catastrophic frame loss with no warning.** Carbon-fibre or aluminium frames fail suddenly — carbon shatters without warning, aluminium fatigue-cracks within 2-5 years — leaving the rider stranded with an unrepairable machine. **Prevention:** Acquire only steel-frame touring bikes in Phase 0; steel bends before it breaks and can be welded at a forge.

5. **Tyre exhaustion with no rubber reserve.** Bicycle tyres shred beyond patching (1,000-3,000 mile life) and no replacement rubber exists, halting the most efficient transport mode. **Prevention:** Stock spare tubes and patches; in extremis wrap the rim with multiple layers of cloth or leather secured with cord for 10-20 miles of slow riding — still better than walking with a 50 kg trailer — or execute the bicycle-to-cart conversion.

6. **Missing the animal acquisition window.** A group waits until Phase 2 to seek pack animals, by which point no owner will trade them for anything less than irreplaceable skills or supplies. **Prevention:** Secure animals in Phase 0 or early Phase 1 while roads are passable and barter still functions.

7. **Total loss of fire capability.** A traveller carries only a ferrocerium rod, loses it, and the entire cooking, water-purification, and warmth chain collapses — lethal in cold within hours. **Prevention:** Carry three independent ignition methods; if all fail, carry an ember in a slow-burning fire bundle (packed dry punk wood or fungus wrapped in bark) that smoulders 6-12 hours, the method by which humans moved fire for 100,000 years before striking tools existed.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Abandonment Threshold** — The pre-defined failure condition that triggers retiring a transport mode (fuel exhausted, EV at 20%, tyres unpatchable, bearings seized). Set in advance and honoured to prevent wasting time on a dead machine.
- **Acquisition Window** — Phase 0, the pre-collapse period (roughly months -6 to 0) in which transport assets — fuel, bicycles, maps, animal relationships — can still be obtained at functioning markets. It does not reopen.
- **Bicycle-to-Cart Conversion** — Repurposing a broken bicycle's wheels, axle, and chain into a human-pulled cart that rolls on bearings — more efficient than a travois on flat terrain.
- **Char Cloth** — Cotton or linen fabric carbonised by pyrolysis (heated without oxygen). It catches a single spark and holds a glowing ember, which then ignites a tinder bundle.
- **Degradation Sequence** — The ordered series of failures by which transport technologies die after collapse: motor vehicles first (fuel-bound), then bicycles (rubber-bound), with animals and the travois as the surviving permanent modes. Understanding the sequence lets a traveller hand off between modes before each one dies.
- **Ferrocerium Rod** — A metallic rod (mischmetal alloy) that produces extremely hot sparks (3,000 degrees C) when scraped with a hard edge. The primary fire-starting tool in the Aadhiyandham kit; lasts 10,000+ strikes.
- **Fire Bundle** — A tightly packed mass of dry punk wood or fungus wrapped in bark that smoulders for 6-12 hours, used to transport a live ember between campsites when no ignition method is available.
- **Fuel Stabilizer** — A chemical additive that slows the oxidation and evaporation of gasoline, extending its usability from 3 months to 12-18 months. Not required for diesel.
- **The EV Trap** — The condition in which an electric vehicle, having no field-expedient charging path, becomes a 4,000-pound immovable object once grid power fails. Charging a 60-100 kWh pack would need 20-30 residential solar panels and 3-5 days of full sun.
- **Travois** — An A-frame cargo sled dragged by a human or animal, consisting of two long poles with cross-bars. Functions on any terrain including mud, rock, and dense forest where wheeled vehicles cannot pass. The permanent transport baseline.
