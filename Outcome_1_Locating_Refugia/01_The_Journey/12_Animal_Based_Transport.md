## 1. Why This Is Important

Module 10 (Transport Technology Lifecycle) establishes that animal transport is the only mode that persists indefinitely — animals breed, fuel themselves on grass, and navigate terrain no machine can handle. But the existing modules mention pack animals exactly once ("mules and donkeys recommended for Appalachian/Ozark terrain") with zero operational detail. A mule that is overloaded dies. A horse with untreated hoof rot becomes unable to walk within days. A donkey that is not properly watered in heat collapses.

This module provides everything needed to acquire, manage, load, feed, water, and care for pack animals during extended overland transit and permanent settlement operations.

## 2. Species Selection

### 2.1. The Three Viable Pack Animals

| Species | Load Capacity | Daily Range | Terrain | Temperament | Feed Requirement | Breeding |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Mule** (horse x donkey) | 90-130 kg (200-290 lbs) | 25-35 km | Excellent on steep, rocky, narrow trails | Cautious, sure-footed, stubborn if overloaded | 2-2.5% body weight in forage/day | Sterile — cannot breed; must cross horse + donkey |
| **Donkey** | 50-70 kg (110-155 lbs) | 20-30 km | Excellent on rocky/arid terrain; poor in deep mud | Calm, hardy, bonds strongly to handler | 1.5-2% body weight; thrives on poor forage | Self-sustaining; 12-month gestation |
| **Horse** | 90-115 kg (200-250 lbs) | 30-50 km | Good on open/rolling terrain; poor on steep rock | Responsive but anxiety-prone; needs experienced handler | 2-2.5% body weight; requires better forage than donkey | Self-sustaining; 11-month gestation |

### 2.2. Recommendation

**For transit:** Mules are the gold standard. They combine the horse's size with the donkey's sure-footedness and hardiness. Their one fatal flaw is sterility — you must maintain horse and donkey breeding stock to produce new mules.

**For settlement:** Donkeys are the most sustainable. They eat less, survive on rougher forage, breed reliably, and require less veterinary knowledge. Every settlement should maintain at least 2 jennies (female donkeys) and 1 jack (male).

**Horses** are the fastest but the most fragile and feed-demanding. Reserve for messenger/courier use between settlements, not daily cargo.

## 3. Acquisition

### 3.1. Pre-Collapse (Phase 0)

Buy or barter for animals while transport infrastructure exists. Prioritize:
*   Mules or donkeys from working farms (already trained to carry loads).
*   Animals under 10 years old (15-25 year working lifespan remaining).
*   Sound hooves, clear eyes, no lameness, no cough.
*   Ask the seller to demonstrate loading — an untrained animal that panics under load is dangerous.

### 3.2. Post-Collapse

Feral horse and donkey populations exist across the Americas, Australia, and parts of Europe/Asia. Capture requires:
*   **Passive trapping:** Build a large corral (30m diameter) at a known water source with a funnel entrance. Leave the gate open for 1-2 weeks so animals habituate to entering. Close the gate when a suitable animal is inside.
*   **Approach and haltering:** Move slowly, speak softly, avoid direct eye contact (predator signal). Offer food (grain, apple) from an open hand. A feral animal requires 2-4 weeks of daily handling before it will accept a load.

## 4. Loading and Pack Saddle

### 4.1. The 20% Rule

**Never load an animal above 20% of its body weight for sustained travel.** A 400 kg mule carries a maximum of 80 kg. A 200 kg donkey carries a maximum of 40 kg. Exceeding this causes spinal injury, joint damage, and pressure sores that can kill the animal within weeks.

### 4.2. Improvised Pack Saddle

If no commercial pack saddle is available:

1. **The frame:** Two forked branches (Y-shaped), each approximately 60cm long, placed on either side of the spine over a thick padding of folded blankets or wool.
2. **The padding:** Critical. At least 5cm of compressible material between the frame and the animal's back. Bare wood on spine = pressure sores within hours.
3. **The girth:** A wide strap (10cm minimum) of leather or folded canvas passing under the belly, snug but not constricting. You should be able to slide a flat hand between the girth and the belly.
4. **The breast collar:** A strap from the front of the saddle around the chest prevents the load from sliding backward on downhill sections.
5. **Balance:** Loads must be equal weight on both sides (within 2-3 kg). Unbalanced loads cause the saddle to shift, creating sores and spooking the animal.

### 4.3. Loading Sequence

1. Saddle and pad first. Check for wrinkles in the pad (wrinkle = pressure point = sore).
2. Hang the heaviest items lowest and closest to the animal's center of gravity.
3. Lighter, bulkier items on top.
4. Secure all loads with diamond hitch or box hitch lashing. Nothing should swing or bounce.
5. Walk 100 meters and stop. Re-check all lashings. Adjust any shifted load.
6. Check again after 1 hour of travel. This becomes routine.

## 5. Daily Care and Management

### 5.1. Water

| Species | Minimum Daily Water | Hot Weather / Heavy Work |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Horse | 30-50 liters (8-13 gallons) | 50-75 liters |
| Mule | 25-40 liters | 40-60 liters |
| Donkey | 15-25 liters | 25-40 liters |

**Water before feeding.** An animal that drinks heavily after eating dry feed risks colic (gut obstruction) — the leading natural cause of death in equines. Allow 30 minutes between drinking and feeding.

**A dehydrated animal** shows: sunken eyes, dry gums, skin that when pinched holds its shape (tenting) instead of snapping back. Stop immediately. Offer water in small amounts every 15 minutes. Do not resume travel until the animal drinks freely and skin elasticity returns.

### 5.2. Feeding

*   **Grazing:** Allow 4-6 hours of grazing daily on available grass/forage. Hobble the front legs (tie a short rope between the front ankles) to prevent wandering while allowing movement to graze.
*   **Supplemental feed:** If grazing is poor (winter, arid terrain, dense forest), supplement with grain (oats, barley) at 0.5% of body weight per day. Grain is concentrated calories; do not overfeed (causes laminitis — a crippling hoof inflammation).
*   **Salt:** Animals need salt. If no salt lick is available, add a pinch of salt to water or rub salt on a flat stone near the picket line. Sodium deficiency causes lethargy and muscle cramping.

### 5.3. Hoof Care

Hoof problems are the #1 cause of pack animal failure on the trail.

**Daily inspection (every evening):**
1. Pick up each foot. Use a hoof pick (or hardened bent nail) to remove all packed mud, stones, and debris from the sole and frog (the triangular pad on the underside).
2. Check for:
   *   **Stones lodged in the sole:** Remove immediately. A bruised sole = lameness within hours.
   *   **Cracks in the hoof wall:** Small surface cracks are normal. A crack extending from the bottom to the coronary band (top of the hoof) is serious — the hoof may split under load.
   *   **Foul smell or black discharge from the frog:** This is thrush (fungal infection from wet conditions). Pack the frog with a paste of wood ash and allow to dry. Keep the animal on dry ground overnight.
   *   **Heat in the hoof:** A hoof that feels significantly warmer than the others indicates inflammation (laminitis or abscess). Do NOT load this animal until the heat resolves.

**Trimming:** Every 6-8 weeks, hooves need trimming. Without a farrier, use a rasp (coarse file) to level the hoof wall to the same plane as the sole. The hoof should bear weight evenly. Over-trimming is worse than under-trimming — file conservatively.

**Shoeing:** In rocky terrain, unshod hooves wear faster than they grow. If no iron shoes are available, leather boots (cut from heavy hide, lashed around the hoof) provide temporary protection for 3-5 days of rocky travel.

### 5.4. Overnight Picket

*   **Highline:** String a rope between two trees at head height. Tie each animal to the highline with a 3m lead, spaced 4m apart so they cannot tangle. This prevents them from eating toxic plants, wandering off, and fighting.
*   **Hobbles:** If no trees are available (grassland, desert), hobble the front legs with a soft rope or leather strap. The animal can walk slowly and graze but cannot run. Always hobble + bell — the bell lets you locate a hobbled animal that has wandered in the night.

## 6. Trail Management

### 6.1. Pace and Rest

*   Walk beside the animal, not on it (saves the animal's back for cargo).
*   Stop for 10 minutes every 2 hours to let the animal graze and drink if water is available.
*   Unload completely during the midday rest (minimum 1 hour). The back needs pressure relief.
*   Full rest day every 5-6 travel days for animals (same as humans — see Module 11).

### 6.2. Terrain Limits

| Terrain | Horse | Mule | Donkey |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| Open trail / road | Excellent | Excellent | Excellent |
| Steep switchbacks (>20%) | Poor (anxiety) | Excellent | Excellent |
| Rocky scree / talus | Dangerous | Good (careful) | Good |
| Deep mud (>20cm) | Moderate | Poor (fights mud) | Very poor (small hooves sink) |
| Snow (>30cm) | Moderate | Poor | Poor (low ground clearance) |
| River crossing (<belly deep) | Good (strong swimmer) | Good | Moderate (shorter legs) |
| Dense forest / blowdown | Poor (wide body) | Moderate | Good (smaller, more agile) |

### 6.3. River Crossings With Animals

1. Scout the crossing on foot first. Select the widest, shallowest point (slower current).
2. Unload the animal completely. Carry cargo across separately or waterproof it.
3. Lead the animal in from downstream, angling upstream. The animal faces the current.
4. One handler leads from the front; a second handler follows behind to prevent the animal from turning back.
5. If the animal panics: release the lead rope immediately. A panicking equine in water can drown both itself and the handler. It will swim to the nearest bank on its own.

## 7. Breeding for Long-Term Transport Sovereignty

### 7.1. Minimum Breeding Stock

| Species | Minimum Herd | Why |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Donkey | 1 jack + 2 jennies | Produces 1-2 foals/year. Maintains genetic diversity for 2-3 generations. |
| Horse | 1 stallion + 3 mares | Produces 2-3 foals/year. Horse stallions can cross with donkey jennies for mules. |
| Mule production | 1 male donkey (jack) + 1+ female horse (mare) | The preferred cross. Male horse x female donkey (hinny) is smaller and less useful. |

### 7.2. Foaling and Care

*   Gestation: 11 months (horse), 12 months (donkey).
*   Foaling usually occurs at night. Provide a dry, sheltered area.
*   The foal must stand and nurse within 2 hours of birth. If it cannot stand, assist it to the teat — colostrum (first milk) provides critical immunity.
*   Foals can begin light pack training at age 2, full loads at age 3-4.
*   Keep detailed breeding records. Do not breed parent to offspring or full siblings — maintain a minimum of 2 unrelated breeding lines.

## 8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Colic:** Abdominal pain in equines caused by gas, impaction, or gut twist. The leading natural cause of death. Signs: pawing, rolling, looking at the flank, refusal to eat. Walk the animal continuously — do not allow it to lie down and roll (can twist the gut fatally).
*   **Laminitis (Founder):** Inflammation of the hoof laminae caused by overfeeding grain, lush grass, or excessive work on hard surfaces. Signs: reluctance to walk, rocking back on the heels, warm hooves. Immediate rest and hoof cooling (standing in a cold stream) is essential.
*   **Hobble:** A short rope or leather strap connecting the two front legs, allowing walking but preventing running. Used for overnight grazing management.
*   **Diamond Hitch:** A lashing pattern that secures a pack load to a saddle using a single continuous rope crossed in a diamond pattern over the top of the load, providing stability on uneven terrain.

## 9. Scientific Validation & Research Context

*   **20% Body Weight Rule:** The U.S. Army Veterinary Corps and the Donkey Sanctuary (UK) both establish 20% of body weight as the maximum sustained load for equines without risk of spinal injury, based on decades of veterinary and field data.
*   **Mule Superiority on Trails:** The U.S. Forest Service's 100+ year history of mule packing in the Sierra Nevada, Cascades, and Rocky Mountains provides empirical validation that mules outperform horses on steep, rocky, narrow trails due to their narrower hoof placement, lower center of gravity, and superior proprioception inherited from the donkey parent.
*   **Donkey Hardiness:** Bordonaro et al. (2012, Journal of Animal Science) documented that donkeys maintain body condition on 30-40% less feed than horses of equivalent body weight, confirming their suitability as the primary transport animal in resource-limited settings.

## 10. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 10.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Phase 0 (Pre-Collapse):** Acquire 2-3 donkeys or mules from a working farm. Spend 1-2 months learning to halter, lead, load, and pick hooves under the previous owner's guidance. This apprenticeship is irreplaceable.
*   **Phase 1 (Transit):** Load to 15% body weight for the first week (below maximum). Increase to 20% only after the animal is acclimatized to the terrain and pace. Inspect hooves every evening without exception.
*   **Phase 2 (Settlement):** Establish a grazing paddock and breeding program. Train a second handler from the next generation. Begin mule production if both horse and donkey stock are available.

### 10.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes
*   **Animal refuses to move:** Never beat a balking mule or donkey. They stop because something is wrong — check for hoof pain, unbalanced load, girth too tight, or a trail hazard they can see that you cannot. Mules are smarter than horses; trust their refusal.
*   **Saddle sore developing:** Any reddened, hairless, or raw patch under the saddle means the padding is inadequate or the load is unbalanced. Stop loading that area immediately. Pad with additional wool/cloth. Allow the sore to dry and scab before reloading (2-3 days minimum).
*   **Animal lost at night:** If an animal slips its hobble, listen for the bell. Walk in widening circles from the picket site at first light. Check the last known water source — equines return to water.

### 10.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** Animal handling cannot be learned from a book. Every settlement must maintain at least 2 experienced handlers, and every handler must train an apprentice. The knowledge chain is: experienced handler → apprentice → animals. Break any link and the transport system fails within a generation.
