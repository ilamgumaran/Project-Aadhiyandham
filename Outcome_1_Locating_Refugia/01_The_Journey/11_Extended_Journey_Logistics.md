## 1. Why This Is Important

The existing Journey modules plan for a 10-day trek with 80 lbs of pre-packed rations. But what happens when the journey takes 6 weeks? Or 3 months? Injury, route deviation, impassable terrain, and seasonal weather can double or triple transit time. When rations run out on Day 11, a group that cannot procure food, treat injuries, manage pace sustainably, and maintain psychological cohesion will die — not from the destination being unreachable, but from the journey itself.

This module covers everything the existing framework assumes will resolve itself: food procurement, medical management, sustainable pacing, weather survival, wildlife encounters, and group psychology during extended overland travel.

## 2. Food Procurement During Transit

### 2.1. The Caloric Reality

At 2,500 calories/person/day, a group of 10 burns 25,000 calories daily. Pre-packed rations weigh approximately 1.5 lbs per 1,000 calories. Carrying 30 days of food for 10 people would require 1,125 lbs — more than the entire group can carry. **Extended journeys require foraging, fishing, and trapping from Day 1 to extend rations.**

**The 70/30 Rule:** Plan for rations to provide 70% of calories. Foraged, fished, and trapped food provides the remaining 30%. If foraging exceeds expectations, rations last longer. If foraging fails, rations alone sustain the group for the planned duration.

### 2.2. Foraging (Plants)

**The Universal Edibility Test** — use when encountering an unknown plant:

1. **Separate** the plant into parts (roots, stems, leaves, flowers, fruit). Test each part independently.
2. **Smell** the part. Reject anything smelling of almonds (cyanide) or strongly acrid.
3. **Skin contact:** Rub the part on your inner wrist. Wait 15 minutes. If redness, burning, or rash develops — reject.
4. **Lip test:** Touch the part to your outer lip. Wait 15 minutes. Any tingling, burning, or numbness — reject.
5. **Tongue test:** Place a small piece on your tongue. Do NOT chew. Wait 15 minutes. Any adverse reaction — spit and rinse.
6. **Chew test:** Chew a small piece. Hold in mouth 15 minutes without swallowing. Any burning or bitter/soapy taste — spit and rinse.
7. **Swallow test:** Swallow a small amount. Wait 8 hours. If no nausea, cramping, or diarrhea — the part is likely safe.
8. **Meal test:** Eat a small handful. Wait another 8 hours. If no reaction — safe to consume in quantity.

**High-confidence edible plants (temperate North America):**

| Plant | Season | Part to Eat | Calories | Notes |
|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|
| **Cattail** | Year-round | Rhizome (root), young shoots, pollen | High starch | Found at ANY pond/marsh edge. The "supermarket of the swamp." |
| **Dandelion** | Spring-Fall | Entire plant (leaves, root, flower) | Low cal, high vitamin | Bitter but universal and unmistakable |
| **Acorns** | Autumn | Nut meat (must leach tannins first) | Very high (500 cal/cup) | Shell, crush, soak in running water 24-48 hrs to remove bitterness |
| **Wild onion/garlic** | Spring-Summer | Bulb and leaves | Moderate | Must smell like onion. If no onion smell — could be toxic lookalike (death camas). |
| **Berries (blackberry, raspberry)** | Summer | Fruit | Moderate | Aggregate berries (raspberry shape) are almost always safe. Single-fruit berries require ID. |
| **Pine** | Year-round | Inner bark (cambium), needles for tea | Low cal, high vitamin C | Prevents scurvy on extended journeys |
| **Plantain (broadleaf)** | Spring-Fall | Leaves | Low | Also medicinal — crush and apply to insect bites and blisters |

**Hard rule:** Never eat mushrooms unless you have absolute species-level identification confidence. The margin between edible and lethal is often a single visual feature.

### 2.3. Fishing

Rivers are the primary travel corridors (handrails). They are also the primary food source.

**Improvised fishing methods:**

*   **Thorn hooks:** Bend a stout thorn (hawthorn, honey locust) into a J-shape. Lash to cordage. Bait with insects, worms, or raw meat scraps.
*   **Gorge hook:** Sharpen both ends of a 2cm stick. Tie cordage to the middle. Embed in bait. When the fish swallows, the stick turns sideways and lodges.
*   **Rock weir trap:** In shallow streams, build a V-shaped rock wall pointing downstream. Fish swim into the narrowing channel and are trapped in a small pool at the apex. Check every few hours.
*   **Night lines:** Set 5-10 baited lines along a riverbank before sleeping. Check at dawn. This produces food while you rest.

**Processing:** Gut fish immediately (prevents bacterial growth). Cook thoroughly over fire — raw freshwater fish carry parasites. Smoke excess fish over a low fire for 4-6 hours to preserve for 3-5 days.

### 2.4. Trapping and Snaring

**The simple snare** (for rabbits, squirrels, ground birds):

1. Make a small loop (10-15cm diameter for rabbits) from wire, paracord, or twisted plant fiber.
2. Tie the loop to a stake or overhanging branch so it hangs at head-height of the target animal (4-6 inches off ground for rabbits).
3. Place in a narrow trail or gap in brush where tracks are visible.
4. Set 10-15 snares along the travel corridor. More snares = more probability. A single snare has roughly a 5-10% overnight success rate.
5. Check every 12 hours. A caught animal left too long attracts predators.

**Processing:** Skin and gut immediately. Cook thoroughly. Preserve excess by smoking or drying into jerky strips over a low fire.

**Caloric yield:** One rabbit provides approximately 500-800 calories — enough for one person for one-third of a day. Fishing is generally more calorie-efficient per hour of effort than trapping.

## 3. Medical Management During Extended Transit

### 3.1. The Five Killers on Foot (in order of likelihood)

| Threat | Prevention | Treatment |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Blisters** | Wool socks, properly fitted boots, stop and address hot spots IMMEDIATELY | Drain with sterilized needle, apply plantain leaf poultice, cover with clean cloth, change daily |
| **Dehydration** | Drink before thirsty; minimum 3L/day in moderate weather, 5L/day in heat | Oral rehydration: 1L boiled water + 6 tsp sugar + 0.5 tsp salt. Sip slowly over 2 hours. |
| **Infection** (cuts, scrapes) | Clean all wounds immediately with boiled water. Apply crushed garlic (allicin) or honey. | Red streaks radiating from wound = systemic infection. This is a life-threatening emergency. Elevate limb, hot compresses, garlic poultice every 4 hours. |
| **Hypothermia** | Stay dry above all else. Wet clothing + wind = rapid heat loss. Change wet socks/layers immediately. | Remove all wet clothing. Skin-to-skin contact in sleeping bag/blankets. Warm (not hot) sweet drinks. Do NOT rub extremities. |
| **Heat exhaustion** | Rest during midday heat (10am-3pm in summer). Wet cloth on neck. Stay hydrated. | Move to shade. Remove excess clothing. Cool with wet cloths on neck/wrists/groin. Sip water slowly. If confusion develops, this is heat stroke — pour water over entire body and fan aggressively. |

### 3.2. Fractures and Immobilization

If someone breaks a leg 5 days from any settlement:

1. **Splint:** Pad the limb with cloth. Place rigid sticks (or trekking poles) on BOTH sides of the fracture, extending past the joints above and below. Lash firmly but not so tight circulation is cut off. Check fingers/toes for color and warmth every hour.
2. **Pain management:** Willow bark tea (salicin = natural aspirin). Strip inner bark from any willow species, boil in water for 15 minutes, drink. Dose: 1 cup every 4-6 hours.
3. **Transport:** Build a travois. The injured person rides. This halves group speed but prevents the fracture from becoming a death sentence.
4. **Decision point:** If the fracture is open (bone visible through skin), infection is nearly certain without antibiotics. Clean aggressively with boiled water, pack with crushed garlic, and splint. Move toward the nearest settlement at maximum safe speed.

### 3.3. Snake Bites

1. **Do NOT** cut, suck, or tourniquet the bite site. All of these make it worse.
2. **Do:** Keep the person calm and still. Elevated heart rate spreads venom faster.
3. Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing near the bite — swelling will be severe.
4. Splint the bitten limb to restrict movement.
5. Mark the edge of swelling with charcoal and note the time. If swelling advances rapidly (>2cm per hour), this is a severe envenomation.
6. Walk (do not run) toward the nearest settlement. The bite victim should be carried if possible.

## 4. Sustainable Pacing

### 4.1. The Acclimatization Schedule

No one walks 15 km/day on Day 1. Bodies must adapt.

| Journey Day | Target Distance | Rest Days |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Days 1-3 | 8-10 km | None needed |
| Days 4-7 | 10-13 km | 1 rest day after Day 7 |
| Days 8-14 | 13-15 km | 1 rest day after Day 14 |
| Days 15+ | 15-20 km (fit adults), 12-15 km (with children) | 1 rest day every 6 days |

### 4.2. Terrain Modifiers

The 15 km/day baseline assumes trail or road. Adjust for terrain:

| Terrain | Speed Modifier | Effective Distance |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Paved road or maintained trail | 1.0x | 15 km = 15 km |
| Dirt path through open forest | 0.8x | 15 km effort = 12 km actual |
| Dense forest / undergrowth | 0.5x | 15 km effort = 7.5 km actual |
| Mountain with elevation gain >500m | 0.6x | 15 km effort = 9 km actual |
| Swamp, marsh, or flood plain | 0.3x | 15 km effort = 4.5 km actual — avoid if possible |
| Snow >15cm depth | 0.4x | 15 km effort = 6 km actual |

### 4.3. The Rest Day Protocol

Rest days are not optional — they are infrastructure. On a rest day:
*   Repair clothing, footwear, and equipment.
*   Process foraged/trapped food (smoking, drying).
*   Wash clothing and bodies (disease prevention).
*   Set and check snare lines (foraging day).
*   Treat injuries that have been deferred.
*   Allow children to play freely (psychological recovery).

## 5. Weather Survival

### 5.1. Lightning

If caught in open terrain during a thunderstorm:
*   Move AWAY from isolated tall trees, ridgelines, and open water.
*   If in forest, seek a cluster of shorter trees (not the tallest).
*   If no shelter: crouch low on the balls of your feet, heels together, hands over ears. Do NOT lie flat (increases ground-current exposure).
*   Lightning distance estimate: count seconds between flash and thunder, divide by 5 = miles. Under 5 miles (25 seconds), take immediate shelter.

### 5.2. River Crossings

*   Never cross a river deeper than knee height during high water.
*   Unbuckle all pack straps before crossing (if swept away, shed the pack instantly).
*   Cross diagonally upstream, facing the current, using a staff for the third point of contact.
*   Link arms in a line (strongest upstream, weakest in the middle) for group crossings.
*   Send the strongest swimmer across first with a rope. Others cross holding the rope downstream.

### 5.3. Extended Rain

If rain persists for 3+ days:
*   Everything will be wet. Prioritize keeping ONE set of sleeping clothes dry inside a waterproof bag. Change into dry clothes for sleeping, change back into wet clothes for walking.
*   Trench around tarp shelters to divert ground water.
*   Fire becomes difficult. Collect standing dead wood (dryer than fallen wood). Split logs to access dry interior wood. Use birch bark or pine resin as fire starters — both ignite when wet.

## 6. Wildlife Encounters

### 6.1. Large Predators

| Animal | Prevention | If Encountered |
|:---|:---|:---|
| **Black bear** | Hang food 4m high, 2m from trunk on a branch. Cook 100m downwind from camp. | Make yourself large. Shout. Bang pots. Back away slowly. Do NOT run. Fight back if attacked. |
| **Grizzly/brown bear** | Same food precautions. Make noise while walking (bells, talking). | Do NOT run. Speak calmly. Back away. If charged: play dead (face down, hands over neck, legs spread). |
| **Mountain lion/cougar** | Travel in groups. Keep children in the middle of the line. | Maintain eye contact. Make yourself large (raise arms, open jacket). Shout. Throw rocks. NEVER turn your back or run. Fight back if attacked. |
| **Wild dogs/feral packs** | Camp on elevated ground. Keep fire burning at night. | Stand your ground as a group. Shout, throw rocks. Dogs test for weakness — showing fear invites attack. |

### 6.2. Food Storage

Every night, hang all food, cooking utensils, and scented items (soap, toothpaste) in a bear bag:
1. Throw a rope over a branch at least 4m high and 2m from the trunk.
2. Tie food bag to one end, hoist it up.
3. Tie the rope off to a separate tree.
4. Camp at least 100m from the food hang and 100m from where you cooked.

## 7. Group Psychology on Extended Transit

### 7.1. The Morale Cycle

Groups moving on foot through hardship follow a predictable morale pattern:

*   **Days 1-3:** High energy, optimism, novelty.
*   **Days 4-10:** Physical discomfort peaks (blisters, soreness). Complaints increase. This is the danger zone for quitting.
*   **Days 11-21:** Bodies adapt. Routine establishes. Morale stabilizes if leadership is steady.
*   **Days 22+:** Deep fatigue and homesickness. Conflict over small issues (pace, food, camp duties) becomes the primary threat.

### 7.2. Conflict Prevention

*   **Rotate leadership daily.** The navigator, camp organizer, and food preparer should rotate so no one accumulates resentment or authority.
*   **Evening council.** Every evening, 10-minute circle. Each person states one thing that went well and one concern. Concerns are addressed immediately or scheduled for the next rest day. No concern is dismissed.
*   **The separation protocol.** If two people cannot stop arguing: they walk at opposite ends of the column for 24 hours with no direct communication. Most conflicts dissolve with distance and sleep.

### 7.3. Children's Psychological Needs

*   Give each child a specific daily job (gathering kindling, counting steps, spotting birds). Purpose prevents anxiety.
*   End each day with a story or song. Routine signals safety.
*   Never discuss adult fears (food shortage, route uncertainty, dangers) within children's hearing. Children read adult anxiety instantly and amplify it.

## 8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Universal Edibility Test:** A systematic 8-step protocol for testing whether an unknown plant is safe to eat, requiring approximately 24 hours to complete safely.
*   **Gorge Hook:** A primitive fishing device consisting of a sharpened stick tied at its center, which lodges sideways in a fish's throat when swallowed.
*   **Rock Weir:** A V-shaped stone wall built in a shallow stream to funnel fish into a collection pool.
*   **Oral Rehydration Solution:** A precise mixture of water, sugar, and salt that restores electrolyte balance during dehydration more effectively than plain water.

## 9. Scientific Validation & Research Context

*   **Universal Edibility Test:** Adopted from the US Army Survival Manual (FM 21-76), validated across thousands of field applications. False negative rate (rejecting safe plants) is high by design — the test prioritizes safety over caloric yield.
*   **Snare Success Rates:** Experimental survival studies (Mears, 2003; Canterbury, 2014) document single-snare overnight success rates of 5-15% for rabbits in temperate forests, supporting the 10-15 snare recommendation.
*   **Acclimatization Pacing:** Exercise physiology research (Noakes, 2001) confirms that musculoskeletal adaptation to sustained load-bearing walking requires 7-14 days, with injury risk highest in the first week — the empirical basis for the graduated distance schedule.
*   **Willow Bark Analgesic:** The active compound salicin is the botanical precursor to aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid). Vlachojannis et al. (2011, Phytotherapy Research) confirmed analgesic efficacy equivalent to 50-100mg aspirin per cup of willow bark tea.

## 10. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 10.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Pre-Journey (Weeks 1-2):** Practice the Universal Edibility Test on 3 known-safe plants to learn the process. Build and set 5 snares. Catch and clean a fish with improvised tackle. Start a fire with each of the 3 methods from Module 10. These are pass/fail skills — you do not depart until every adult in the group can do all four.
*   **Pre-Journey (Weeks 3-4):** Walk the acclimatization schedule with full pack weight on local terrain. Identify and treat your first blister. Practice the river crossing technique at a shallow, safe location.

### 10.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes
*   **Foraging yields nothing:** Some terrain (dense conifer forest, arid scrubland) has very low edible plant density. Shift effort to fishing and trapping. If near water, prioritize cattail (nearly always present at pond/marsh edges).
*   **Group morale collapse:** If multiple people refuse to continue, declare a mandatory 2-day rest. On Day 2, hold a full group council to honestly assess options (continue, return, split). Forced marching a demoralized group produces injuries and desertion.
*   **Rations exhausted with days remaining:** Cut to 1,500 calories/day from foraged sources. Prioritize fats and starches (acorns, cattail root, fish, animal fat) over greens. Reduce pace to 10 km/day to lower caloric burn. Arrive hungry but alive.

### 10.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** At least 3 adults in every traveling group must be competent foragers, and at least 2 must be competent in trauma medicine (splinting, wound care, rehydration). Single points of failure in these skills are lethal.
