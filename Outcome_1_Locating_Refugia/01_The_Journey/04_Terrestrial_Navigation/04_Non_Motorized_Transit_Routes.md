# Non-Motorized Transit: Moving Without Fuel

**Alignment:** Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia
# மோட்டார் இல்லாத போக்குவரத்து: எரிபொருள் இல்லாமல் நகர்தல்

When motorized transit is unavailable — whether due to fuel exhaustion, infrastructure collapse, or deliberate avoidance of monitored road networks — the traveler must fall back on the oldest transport infrastructure on Earth: trails, waterways, and animal power. This module maps the specific non-motorized route network available in the eastern United States (originating from Georgia) and establishes the general principles for route selection that apply globally.

> **CAUTION**: Long-distance non-motorized transit through unfamiliar terrain carries significant risk. Never attempt a multi-day route without advance scouting, adequate caloric reserves (minimum 3 days surplus), water purification capability, and medical supplies. Travel in groups of 2+ adults minimum.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Physics of Route Selection

Route selection is an optimization problem with three competing variables: **distance**, **energy expenditure**, and **exposure risk**. The optimal route minimizes the sum of these costs, not any single one.

**Distance vs. Energy:** A direct route of 100 km through a mountain pass may require less distance than a 140 km route following a valley, but if the pass involves 2,000 m of cumulative elevation gain, the energy cost is dramatically higher. Using Margaria's equation (see [01. Rationale](../01_Rationale_and_Importance.md)):

*   Valley route (140 km, 200 m total gain): ~140 × 297 kJ/km + 200 × 1,080 kJ/100m = 41,580 + 2,160 = **43,740 kJ** (~10,450 kcal) for a 90 kg person+pack
*   Mountain pass (100 km, 2,000 m total gain): ~100 × 297 + 2,000 × 1,080 = 29,700 + 21,600 = **51,300 kJ** (~12,250 kcal)

The 40% longer valley route costs 15% less energy. In a calorie-limited scenario, the valley route extends your logistical reach by approximately 30 km.

**Energy vs. Exposure:** Following a major highway is the flattest, most direct path between most destinations. It is also the most visible, most patrolled, and most likely to be controlled by hostile actors during a crisis. Off-road routes through forest or along minor waterways trade energy efficiency for concealment. The security-energy tradeoff depends entirely on the threat environment.

### The Rail-Trail Network: Hidden Infrastructure

Across North America (and increasingly in Europe and Australasia), thousands of kilometers of abandoned railway lines have been converted to multi-use paths — **rail-trails**. These represent an extraordinary hidden asset for non-motorized transit:

*   **Grade-controlled:** Railways were engineered to maximum grades of 1-3% (compared to 5-15% for roads). This makes them ideal for wheeled carts, bicycles, and pack animals.
*   **Bridge infrastructure:** Many rail-trails retain their original bridges over rivers and gorges. These are critical chokepoints — a bridge that remains passable saves days of detour to find a ford.
*   **Low visibility:** Rail-trails typically pass through rural corridors, below ridgelines, and along river valleys. They are less monitored than roads and less visible from high ground.
*   **Surface quality:** Crushed gravel or packed earth surfaces are suitable for single-wheel carts and bicycle tires. Paved rail-trails offer the lowest rolling resistance available.

As of 2025, the United States alone has over 40,000 km of rail-trail. The Rails-to-Trails Conservancy maintains a comprehensive atlas. Key strategic corridors:

| Trail / Route | Length | From → To | Grade | Surface |

| Katy Trail (MO) | 386 km | Clinton → Machens (St. Louis area) | <1% (river valley) | Crushed limestone |
| Great Allegheny Passage + C&O Canal | 530 km | Pittsburgh → Washington DC | 1-2% | Crushed limestone / towpath |

### Pack Animals: Multiplying Human Carrying Capacity

When wheeled transport is impractical (dense forest, steep terrain, stream crossings), pack animals extend the group's logistical reach by a factor of 3-5x:

| Animal | Carry Capacity | Daily Range | Terrain Suitability | Feed Requirements |
|--------|---------------|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Mule | 60-90 kg (loaded saddle) | 25-35 km/day on trail | Excellent on rocky/steep terrain, sure-footed | 5-7 kg grain + 7-10 kg hay/forage per day |
| Donkey | 30-50 kg | 15-25 km/day | Excellent on rough terrain, heat-tolerant | 3-5 kg grain + 5-7 kg forage per day |
| Horse | 70-100 kg (loaded) | 30-50 km/day on road | Good on trails, poor on very rocky terrain | 7-10 kg grain + 10-15 kg hay/forage per day |
| Llama | 25-35 kg | 15-25 km/day | Good on mountain terrain, low feed needs | 2-3 kg grain + browse available forage |
| Human (backpack) | 15-25 kg | 15-25 km/day | Universal | 3,000-3,500 kcal food + 3-4 L water |
| Human (cart on trail) | 40-60 kg | 15-20 km/day | Requires trail/road | 3,000-3,500 kcal food + 3-4 L water |

**Mules** are the optimal pack animal for the Appalachian and Ozark terrain: they are more sure-footed than horses on steep, rocky ground; more disease-resistant; they require less feed per kilogram of cargo carried; and they have a practical working lifespan of 30-40 years. The US military used mule trains in mountainous terrain through World War II for precisely these reasons.

## Core Principles

1.  **The Gradient Priority:** Choose routes that minimize total elevation gain, even at the cost of greater horizontal distance. A 1% grade trail allows cart travel at 3-4 km/hr; a 10% grade trail slows to 1-2 km/hr and quintuples energy expenditure per kilometer. Rail-trails are the highest-value transit corridors because they were engineered for minimal grade.

2.  **The Water Proximity Principle:** Route along watercourses whenever possible. Rivers and streams provide: drinking water (after purification), route navigation (rivers are natural "handrails"), cooling in hot weather, fish protein for supplementing carried calories, and concealment (valley routes are harder to observe from surrounding high ground). The tradeoff: river valleys may flood, and valley routes may be controlled by settlements at natural chokepoints (bridges, fords, narrow gorges).

3.  **The Bridge Audit:** Before committing to a route, identify every river crossing. Determine whether a bridge exists and is likely passable. A single impassable river crossing can add 10-50 km of detour to find a ford. In many cases, the availability of bridges determines the entire route.

4.  **The Night Transit Rule:** In high-threat environments, travel between dusk and dawn. Human night vision adapts in 20-30 minutes of darkness (rod cells in the retina reach full sensitivity). Movement at night reduces visual detection to under 100 m in most terrain. The tradeoff: pace drops to 1-2 km/hr on trail, navigation requires compass (not visual landmarks), and injury risk from uneven terrain increases substantially. Reserve night transit for high-risk corridor crossings, not routine travel.

5.  **The Seasonal Window:** Long-distance non-motorized transit must be timed to the seasonal window that maximizes: forage availability (spring/summer/early fall), daylight hours, passable water crossings (avoid spring snowmelt flood season), and moderate temperatures. In the eastern US, the optimal transit window is **April 15 — October 15**. Winter transit is possible but caloric cost increases 30-50% due to cold-weather metabolism and difficult terrain (mud, ice, short days).

## Practical Implementation

### Primary Routes from Georgia, USA

**Route 1: Georgia → Tennessee (Sequatchie Valley) — ~250 km**

*   **Cycling:** US Bicycle Route 21 (USBR 21) connects Atlanta to Chattanooga via a mix of low-traffic roads and designated bike paths. Distance: ~200 km. Time: 2-3 days by bicycle, 10-14 days on foot.
*   **Hiking:** The Appalachian Trail begins at Springer Mountain, GA and enters Tennessee near Great Smoky Mountains NP. This route is extremely steep (cumulative gain >15,000 m) and unsuitable for carts. It is a foot-only route for fit travelers with minimal gear.
*   **Recommended for families with carts:** Follow USBR 21 on road/trail to Chattanooga, then take local rural roads east into the Sequatchie Valley. Total distance ~280 km. Average grade: 2-3%.

**Route 2: Georgia → Missouri (Ozark Plateau) — ~900 km**

*   Atlanta → Nashville via USBR 23 (~400 km). Mix of designated bike routes and rural roads through rolling Appalachian foothills.
*   Nashville → join the TransAmerica Trail (USBR 76) westward into Missouri.
*   Once in Missouri, the **Katy Trail** (386 km of flat, crushed-limestone rail-trail along the Missouri River) provides the fastest, safest, and most energy-efficient transit corridor in the entire route. Grade: <1%. Cart-compatible.
*   Total time estimate: 6-8 weeks on foot with carts (15-20 km/day).

**Route 3: Georgia → Pacific Northwest (WA/OR) — ~5,000 km**

*   Follow the TransAmerica Trail (USBR 76) from Virginia to Astoria, Oregon. This is a multi-month expedition.
*   The route crosses the Great Plains (flat, minimal water, extreme heat in summer), the Rocky Mountains (multiple passes >2,500 m elevation), and the Cascades.
*   **Logistical reach limitation:** At ~200-260 km on carried supplies, this route requires pre-positioned caches or resupply from allied settlements every 10-15 days. Without a waystation network, this route is not self-supported.
*   Recommended only with advance preparation, established waystation chain, and seasonal timing (depart April, cross Rockies by July-August before snow).

### Route Planning Procedure

1.  **Map the corridor:** Using 1:100,000 or 1:250,000 regional maps, draw a straight line from origin to destination. This is the "as-crow-flies" reference.
2.  **Identify rail-trails and waterways** within 20 km of the reference line. These are your primary transit corridors.
3.  **Audit bridges:** Mark every river crossing. Determine bridge status (intact, damaged, destroyed) from the most recent available information. Plan alternative fords for critical crossings.
4.  **Identify resupply points:** Mark water sources at 15-20 km intervals along the route. Identify potential foraging zones (orchards, agricultural land, wild food areas).
5.  **Assess threat corridors:** Mark population centers, military installations, and major highway intersections. Plan routes that maintain minimum 5 km distance from these. Identify alternative routes for high-threat bypass.
6.  **Calculate time and calories:** Using Naismith's Rule and the caloric budget from [01. Rationale](../01_Rationale_and_Importance.md), calculate total transit days and food weight required.

## Common Failure Modes

1.  **Road Dependency:** Defaulting to paved roads because they appear faster on the map. Roads are designed for vehicles, not foot travelers — they follow political boundaries, not terrain logic. They are surveilled, exposed, and often lack water access. **Prevention:** Default to rail-trails and rural paths. Use roads only as connecting segments between trail networks.

2.  **Bridge Assumption:** Planning a route that depends on a bridge without verifying its current condition. Flood damage, deliberate destruction, and structural decay can render bridges impassable. A bridge failure mid-route forces multi-day detours. **Prevention:** Scout critical bridges in advance. Identify fording points as backup for every bridge on the route.

3.  **Water Source Gaps:** In arid regions or during drought, the planned water sources may be dry. Dehydration begins affecting performance within 4-6 hours of inadequate intake. **Prevention:** Carry minimum 3-day water supply (9-12 liters per person). Never allow reserves to drop below 1 day's supply before reaching the next confirmed water source.

4.  **Pack Animal Overwork:** Mules and donkeys that are overloaded or pushed beyond their daily capacity develop back sores, hoof problems, and refuse to move. A lame pack animal becomes a stationary liability. **Prevention:** Follow the 20% rule for pack animals (load should not exceed 20-25% of the animal's body weight for sustained multi-day travel). Rest animals for 10 minutes every 2 hours and remove saddle/panniers during extended rest stops.

5.  **Seasonal Miscalculation:** Starting a multi-week transit too late in the season. Being caught by early snow in mountain passes, or by spring floods at river crossings, can trap a group for days or weeks with dwindling supplies. **Prevention:** Time departure to arrive at the highest/most exposed segment of the route during the mildest weather window. Build 5-7 days of schedule buffer for weather delays.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Rail-Trail:** An abandoned railway corridor converted to a multi-use path. Retains the original railway grade (typically 1-3%) and often retains bridge infrastructure.
*   **Logistical Reach:** The maximum distance a group can travel on carried supplies before caloric reserves are exhausted. Typically 200-260 km for a family of four with 36 kg of rations.
*   **Handrail:** A long linear terrain feature (river, ridge, road, rail-trail) followed to maintain direction and reduce navigation effort.
*   **Waystation:** A concealed supply cache and rest point along a trade or transit route, positioned at one day's travel interval (~15-25 km).
*   **Ford:** A shallow point in a river or stream where the water can be crossed on foot. Identified by wide, shallow sections with gravel or rock bottom.
*   **Travois:** A wheeled or slide-based frame for hauling cargo, pulled by a human or animal using a harness.
*   **USBR (US Bicycle Route):** A federally designated cycling route, typically following a combination of low-traffic roads, bike paths, and rail-trails.
*   **The 50% Rule:** Maximum daily distance for a mixed-ability group equals 50% of the slowest member's comfortable capacity.
*   **Threat Corridor:** A zone of elevated risk along a transit route, such as proximity to population centers, military installations, or controlled chokepoints.
