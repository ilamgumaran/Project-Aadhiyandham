# Geometric Trail Engineering: Strategic Pathfinding

**Alignment:** Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

## 1. Why This Is Important (The Rationale)
A trail is not just a path; it is a piece of infrastructure. If a trail is too steep, it will erode and become impassable for Travois carts. If it is too visible, it compromises the security of the settlement. Geometric Trail Engineering ensures that transit routes are sustainable, energy-efficient, and strategically concealed.

---

## 2. The Geometry of the Slope

### 2.1. The 5% Rule (The Golden Grade)
To move heavy loads (seeds, tools, or building materials) using a manually pulled Travois or cart, the trail must maintain a **5% average grade**.
*   **Calculation:** For every 100 meters of horizontal distance, the trail should rise no more than 5 meters.
*   **The Limit:** Grades exceeding 10% cause rapid fatigue in humans and pack animals and lead to catastrophic water erosion during storms.

### 2.2. Engineering Switchbacks
When the terrain is steeper than 10%, you must use "Switchbacks"—a zig-zag pattern that artificially lengthens the trail to reduce the grade.
1.  **The Turning Platform:** The "apex" of the turn must be a flat, reinforced area (using stone or heavy logs) to allow a cart to turn without tipping.
2.  **The Outslope:** The trail surface must be tilted slightly downward toward the outer edge (approx. 2-3%) to allow rainwater to sheet off the trail rather than running down its length.

---

## 3. Topographical Masking (Stealth Transit)

A refugia depends on being "hard to find." Your trails must not give away your location.

### 3.1. Visual Shadowing
*   **Ridge Line Avoidance:** Never build a trail on the very top of a ridge (the "skyline"). A traveler on a ridge is visible from miles away.
*   **The Military Crest:** Build the trail 10-20 meters below the ridge line on the interior slope. This hides the traveler from observers in the valley below.

### 3.2. Acoustic Masking
*   **Sound Pockets:** Route trails behind natural rock formations or through dense evergreen thickets to dampen the sound of footsteps and cart wheels.
*   **Water Buffering:** If a trail must be near a high-traffic area, route it parallel to a noisy stream or waterfall to mask acoustic signatures.

---

## 4. 🔬 Scientific Validation: Soil Shear Strength
Trail sustainability is a function of civil engineering. 
*   **Compaction:** Intentional compaction of the trail tread increases the soil's "shear strength," making it resistant to the weight of carts.
*   **The Half-Rule:** A trail's grade should never exceed half the grade of the hillside it is built on. If the hill is a 20% slope, the trail must be 10% or less to prevent the hill from "sliding" onto the path.

## 5. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 5.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Phase 1: Sighting:** Use your Clinometer (Module 02.02) to flag a 5% route up your primary access slope.
*   **Phase 2: The Bench Cut:** Dig into the hillside to create a flat "bench" for the trail. Use the excavated soil to build the outer edge (the "fill").
*   **Phase 3: Armoring:** Place flat stones in areas where the trail crosses seasonal water runoff to prevent mud-pits.

### 5.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes
*   **Gullying:** If a trail turns into a stream during rain, your Outslope is insufficient. Re-rake the trail to ensure water flows *across* it, not *down* it.
*   **Shortcut Erosion:** If people cut across switchbacks, they will create vertical erosion scars. Plant thorny vegetation (Module 03.05) in the gaps between switchbacks to force adherence to the geometric path.

### 5.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **The Trail Master:** Trails require annual maintenance. Every spring, the community should perform a "Perimeter Walk" to clear fallen trees and restore the outslope.
