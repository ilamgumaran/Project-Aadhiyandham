## 1. Why This Is Important

You have walked for weeks or months. Rations are low, the group is exhausted, and everyone wants to stop. The temptation to declare "this is the place" without rigorous assessment is overwhelming. But settling on the wrong site — a floodplain, an unstable slope, infertile soil, or a location that cannot be found again by others — wastes months of construction labor and may force a second relocation that the community cannot survive.

This module provides the 14-day arrival protocol: a structured assessment that prevents exhaustion-driven bad decisions while producing a definitive stay/continue/adapt verdict.

---

## 2. The First 48 Hours: Secure Before You Assess

Do NOT begin site assessment on arrival. The group needs safety, rest, and water before making rational decisions.

### 2.1. Hour 0-2: Immediate Perimeter

1. **Halt on high ground** overlooking the candidate area — not in it. Hilltops, ridgelines, or elevated terraces provide observation and drainage.
2. **360-degree visual scan.** Note: water sources visible? Open terrain or dense forest? Signs of human habitation? Animal trails? Smoke?
3. **Designate a temporary camp** on the high ground. Set up tarp shelters, establish a fire, post a sentry.

### 2.2. Hours 2-12: Water and Reconnaissance

4. **Water party (2-3 people):** Locate the nearest flowing water. Boil and drink before filtering — exhausted groups need water immediately. Assess: Is it clear? Does it flow year-round (check for wet-season debris marks above current level)? Is it upstream of any contamination source?
5. **Scout party (2-3 people):** Walk the immediate perimeter (500m radius). Note: ground slope and stability, vegetation type and density, animal signs, evidence of flooding (debris lines, grey soil), and any structures or sign of prior habitation.
6. **Everyone else rests.** No assessment decisions are made on Day 1.

### 2.3. Hours 12-48: Rest and Repair

*   Full rest. Repair equipment, treat injuries, wash clothing.
*   Set snare lines and fishing lines (food procurement continues).
*   Boil and store water.
*   **No construction. No permanent decisions.**

## 3. Days 3-7: The Rapid Site Assessment

Beginning on Day 3, execute these tests systematically. Each test produces a PASS, MARGINAL, or FAIL result. Record everything.

### 3.1. Test 1: Water Sovereignty (Day 3)

**From Module 05 (Hydrological Analysis):**

1. Identify ALL water sources within 1 km: springs, streams, ponds, seeps.
2. Measure the primary stream flow using the float method (Module 10, Climate Thresholds): velocity × cross-section = liters/second.
3. Calculate: Can this flow sustain the group at 50 L/person/day?
4. **Confirm gravity feed:** Is the water source ABOVE the candidate building area? If you must pump water uphill, the site is marginal.

| Result | Verdict |
|:---|:---|
| 2+ independent water sources, gravity-fed, flow sustains 50 L/person/day | **PASS** |
| 1 water source, gravity-fed, flow sustains 15 L/person/day minimum | **MARGINAL** — develop backup (rainwater catchment, well) |
| No flowing water, or flow below 15 L/person/day | **FAIL** — do not settle |

### 3.2. Test 2: Soil Fertility (Days 3-4)

**From Module 11 (Soil and Water Quality Baselines):**

1. Dig 5 test pits across the candidate growing area. Record depth to rock/hardpan.
2. Observe wild plant communities (nitrogen indicators).
3. Pull 3 wild plants and examine roots (phosphorus indicator via mycorrhizal colonization).
4. Perform the ash-water test on burned local vegetation (potassium indicator).
5. Score using the Site Fertility Scorecard (Module 11).

| Scorecard Total | Verdict |
|:---|:---|
| 18-24 | **PASS** — prime growing land |
| 12-17 | **MARGINAL** — viable with 2-3 years of composting/biochar amendment |
| 0-11 | **FAIL** — land cannot support sustained agriculture |

### 3.3. Test 3: Geological Stability (Days 4-5)

**From Module 09 (Geological Stability Assessment):**

1. Walk all slopes above and below the candidate site. Look for curved trees, tension cracks, mid-slope springs, crescent-shaped scars.
2. Measure the steepest slope angle above the site.
3. Test bedrock with vinegar (limestone = karst risk). Look for slickensides (fault activity).
4. Identify the flood boundary (debris line, vegetation transition, soil color change).
5. Note any volcanic indicators.

| Result | Verdict |
|:---|:---|
| No active geological indicators. Slope <20°. Above flood line. No karst. | **PASS** |
| 1-2 moderate indicators with clear mitigation (e.g., minor karst but good spring system; moderate slope but stable vegetation) | **MARGINAL** — settle with monitoring protocol |
| Active tension cracks, slope >30° above site, on floodplain, active sinkholes, or in volcanic lahar valley | **FAIL** — do not settle |

### 3.4. Test 4: Solar and Thermal Viability (Days 5-6)

**From Module 03 (Solar and Thermal Balance):**

1. Observe sun exposure at the candidate building site. Does it receive direct morning light within 30 minutes of sunrise?
2. Is there a south-facing (Northern Hemisphere) or north-facing (Southern Hemisphere) slope for primary food production?
3. Estimate latitude using Polaris or noon shadow method (Module 08, Location Recording).
4. Assess wind exposure — is the site sheltered from prevailing winter winds by terrain or forest?

| Result | Verdict |
|:---|:---|
| Morning light, equator-facing slope, wind-sheltered, in the thermal belt | **PASS** |
| Partial shading from terrain/forest, but correctable (tree removal, site adjustment) | **MARGINAL** |
| North-facing (NH) or south-facing (SH), permanent shadow, fully wind-exposed | **FAIL** |

### 3.5. Test 5: Defensibility and Isolation (Days 6-7)

**From Modules 02 (Topographical Resilience) and 07 (Passive Defense):**

1. How many approaches lead to the site? Fewer is better (2-3 controllable access points is ideal).
2. Is the site visible from long distance? (Visible = vulnerable to discovery by hostile groups.)
3. Are natural barriers present (cliffs, rivers, dense forest) that limit approach?
4. Is the site close enough to a major route/river for trade networking but far enough for security? Target: 5-15 km from a primary travel corridor.

| Result | Verdict |
|:---|:---|
| Limited approaches, natural barriers, concealed from distance, 5-15 km from trade route | **PASS** |
| Partially concealed, some natural barriers, 3-5 km or 15-30 km from trade route | **MARGINAL** |
| Fully exposed, unlimited approach angles, on or adjacent to a major route | **FAIL** |

## 4. Days 8-14: The Verdict

### 4.1. The Scorecard

| Test | Result | Weight |
|:---|:---|:---|
| 1. Water Sovereignty | PASS / MARGINAL / FAIL | **Critical** — FAIL = automatic rejection |
| 2. Soil Fertility | PASS / MARGINAL / FAIL | High |
| 3. Geological Stability | PASS / MARGINAL / FAIL | **Critical** — FAIL = automatic rejection |
| 4. Solar/Thermal | PASS / MARGINAL / FAIL | High |
| 5. Defensibility | PASS / MARGINAL / FAIL | Moderate |

### 4.2. Decision Matrix

| Scenario | Decision |
|:---|:---|
| **All PASS** | Settle. Begin permanent construction (Module 07, Site Layout). |
| **No FAIL, 1-2 MARGINAL** | Settle with mitigation. Record which parameters are marginal and establish monitoring per Modules 09-11. Plan remediation (composting for soil, water infrastructure for marginal hydrology). |
| **1 FAIL on non-critical test** (Soil or Solar) | Consider settling IF all other tests PASS and the FAIL is remediable within 2-3 years (e.g., poor soil can be amended; partial shade can be cleared). If the group is too exhausted to continue, this is the acceptable compromise. |
| **1 FAIL on critical test** (Water or Geology) | Do NOT settle. These cannot be remediated. Rest for 3-5 days, resupply water and food, then continue to the next candidate site. |
| **2+ FAIL** | Do NOT settle under any circumstances. |

### 4.3. The "Good Enough" Doctrine

Perfection does not exist. Every site has compromises. The question is not "Is this site perfect?" but "Can we survive here for 50 years if we invest the effort to remediate the marginal parameters?"

A site with PASS on water and geology, MARGINAL on soil and defensibility, and PASS on solar is a viable long-term home. A site with PASS on everything except water is a death trap regardless of how beautiful or fertile it appears.

## 5. If the Site Fails: The Continue Protocol

If the verdict is FAIL:

1. **Do not panic.** A failed site assessment is a success — you avoided a catastrophic mistake.
2. **Rest 3-5 days minimum.** Resupply water, forage, repair equipment.
3. **Reassess route.** Identify the next candidate site from pre-journey research or from information gathered during transit (local knowledge, visible terrain features suggesting a better valley/ridge).
4. **Record the failed site.** Document WHY it failed — this data prevents future groups from wasting time assessing the same location. Inscribe on stone or durable hide and leave at the approach point.
5. **Morale management.** The group will be devastated. Acknowledge the disappointment explicitly. Remind the group that the assessment protocol WORKED — it prevented them from building on a site that would have killed them. Then walk.

## 6. Matching a Pre-Selected Candidate Site

If the group is traveling to a specific location from the Candidate Atlas:

### 6.1. Verification Checklist

| Atlas Parameter | Field Verification Method |
|:---|:---|
| Coordinates / Latitude | Polaris angle or noon shadow ratio (Module 08) — should match within ±2° |
| Elevation | Relative: are you above the valley floor? Absolute: compare vegetation zones to latitude expectations |
| Sentinel landmarks | Identify the 3 landmarks described in the atlas entry. If 2+ match, you are in the correct area. |
| Primary water source | Find the river/spring described. Measure flow. Compare to atlas estimate. |
| Soil type | Perform the ribbon test and vinegar test. Compare to atlas description. |
| Biome | Does the forest/grassland type match? (Temperate broadleaf ≠ conifer; the atlas entry specifies which.) |

### 6.2. If the Site Has Changed

Climate shift, wildfire, flood, or landslide may have altered the site since the atlas entry was written. If the terrain does not match:

*   **Minor change** (fire cleared some forest, river shifted 50m): The site is still valid. Adjust plans.
*   **Major change** (landslide destroyed the candidate bench, river has cut a new channel through the building area, spring has dried up): Treat as a FAIL. The geological or hydrological conditions that made this site attractive no longer exist. Continue to the next candidate.

## 7. The First Permanent Action

Once the verdict is SETTLE:

1. **Mark the site.** Build the arrival cairn (Module 08, Location Recording) on the most visible approach ridgeline.
2. **Record the Sentinels.** Measure bearings to 3 permanent landmarks using the equinox fist-width method (or compass with declination correction).
3. **Establish the water point.** The first permanent construction is the water collection/filtration system, not shelter. Tarp shelters sustain for weeks; dehydration kills in days.
4. **Plant the first seeds.** If arriving in a planting season, get heirloom seeds into the ground within the first week. Even a small test plot begins the food sovereignty clock.

## 8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

*   **Rapid Site Assessment:** A structured 5-test evaluation protocol executed over 5 days to determine whether a candidate location meets minimum viability thresholds for permanent settlement.
*   **Critical Test:** A site evaluation parameter (water sovereignty, geological stability) whose failure cannot be remediated and therefore automatically disqualifies a site regardless of other scores.
*   **The Good Enough Doctrine:** The principle that site selection should optimize for remediable compromises rather than seeking perfection — a site with fixable weaknesses is superior to continued travel that exhausts the group.
*   **Continue Protocol:** The structured decision to abandon a failed candidate site, incorporating rest, resupply, documentation, morale management, and route reassessment.

---

## 9. Scientific Validation & Research Context

*   **Settlement Siting Failures:** Archaeological research on Norse Greenland settlements (Diamond, 2005; Dugmore et al., 2012) documents that Norse colonists selected sites based on immediate visual appeal (green pastures) without assessing long-term soil depletion rates or climate trajectory. The resulting agricultural collapse after 400 years of occupation validates the requirement for quantified soil and climate thresholds before commitment.
*   **Decision Fatigue Under Stress:** Kahneman (2011, "Thinking, Fast and Slow") documents that cognitive performance degrades under physical exhaustion and emotional stress, producing systematic bias toward optimistic assessment. The 48-hour rest period before assessment is designed to counter this effect.
*   **Water as Primary Constraint:** The WHO and UNHCR refugee settlement guidelines establish 15 L/person/day as the absolute minimum for survival and 50 L/person/day as the standard for sustainable community living, confirming the water thresholds used in Test 1.

## 10. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

### 10.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan
*   **Days 1-2:** Secure. Rest. Do not assess. Do not build. Drink water, treat injuries, sleep.
*   **Days 3-7:** Execute the 5 tests, one per day. Record results on paper or bark. Do not discuss the verdict until all 5 tests are complete — partial data produces premature conclusions.
*   **Day 8:** Gather the group. Present all 5 results. Apply the decision matrix openly. The verdict must be reached by consensus — if even one experienced member believes the site is FAIL on a critical parameter, their concern must be addressed with evidence, not overruled by exhaustion.
*   **Days 9-14:** If SETTLE: begin the first permanent actions (cairn, sentinels, water point, seeds). If CONTINUE: rest, resupply, and depart on Day 14.

### 10.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes
*   **Group overrides FAIL verdict out of exhaustion:** This is the single most dangerous decision a group can make. If water or geology fails, no amount of effort will fix it. A community built on a failing water source will spend increasing labor hauling water from farther away until the labor cost exceeds the group's capacity. Remind the group: walking for 2 more weeks is survivable. Building for 6 months on a bad site and then being forced to relocate is not.
*   **Cannot complete all 5 tests (weather, injury, terrain):** If a test cannot be completed, score it MARGINAL by default. Do not score it PASS — an untested parameter is an unknown risk.

### 10.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing
*   **Apprenticeship:** The arrival assessment requires competence in hydrology, soil science, geology, and navigation. At least 2 people in the group must be able to execute all 5 tests independently. Train before departure, not on arrival.
