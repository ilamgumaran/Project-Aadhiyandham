# Topographical Resilience: Land Selection Guide

**Alignment:** Outcome 1 — Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

> **CAUTION**: Slope assessment requires traversing steep terrain. Never approach unstable slopes, cliff edges, or saturated clay hillsides after rain. Test soil stability from a safe position before walking on suspect slopes.

---

## Theoretical Foundation

Topography is the single most consequential variable in settlement siting. Unlike structures, which decay, or crops, which fail seasonally, the shape of the land persists on geological timescales. A settlement placed correctly with respect to slope, aspect, drainage, and wind exposure inherits centuries of passive protection at zero energy cost. A settlement placed incorrectly — on a ridgeline, a floodplain, or a saturated clay slope — inherits centuries of compounding risk.

The following principles are drawn from geomorphology, micrometeorology, and hydrology. They are quantitative. Feelings about a "nice view" or "flat ground" are not substitutes for measurement.

### Slope Stability and the Angle of Repose

Every soil type has a maximum stable angle — the steepest slope at which loose material will remain stationary under gravity alone. Exceeding this angle guarantees eventual failure.

| Material | Angle of Repose (dry) | Angle of Repose (saturated) |
|---|---|---|
| Fine sand | 30-35° | 15-25° |
| Coarse gravel | 35-40° | 30-35° |
| Dry clay | 40-45° | 15-25° |
| Rocky talus | 35-40° | 30-38° |
| Vegetated soil (dense roots) | Up to 60° | 35-45° |

Root cohesion adds 5-20 kPa of shear strength to soil. This is why deforested slopes fail catastrophically — the removal of root networks eliminates the mechanical reinforcement that was holding the slope together.

The **Factor of Safety (FoS)** is the ratio of resisting forces to driving forces acting on a slope:

    FoS = Resisting Forces / Driving Forces

- FoS < 1.0: slope is actively failing.
- FoS = 1.0-1.25: marginally stable; any disturbance (rain, vibration, excavation) triggers failure.
- FoS = 1.25-1.5: conditionally stable; acceptable for temporary structures only.
- FoS > 1.5: minimum threshold for permanent structures and habitation.

Settlements on slopes steeper than the material's angle of repose will experience landslides. This is not a probability — it is a certainty. The only variable is timing.

### Drainage and Flood Recurrence

Every stream has a floodplain defined by recurrence intervals. The "100-year floodplain" has a 1% chance of flooding in any given year. This sounds rare. Over a 500-year settlement horizon, the probability of at least one such flood is:

    P = 1 - (1 - 0.01)^500 = 1 - 0.99^500 = 0.9934 = 99.3%

Never build in the 100-year floodplain. Over multi-generational timescales, flooding is not a risk — it is a scheduled event.

Identify the floodplain by observable field evidence:

1. **Flat terrain adjacent to a stream** — alluvial plains are depositional surfaces created by repeated flooding.
2. **Alluvial soil deposits** — rounded gravel, sorted silt layers, and fine clay deposits indicate water-transported material.
3. **Flood debris high-water marks on trees** — look for lines of deposited debris, bark scarring, and sediment staining on tree trunks above the current water level.
4. **Vegetation transition** — the boundary between wetland species (cattails, willows, sedges) and upland species (oaks, pines) often marks the edge of the active floodplain.

### Wind Dynamics and Terrain

Wind accelerates over ridgelines due to the Venturi effect — compression of airflow as it is forced over a terrain obstacle increases velocity by 20-50% compared to the free-stream wind speed. A ridgeline that appears "sheltered" on a calm day becomes a wind tunnel during storms.

The **military crest** — the position just below the true ridgeline on the leeward side — experiences 40-60% less wind than the true ridgeline while maintaining good visibility of the surrounding terrain. This is the optimal position for structures that require both shelter and defensive sightlines.

Wind pressure scales with the square of velocity:

    P = 0.5 * ρ * v²

Where ρ is air density (~1.225 kg/m³ at sea level) and v is wind speed in m/s. A 50% increase in wind speed (from ridgeline acceleration) produces a 125% increase in wind pressure on structures. This is why ridgeline buildings fail first.

### Thermal Belt Physics

Radiative cooling on clear nights causes the ground surface to lose heat at approximately 50-100 W/m². The air in contact with the cooling ground becomes cold and dense. Cold air is measurably heavier than warm air:

- At 0°C: ρ ≈ 1.29 kg/m³
- At 20°C: ρ ≈ 1.20 kg/m³

This density differential drives **katabatic winds** — gravity-driven drainage of cold air downslope at 1-3 m/s on smooth slopes. The cold air pools in valley floors and topographic depressions, creating temperature inversions where the valley floor may be 5-10°C colder than the mid-slope position.

The **thermal belt** is the elevation band between the cold valley floor and the wind-exposed ridgeline. Its characteristics depend on valley geometry:

- **Narrow valleys** (V-shaped, <500m wide): thin, well-defined thermal belts, typically 50-150m in vertical extent. Cold air drains efficiently; the thermal belt is sharp and reliable.
- **Wide valleys** (U-shaped, >1km wide): broad, diffuse thermal belts. Cold air pools in a wide, shallow lake; the thermal belt boundaries are less distinct.
- **Enclosed basins** (no drainage outlet): severe cold air pooling. The entire basin floor becomes a frost hollow. The thermal belt may be the only habitable zone.

Measure thermal belt boundaries by placing thermometers — or, in the absence of instruments, water containers in tin cups — at 50m elevation intervals from valley floor to ridgeline during clear, calm nights. The elevation band where temperatures remain warmest (or where water does not freeze when it freezes at lower elevations) is the thermal belt.

### Aspect and Microclimate

The orientation of a slope relative to the sun — its **aspect** — determines the total solar radiation received and thus the microclimate. The effect is large and quantifiable.

At 40°N latitude, a 20° south-facing slope receives approximately 35% more annual solar radiation than a 20° north-facing slope. This translates to measurable agricultural and habitability differences:

- **2-4 weeks longer growing season** on south-facing slopes.
- **3-5°C higher mean soil temperature in spring**, which is critical for seed germination (most temperate crops require soil temperatures above 10°C to germinate).
- **20-30% higher evapotranspiration**, which means south-facing slopes are warmer but drier. This enables warm-season crops at higher elevations but demands reliable water access.

East-facing slopes warm earlier in the day (receiving morning sun) but cool faster in the afternoon. West-facing slopes accumulate afternoon heat but are exposed to afternoon convective storms in many climates. The optimal compromise for habitation is south-to-southeast aspect, which captures the morning warming advantage while avoiding the worst afternoon storm exposure.

### Historical Precedent: The Ancestral Puebloans

The Ancestral Puebloans at Mesa Verde (600-1300 CE) selected south-facing cliff alcoves for their settlements. The geometry was precise: in winter, the low sun angle allowed solar radiation to penetrate deep into the alcove, providing passive heating. In summer, the high sun angle meant the overhang blocked direct sunlight, providing natural shade and cooling.

This is identical to the principle underlying modern passive solar architecture — the relationship between solar altitude angle and overhang geometry. The Puebloans discovered it empirically through centuries of observation and applied it without instruments or formal physics. Their site selection persisted for 700 years. The principle is timeless because it is rooted in orbital mechanics, not technology.

## Core Principles

1. **The Military Crest Rule.** Build just below the ridgeline on the leeward side. This position provides maximum wind shelter (40-60% reduction compared to the true ridgeline) while maintaining visibility of the surrounding terrain. Never build on the ridgeline itself — wind exposure, lightning strike frequency, and lack of water access make ridgeline habitation unsustainable.

2. **The Floodplain Prohibition.** Never build in the 100-year floodplain. Over a 500-year settlement horizon, the probability of catastrophic flooding approaches certainty (99.3%). Identify the floodplain by soil composition (alluvial deposits), vegetation (wetland species), debris evidence (high-water marks), and terrain geometry (flat ground adjacent to streams). When in doubt, build higher.

3. **The Slope Stability Margin.** All permanent structures require a Factor of Safety greater than 1.5. Before building, determine the soil type and its angle of repose. Test slope stability in the field (see Practical Implementation below). Saturated clay slopes that appear stable in dry weather may have an FoS below 1.0 after prolonged rain — always assess worst-case conditions.

4. **The Aspect Optimization.** In the Northern Hemisphere, orient primary growing areas and habitation to south-facing slopes. The 35% solar radiation advantage on south-facing slopes translates directly to longer growing seasons, warmer soils, and the ability to grow warm-season crops at higher elevations. Verify that water supply is sufficient to offset the increased evapotranspiration on these warmer slopes.

5. **The Thermal Belt Priority.** Site habitation within the thermal belt — the elevation band between the cold valley floor and the wind-exposed ridgeline. Verify the thermal belt location through multi-night temperature observations at multiple elevations. A minimum of 3 clear-night observations is required to establish reliable boundaries, as weather patterns shift thermal belt position.

## Practical Implementation

### Clinometer Construction (The Slope Meter)

To identify the 2% to 10% grade needed for drainage — and to measure steeper slopes for stability assessment — you must be able to measure the slope of the land.

**Materials Needed:**

- **A Base:** A flat piece of wood or a salvaged protractor.
- **The Plumb Line:** A piece of string or fishing line, 15-20 cm.
- **The Weight:** A small stone or heavy nut tied to the string end.
- **Sighting Tube:** A hollow reed or a straw affixed along the flat edge.

**Assembly Instructions:**

1. Attach the string to the center-point of your protractor (or the top center of your flat wood piece).
2. Hang the weight at the end of the string so it swings freely.
3. Affix the sighting tube along the flat, straight edge of the base.
4. **To Use:** Look through the tube at a target (another person of the same height, or a marked stick at eye-height) up or down the slope. The angle where the plumb string crosses the protractor scale indicates the slope angle.

**Calibration Procedure:**

1. On a known level surface (still water, a spirit level reference), verify that the plumb line reads 0°.
2. To verify angle accuracy, construct a right triangle with known dimensions — a 1m horizontal base and a 0.577m vertical rise produces a 30° angle. Sight along the hypotenuse and confirm the clinometer reads 30° (±2°).
3. If the reading is consistently off by a fixed amount, note the offset and apply it as a correction to all future readings.

**Accuracy:** A well-constructed field clinometer achieves ±1-2° accuracy, which is sufficient for slope stability assessment and site selection.

**Degrees to Percent Grade Conversion Table:**

| Degrees | Percent Grade | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 2° | 3.5% | Minimum drainage slope |
| 5° | 8.7% | Good drainage, easy walking |
| 10° | 17.6% | Moderate slope, terracing beneficial |
| 15° | 26.8% | Steep for agriculture, terracing required |
| 20° | 36.4% | Limit for most cultivation |
| 30° | 57.7% | Approaching angle of repose for sand |
| 45° | 100.0% | 1:1 rise-to-run ratio; very steep |

### The Slope Stability Field Test

Two field methods allow comparative assessment of slope stability without laboratory equipment.

**Stake Loading Test:**

1. Drive a sharpened wooden stake into the slope at a 45° angle to the surface, with the stake leaning into the uphill direction.
2. Hang a bag from the uphill side of the stake at the soil surface.
3. Progressively add weight to the bag (rocks of known approximate mass).
4. When the stake begins to tilt downhill, record the approximate load. This force approximates the soil's shear resistance at that point.
5. Repeat at multiple candidate sites. The site where the stake supports the greatest load before tilting has the highest shear strength.

**Shovel Test (Slip Plane Detection):**

1. Dig a pit approximately 60 cm deep on any suspect slope.
2. Examine the exposed soil profile. Look for distinct layers of different soil types — particularly clay over gravel, or clay over bedrock.
3. If you observe such layering, the interface between layers is a potential **slip plane**. Water infiltrating through permeable upper layers accumulates at the impermeable clay boundary, lubricating the interface and enabling the entire upper mass to slide.
4. Slopes with visible slip planes in the soil profile are unsuitable for permanent structures unless the FoS can be verified by other means.

### Floodplain Identification Checklist

Evaluate each candidate building site against the following 8-point checklist. Answer each question "Yes" or "No."

| # | Indicator | Yes/No |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Flat terrain within 200m horizontal distance of a stream or river? | |
| 2 | Rounded gravel or sorted silt deposits visible in the soil? | |
| 3 | Debris lines on trees more than 1m above current water level? | |
| 4 | Wetland vegetation (cattails, willows, sedges) present nearby? | |
| 5 | Soil saturated within 30 cm of the surface (dig a test pit)? | |
| 6 | Multiple old stream channels or braided flow paths visible? | |
| 7 | Local residents or oral history report past flooding at this location? | |
| 8 | Terrain elevation lower than the surrounding stream banks? | |

**Decision rule:** If 3 or more answers are "Yes," the site is in or adjacent to the active floodplain. Do not build here. Move to higher ground and re-evaluate.

### Thermal Belt Mapping Protocol

1. Select a clear, calm evening with no forecast for wind or cloud cover. Wind mixes the air column and destroys the thermal stratification you are trying to measure.
2. Place water containers (tin cups or shallow metal vessels filled to a uniform depth of 2 cm) at 50m elevation intervals from the valley floor to the ridgeline. If thermometers are available, place those as well.
3. At dawn, check all containers. The lowest elevation where water did NOT freeze, while water at lower elevations DID freeze, marks the **bottom boundary** of the thermal belt.
4. The **top boundary** of the thermal belt is the elevation where sustained wind becomes noticeable — where the sheltering effect of terrain gives way to ridgeline exposure. Assess this with cloth flag indicators (see Wind Exposure Assessment below).
5. **Repeat on a minimum of 3 different clear nights** over at least a 2-week period. Thermal belt boundaries shift with seasonal changes in sun angle, soil moisture, and prevailing weather patterns. A single observation is unreliable.
6. Mark the verified thermal belt boundaries on your site map. All primary habitation and perennial crop plantings should be sited within this band.

### Wind Exposure Assessment

1. Place lightweight flags — strips of cloth (approximately 5 cm x 30 cm) tied to stakes at 1.5m height — at each candidate building site.
2. Observe the flags daily for a minimum of 7 consecutive days, at both morning and evening. Record the condition using the following scale:

| Rating | Flag Behavior | Wind Speed (approx.) |
|---|---|---|
| Calm | Flag hangs limp | < 1 m/s |
| Light | Flag barely moves, occasional flutter | 1-3 m/s |
| Moderate | Flag extends at ~45° from pole | 3-6 m/s |
| Strong | Flag extends horizontally, snapping | 6-10 m/s |
| Severe | Flag horizontal, fabric begins to tear | > 10 m/s |

3. **Decision rule:** Sites with more than 3 days of "Strong" or worse during the observation period are unsuitable for primary habitation without substantial windbreak investment (living hedgerows requiring 5-10 years to mature, or stone walls of 2m+ height).

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Building on the ridgeline.** Wind exposure at the ridgeline is 20-50% higher than at the military crest. Lightning strike frequency increases with elevation and exposure. Water must be carried uphill or pumped. Every structural repair is more difficult in constant wind. The view is not worth the cost.

2. **Valley floor habitation.** Cold air pools on the valley floor, creating temperature inversions 5-10°C below the thermal belt. Flooding risk is highest at the lowest elevations. Humidity from standing water and poor air drainage breeds insects and fungal pathogens. Drainage is slow on flat ground, saturating foundations. The ease of building on flat ground is a trap.

3. **Ignoring saturated clay slopes.** Dry clay has an angle of repose of 40-45° and appears impressively stable. Saturated clay drops to 15-25°. A slope that held firm through an entire dry season can fail catastrophically after 48 hours of rain. Always assess slope stability under worst-case (saturated) conditions, not fair-weather conditions.

4. **South-slope water deficit.** South-facing slopes receive 35% more solar radiation, which is excellent for growing seasons — but also drives 20-30% higher evapotranspiration. A south-facing slope without reliable water access will desiccate crops and soil faster than a north-facing slope. Always verify that water supply matches the increased demand before committing to south-aspect siting.

5. **Single-night thermal measurement.** One clear night of temperature observation is not sufficient to establish thermal belt boundaries. Weather patterns, seasonal shifts, and transient conditions (recent rain saturating the ground, unusual wind patterns) can move the thermal belt by 50-100m in elevation. A minimum of 3 observations on separate clear, calm nights is required for a reliable determination.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Angle of Repose:** The steepest angle at which a given granular material remains stable under gravity. Exceeding this angle guarantees slope failure.
- **ASL (Above Sea Level):** Elevation measured relative to mean sea level. The fundamental vertical reference for all site assessment.
- **Aspect:** The compass direction a slope faces. Determines solar radiation receipt, temperature regime, and moisture balance.
- **Cold Air Drainage:** The gravity-driven flow of dense, cold air downslope into valleys and depressions during clear nights. Synonymous with katabatic flow.
- **Factor of Safety (FoS):** The ratio of forces resisting slope failure to forces driving slope failure. FoS > 1.5 is the minimum for permanent structures.
- **Floodplain:** The flat area adjacent to a stream or river that is periodically inundated during high-water events. Defined by recurrence interval.
- **Katabatic Wind:** A drainage wind caused by radiative cooling of air on a slope surface. Cold, dense air flows downhill under gravity at 1-3 m/s.
- **Leeward:** The side of a terrain feature sheltered from the prevailing wind. The "shadow" side.
- **Military Crest:** The position on a slope just below the true ridgeline on the leeward side. Provides wind protection while maintaining visibility.
- **Orography:** The study of mountains and elevated terrain and their effects on atmospheric processes, including orographic lift, rain shadows, and wind acceleration.
- **Recurrence Interval:** The average time between events of a given magnitude (e.g., a "100-year flood" has a 1% annual probability). Does not mean the event occurs on a regular schedule.
- **Slip Plane:** An interface between soil layers (typically clay over gravel or bedrock) where water accumulation reduces friction and enables mass movement of the overlying material.
- **Thermal Belt:** The elevation band on a slope between the cold valley floor and the wind-exposed ridgeline where nighttime temperatures are warmest due to cold air drainage below and radiative exposure above.
- **Venturi Effect:** The increase in fluid (or air) velocity when it is forced through a constriction — in terrain, this occurs as wind is compressed over ridgelines and through gaps, increasing speed by 20-50%.
- **Windward:** The side of a terrain feature facing directly into the prevailing wind. The "exposed" side.

- Geiger, R. *The Climate Near the Ground.* Harvard University Press, 1965. The foundational reference on micrometeorology — thermal belts, cold air drainage, slope climates, and the physics of the atmospheric boundary layer at terrain scale.
- Selby, M.J. *Hillslope Materials and Processes.* Oxford University Press, 1993. Comprehensive treatment of slope stability, soil mechanics, mass movement classification, and the geomorphic processes that shape hillslopes over time.
- Leopold, L.B., Wolman, M.G., and Miller, J.P. *Fluvial Processes in Geomorphology.* W.H. Freeman, 1964. The standard text on river dynamics, flood recurrence, channel geometry, and the formation of floodplains and alluvial terraces.
