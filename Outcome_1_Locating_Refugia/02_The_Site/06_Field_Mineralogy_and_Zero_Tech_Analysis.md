# Field Mineralogy: Zero-Tech Chemical Analysis

**Alignment:** Outcome 1 — Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

> **CAUTION**: When testing minerals with vinegar (acetic acid), avoid contact with eyes. When digging test pits deeper than 1 meter, shore the sides to prevent collapse — soil cave-ins are a leading cause of excavation death.

---

## Theoretical Foundation

### Why Minerals Matter for Civilization

Every technological advance from the Neolithic to the Industrial Revolution required specific mineral resources. A settlement that cannot identify and access local minerals is condemned to stagnation. Three minerals form the irreducible foundation:

**Lime**, derived from limestone (CaCO3), is the single most critical mineral resource for a rebuilding civilization. Limestone, when calcined at approximately 900 degrees Celsius, yields quicklime (CaO), which in turn enables mortar for permanent construction, plaster for waterproof surfaces, water purification through flocculation, soil amendment for acidic fields, tanning of hides into leather, and soap-making when combined with rendered fat. No other mineral provides such breadth of application. A site without limestone within practical hauling distance — roughly 10 kilometers by hand-cart — is severely compromised.

**Clay**, composed of hydrated aluminum silicates (primarily kaolinite, Al2Si2O5(OH)4), enables the second pillar of material civilization: ceramics. Fired clay produces storage vessels for grain and water, waterproof linings for reservoirs and cisterns, brick for construction, kiln furniture for metallurgy, and pipe for water conveyance. Unfired clay serves as daub for wattle walls, sealant for wooden vessels, and filtration medium for water treatment. Clay deposits are geologically common but vary enormously in quality; the field tests described below distinguish usable pottery clay from inadequate silty deposits.

**Iron**, present as hematite (Fe2O3), magnetite (Fe3O4), or bog iron (FeOOH), enables the third pillar: durable tools. Stone tools suffice for initial survival, but agricultural expansion, timber harvesting, and construction all require iron edges. Bog iron — hydrated iron oxide precipitated in marshes and stream beds by iron-oxidizing bacteria — is the most accessible form, requiring no mining. It appears as orange-brown, porous, crusty masses in wetland soils and stream banks. Without local access to at least one iron source, a settlement cannot progress beyond a Neolithic level of tool capability.

Without local access to all three of these minerals, a settlement cannot sustain technological progress beyond the Paleolithic.

### Soil Chemistry Fundamentals

Soil pH governs nutrient availability and is therefore the single most important chemical parameter for agricultural site selection. At pH 6.0 to 7.0, most plant nutrients — nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, calcium, magnesium, and sulfur — are maximally available in the soil solution. Below pH 5.5, aluminum ions become soluble and toxic to most crop plants, while calcium and magnesium become deficient. Above pH 8.0, iron and phosphorus form insoluble compounds and become unavailable despite being physically present in the soil.

Soil pH is determined primarily by parent rock. Limestone parent material produces alkaline soils in the range of pH 7.0 to 8.5. Granite parent material weathers to acidic soils in the range of pH 4.5 to 6.0. Sandstone-derived soils vary depending on the cementing mineral — silica-cemented sandstone yields acidic soils, calcite-cemented sandstone yields alkaline. Rainfall also matters: in high-rainfall areas (greater than 1000 millimeters annually), base cations leach from the topsoil, driving pH downward over time regardless of parent rock.

The vinegar test (fizzing when acid contacts rock or soil) confirms the presence of carbonates and therefore alkaline conditions. The indicator plant method confirms acidic conditions — blueberry, fern, and rhododendron colonize only soils below approximately pH 5.5. Both methods are field-deployable without instrumentation and are detailed in the Practical Implementation section.

### The Mohs Hardness Scale

Friedrich Mohs devised a relative hardness scale in 1812 that remains the standard field identification tool because it requires no equipment beyond common objects. The scale runs from 1 (talc, softest) to 10 (diamond, hardest). For field mineralogy, the following reference objects suffice:

- Fingernail: hardness 2.5
- Copper coin or wire: hardness 3.5
- Iron nail: hardness 4.5
- Steel knife blade: hardness 5.5
- Quartz crystal (clear or milky white, found in most gravel beds): hardness 7
- Hardened steel file: hardness 6.5

The testing protocol is simple: attempt to scratch the unknown mineral with each reference object in ascending order. If your steel knife scratches the mineral, it is softer than 5.5. If the mineral scratches the knife, it is harder than 5.5. Combined with color, streak (the color of the powder left when the mineral is scraped across an unglazed porcelain tile or rough stone surface), and acid reaction, this system identifies the majority of construction-relevant minerals without laboratory equipment.

```text
    MOHS HARDNESS FIELD TEST — DECISION FLOWCHART

    FINGERNAIL scratches it?
      YES --> Hardness <2.5   [Talc, Gypsum, Clay]
      NO  |
          v
    COPPER COIN scratches it?
      YES --> Hardness 2.5-3.5 [Calcite, Limestone, Gold]
      NO  |
          v
    STEEL KNIFE scratches it?
      YES --> Hardness 3.5-5.5 [Fluorite, Slate, Iron ore]
```

### The Soil Texture Triangle

Soil particles are classified by diameter into three size fractions:

- **Sand**: greater than 0.05 millimeters. Feels gritty. Visible to the naked eye.
- **Silt**: 0.002 to 0.05 millimeters. Feels smooth, like flour. Not individually visible.
- **Clay**: less than 0.002 millimeters. Feels sticky when wet, hard when dry. Invisible individual particles.

Every soil is a mixture of these three fractions. The proportions determine the soil's agricultural and construction properties. The ribbon test provides a rapid field estimate: a ribbon exceeding 5 centimeters indicates greater than 30 percent clay content. Ideal agricultural soil (loam) is approximately 40 percent sand, 40 percent silt, and 20 percent clay — it drains well, retains moisture, and holds nutrients. Ideal brick or pottery clay contains greater than 50 percent clay-fraction particles. The jar settle test (described in Practical Implementation) provides more precise ratios by exploiting differential settling rates in water.

### Phosphorus — The Limiting Element

Phosphorus deserves special attention because it is the one essential nutrient that cannot be synthesized, substituted, or extracted from the atmosphere. Nitrogen can be fixed biologically (legumes) or from the air (lightning, Haber process). Potassium is abundant in most rocks. But phosphorus exists only in specific geological deposits, and once depleted from agricultural soil, it can only be replaced from an external source.

Phosphorus is essential for DNA replication, ATP energy transfer, and bone formation in all living organisms. Natural sources accessible without industrial chemistry include:

- Bird guano: 10 to 25 percent P2O5 by weight. Found beneath seabird colonies and in caves.
- Bat cave deposits: similar concentrations, found in limestone cave systems with bat populations.
- Bone: calcium phosphate. Bone ash contains approximately 35 percent P2O5. Collect, burn, and crush.
- Phosphorite rock: a sedimentary rock containing greater than 15 percent P2O5. Identified by dark color, greasy luster, and association with marine sedimentary sequences.

A settlement without phosphorus access will experience declining crop yields within 10 to 30 years as soil phosphorus depletes through harvest removal. This is the single most commonly overlooked limiting factor in site selection. Every site evaluation must include a phosphorus audit — identifying at least one accessible source within logistical reach.

### Historical Context

The Roman Empire's expansion was partly driven by access to mineral resources. Roman concrete — the most durable construction material of the ancient world — used volcanic pozzolana mixed with lime derived from local limestone. The Romans identified and quarried limestone deposits across their empire, and their road network was in part a logistics system for mineral transport. Their iron mines in Hispania, Noricum, and Britain supplied the tools and weapons that maintained imperial power.

Medieval European settlement patterns closely tracked limestone outcrops (for building material and soil fertility) and iron ore deposits (for tools and plowshares). The limestone belt running from the Cotswolds through northern France into the Rhine Valley correlates precisely with zones of dense medieval settlement, cathedral construction, and agricultural prosperity. Conversely, regions underlain by granite and sandstone — the Scottish Highlands, the Massif Central, much of Scandinavia — remained sparsely populated and technologically limited until modern transport made mineral importation feasible.

## Core Principles

1. **The Three Minerals Rule.** A viable long-term settlement must have local access — within approximately 10 kilometers — to limestone, clay, and iron ore. Without all three, the settlement cannot produce mortar, ceramics, or durable tools, and long-term technological capability is impossible. This is a non-negotiable site selection criterion.

2. **The Phosphorus Audit.** Before committing to a permanent settlement site, identify at least one phosphorus source within logistical reach. Acceptable sources include bird colonies, bat caves, bone deposits, or phosphorite rock. Without phosphorus replenishment, agricultural yields will decline irreversibly within a generation.

3. **The pH First Principle.** Test soil pH before planting any crop. Indicator plants give immediate field data without any equipment. The vinegar and baking soda test provides confirmation. Do not waste seed on soil whose pH you have not assessed — a crop planted in pH 4.5 soil will fail regardless of other conditions.

4. **The Depth Profile Rule.** Always dig test pits to at least 1 meter depth. Surface soil is misleading: it reveals nothing about the clay layer (typically at 30 to 80 centimeters), the water table, or the bedrock type. A site that appears sandy at the surface may have excellent clay deposits at 60 centimeters depth, or conversely, a site with good topsoil may overlie impermeable hardpan that prevents root penetration.

5. **The Mineral Walk.** Survey a 5-kilometer radius from the proposed settlement center for all mineral resources before committing to the site. What you find within that radius determines what you can build, what you can grow, and what level of technology you can sustain. This survey should be conducted systematically using the protocol in the Practical Implementation section.

## Identifying Construction Minerals

### The Clay Ribbon Test

Clay is essential for waterproofing reservoirs, lining kilns, and creating pottery.

1. **Extract:** Take a handful of moist soil from 30 centimeters below the surface.
2. **Squeeze:** Roll the soil into a ball. If it crumbles, it is too sandy.
3. **The Ribbon:** Attempt to roll the ball into a long ribbon between your palms.
4. **Analysis:**
   - **High Quality (pottery-grade):** Ribbon extends greater than 5 centimeters before breaking. Greater than 30 percent clay content.
   - **Medium Quality (daub, brick):** Ribbon extends 2 to 5 centimeters.
   - **Loam/Silt (agricultural, not construction):** Soil forms a ball but cannot form a ribbon.

### The Limestone Fizz Test

Limestone (calcium carbonate, CaCO3) is the precursor to lime mortar and cement.

1. **Collect:** Find a white or light-grey rock with visible sedimentary layers or fossil inclusions.
2. **The Acid:** Drop strong vinegar (acetic acid, minimum 5 percent concentration) onto the rock surface.
3. **Observation:**
   - **Positive (limestone confirmed):** The rock will audibly hiss and produce tiny bubbles of CO2 gas. The reaction is CaCO3 + 2CH3COOH -> Ca(CH3COO)2 + H2O + CO2.
   - **Negative:** No reaction. The rock may be quartz, granite, sandstone, or another non-carbonate mineral.

## Biological Barometers: Indicator Plants

Plants are living sensors that reveal the chemistry of the soil beneath them. Their presence integrates years of soil conditions into a single observable signal.

### Identifying Soil pH

- **Acidic Soil (pH less than 6.0):** Look for blueberries, ferns, moss, pine trees, and rhododendron.
- **Alkaline/Basic Soil (pH greater than 7.5):** Look for clematis, asparagus, and heavy growth of wild clover.
- **Neutral/Ideal Soil:** Look for healthy stands of oak, maple, and chicory.

### Identifying Water Table Depth

- **Shallow Water (less than 3 meters):** Look for willows, alders, and cattails. If these plants are green in the dry season, a permanent well is viable at that location.
- **Deep Water:** Look for oak and hickory. These trees have deep taproots and indicate a stable but lower water table.

## Practical Implementation

### The Zero-Tech Mineral Identification Table

The following table covers the ten most settlement-relevant minerals, identifiable using only a knife, vinegar, and a rough stone surface for streak testing.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Limestone | White to grey | 3 | Fizzes in vinegar | White | Mortar, lime, water treatment, soil amendment |
| Sandstone | Tan to brown | 6-7 | None | Gritty residue | Building stone, grinding/sharpening stones |
| Granite | Speckled (black, white, pink) | 6-7 | None | N/A (too hard to streak) | Foundations, millstones |
| Quartz | Clear to milky white | 7 | None | White | Glassmaking, flint-knapped tools, hardness reference |
| Hematite (iron ore) | Red-black, metallic | 5.5-6.5 | None | Red-brown (diagnostic) | Ironworking, pigment |
| Clay (kaolinite) | White, grey, tan, red | 1-2 | None | Varies with impurities | Ceramics, brick, waterproofing, filtration |

**How to use this table:** For each unknown mineral, determine (1) color, (2) whether your knife scratches it or it scratches your knife, (3) whether vinegar causes fizzing, and (4) the streak color on a rough stone. Match these four observations to the table. Most identifications require only two or three matching characteristics.

### The pH Test Protocol (Expanded)

Four field methods in order of increasing precision:

1. **Indicator plant survey.** Walk the site and record the dominant plant species. Cross-reference with the Indicator Plant Reference Table below. This provides an immediate estimate accurate to approximately 1 pH unit, integrating years of soil conditions.

2. **Vinegar test (alkaline detection).** Place a tablespoon of moist soil in a non-metallic container. Add several drops of vinegar (5 percent acetic acid). Fizzing or audible hissing indicates alkaline soil with pH greater than 7. The stronger the fizzing, the higher the pH. No fizzing means the soil is neutral or acidic.

3. **Baking soda test (acid detection).** Mix a tablespoon of soil with enough water to make a slurry. Add a pinch of baking soda (sodium bicarbonate, NaHCO3). Fizzing indicates acidic soil with pH less than 6. No fizzing means the soil is neutral or alkaline.

4. **Red cabbage juice indicator.** Boil half a head of red cabbage in water for 30 minutes. Strain and reserve the purple liquid. Add soil to the liquid and observe color change: red or pink indicates acidic (pH 2 to 5), purple indicates near-neutral (pH 6 to 7), blue-green indicates alkaline (pH 8 to 9), yellow indicates strongly alkaline (pH 10 or above). Accuracy is approximately plus or minus 1 pH unit. This is the most precise zero-tech field method available.

**Interpreting results:** If vinegar fizzes on the soil, the soil is alkaline. If baking soda fizzes, the soil is acidic. If neither fizzes, the soil is near neutral (pH 6 to 7) — the ideal range for most agriculture.

### The Jar Settle Test (Soil Texture)

This test determines the sand, silt, and clay percentages of a soil sample by exploiting the fact that larger particles settle faster in water.

1. **Collect** a representative soil sample from 20 centimeters depth. Remove rocks, roots, and organic debris. Allow to dry, then crush to uniform consistency.
2. **Fill** a straight-sided glass jar (a preserved-food jar works well) one-third full with the prepared soil.
3. **Add water** to two-thirds full. Add a pinch of salt (improves clay dispersion and separation).
4. **Shake** vigorously for 2 minutes. Ensure all soil is fully suspended.
5. **Mark** the jar at the top of the settled layer after 1 minute — this is the sand fraction.
6. **Mark** again after 4 hours — the new material above the first mark is the silt fraction.
7. **Mark** again after 24 hours — the new material above the second mark is the clay fraction. The water above may remain cloudy with the finest clay particles for days.
8. **Calculate** each fraction as a percentage of the total settled depth.

**Interpretation:** Ideal agricultural loam shows roughly equal sand and silt layers with a thinner clay layer (approximately 40/40/20). Pottery-grade clay deposits show the clay layer comprising more than half the total. Pure sand (all settles in 1 minute) indicates poor water retention and nutrient holding capacity — amend heavily with organic matter or seek a different field location.

```text
         JAR SETTLE TEST — SIDE VIEW AFTER 24 HOURS

         |           |
         |   water   |  <-- may remain slightly cloudy
         |  (clear)  |      with finest clay particles
         |___________|
         |:::::::::::| ~10%  CLAY (settles in 24+ hours)
         |:::::::::::|       finest particles, sticky
         |___________|
         |...........| ~20%  SILT (settles in 2-4 hours)
         |...........|       smooth, flour-like
         |...........|
         |___________|
         |ooooooooooo| ~20%  FINE SAND (settles in 30 min)
         |ooooooooooo|       visible grains
         |___________|
         |OOoOOoOOoOO| ~50%  COARSE SAND + GRAVEL
         |OOoOOoOOoOO|       (settles in <1 minute)
         |OOoOOoOOoOO|       largest, heaviest particles
         |___________|

         Measure each layer as % of total settled depth.
         Example above: sandy soil, poor for agriculture.
         Ideal loam:  sand ~40% | silt ~40% | clay ~20%
```

### The Mineral Walk Protocol

A systematic 6-step survey to map mineral resources within settlement range:

1. **Walk** 5 kilometers in each of the four cardinal directions from the proposed settlement center. Where terrain allows, walk additional diagonal transects. Total survey covers approximately 80 square kilometers.
2. **At each distinct rock outcrop**, perform the four-test identification sequence: (a) visual color and texture identification, (b) hardness test using fingernail, knife, and quartz scratch sequence, (c) vinegar fizz test on a fresh surface, (d) streak test on a rough stone surface.
3. **Record** each find with: bearing and estimated distance from center, mineral type (using the identification table), estimated abundance (isolated boulder versus continuous outcrop versus extensive formation), and accessibility (surface exposure versus buried versus cliff face).
4. **Collect** a fist-sized sample of each distinct mineral type encountered. Label with location data. These samples form the settlement's reference collection for training others.
5. **Map** all mineral locations on the site plan. Use consistent symbols: triangle for limestone, circle for clay, square for iron, diamond for other minerals.
6. **Assess completeness:** Are limestone, clay, and iron all present within 5 kilometers? If any of the three is absent, this is a critical gap that must be weighed against other site advantages. A site lacking limestone may still be viable if chalk or marble (both carbonates) is present. A site lacking visible iron may have bog iron in wetland areas — investigate all marshes and stream beds for orange-brown precipitates.

### The Clay Prospecting Protocol

Clay deposits are found in predictable geological settings:

- **Stream banks and river terraces.** Look for smooth, plastic-textured exposed layers, typically grey, tan, or red, that differ visually from the surrounding sandy or gravelly bank material. River meanders deposit fine clay on the inside of bends.
- **Base of slopes.** Weathering products wash downhill and accumulate at slope bases. Dig test pits where slope transitions to flat ground.
- **B-horizon of soil profiles.** In most soil types, clay accumulates in the B-horizon, typically found at 30 to 80 centimeters depth. The A-horizon (topsoil) above is darker and more organic; the B-horizon is lighter in color, denser, and stickier.

**Field quality tests for clay:**

- **Ribbon test:** As described above. A ribbon greater than 5 centimeters indicates pottery-grade clay.
- **Bite test:** Place a small amount between your front teeth and grind gently. Clay feels smooth and slippery. Silt feels gritty, like fine flour. Sand feels distinctly gritty with individual particles detectable. This test distinguishes clay from silt with high reliability.
- **Fire test:** Form a small ball (2 centimeters diameter) and place at the edge of a campfire for 2 hours. Good pottery clay fires to a hard, red-brown or buff ceramic that rings when tapped. Poor clay crumbles, cracks excessively, or remains soft.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Overlooking phosphorus.** The site has limestone for mortar and clay for ceramics, but no phosphorus source has been identified. Agriculture succeeds initially using stored soil phosphorus, then yields decline steadily over 10 to 30 years as harvests remove phosphorus faster than natural weathering replaces it. By the time the decline is noticed, the community is established and relocation is extremely costly. The phosphorus audit must be conducted before site commitment.

2. **Surface sampling bias.** Testing only the top 10 centimeters of soil reveals the A-horizon but nothing about the B-horizon clay layer, the depth to bedrock, or the water table. A site that appears sandy at the surface may have a thick clay deposit at 50 centimeters — excellent for ceramics. Conversely, a site with loamy topsoil may overlie hardpan at 40 centimeters that prevents root penetration and causes waterlogging. Always dig test pits to at least 1 meter.

3. **False positive fizz test.** Seashell fragments, coral debris, or calcareous fossils in soil will fizz when vinegar is applied, but their presence does not indicate limestone bedrock. Ensure you are testing in-place bedrock or large boulders clearly derived from local geology, not transported fragments. In coastal areas, marine shell deposits may be meters thick — useful for lime production but not indicative of the underlying geology.

4. **Confusing shale with slate.** Both are fine-grained, grey-black, and layered. Shale crumbles along bedding planes and is useless for construction — it absorbs water and disintegrates. Slate cleaves into thin, flat, durable sheets suitable for roofing, writing surfaces, and flagstone flooring. The test is simple: attempt to split a thin piece. If it produces a clean, flat sheet with conchoidal edges, it is slate. If it crumbles into flaky fragments, it is shale.

5. **Ignoring the iron source.** A settlement that stockpiles steel tools from pre-collapse scavenging will eventually exhaust that supply as edges dull, handles break, and tools are lost. Without a local iron source — hematite, magnetite, or bog iron — replacement is impossible. Bog iron is the most commonly overlooked source: it forms as orange-brown, porous, crusty masses in marsh soils and stream beds and can be smelted in a simple bloomery furnace. Survey all wetlands within the settlement radius for bog iron before concluding that iron is unavailable.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Mohs Hardness Scale:** A relative scale from 1 to 10 for mineral hardness, where each mineral can scratch those below it. Field references: fingernail (2.5), knife (5.5), quartz (7).
- **Streak:** The color of a mineral's powder when scraped across an unglazed porcelain tile or rough stone. Often differs from the mineral's surface color and is therefore more diagnostic.
- **Clay (Kaolinite):** A hydrated aluminum silicate mineral, Al2Si2O5(OH)4, that forms from the weathering of feldspar. The primary material for ceramics, brick, and waterproofing.
- **Limestone (CaCO3):** A sedimentary rock composed primarily of calcium carbonate. The precursor to quicklime, mortar, plaster, and the foundation of pre-industrial chemistry.
- **Quicklime (CaO):** Calcium oxide, produced by calcining (heating) limestone above 900 degrees Celsius. Reacts vigorously and exothermically with water. The basis for lime mortar, plaster, water purification, and sanitation.
- **Hematite:** Iron(III) oxide, Fe2O3. The most common iron ore mineral. Identified by its red-brown streak, which is diagnostic even when the surface appears metallic black.
- **Magnetite:** Iron(II,III) oxide, Fe3O4. A magnetic iron ore. Can be identified in the field by its attraction to a magnet or, in larger deposits, by the deflection of a compass needle.
- **Bog Iron:** Hydrated iron oxyhydroxide, FeOOH. Precipitated in wetlands by iron-oxidizing bacteria. Appears as orange-brown, porous, crusty masses. Historically the most accessible iron source for early settlements, smelted in simple bloomery furnaces.
- **Phosphorite:** A sedimentary rock containing greater than 15 percent P2O5. Dark-colored with a greasy luster. The primary geological source of phosphorus for agriculture.
- **Soil pH:** A measure of hydrogen ion concentration in soil solution on a logarithmic scale from 0 (most acidic) to 14 (most alkaline). Agricultural optimum is 6.0 to 7.0.
- **Ribbon Test:** A field test for clay content in which moist soil is rolled into a ribbon between the palms. Ribbon length correlates with clay percentage: greater than 5 centimeters indicates greater than 30 percent clay.
- **Jar Settle Test:** A field test for soil texture in which soil is suspended in water and allowed to settle. Sand settles in 1 minute, silt in 2 to 4 hours, clay in 24 or more hours. Layer thicknesses give percentage composition.
- **Indicator Species:** A plant whose presence reliably indicates specific soil or environmental conditions, such as pH range, water table depth, or nutrient status.
- **B-Horizon:** The subsoil layer beneath the organic-rich A-horizon (topsoil). Typically lighter in color, denser, and enriched in clay that has migrated downward from above. Usually found at 30 to 80 centimeters depth.
- **Parent Rock:** The underlying geological material from which a soil is derived through weathering. Determines the soil's mineral content, pH tendency, and texture.
