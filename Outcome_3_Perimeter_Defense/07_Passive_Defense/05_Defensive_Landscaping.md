# Defensive Landscaping: Botanical Engineering for Perimeter Security

**Alignment:** Outcome 3: Perimeter Sovereignty and Passive Defense

Defensive landscaping uses living plants as permanent, self-repairing, self-reinforcing barriers. Unlike manufactured fences — which require industrial materials, regular maintenance, and eventually fail — a properly established thorny hedge becomes MORE impenetrable every year. The biology is straightforward: each growing season adds new lateral branches, each pruning cycle forces denser regrowth, and each year the root system anchors more deeply. After 10-15 years of growth, a mature defensive hedge represents a barrier that would require heavy machinery or hours of sustained labor with cutting tools to breach. No bolt cutter, no wire stretcher, no pry bar will open a gap in a well-laid Osage orange hedge.

The concept is not new. Living hedges were the PRIMARY fencing technology for thousands of years before barbed wire (patented 1874). Osage orange hedges planted across the American Midwest in the 1850s are still standing 170 years later. English hawthorn hedges from the Parliamentary Enclosure Acts still define field boundaries today. The Irish blackthorn hedge, the East African thornbush boma, the French bocage — every agricultural civilization developed living barrier technology independently, because it works. What barbed wire replaced in convenience, it never matched in longevity or self-repair capacity.

This module covers the botany, establishment techniques, and long-term management of defensive plant barriers: species-specific data for temperate climates, construction sequences with precise dimensions, and the hedgelaying technique that transforms a row of saplings into an interlocking living wall. Every species recommended here has been field-proven across centuries of agricultural use.

> **CAUTION**: Working with thorny species (Osage orange, hawthorn, blackthorn, honey locust) involves constant risk of deep puncture wounds. Thorns frequently break off beneath the skin and cause infection. Wear heavy leather gloves (minimum), long sleeves, and eye protection at all times when handling thorny material. Hedgelaying requires sharp billhook and chainsaw-equivalent tools — follow cutting tool safety protocols without exception. Honey locust thorns can penetrate standard boot soles — wear thick-soled leather footwear. Any puncture wound that shows redness, swelling, or heat within 24 hours requires immediate attention and wound cleaning; thorn wounds are notorious for introducing soil bacteria deep into tissue.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Botany of Defensive Plants

Thorns, spines, and prickles are botanically distinct structures, and the distinction matters for understanding barrier performance:

- **Thorns** are modified stems. They emerge from the wood itself and are anchored in the vascular structure of the branch. Osage orange and hawthorn produce true thorns. Because thorns are structural extensions of the stem, they cannot be brushed off, stripped away, or broken without damaging the branch itself. They are the hardest and most deeply anchored of the three structures.
- **Spines** are modified leaves or stipules. Black locust (Robinia pseudoacacia) produces paired stipular spines at each leaf node. Like thorns, spines are firmly anchored in the stem tissue and resist removal.
- **Prickles** are outgrowths of the epidermis (bark surface). Roses and brambles produce prickles. These are the least firmly anchored — they can sometimes be snapped off with force — but compensate through extreme density of coverage. A bramble cane may carry hundreds of recurved prickles along its length.

For defensive hedge construction, thorns and spines provide the primary barrier, while prickles (from ground-layer species like wild rose and bramble) fill the base zone.

Why do plants develop thorns? The answer is herbivore defense, and this directly serves our purpose. Thorns are most dense on juvenile growth and on branches within herbivore browse height (0-2 m). Mature canopy branches on the same tree may have fewer or no thorns. This means that the zone of densest armament is precisely the zone needed for a defensive barrier — the lower 2 meters where human passage occurs. Techniques that promote juvenile regrowth (coppicing, hedgelaying) keep the hedge permanently in its most heavily armed state.

**Thigmomorphogenesis** is the phenomenon by which plants exposed to mechanical stress — wind, pruning, lateral bending — develop shorter, denser, tougher growth. Stems become thicker relative to length, internodes shorten, and lateral branching increases. Hedgelaying directly exploits this: the partial cut and forced bending triggers extreme lateral branching along the entire laid length, producing a denser barrier than unpruned growth ever achieves. Every act of maintenance that stresses the hedge makes it stronger.

### Hedge Physics: Why Living Barriers Work

The mechanical properties of living hedges explain why they outperform manufactured barriers:

- **Tensile strength of green wood.** Osage orange has a modulus of rupture of approximately 120 MPa — comparable to mild steel. A 5 cm diameter green stem requires 150-200 kg of force to break. A hedge containing dozens of interlocking stems per linear meter presents aggregate structural resistance exceeding most fence designs.
- **Interlocking structure.** Hedgelaid stems create a woven lattice where each stem supports its neighbors. Cutting one stem does not create a usable gap — adjacent stems spring into the vacated space. To breach a laid hedge, an attacker must cut through stem after stem in sequence, each requiring individual tool access in a confined, thorny space.
- **Self-healing.** When damaged by storm or deliberate cutting, the hedge regrows from the wound site. New growth at wound sites is typically denser than the original due to callus formation and adventitious bud activation. A fence cut with bolt cutters stays cut. A hedge cut with a machete grows back thicker.
- **Growth rate.** Most thorny hedge species add 30-60 cm of height per year in temperate climates. A hedge planted at year 0 reaches functional barrier density (impassable to humans without tools) in 3-5 years and maximum density in 8-15 years. After first hedgelaying (year 8-15), density increases dramatically and the hedge enters its permanent mature phase.

### Windbreak Science

Dense hedges provide significant wind reduction that extends well beyond the hedge itself. Agricultural wind science confirms that a hedge of height H reduces wind speed by 50-70% for a downwind distance of approximately 10H. A 3-meter hedge therefore creates a sheltered zone extending 30 meters into the settlement interior. At 20H distance, wind reduction still measures 20-30%.

This windbreak effect provides thermal buffering for structures and gardens, reduced evaporation from crop soil, reduction of wind-carried sound (complementing acoustic sovereignty per Module 04), and disruption of airborne scent trails that might guide animals to food stores.

The optimal windbreak is 40-60% permeable. A solid barrier creates turbulent eddies on the lee side that can increase ground-level wind speed close to the barrier. A semi-permeable hedge allows some airflow through while reducing overall velocity, producing a longer, more uniform shelter zone. Living hedges naturally achieve this permeability through their branching structure.

### Historical Hedge Systems

- **English enclosure hedges (1750-1850).** The Parliamentary Enclosure Acts drove planting of over 320,000 km of hawthorn hedges across England and Wales. Standard technique: double-row planting with hawthorn, laid every 10-15 years. Many are still functional after 200+ years. Hooper's Rule estimates hedgerow age: woody species count per 30 m section roughly equals age in centuries.

- **American Osage orange hedges (1840-1880).** Before barbed wire, Osage orange was THE fencing material of the American Midwest. Jonathan Turner promoted the "Osage Orange Fence" from the 1840s; by 1870 millions of miles lined American farms. The standard: "horse-high, bull-strong, and pig-tight" within 4-5 years. Barbed wire (1874) ended new planting, but many original hedges survive 170 years later, still functional.

- **Irish and Scottish blackthorn hedges.** Prunus spinosa, with 3-5 cm thorns, was laid to create cattle-proof barriers. Blackthorn hedges also served as military obstacles — referenced in Irish and Scottish military history for ambush and territorial control. A mature blackthorn hedge is impenetrable without heavy tools.

- **East African boma (thornbush enclosure).** Maasai and Samburu pastoralists construct circular enclosures from cut thorny acacia to protect livestock from predators. Rebuilt annually from fresh material. The living defensive hedge is the permanent version — a boma that rebuilds itself.

### Allelopathy as a Defensive Tool

Allelopathy is the biochemical inhibition of neighboring plant growth through root exudates, leaf litter leachates, or volatile compounds. Certain tree species suppress undergrowth within their root zone, and this suppression can be deliberately employed as a defensive sightline tool.

**Black walnut (Juglans nigra)** is the most well-documented allelopathic tree in temperate North America. It produces juglone (5-hydroxy-1,4-naphthoquinone), which inhibits cellular respiration in susceptible species. Juglone is present in all parts of the tree — roots, leaves, bark, nut husks — and leaches into soil within 15-20 m of the trunk. Susceptible plants: tomatoes, potatoes, peppers, eggplant, azaleas, birch. Tolerant plants: most grasses, corn, beans, beets, carrots, squash.

The defensive application: plant black walnut at strategic perimeter positions to create suppression zones where undergrowth is chemically inhibited. Within the juglone radius, brush and saplings that would provide concealment for approaching intruders are suppressed, maintaining clear sightlines without manual clearing labor.

The trade-off: allelopathic zones cannot support most food crops. Position black walnut on the OUTER perimeter where food production is not planned. The juglone zone is exclusively a defensive sightline and should be mapped during site planning.

## Core Principles

1. **The Living-Over-Dead Principle.** Living barriers appreciate in value (grow stronger, denser, more deeply rooted). Dead barriers depreciate (wood rots, wire rusts, posts heave). Every hour of labor invested in establishing a living hedge pays dividends for decades or centuries. Every hour invested in a wooden or wire fence depreciates to zero within 10-20 years. This is not a philosophical preference — it is an engineering calculation. Over a 50-year time horizon, the living hedge requires less total labor than the fence it replaces.

2. **The Layered Canopy Principle.** An effective defensive hedge has THREE vertical layers: (a) ground layer — low thorny shrubs or brambles covering 0-1 m height, (b) mid-layer — hedgelaid thorny trees covering 1-3 m height, (c) canopy layer — standard trees providing overhead screening at 3-10 m height. An intruder cannot go under, through, or over a three-layer hedge. Each layer is composed of different species optimized for that height zone.

3. **The Species Diversity Principle.** Plant at least 3-4 thorny species in every hedge. Monoculture is vulnerable to species-specific disease — a single pathogen can destroy the entire barrier in one season. A diverse hedge maintains integrity even if one species is affected. Alternate species so no continuous section longer than 10 m is single-species.

4. **The Maintenance Imperative.** A neglected hedge grows tall and thin, losing density at the base. Annual pruning of top growth forces lateral branching; removing dead material prevents fire risk. A hedge laid every 10-15 years remains impenetrable indefinitely. Neglect is the single most common cause of hedge failure.

5. **The Multi-Function Principle.** Defensive hedges should simultaneously produce food (hawthorn berries, sloe berries, rose hips, honey locust pods), medicine (hawthorn leaves and flowers for cardiac support), fuel (pruned wood for heating and cooking), and wildlife habitat (nesting birds serve as biological sentinels per Module 04, alerting to disturbance through alarm calls). A hedge that ONLY provides defense is an underutilized asset.

**Key species notes:**

- **Osage orange** deserves special emphasis. Wood density (specific gravity 0.76-0.86) makes it one of the hardest North American timbers. Thorns are stout, sharp, and positioned at every leaf axil. When hedgelaid, interlocking stems create a barrier historically described as capable of stopping a charging bull. The wood burns hot and long, making prunings excellent fuel. Propagation from seed (soak 48 hours before planting) or root cuttings.

- **Hawthorn** is the most proven hedge species in Europe. It tolerates poor soil, heavy pruning, exposure, and neglect better than any other hedge species. Berries are edible raw or as preserves; leaves and flowers have documented cardiac tonic use. Hawthorn is the default choice if only one species can be planted — but monoculture should always be avoided.

- **Black locust** serves as a canopy-layer component rather than primary hedge species. Rapid growth, nitrogen fixation (Rhizobium symbiosis), and rot-resistant timber make it an ideal nurse tree at 5-10 m intervals within the hedge line. The nitrogen it fixes enriches soil for neighboring species. Paired stipular spines add armament in the upper canopy.

- **Prickly ash** fills the critical understory role. Shade-tolerant, extremely dense in branching, with small sharp thorns along every stem. Planted at the base of taller species, it fills the 0-2 m zone with impenetrable growth. Bark has traditional medicinal use (toothache remedy, circulatory stimulant).

**Recommended combinations by climate zone:**
- Zones 3-5 (cold continental): hawthorn + black locust + wild rose + prickly ash
- Zones 5-7 (temperate): Osage orange + hawthorn + blackthorn + holly
- Zones 7-9 (warm temperate): Osage orange + honey locust + pyracantha + wild rose

### Hedge Establishment Protocol

1. **Site preparation.** Clear a strip 2 m wide along the entire planting line. Remove all existing vegetation, roots, and debris. Mark the centerline with stakes and string.

2. **Trench excavation.** Dig a planting trench 30-40 cm deep by 30 cm wide along the centerline. If soil is heavy clay, break up the bottom of the trench with a fork to improve drainage. Set excavated soil to one side for backfilling.

3. **Planting pattern.** Plant in a double-stagger pattern: two rows 40 cm apart, with plants offset at 50-80 cm spacing within each row. This yields 2.5-5 plants per linear meter of hedge. Closer spacing (50 cm) produces faster closure but requires more nursery stock. For a 100 m hedge perimeter at 50 cm spacing, budget 500 plants.

4. **Mulching.** Apply 10-15 cm of woodchip, leaf litter, or straw mulch in a 1 m band on each side of the planting line. Mulch suppresses competing weeds (the primary threat to establishment), retains soil moisture, and moderates root-zone temperature.

5. **Years 1-2: Establishment care.** Water during drought periods (minimum 20 liters per linear meter per week during dry spells). Remove competing weeds within the mulch zone monthly. Protect from browsing animals with temporary post-and-wire fencing or individual spiral tree guards. Browsing damage during establishment is the most common cause of hedge failure.

6. **Year 3: First pruning.** Cut top growth to 1.5 m height to force lateral branching below the cut. Remove no more than one-third of total growth per year. This is the critical intervention that transforms a row of saplings into a dense hedge rather than a row of trees.

7. **Years 4-5: Functional density.** The hedge reaches functional barrier density — impassable to humans without cutting tools. Integrate dead hedge material (Benjes hedge technique) into any remaining gaps: pile brush and prunings between the living stems to create immediate physical obstruction while living growth fills in.

8. **Years 8-15: First hedgelaying cycle.** The hedge is now ready for its first full laying (see below). After laying, the hedge enters its mature phase of maximum density.

### The Benjes Hedge: Interim Dead-Hedge Barrier

The Benjes hedge (named for Hermann Benjes, German landscape ecologist) provides immediate barrier function while living hedge plants establish. Construction is simple:

Drive two parallel rows of stakes (5-8 cm diameter, 1.5 m tall) at 1 m spacing, with rows 50-80 cm apart. Pile brush, prunings, and thorny cuttings between the stake rows to a height of 1-1.5 m. As living hedge plants grow through the dead material, they incorporate its structure, using the brush pile as a scaffold. Within 3-5 years, the living plants have colonized the Benjes hedge entirely and the dead material decomposes in place, adding organic matter to the soil.

The Benjes hedge is also valuable as a wildlife habitat — the dense brush pile provides nesting sites for birds and shelter for beneficial predator species (hedgehogs, weasels) that contribute to pest control.

### Hedgelaying (Pleaching): The Master Technique

Hedgelaying is the technique that transforms a tall, thin hedge into a dense, impenetrable living wall — the single most important skill in defensive landscaping. The technique has been practiced continuously in the British Isles for at least 800 years, with distinct regional styles (Midland, South of England, Welsh, Lancashire).

**Timing.** Late winter dormancy only. January through early March in the Northern Hemisphere. Sap must be fully down. Laying during active growth kills stems.

**Tool requirements.** A sharp billhook (the traditional hedgelaying tool), a pruning saw or bowsaw, a lightweight axe, heavy leather gloves, eye protection, and thick-soled boots. For large stems (over 10 cm diameter), a chainsaw may be necessary for the initial cut.

**Step-by-step process:**

1. **Clear the base.** Remove dead wood, bramble, and debris from the base of the hedge to expose the main stems. Identify which stems will be laid ("pleachers") and which will be removed entirely (dead, diseased, or poorly positioned stems).

2. **Select the pleachers.** Choose stems of 5-10 cm diameter growing at approximately correct spacing (every 30-50 cm along the hedge line). Each pleacher will be partially cut and laid at an angle to form the lattice.

3. **The cut.** This is the critical operation. Using a billhook or sharp axe, cut into the stem at 15-30 cm above ground level. Cut AWAY from the direction you intend to lay the stem. Cut through approximately 70% of the stem diameter, leaving 30% of the wood and bark intact on the LAYING side. This remaining connection — called the "hinge" — must include live cambium tissue. The cambium is the thin green layer between bark and wood; it is the stem's lifeline. If the hinge includes intact cambium, the laid stem will survive and regrow.

4. **The lay.** Bend the partially cut stem down to approximately 30-35 degrees from horizontal. Lay it in the direction of the hedge line (all pleachers laid the same direction). The stem should rest against the next stake in line. Weave it between the vertical stakes where possible.

5. **Stakes.** Drive sharpened stakes (5-8 cm diameter, 1.5 m long, typically hazel or other straight-grained wood) vertically into the hedge line at 50-60 cm intervals. Stakes hold the laid pleachers in position and provide the vertical structure of the finished hedge.

6. **Binders (heathering).** Weave long, flexible stems (hazel rods, willow withies) horizontally along the top of the stakes, binding them together. The binder row caps the hedge at a uniform height and locks the entire structure into a rigid unit.

7. **Result.** The laid pleachers survive through their cambium hinges and produce vigorous vertical shoots along their entire laid length — not just from the top, but from every dormant bud along the stem. Within one growing season, the laid hedge produces a dense wall of new vertical growth from ground level to 2 m height. Within 2-3 seasons, the new growth has filled in completely. The hedge is now denser than it was before laying and will remain so for 10-15 years until the next laying cycle.

### The Ditch-and-Hedge System

The most formidable version of the defensive hedge combines a ditch, a berm, and a living hedge into a single integrated obstacle:

1. Excavate a V-shaped ditch on the OUTER side of the hedge line: 1.5 m deep, 2 m wide at the top, tapering to 30 cm at the bottom.
2. Place all excavated soil as a raised berm on the INNER side of the ditch, compacting it to form a bank 0.5-1 m above the original ground level.
3. Plant the defensive hedge on TOP of the berm.
4. Total effective obstacle height from ditch bottom to hedge top: 1.5 m (ditch depth) + 0.5-1 m (berm height) + 2-3 m (hedge height) = 4-5.5 m of vertical obstacle.

An approaching intruder must descend into the ditch, climb the berm, and then face the hedge — all while exposed and slowed. This is identical in principle to the Roman castrum ditch-and-rampart, the medieval English "bank and ditch" boundary, and Iron Age hillfort defenses. The living hedge replaces the wooden palisade that originally crowned these earthworks.

The ditch also serves as a drainage channel, directing water away from the settlement interior and preventing waterlogging at the hedge base.

### Allelopathic Buffer Zones

Plant 3-5 black walnut trees at each major approach route, positioned 30-50 m from the outer perimeter hedge. Space individual trees 10-15 m apart so that their juglone zones overlap, creating a continuous suppression band.

Within the juglone radius (15-20 m from each trunk), undergrowth is chemically suppressed. Grasses may persist (most are juglone-tolerant), but brush, saplings, and most broadleaf weeds are eliminated. The result is a clear-sightline buffer zone requiring no manual clearing — the trees maintain it biochemically.

Do NOT plant food crops within the juglone zone. Do not position the walnut trees uphill from garden areas, as juglone leaches downslope with groundwater. The allelopathic zone is exclusively a defensive sightline feature and should be mapped during site planning with stakes or paint marks at the estimated juglone boundary.

Black walnut trees also produce edible nuts (a valuable calorie and fat source) and extremely durable timber, ensuring that the allelopathic trees serve multiple functions beyond sightline maintenance.

### Annual Maintenance Calendar

Proper timing of hedge maintenance follows the seasonal cycle of dormancy and growth:

- **January-March (dormancy).** This is the hedgelaying and major pruning window. All structural work — laying, coppicing, stake replacement, gap repair — must be completed before sap rises. Remove dead material from within the hedge structure. Replace broken or rotted stakes in laid sections.
- **April-May (spring growth).** Inspect for winter damage, browsing damage, and disease. Replace any dead plants with nursery stock of the same species. Apply fresh mulch around the base if the previous year's mulch has decomposed.
- **June-August (active growth).** Light trimming only — trim the top and sides to maintain the target profile (wider at base, narrower at top, an A-shape or trapezoid cross-section). Do not remove more than the current season's new growth. Monitor for pest or disease issues.
- **September-October (late season).** Harvest any edible products (hawthorn berries, sloe berries, rose hips, walnuts). Collect seed for the nursery bed. Note any sections showing thinning or weakness for winter repair.
- **November-December (pre-dormancy).** Final inspection before winter. Ensure temporary fencing around young sections is intact. Plan the winter work schedule for January-March.

A hedge that receives this annual cycle of attention will remain in peak defensive condition indefinitely. The total annual labor investment for a 200 m hedge perimeter is approximately 20-30 hours — a small fraction of the labor required to build and maintain an equivalent fence.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Base gap development.** The hedge grows tall but thin at the base ("leggy growth"), creating gaps at ground level that an intruder can crawl under or push through. This is the most common failure mode and results from insufficient pruning. **Prevention:** annual top-pruning to force lateral growth below the cut line. Hedgelay every 10-15 years to reset the entire structure. Plant ground-layer species (bramble, wild rose) at the base to fill the lowest 0-1 m zone independently.

2. **Species disease wipeout.** A monoculture hedge (single species) is struck by a species-specific pathogen and the entire barrier is compromised simultaneously. **Prevention:** plant minimum 3 species. Alternate species along the hedge line so no continuous section longer than 10 meters is single-species. If one species dies, the others maintain barrier integrity while replacements establish.

3. **Browsing damage during establishment.** Deer, rabbits, or escaped livestock eat young hedge plants before they develop thorny defense. Seedlings are palatable and vulnerable for the first 1-2 years. **Prevention:** protect with temporary fencing (post-and-wire) or individual spiral tree guards for the first 2-3 years until thorns are well-developed. This is a non-negotiable investment — losing seedlings to browsing wastes years of establishment time.

4. **Hedgelaying mortality.** Too much wood removed during the laying cut — the hinge is too thin or the cambium is severed — and the laid stem dies, creating a gap. **Prevention:** always leave at minimum 30% of stem cross-section intact, ensuring live cambium is included in the hinge. If uncertain, leave more rather than less. A stem laid too high (insufficient cut) can be recut next year; a dead stem is a permanent gap that must be replanted.

5. **Fire damage.** Dense hedge structure accumulates dead material (fallen leaves, dead branches, bird nests) within its interior, creating fuel load. In drought conditions, a fire can destroy years of growth. **Prevention:** remove dead material from within the hedge during annual winter maintenance. Maintain Zone 2 fire clearance (per Module 03) between the hedge and any structures. Living green hedges are significantly more fire-resistant than dead hedges or wooden fences — this is another argument for the Living-Over-Dead Principle. Never store flammable materials (hay, fuel) adjacent to the hedge.

6. **Wrong species for the climate.** Planting outside hardiness zone results in winter kill or establishment failure. **Prevention:** verify hardiness zone before species selection. Err toward cold-hardy species — Zone 4 stock planted in Zone 6 thrives, but Zone 7 stock in Zone 5 may die in the first hard winter.

7. **Inadequate spacing at planting.** Plants set too far apart (over 1 m) never achieve hedge density — they grow as individual trees with gaps. **Prevention:** maintain 50-80 cm spacing in the double-stagger pattern. Better too dense than too sparse; excess stems can be removed during hedgelaying, but gaps require replanting.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Defensive landscaping** — the use of living plants as engineered barriers for perimeter security
- **Living wall / living hedge** — a barrier composed of growing, rooted plants that gains strength over time
- **Hedgelaying / pleaching** — the technique of partially cutting and bending hedge stems to create a dense interlocking lattice
- **Pleacher** — an individual stem selected for laying during the hedgelaying process
- **Billhook** — the traditional cutting tool for hedgelaying; a heavy, hooked blade designed for cutting partially through stems
- **Binder / heathering** — flexible stems woven along the top of a laid hedge to bind stakes and pleachers together
- **Thorn / spine / prickle** — botanically distinct defensive structures (modified stems, modified leaves, epidermal outgrowths respectively)
- **Thigmomorphogenesis** — the development of shorter, denser, tougher plant growth in response to mechanical stress
- **Allelopathy** — biochemical suppression of neighboring plant growth through root exudates or leaf litter compounds
- **Juglone** — the specific allelopathic compound (5-hydroxy-1,4-naphthoquinone) produced by black walnut
- **Ditch-and-hedge** — an integrated obstacle combining excavated ditch, raised berm, and living hedge for maximum barrier height
- **Coppicing** — cutting a tree or shrub to ground level to stimulate regrowth of multiple stems from the stool
- **Osage orange** (*Maclura pomifera*) — the benchmark defensive hedge species; extremely dense wood, aggressive thorns, 120 MPa tensile strength
- **Benjes hedge** — a dead-hedge constructed by piling brush between stake rows, used as an interim barrier while living hedges establish
