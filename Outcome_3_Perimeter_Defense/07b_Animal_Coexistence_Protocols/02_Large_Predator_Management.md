**Alignment:** Outcome 3: Perimeter Sovereignty and Passive Defense

Large predators — bears, wolves, large cats, and in some regions hyenas — represent the most dangerous wildlife threat to any settlement. The numbers are stark: a single bear in autumn hyperphagia can consume 20,000 kilocalories per day and will destroy an entire season of stored food in one night if it gains access. A wolf pack can kill multiple livestock animals in a single attack, often more than they consume (a behavior called surplus killing). A large cat — mountain lion, leopard, tiger — can kill a human being. These are not abstract dangers. They are engineering problems that demand engineered solutions.

However, these same predators are irreplaceable ecological regulators. When wolves were absent from Yellowstone for seventy years, unchecked elk browsing destroyed riparian forests, collapsed stream banks, and eliminated beaver habitat across the entire ecosystem. When wolves returned in 1995, the forests recovered, the streams recovered, and species across dozens of trophic levels rebounded. This is the trophic cascade in action: remove the apex predator, and the damage propagates downward through every layer of the food web. The settlement that kills its local predators may solve a short-term livestock problem, but it creates a long-term ecological catastrophe — herbivore overpopulation, crop destruction, forest degradation, and soil erosion — that ultimately causes far more damage than the predators ever did.

This module provides species-specific, non-lethal deterrence protocols that reduce predator encounters by 90% or more while maintaining the ecological balance that Outcome 2 demands. The approach follows a strict hierarchy: (1) attractant removal, which prevents 90% of encounters before they begin; (2) physical exclusion through barriers and enclosures; (3) species-specific deterrence that exploits the behavioral biology of each predator type; and (4) aversive conditioning that teaches individual animals to avoid the settlement permanently. Lethal control is not included in this protocol. It is reserved for the immediate defense of human life only, and is covered under the emergency response framework, not predator management.

> **DANGER**: Large predator encounters can be fatal. NEVER approach a bear with cubs, a wolf pack defending a kill, or any large cat. The Predator Response Protocol below is designed for encounters that occur DESPITE deterrence. If a predator is behaving aggressively within 30 meters, do NOT attempt deterrence — activate the community response (continuous horn blast) and retreat to structures. Bears can run at 50 km/h, wolves at 60 km/h, mountain lions at 80 km/h in short bursts. You CANNOT outrun any of them.

## Theoretical Foundation

### Behavioral Ecology of Large Predators

Every deterrence method in this module exploits a specific behavioral vulnerability. Understanding the behavioral ecology of each predator species is not academic background — it is the engineering specification that determines which deterrent to deploy, where, and why.

**Bears (Ursidae):**

Bears are primarily driven by food. A bear's entire behavioral cycle revolves around caloric intake. In autumn, bears enter hyperphagia — a period of obsessive feeding where they consume 20,000 or more kilocalories per day to build the fat reserves required for winter hibernation. During hyperphagia, a bear will take extraordinary risks to access high-calorie food sources, including entering human structures.

Bears are olfactory-dominant animals. A bear's nasal mucosa has roughly 100 times the surface area of a human's, giving it a sense of smell that can detect food from 3 or more kilometers downwind. This is the critical insight: bears approach settlements because they detect food. Remove the olfactory signal and you remove the bear. Attractant elimination is not merely the first step in bear management — it is the solution for 90% of bear encounters.

The risk profile varies by species. Black bears are rarely aggressive toward humans and are defensive only when cubs are threatened or when cornered. Grizzly and brown bears are more aggressive and more dangerous, but still prefer to avoid humans when given the option. Polar bears are actively predatory toward humans, but polar regions fall outside the scope of Aadhiyandham settlement planning.

The key behavioral exploit for bears is noise aversion. Bears avoid confrontation with loud, large-seeming opponents. Noise deterrence — banging metal, shouting, air horns — is 80% or more effective at causing bears to flee. This is not a bluff; it exploits the bear's genuine cost-benefit calculation. A fight with a noisy, unpredictable opponent risks injury, and an injured bear cannot feed efficiently during hyperphagia. The bear's survival instinct says: leave.

**Wolves (Canidae):**

Wolves primarily target livestock, not humans. Wolf attacks on humans are extremely rare. Linnell et al. (2002) documented only nine fatal wolf attacks in all of North America over a hundred-year period. The danger from wolves is economic, not mortal — they kill livestock, and they kill efficiently.

Wolves are social hunters that attack in coordinated packs, targeting the weakest, slowest, or most isolated animals. This behavioral pattern makes livestock — slow, confined, predictable, and unable to flee effectively — ideal prey. Wild ungulates can outrun, outmaneuver, or collectively defend against wolves. Domestic sheep cannot.

Wolves are strongly neophobic: they have an innate fear of novel objects and unfamiliar environments. This neophobia is why fladry works. A line of fluttering red fabric strips is a novel visual stimulus that wolves will not cross — for a time. The limitation is that neophobia habituates. After 60 to 90 days of exposure without negative consequence, wolves learn that the flags are harmless and begin to ignore them.

Most wolf attacks on livestock occur between dusk and dawn. Wolves are crepuscular and nocturnal hunters. Night-specific deterrence — enclosures, guardian animals, lighting — is therefore critical. Daytime deterrence is secondary.

The key behavioral exploit for wolves is their avoidance of confrontation with large guardian animals. A dog weighing more than 35 kilograms, a donkey, or a llama represents an unpredictable, aggressive opponent that wolves assess as not worth the risk of injury. A wolf that sustains a broken jaw or a crushed rib from a donkey kick cannot hunt and will die. The pack's risk calculus consistently favors avoiding the guardian.

**Large Cats (Felidae — Mountain Lion, Leopard, Jaguar, Tiger):**

Large cats are ambush predators. They attack from concealment, typically targeting prey that is unaware of their presence. This is not a preference — it is a hardwired behavioral rule. Large cats almost never attack prey that is facing them and visibly alert. The energy cost of a failed ambush is high, and the risk of injury from alert prey that fights back is unacceptable to a solitary predator with no pack to compensate.

Unlike wolves, large cats hunt alone. This solitary nature means that a single guardian animal or a single aware human can deter a single cat. There is no coordinated pack attack to overwhelm defenses.

Large cats are visual hunters that primarily use sight rather than smell. Clearing brush and eliminating ambush cover is therefore the primary defense — it removes the concealment that the cat's entire hunting strategy depends on.

The key behavioral exploit is that large cats will not attack what they believe can see them. The backward-facing mask — a human face worn on the back of the head — works because it exploits this hardwired rule. In the Sundarbans mangrove forests of India, a 1987 pilot program issued backward-facing masks to woodcutters who were being killed by tigers. Tiger attacks on mask-wearing workers dropped by over 80% in the first year. The tigers could not identify an "unaware" angle of approach and abandoned the stalk.

### The Attractant Cascade

Attractants operate on a predictable gradient of strength and range:

**Level 1 attractants (strongest, longest range):** Exposed meat or fish, open garbage, pet food left outdoors, unprotected beehives, fruit trees with dropped fruit on the ground. Level 1 attractants draw bears from 3 or more kilometers and wolves from 5 or more kilometers as they follow scent trails.

**Level 2 attractants (moderate):** Sealed but accessible food in thin-walled containers or low-elevation caches, open compost piles, livestock feed stored in bags or bins. Level 2 attractants draw predators from 1 to 2 kilometers.

**Level 3 attractants (weak but unavoidable):** The livestock themselves, cooking odors, garden produce. Level 3 attractants draw predators from less than 500 meters.

The "90% Solution" is this: eliminating Level 1 attractants reduces predator encounters by approximately 90%. Parks Canada confirmed this figure — 95% of bear-human conflicts in campgrounds were eliminated by implementing bear-proof food storage alone, before any active deterrence was deployed. Eliminating Level 2 attractants adds another 5 to 8% reduction. Level 3 attractants cannot be fully eliminated — the settlement must have livestock and gardens — but they can be managed through physical exclusion (roofed enclosures, fencing).

### Aversive Conditioning Theory

Aversive conditioning pairs an unpleasant stimulus — pain, fear, nausea — with a specific location or behavior, creating a learned avoidance that persists long after the stimulus is removed. Unlike passive deterrents (fladry, scarecrows, noise devices), aversive conditioning modifies the individual animal's behavior permanently. A bear that receives a capsaicin blast to the face while approaching a settlement will avoid that settlement for months or years, even if the capsaicin barrier is later removed.

**Capsaicin (hot pepper extract):** Triggers intense pain in the nasal mucosa and eyes of all mammals. Bears and canids, which investigate primarily by nose, receive maximum aversive effect. Smith et al. (2008) demonstrated that capsaicin spray was 92% effective at stopping aggressive bear charges in Alaska — significantly more effective than firearms in the same situations.

**Electric fencing:** Delivers a sharp but non-injurious shock at 5,000 to 10,000 volts with very low amperage. Animals that contact the fence once learn to avoid it permanently. Electric fencing is more effective than any passive barrier of equivalent height and cost. For bears specifically, baiting the electric wire with bacon grease or fish oil ensures first-contact shock — the bear investigates the smell, touches the wire with its nose (the most sensitive area), and receives an immediate, memorable aversive lesson.

**Rubber bullets and beanbag rounds:** Used by wildlife management agencies for aversive conditioning of individual habituated bears. Each non-lethal projectile teaches the animal that approaching humans equals pain. Not available in all settlement contexts but highly effective where obtainable.

The critical distinction: passive deterrents (fladry, noise, scarecrows) habituate within 60 to 90 days. Aversive deterrents (capsaicin, electric fence, guardian animals) do not habituate because they deliver real consequences on every encounter. Build the deterrence system on an aversive foundation and supplement with rotated passive methods.

## Core Principles

**The Attractant Hierarchy.** Always eliminate attractants before deploying deterrents. Deterrence without attractant removal is fighting the symptom while feeding the cause. Fix Level 1 attractants (exposed food) completely. Fix Level 2 attractants (accessible food) as fully as possible. Manage Level 3 attractants (livestock, gardens) with physical barriers. A settlement that deploys capsaicin fencing around an open garbage pile is wasting capsaicin.

**The Species-Specific Principle.** Each predator species has different sensory systems, behavioral patterns, and vulnerability points. A method that works on bears (noise) has minimal effect on large cats (visual ambush predators that largely ignore noise). A method that works on wolves (fladry) has no effect on bears (who do not share wolf neophobia). Identify the specific species present in the region and deploy the matching deterrence. Do not apply a generic "predator defense" — it does not exist.

**The Rotation Principle.** Non-aversive deterrents habituate within 60 to 90 days. Aversive deterrents do not habituate. Use aversive methods (capsaicin barriers, electric fencing, guardian animals) as the permanent base layer. Use non-aversive methods (fladry, noise devices, scarecrows, night lighting) as supplementary layers rotated on a 60-day cycle. Maintain at least three different non-aversive deterrent types per species so that each rotation introduces a genuinely novel stimulus.

**The Nighttime Priority.** Most large predator attacks on livestock and most dangerous encounters with humans occur between dusk and dawn. Nighttime livestock security — roofed enclosures with latched doors, guardian animals inside the enclosure, exterior lighting — is the single highest-impact intervention. A settlement that secures its livestock every night from dusk to dawn will prevent the majority of predator-caused losses regardless of what other measures it does or does not take.

**The Escape Route Principle.** Never corner a large predator. Always leave it an escape route. A predator that can flee will flee. A predator that is trapped will fight, and a trapped bear or large cat fighting for its life is capable of killing multiple armed humans. Every element of the Predator Response Protocol is built on this principle. When a predator enters a structure, you open exits and withdraw. When a predator is in contact with livestock, you open the enclosure. The livestock loss is regrettable. The human death that results from cornering a panicked bear is catastrophic.

## Practical Implementation

### Phase 1: Attractant Elimination (The 90% Solution)

Most predator encounters are caused by the settlement, not the predator. Before deploying any deterrence system, eliminate the invitation.

**Food waste:** All organic waste composted in sealed, heavy-lidded bins or buried 30 or more centimeters deep. Never leave food scraps in the open. Kitchen waste bins emptied and sealed before nightfall every day without exception.

**Food storage:** All preserved food stored in elevated caches (4 or more meters high on smooth poles that bears cannot climb) or in stone and earthen cellars with heavy doors and no gaps greater than 2 centimeters. A bear can pry open any wooden door that is not latched with metal hardware. Sealed ceramic or metal containers for all stored grain, dried meat, and preserved foods. Thin-walled plastic or woven containers are not bear-proof.

**Livestock at night:** All livestock secured inside roofed enclosures from dusk to dawn. No exceptions. No "just tonight" exceptions. No "the goat doesn't like being enclosed" exceptions. Every exception is a potential livestock kill.

**Fish and meat processing:** All butchering and fish cleaning done at a designated site downwind and 200 or more meters from the settlement. Wash the site with vinegar after each use. Rotate the processing site seasonally — every 3 to 4 months — to prevent permanent scent buildup that draws predators to a predictable location.

**Compost:** Enclosed bin with a latching lid, or buried composting at 30 or more centimeters depth. No open pile composting in predator-active areas. An open compost heap is a Level 1 attractant that broadcasts a continuous food signal.

**Beehives:** If the settlement keeps bees, surround hives with electric fencing. Bears are the primary threat to apiaries worldwide, and electric fencing is the single most effective deterrent for protecting hives. Without electric fencing, elevate hives on platforms at least 3 meters high.

**Fruit trees:** Collect all dropped fruit daily during fruiting season. Dropped fruit fermenting on the ground is a powerful Level 1 attractant, especially for bears.

**Attractant Audit Checklist** (conduct at dawn, when nocturnal predator activity is most recent):

1. Open garbage or food waste visible anywhere in the settlement
2. Food storage containers that are damaged, unsealed, or accessible at ground level
3. Livestock feed stored outside enclosures
4. Pet food bowls left outdoors
5. Fish or meat drying racks within 100 meters of sleeping areas
6. Butchering site showing blood, offal, or fat residue
7. Compost pile uncovered or unlatched
8. Dropped fruit under fruit trees
9. Beehives without electric fencing or elevation
10. Grease or fat residue on outdoor cooking surfaces
11. Livestock enclosure doors that do not latch securely
12. Gaps in livestock enclosure walls greater than 10 centimeters
13. Food-scented clothing or equipment stored outdoors
14. Bird feeders (attract rodents, which attract predators)
15. Water sources with accumulated food residue (wash basins, etc.)

### Phase 2: Species-Specific Deterrence

#### Bears (Black Bear, Brown/Grizzly Bear)

**Noise deterrence:** Station metal pot and lid sets at 50-meter intervals along primary approach routes (typically creek corridors, forest edges, and trails leading from known bear habitat). Instruction: bang loudly and continuously for 30 or more seconds. Add voice — shouting is more effective than metallic noise alone because it signals "large aggressive primate" rather than "random noise." Multiple people shouting simultaneously is more effective than one person.

**Capsaicin fencing:** Soak heavy cotton rope in capsaicin oil. To prepare capsaicin oil: grind dried hot peppers (the hotter the variety, the better), steep in rendered animal fat over low heat for 2 to 4 hours, strain through cloth. Hang the soaked rope at 1 to 1.5 meters height — bear nose height — around food storage areas, compost bins, and settlement entry points. Replace every 2 to 4 weeks as potency degrades with weather exposure. Rain accelerates degradation; shelter the rope under a simple overhang where possible.

**Bear-hang food cache:** Suspend food bags on a rope between two trees, 4 meters high and 2 meters from either trunk. The PCT counterbalance method is most effective: throw a rope over a high branch (5 or more meters), attach a food bag to one end and hoist it up, then attach a second bag of equal weight to the other end and push it up with a stick. Both bags hang at equal height, neither touching the ground, and neither reachable from the trunk. Retrieve by hooking the rope with a long stick.

```text
   Bear-Hang: PCT Counterbalance Method

   Branch (5+ meters high)
       │
   ────┼────
       │
   ┌───┴───┐
   │ rope  │
   │       │
  [Bag A] [Bag B]    ← both at 4+ meters
   equal   equal      ← 2+ meters from trunk
   weight  weight
```

**Electric fencing (if available):** Two-wire system at 25 centimeters and 75 centimeters height. Bait the lower wire with bacon grease or fish oil to ensure the bear investigates with its nose and receives first-contact shock at the most sensitive point. Bears learn from a single shock. This is the most effective single deterrent for bears after attractant removal.

#### Wolves and Wild Dogs

**Guardian animals:** One guardian dog weighing more than 35 kilograms per 50 to 100 livestock animals. Donkeys and llamas are effective for smaller flocks of sheep or goats. See Module 04 for the full guardian animal selection, training, and deployment protocol. The guardian must live with the livestock full-time — an animal that is only present during the day provides no protection during the critical nighttime hours.

**Fladry (flag line):** Red fabric strips 30 centimeters long and 10 centimeters wide, hung 50 centimeters apart on a rope at 80 to 100 centimeters height around livestock enclosures. The flags must flutter in wind — rigid flags do not trigger neophobia. Fladry is effective for 60 to 90 days (Musiani et al., 2003: 100% wolf deterrence for the first 60 days, 60 to 80% thereafter when combined with other methods). After habituation begins, switch to turbo-fladry: the same flag line but with an electrified wire running through the rope. The flags draw the wolf's attention; the electric shock delivers aversive conditioning. Turbo-fladry does not habituate because it delivers real consequences.

**Night lighting:** A single maintained fire or oil lamp near the livestock enclosure deters wolf approach. The mechanism: wolves associate human fire with human presence. This is effective as a supplementary deterrent but not as a standalone measure. A fire that burns out at 2 AM provides no protection from 2 AM to dawn.

**Livestock enclosure:** Roofed structure with solid walls or heavy-gauge wire mesh, door latched with metal hardware at night. No livestock left unenclosed from dusk to dawn. This is the non-negotiable baseline for wolf country. The enclosure must have no gaps greater than 10 centimeters (wolves can squeeze through surprisingly narrow spaces) and the door must latch securely against being pushed open. Quarterly inspection of all enclosure integrity.

#### Large Cats (Mountain Lion, Leopard, Tiger)

**Ambush cover elimination:** Clear all dense brush, tall grass, and low-hanging vegetation within 50 meters of livestock areas, walking paths, children's play areas, and water collection points. This is the primary intervention for large cats. A cat that cannot conceal itself within stalking distance cannot execute an ambush. Maintain cleared zones through regular cutting on a monthly cycle during growing season.

**Backward-facing masks:** When working alone at the settlement perimeter, wear a mask depicting a human face on the back of the head. The mask must have prominent eyes — the eye pattern is the critical signal. Historical validation: the Sundarbans program in India (1987) issued backward-facing masks to woodcutters working in tiger habitat. Tiger attacks on mask-wearing workers dropped significantly in the first year. The tigers could not identify a moment when the woodcutter's back was turned and abandoned the stalk.

**Height advantage:** Construct elevated walkways, platforms, and work areas wherever people work near the perimeter. Large cats stalk on the ground and attack from a horizontal or slightly upward angle. Being above cat eye-height removes the predatory trigger — the cat does not recognize an elevated figure as stalkable prey. Work platforms at 2.5 to 3 meters height are sufficient.

**Lighting at night:** Solar-powered, oil-based, or fire-based lighting around livestock enclosures and perimeter paths. Large cats depend on darkness for concealment during approach. Well-lit areas eliminate the ambush advantage. Multiple light sources are more effective than a single bright source because they eliminate shadows. Intermittent or flickering light (fire) is more effective than steady light because it is harder for the cat to predict safe approach routes.

### The Predator Response Protocol

When a large predator is spotted within or approaching the settlement perimeter:

| Scenario | Response |
|:---|:---|
| Predator at 100+ meters, moving away | Log it. No action. Record species, direction, time of day. |
| Predator at 50-100 meters, stationary | Group together. Make noise. Do not approach. It will leave. |
| Predator within 50 meters, approaching | Continuous loud noise, throw rocks (not at it, near it). Back away slowly. Do NOT run. |
| Predator in direct contact with livestock | All available adults respond with noise. Open the enclosure to let the predator escape. Accept the livestock loss — a cornered predator is lethal. |
| Predator has entered a structure | Evacuate the structure. Open all exits. Give it a clear escape route. Wait. |

**Absolute Rule:** Never corner a large predator. Always leave it an escape route. A predator that can flee will flee. A predator that is trapped will fight.

**Cubs and pups:** If you encounter a bear with cubs, a wolf den with pups, or a large cat with kittens, back away immediately. Do not pass between the mother and offspring. Maternal aggression is the number one cause of bear attacks on humans. The mother is not deciding whether you are a threat — she has already decided you are. Your only option is to increase distance.

**Eye contact rules by species:** Maintain eye contact with large cats and wolves. Direct eye contact signals awareness, and awareness is the primary deterrent for ambush predators and social predators that assess threat before attacking. Do NOT maintain direct eye contact with bears. Bears interpret sustained direct eye contact as a challenge or threat display. Look at the bear, but avert your gaze slightly — do not stare.

## Common Failure Modes

**Habituation.** A deterrent — fladry, noise device, scarecrow — works reliably for 60 to 90 days, then the predator begins to ignore it. This is not a failure of the deterrent; it is a predictable feature of animal learning. Any stimulus that is not paired with a real consequence will eventually be reclassified as background noise. Prevention: rotate non-aversive methods on a 60-day cycle. Maintain three or more different deterrent types per species so that each rotation introduces a genuinely novel stimulus. Anchor the system on aversive deterrents (capsaicin, electric fencing, guardian animals) that do not habituate.

**Attractant regression.** After months without predator encounters, the community relaxes food security discipline. Compost bins are left unlatched. Food is stored at ground level. Butchering waste is not cleaned up promptly. Encounters resume — but now at higher intensity, because the predators that return are habituated to human food and more persistent. Prevention: attractant management is permanent infrastructure, not a seasonal response. The attractant audit checklist is conducted monthly regardless of encounter frequency.

**Incomplete enclosure.** The livestock enclosure has a gap in the wall, a weak or unlatched door, or no roof. A predator enters, and instead of killing one animal and leaving, it kills multiple animals in a single event. This surplus killing occurs because confined livestock cannot flee, and the predator's chase instinct is triggered repeatedly by panicking animals in an enclosed space. Prevention: quarterly enclosure inspection using a formal checklist. No gaps greater than 10 centimeters. All doors must latch securely with metal hardware. Roofing is required in large cat territory (cats can scale walls).

**Retaliatory killing.** A community member kills a predator after a livestock loss, believing this solves the problem. In reality, removing one predator opens the territory for others (often younger, less experienced animals that are more likely to conflict with humans), triggers potential legal consequences where predators are protected, and begins the trophic cascade damage described in the theoretical section. Prevention: community-wide agreement on the non-lethal imperative. Livestock losses are analyzed as infrastructure failures — the question is always "why did the enclosure fail?" not "how do we kill the predator?"

**Children and predator encounters.** A child encounters a predator alone while playing at the settlement perimeter. Children are smaller, less experienced, and more likely to run (triggering chase instinct) than adults. Prevention: children under 12 do not go beyond the inner settlement zone unaccompanied. All children are drilled on the Predator Response Protocol from the time they can understand instructions. The three rules for children: make yourself big and loud, do not run, find an adult. Practice drills quarterly.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

* **Large predator management:** The discipline of reducing predator-human conflict through non-lethal, ecologically sound methods that maintain apex predator populations in the landscape.
* **Attractant removal:** The practice of eliminating food smells, waste, and other signals that draw predators toward human habitation. The single most effective predator management intervention.
* **Attractant cascade:** The gradient of attractant strength and range, from Level 1 (exposed meat, 3+ km range) through Level 3 (livestock presence, <500 m range), which determines the probability and frequency of predator encounters.
* **Aversive conditioning:** The process of pairing an unpleasant stimulus (pain, fear) with a specific location or behavior, creating a persistent learned avoidance in an individual animal.
* **Capsaicin deterrence:** The use of hot pepper extract (capsaicin) as a non-lethal chemical deterrent. Triggers intense pain in mammalian nasal mucosa and eyes. 92% effective against aggressive bears (Smith et al., 2008).
* **Fladry:** A traditional European wolf deterrent consisting of red fabric strips hung on a rope line. Exploits wolf neophobia (fear of novel objects). Effective for 60 to 90 days before habituation.
* **Turbo-fladry:** An electrified version of fladry that combines the visual novelty of flag strips with an electric shock upon contact. Does not habituate because the aversive stimulus (shock) is real.
* **Neophobia:** An innate fear of novel objects, environments, or stimuli. Strongly expressed in wolves and canids. The behavioral basis for fladry effectiveness.
* **Hyperphagia:** The period of obsessive, high-calorie feeding that bears undergo in autumn to build fat reserves for hibernation. During hyperphagia, bears consume 20,000+ kcal/day and take increased risks to access food.
* **Trophic cascade:** The ecological chain reaction triggered by removing an apex predator. Herbivore populations explode, vegetation is overbrowsed, and cascading effects propagate through every level of the ecosystem.
* **Guardian animal:** A large domestic animal (dog >35 kg, donkey, llama) that lives with livestock and aggressively deters predators. The primary non-lethal deterrent against wolves.
* **Predator Response Protocol:** The structured, scenario-based procedure for responding to predator encounters within the settlement, built on the principle of always providing an escape route.
* **Surplus killing:** The behavior in which a predator kills more prey than it can consume in a single event, typically triggered when confined livestock panic and repeatedly trigger the predator's chase instinct.
* **Ambush predator:** A predator (large cats) that hunts by concealment and surprise attack, targeting unaware prey. Deterred primarily by eliminating concealment and signaling awareness.

- Stephen Herrero, *Bear Attacks: Their Causes and Avoidance* (2002) — definitive bear behavior reference
- L. David Mech, *The Wolf: The Ecology and Behavior of an Endangered Species* (1981) — wolf behavioral ecology
- Musiani et al., "Wolf Depredation Trends and the Use of Fladry Barriers" (*Journal of Wildlife Management*, 2003)
- Smith et al., "Efficacy of Bear Deterrent Spray in Alaska" (*Journal of Wildlife Management*, 2008)
- Linnell et al., "The Fear of Wolves: A Review of Wolf Attacks on Humans" (NINA, 2002) — comprehensive risk analysis
- Jim Corbett, *Man-Eaters of Kumaon* (1944) — case studies in large cat behavior and human conflict
- Parks Canada, "Bear-Smart Community Program" — attractant management protocols and efficacy data
