Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

The circadian system is the master regulator of human health. It is not one process but an orchestra of interlocking hormonal, neurological, and metabolic rhythms that govern immune function, tissue repair, cognitive performance, mood stability, and metabolic efficiency. Every cell in the human body keeps time, and when these clocks fall out of synchrony with each other and with the external light-dark cycle, the consequences are severe and systemic: depression, obesity, cancer susceptibility, immune dysfunction, accelerated cognitive decline, and reproductive failure. Industrial civilization, with its shift work, screen exposure, and 24-hour artificial lighting, has produced epidemic levels of precisely these conditions. The World Health Organization classified night-shift work as a probable carcinogen (Group 2A) in 2007. Chronic circadian disruption is not a minor inconvenience --- it is a slow-acting poison.

A post-collapse settlement that has lost access to electric lighting possesses, paradoxically, a massive health advantage. The natural light-dark cycle --- bright days and genuinely dark nights --- is the environment the human circadian system evolved to operate within. For 200,000 years of Homo sapiens history and millions of years of primate evolution before that, the body calibrated itself against sunrise and sunset. The settlement does not need to create healthy circadian conditions; it needs only to not destroy them. This module provides the theoretical foundation and practical protocols to leverage the natural light environment for maximum physiological benefit, and to avoid the specific failure modes that could reintroduce circadian disruption even without electricity.

The goal is the maintenance of the Centered Null --- a state of emotional and physiological equilibrium in which the body's hormonal rhythms are properly synchronized, sleep is deep and restorative, daytime cognition is sharp, and mood is stable. This is not a luxury. It is the baseline condition upon which all other survival tasks depend. A sleep-deprived settlement makes poor decisions, suffers more injuries, heals slowly, fights more, and dies younger.

> SAFE: The protocols in this module involve only behavioral modifications --- light exposure timing, sleep scheduling, temperature management. No substances, procedures, or equipment required.

Theoretical Foundation

The Suprachiasmatic Nucleus (SCN): The Master Clock

The suprachiasmatic nucleus is a bilateral structure in the anterior hypothalamus containing approximately 20,000 neurons that oscillate on an endogenous cycle of roughly 24.2 hours. This is the master pacemaker of the mammalian circadian system. Every peripheral clock in the body --- in the liver, gut, muscle, immune cells, skin, and bone marrow --- takes its timing cue from the SCN. When the master clock drifts, every downstream process drifts with it: digestion, hormone secretion, immune surveillance, wound healing, and cognitive function all lose their temporal coordination.

The SCN synchronizes (or "entrains") to the external light-dark cycle through a dedicated neural pathway. Intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) in the eye contain the photopigment melanopsin, which is maximally sensitive to short-wavelength blue light at approximately 480 nm. These cells do not contribute to image-forming vision; their sole purpose is to measure ambient light intensity and relay that information to the SCN via the retinohypothalamic tract. Morning sunlight, which is rich in 480 nm radiation, is the primary entrainment signal --- the dominant zeitgeber (German: "time-giver"). This is why blind individuals who retain intact ipRGCs can still entrain to the light-dark cycle, while those who have lost these cells often suffer from Non-24-Hour Sleep-Wake Disorder.

Without external light cues, the SCN free-runs at its intrinsic ~24.2-hour period. In classic cave isolation experiments (Siffre, 1962; Aschoff, 1965), subjects' sleep-wake cycles drifted approximately 12 minutes later each day, accumulating a full 24-hour phase shift over roughly 4-5 weeks. This demonstrates that the clock is endogenous but requires daily resetting by light. The practical implication is straightforward: morning light exposure is not optional. It is the mechanism by which the body knows what time it is.

The Hormonal Cascade

The SCN drives a complex, precisely timed sequence of hormonal events across each 24-hour cycle. Understanding this cascade is essential for recognizing both normal function and the signs of disruption.

Cortisol is the primary alertness hormone, secreted by the adrenal cortex under SCN-mediated control via the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Cortisol follows a pronounced diurnal rhythm:

  • Lowest concentration: approximately midnight
  • Begins rising: 2-3 hours before habitual wake time
  • Peak: 30-45 minutes after waking (the Cortisol Awakening Response, or CAR)
  • Steady decline: throughout the afternoon and evening
  • Returns to nadir: midnight

The morning cortisol peak mobilizes glucose from glycogen stores, suppresses non-essential inflammatory processes, sharpens attention, and prepares the body for physical and cognitive demands. A flattened cortisol curve --- where morning peaks are blunted and nighttime levels remain elevated --- is a hallmark of chronic stress and circadian disruption, and is associated with immune suppression, visceral fat accumulation, and impaired wound healing.

Serotonin is synthesized from the amino acid tryptophan in both the gut (where approximately 90% of the body's serotonin resides) and the brain. Bright light exposure, particularly in the morning, drives central serotonin production via the dorsal raphe nuclei. Serotonin regulates mood, appetite, social behavior, and pain sensitivity during waking hours. Critically, serotonin is the biochemical precursor to melatonin --- without adequate daytime serotonin production, the evening melatonin signal is weak. Low serotonin states manifest as depression, anxiety, irritability, and intense carbohydrate craving. Seasonal variation in serotonin production (lower in winter due to reduced light exposure) is the primary driver of Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD).

Melatonin is synthesized from serotonin in the pineal gland when light levels drop below approximately 10 lux. The conversion is mediated by the enzymes arylalkylamine N-acetyltransferase (AANAT) and acetylserotonin O-methyltransferase (ASMT), both of which are suppressed by light and activated by darkness. Melatonin does not directly cause sleep; rather, it signals to the body that darkness has arrived, opening the "sleep gate" and lowering core body temperature. The timing of melatonin onset --- termed Dim Light Melatonin Onset (DLMO) --- occurs approximately 2 hours after exposure to dim light conditions and approximately 14-16 hours after morning light exposure. Even brief exposure to bright light (>100 lux, roughly equivalent to a well-lit room) at night suppresses melatonin secretion by 50% within minutes. This is the mechanism by which artificial lighting destroys sleep quality.

Growth hormone (GH) is secreted in pulsatile fashion by the anterior pituitary, with the largest pulse occurring during the first bout of Stage 3/4 slow-wave sleep (typically within 60-90 minutes of sleep onset). GH drives tissue repair, muscle protein synthesis, bone maintenance, lipolysis (fat mobilization), and immune cell proliferation. In a settlement context where physical labor is intense and injuries are common, GH-mediated recovery during sleep is not a luxury but a survival requirement. Disrupted slow-wave sleep --- from noise, pain, anxiety, or circadian misalignment --- directly reduces GH secretion and slows recovery.

Testosterone and estrogen follow circadian-dependent secretion patterns. Testosterone peaks in the early morning (contributing to the morning alertness signal) and declines through the day. Chronic sleep restriction to 5 hours per night for one week reduces testosterone levels in young men by 10-15%, an effect equivalent to 10-15 years of aging (Leproult & Van Cauter, 2011). Estrogen and progesterone cycling in women is also circadian-sensitive; disrupted sleep affects menstrual regularity, fertility, and premenstrual symptoms. In a settlement where reproductive health and physical performance are critical, these hormonal effects of sleep deprivation cannot be dismissed.

The Glymphatic System: The Brain's Self-Cleaning Mechanism

In 2013, Dr. Maiken Nedergaard and her team at the University of Rochester published "Sleep Drives Metabolite Clearance from the Adult Brain" in Science, describing a previously unknown waste-clearance system in the brain. During deep slow-wave sleep, astrocytes (glial cells that form the structural scaffolding of the brain) shrink by approximately 60%, expanding the interstitial space between neurons. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) then flows through this expanded space in a convective current driven by arterial pulsation, flushing metabolic waste products including beta-amyloid (associated with Alzheimer's disease) and tau protein (associated with frontotemporal dementia and chronic traumatic encephalopathy).

The glymphatic system operates at approximately twice the efficiency during sleep compared to wakefulness. Subsequent research by Lee et al. (2015) demonstrated that sleep position affects clearance efficiency: lateral (side) sleeping improves glymphatic transport compared to supine (back) or prone (stomach) positions, likely due to the anatomical orientation of the CSF drainage pathways.

The practical implication is stark: chronic sleep deprivation is chronic waste accumulation in the brain. Over months and years, this accelerates cognitive decline, impairs judgment, reduces emotional regulation, and may contribute to dementia-like symptoms even in relatively young individuals. In a survival context, where sound judgment and clear thinking are existential requirements, protecting sleep is protecting the settlement's cognitive capacity.

Vitamin D Photosynthesis

Ultraviolet-B radiation (290-315 nm wavelength) penetrating the skin converts 7-dehydrocholesterol in the epidermis to pre-vitamin D3, which is subsequently hydroxylated in the liver and kidneys to the active hormone 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D (calcitriol). This process requires a UV Index of 3 or higher, which in temperate latitudes is typically achieved only between approximately 10:00 and 14:00 during spring through autumn.

Vitamin D is not truly a vitamin --- it is a secosteroid prohormone that regulates the expression of over 2,000 genes involved in immune function (both innate and adaptive), calcium and phosphorus metabolism (bone mineralization and strength), mood regulation (via serotonin synthesis pathways), cell differentiation (cancer suppression), and cardiovascular health. Full-body exposure to midday sun produces approximately 10,000-20,000 IU in 15-20 minutes for light-skinned individuals; darker skin requires proportionally longer exposure due to melanin's absorption of UVB.

At latitudes above approximately 35 degrees, UVB intensity is insufficient for cutaneous vitamin D synthesis from November through February. During these months, dietary sources become critical: fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), organ meats (liver), egg yolks, and, where available, fermented cod liver oil. Deficiency symptoms include bone pain, muscle weakness, increased susceptibility to respiratory infections, depression, and impaired wound healing. In a settlement without supplementation, winter vitamin D management is a genuine health concern that must be addressed through dietary planning and maximizing outdoor time during the limited midday UVB window.

Sleep Architecture

Normal adult sleep cycles through four stages in approximately 90-minute ultradian cycles, with 4-5 complete cycles per night constituting a full sleep period.

  • Stage 1 (N1): Light transitional sleep, the boundary between wakefulness and sleep. Easily disrupted by noise or movement. Accounts for approximately 5% of total sleep time.
  • Stage 2 (N2): Characterized by sleep spindles (bursts of 12-14 Hz oscillations) and K-complexes (sharp negative deflections followed by slower positive waves). These electrophysiological events play critical roles in memory consolidation and sensory gating --- protecting sleep from external disturbance. Accounts for approximately 45% of total sleep time.
  • Stage 3/4 (N3, slow-wave sleep): The deepest stage, characterized by high-amplitude delta waves (0.5-4 Hz). This is when growth hormone secretion peaks, the glymphatic system operates at maximum efficiency, and the immune system performs its most intensive repair and surveillance functions. Accounts for approximately 25% of total sleep time. This is the stage most critical for physical recovery.
  • REM (Rapid Eye Movement): Characterized by brain activity patterns resembling wakefulness, muscle atonia (paralysis preventing motor enactment of dreams), and vivid dreaming. REM sleep is critical for emotional processing, procedural memory consolidation, and creative problem-solving. Accounts for approximately 25% of total sleep time. This is the stage most critical for cognitive and emotional recovery.

The distribution of these stages across the night is not uniform. The first half of the night is dominated by slow-wave sleep --- the physical recovery phase. The second half is dominated by REM sleep --- the cognitive and emotional recovery phase. This has a critical practical implication: cutting sleep short (going to bed late or waking early) preferentially eliminates REM sleep, producing emotional dysregulation, impaired learning, poor decision-making, and interpersonal conflict --- even if the individual "feels fine."

Core Principles

  1. The Morning Light Imperative. Exposure to bright natural light (greater than 2,500 lux --- overcast outdoor light provides this; indoor light near a window does not) within 60 minutes of waking is the single most powerful circadian intervention available. It sets the master clock, triggers the cortisol awakening response, initiates serotonin production, and establishes the timing of evening melatonin onset 14-16 hours later. Ten minutes of direct sun on a clear morning, or 30 minutes under overcast skies, is sufficient. No supplement, medication, or behavioral intervention approaches the potency of this single action. It must be performed outdoors --- window glass filters a significant proportion of the relevant wavelengths.
  2. The Darkness Discipline. After sunset, the settlement must eliminate blue and white light sources. Only firelight, oil lamps, or candles (orange and red spectrum, less than 50 lux) are permissible. Even a brief 5-minute exposure to bright white light at night shifts melatonin onset by 30-60 minutes and fragments subsequent sleep architecture. This is not a guideline or a recommendation --- it is a biological requirement with no workaround. Salvaged LED flashlights and battery-powered lanterns must be restricted to emergency use or fitted with deep orange filters.
  3. The Temperature Drop Rule. Sleep onset is triggered by a 1-2 degree Celsius drop in core body temperature. Sleeping quarters should be maintained 3-5 degrees Celsius cooler than daytime living spaces, with an optimal target of 15-19 degrees Celsius. A warm bath or foot soak 1-2 hours before bed paradoxically accelerates this process: peripheral vasodilation radiates heat from the core through the skin surface, accelerating the core temperature decline that initiates the sleep cascade.
  4. The 7-9 Hour Minimum. Adults require 7-9 hours of sleep for full physiological recovery. In a settlement with heavy daily physical labor, 8-9 hours is optimal. Sleep debt is cumulative: restricting sleep by even 1 hour per night accumulates a deficit that impairs cognitive function, immune response, and physical performance over days and weeks. This debt cannot be repaid by occasional long sleeps. Chronic restriction over months causes measurable and potentially permanent cognitive impairment.
  5. The Consistency Principle. The SCN entrains most effectively to consistent timing. The settlement should establish a fixed wake time (within plus or minus 30 minutes), including on rest days. Variable sleep timing produces "social jet lag" --- the physiological equivalent of changing time zones every few days. Irregular sleepers show higher rates of metabolic syndrome, depression, and cardiovascular disease even when total sleep duration is adequate.

Practical Implementation

The Daily Endocrine Protocol

Phase 1 --- Morning Reset (sunrise to sunrise + 60 minutes)

  • Go outdoors within 60 minutes of waking. Face the direction of the sun (do not stare directly at it --- ambient sky light is sufficient).
  • Duration: 10 minutes on a clear day (greater than 10,000 lux); 20-30 minutes under overcast skies (2,500-5,000 lux).
  • Do not wear sunglasses. Prescription lenses are acceptable (glass and plastic transmit the relevant 480 nm wavelengths adequately).
  • Do not attempt to get this light through windows. Standard glass filters approximately 50-70% of the short-wavelength light the ipRGCs require.
  • Combine with morning physical activity (stretching, walking, labor preparation) to amplify the cortisol awakening response.
  • This single intervention sets the master clock, initiates serotonin synthesis, suppresses residual melatonin, and programs melatonin onset for 14-16 hours later.

Phase 2 --- Midday Vitamin D Synthesis (10:00-14:00, UV Index 3+)

  • Expose maximum skin surface (arms, face, torso) to direct sunlight for 15-20 minutes.
  • Do not apply any barrier (no cloth over target skin areas during this window).
  • After 15-20 minutes, cover skin or move to shade to prevent burning. The goal is sub-erythemal exposure --- enough for vitamin D synthesis, not enough to redden the skin.
  • In winter months at latitudes above 35 degrees, when UVB is insufficient, prioritize dietary vitamin D sources: fatty fish, organ meats, egg yolks.

Phase 3 --- Evening Melatonin Ramp (sunset onward)

  • Beginning at sunset, transition to orange and red light sources only: fire, oil lamps, candles.
  • Lux level must remain below 50 (a single candle at arm's length produces approximately 10-15 lux --- this is ideal).
  • Eliminate any white or blue-white light sources. If salvaged electric lights are used for any reason, they must be filtered through deep orange or red material.
  • Allow 2 hours of dim-light conditions before the target sleep time. This permits full DLMO and the opening of the sleep gate.
  • During this period, favor quiet social activity: conversation, storytelling, gentle craft work, planning discussions. Avoid intense physical exertion or stressful deliberation, both of which elevate cortisol and oppose the melatonin signal.

Settlement Sleep Architecture

Sleeping quarters should be oriented north-facing or heavily shaded to minimize pre-dawn light intrusion during summer months when sunrise occurs early. In winter, this is less critical, but consistency of the sleeping environment year-round simplifies construction.

Temperature should be maintained at 15-19 degrees Celsius. Strategies by climate:

  • Warm climates: Use thermal mass (earth-sheltered construction, stone or rammed-earth walls that absorb daytime heat and release it slowly), cross-ventilation with directional openings, and elevation of sleeping platforms above ground level (where cold air pools at night).
  • Cold climates: Insulate sleeping quarters with layered natural materials (straw, wool, compressed earth) but ensure adequate ventilation --- carbon dioxide accumulation from multiple sleepers in an enclosed space degrades sleep quality and produces morning headaches.
  • Seasonal variation: In winter, a slightly warmer sleeping space (18-19 degrees Celsius) may be necessary for comfort; in summer, prioritize ventilation and shade to reach the lower end (15-17 degrees Celsius).

Darkness must be complete. No fire, no candles, no open windows facing lit common areas. The sleeping area is a dedicated darkness zone. If nightwatch personnel require light, their station must be visually isolated from sleeping quarters by walls, curtains, or distance.

Noise isolation from nightwatch activities, livestock, and other disturbances is important. Sleeping quarters should be positioned at maximum feasible distance from animal shelters, latrine areas, and the nightwatch perimeter. Earth-bermed or partially underground structures provide excellent acoustic isolation.

Nightwatch rotation must respect circadian biology:

  • Maximum shift length: 4 hours
  • Mandatory recovery sleep: 8 hours minimum after any nightwatch duty
  • Never assign the same individual to consecutive nightwatch shifts
  • Maximum frequency: 2 nightwatch shifts per person per week
  • Rotate assignments equitably across all able adults
  • Nightwatch personnel are exempt from early-morning duties the following day
  • Track assignments in writing (rotation log) to prevent informal inequity
  • Late chronotypes ("owls") may be better suited to first-watch (evening) shifts; early chronotypes ("larks") to pre-dawn shifts --- leverage natural variation rather than fighting it

Seasonal Adaptation

Summer (long days, short nights). The body naturally compresses sleep duration to 7-8 hours. Wake time moves earlier with sunrise; sleep onset moves later with delayed sunset. This is the high-productivity season. Maximize outdoor labor, construction projects, and food production during the extended daylight. Ensure that despite the late sunset, the darkness discipline is maintained --- do not allow the excitement of long summer evenings to push sleep onset past the point where 7 hours of sleep opportunity remains.

Winter (short days, long nights). The body naturally extends sleep duration to 9-10 hours. This is normal, expected, and beneficial. Do not fight it. Do not interpret increased sleep need as laziness or depression (though monitor for actual SAD --- see Failure Modes below). Winter is the recovery and consolidation season. Historical evidence strongly supports an ancestral pattern of segmented sleep during long winter nights.

Segmented sleep is documented extensively in pre-industrial European records (Ekirch, 2005). The pattern: a "first sleep" of approximately 4 hours beginning shortly after dark, a waking interval of 1-2 hours during which individuals engaged in quiet activity (prayer, meditation, conversation, gentle craft work, sexual activity), followed by a "second sleep" of approximately 4 hours until dawn. This pattern is not insomnia --- it is the natural human response to 14-16 hours of darkness. The settlement should recognize and accommodate segmented sleep during winter months. The inter-sleep waking period can be used for quiet communal activity, reflection, or individual contemplation. Ensure that lighting during this period remains strictly in the orange/red spectrum and below 50 lux.

Common Failure Modes

1. Chronic Blue Light Exposure. Settlers bring salvaged LED flashlights, battery-powered lanterns, or improvised electric lights into nighttime use. Even a single bright white LED in a shared sleeping space suppresses melatonin across every individual in the room. Within days, sleep onset is delayed, sleep architecture is fragmented, and daytime fatigue accumulates. Within weeks, mood destabilization and increased interpersonal conflict become apparent. Prevention: Strict darkness discipline after sunset. Orange and red light only. Confiscate or disable white-light devices, or restrict them to sealed emergency kits. This policy must be established as a non-negotiable settlement norm from the earliest days.

2. Sleep Deprivation Culture. The settlement leadership treats sleep as unproductive time and pushes for maximum waking work hours. This is the single most destructive health policy a settlement can adopt. Within two weeks of chronic 5-6 hour sleep: injury rates increase (impaired coordination and judgment), infection rates rise (suppressed immune function), wound healing slows (reduced GH secretion), interpersonal conflicts escalate (impaired emotional regulation), and decision quality deteriorates (prefrontal cortex dysfunction). Prevention: Enforce a minimum 8-hour sleep opportunity as settlement policy. Frame sleep correctly: it is not idle time. The body is repairing tissue, consolidating skills learned during the day, clearing metabolic waste from the brain, and conducting immune surveillance. Sleep is among the most productive hours in the 24-hour cycle.

3. Nightwatch Burnout. The same individuals --- often young, single adults --- are assigned disproportionate nightwatch duty. Chronic circadian disruption ensues, producing a cascade of hormonal dysregulation, cognitive impairment, and mood instability that may be mistaken for personality defects or lack of commitment. Prevention: Equitable rotation across all able adults. Maximum 2 nightwatch shifts per person per week. Mandatory 8-hour recovery sleep after each shift. Nightwatch personnel are exempt from early-morning duties the following day. Track assignments in writing to prevent informal inequity.

4. Winter Depression (Seasonal Affective Disorder). Reduced daylight hours in winter decrease serotonin production, producing symptoms including persistent lethargy, social withdrawal, increased sleep beyond functional need (hypersomnia), intense carbohydrate craving, difficulty concentrating, and loss of interest in previously valued activities. In a settlement under winter resource pressure, these symptoms can be misinterpreted as malingering. Prevention: Maximize outdoor exposure during winter daylight hours --- even overcast winter light (2,000-5,000 lux) is an order of magnitude brighter than indoor light and is therapeutically significant. Schedule communal outdoor activities during midday. Maintain social engagement, physical exercise, and ensure dietary adequacy of tryptophan (the serotonin precursor, found in meat, fish, eggs, and legumes) and vitamin D.

5. Caffeine Disruption. If the settlement cultivates or trades for caffeine sources (tea, coffee, yerba mate, guarana), afternoon and evening consumption will disrupt sleep onset and reduce slow-wave sleep depth. Caffeine's half-life is 5-7 hours; a cup of tea at 14:00 still has half its caffeine active at 19:00-21:00. Prevention: Establish a settlement norm of no caffeine consumption after solar noon. Morning caffeine use is acceptable and may enhance the cortisol awakening response, but the cutoff must be respected strictly.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Circadian rhythm: Any biological process that oscillates with an approximately 24-hour period, entrained to the light-dark cycle.
  • Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN): The bilateral hypothalamic structure (~20,000 neurons) that serves as the master circadian pacemaker in mammals.
  • Melanopsin: The photopigment in intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs), maximally sensitive to 480 nm (blue) light, responsible for non-image-forming light detection and circadian entrainment.
  • Melatonin: The pineal hormone synthesized from serotonin in darkness, signaling nighttime to the body and facilitating sleep onset.
  • Serotonin: A monoamine neurotransmitter and precursor to melatonin; regulates mood, appetite, and social behavior. Synthesized from tryptophan under bright light conditions.
  • Cortisol: The primary glucocorticoid hormone of the adrenal cortex; follows a diurnal rhythm with a morning peak (CAR) that promotes alertness and metabolic mobilization.
  • Growth hormone (GH): Anterior pituitary hormone secreted primarily during slow-wave sleep; drives tissue repair, muscle synthesis, and immune cell production.
  • Glymphatic system: The brain's waste-clearance system, operating primarily during slow-wave sleep via CSF convection through expanded interstitial space.
  • Slow-wave sleep (N3): The deepest sleep stage, characterized by delta-frequency EEG activity; critical for physical recovery, GH secretion, and glymphatic clearance.
  • REM sleep: The sleep stage characterized by rapid eye movements, muscle atonia, and vivid dreaming; critical for emotional processing and procedural memory consolidation.
  • Zeitgeber: (German: "time-giver") Any external cue that entrains the circadian clock. The light-dark cycle is the dominant zeitgeber in humans.
  • Dim Light Melatonin Onset (DLMO): The time at which melatonin concentration begins to rise under dim light conditions; the most reliable physiological marker of circadian phase.
  • Centered Null: The state of emotional and physiological equilibrium maintained when circadian rhythms are properly synchronized --- the baseline from which effective action proceeds.
  • Chronotype: An individual's genetically influenced preference for earlier ("lark") or later ("owl") sleep-wake timing. In a settlement, chronotype diversity is an asset: early chronotypes handle dawn duties, late chronotypes handle evening watch.
