Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Trauma — physical injury from mechanical force, thermal energy, or penetrating objects — is the leading cause of death in working-age adults in every society that has ever been studied, from pre-industrial agrarian communities to modern industrial nations. In a post-collapse settlement where people fell trees with axes, build load-bearing structures by hand, handle livestock, forge metal, and traverse rough terrain daily, traumatic injuries are not a possibility but a certainty. Over a ten-year period, a fifty-person settlement performing manual labor should expect multiple severe lacerations, several fractures, at least one major burn, and at least one life-threatening hemorrhage. The question is not whether these injuries will occur, but whether anyone present will know what to do when they do.

The difference between a survivable injury and a fatal one is almost always two variables: TIME and KNOWLEDGE. A severed femoral artery will kill an otherwise healthy adult in under five minutes. A fractured femur with a displaced bone end lacerating the femoral artery will kill in the same timeframe. A deep wound packed with contaminated material and sutured closed will kill in seven to fourteen days from sepsis. In every case, the outcome depends on whether someone nearby knows the correct immediate response and executes it without hesitation. Incorrect action — a tourniquet applied too loosely, a contaminated wound closed prematurely, a fracture splinted without checking distal circulation — can be as lethal as inaction.

This module covers the "Platinum 10 Minutes" — the critical window immediately following a traumatic injury during which correct intervention saves lives and incorrect intervention (or paralyzed inaction) kills. The protocols presented here derive from Tactical Combat Casualty Care (TCCC) principles developed by the Committee on Tactical Combat Casualty Care (CoTCCC) and validated across two decades of battlefield medicine, adapted for zero-technology environments where no hospital, no ambulance, and no surgeon will arrive. Every procedure is designed to be performed with materials available in a functioning settlement: cloth, sticks, cordage, boiled water, honey, and steady hands.

> DANGER: The procedures in this module — tourniquet application, wound packing, traction splinting, wound closure — involve direct manipulation of injured tissue and can cause additional harm if performed incorrectly. Tourniquet misapplication can cause nerve damage or limb loss. Improper wound closure can trap infection and produce fatal sepsis. These procedures should ONLY be performed when the alternative (doing nothing) is likely to result in death or permanent disability. Practice on training models and animal tissue before any human application. Every member of a settlement should practice tourniquet application until they can perform it in under 60 seconds with eyes closed.

Theoretical Foundation

The Physiology of Hemorrhagic Shock

The human body contains approximately 5 liters of blood, or roughly 70 mL per kilogram of body weight. This volume is not a reserve — it is the minimum required to maintain perfusion (oxygen delivery) to the brain, heart, kidneys, and other vital organs. When blood is lost faster than the body can compensate through vasoconstriction and increased heart rate, the result is hemorrhagic shock: a progressive, cascading organ failure that becomes irreversible if not halted.

The American College of Surgeons classifies blood loss into four stages:

  • Class I (<750 mL, <15% of blood volume): Minimal symptoms. Pulse remains normal. The patient may feel slightly anxious. No intervention is needed beyond direct pressure on the wound and observation. The body compensates fully at this level of loss.
  • Class II (750-1,500 mL, 15-30%): The patient becomes noticeably anxious. Pulse increases above 100 beats per minute. Pulse pressure (the difference between systolic and diastolic blood pressure) narrows, though you cannot measure this without a cuff — watch instead for the pulse becoming harder to feel at the wrist. Concerning, but usually survivable with prompt bleeding control and rest.
  • Class III (1,500-2,000 mL, 30-40%): The patient becomes confused, agitated, or combative. Pulse is rapid and weak (above 120 bpm). Skin becomes cold, pale, and clammy. Urine output drops to near zero. THIS IS LIFE-THREATENING. Immediate hemorrhage control is required. Without intervention, death follows within 30-60 minutes.
  • Class IV (>2,000 mL, >40%): The patient loses consciousness. Peripheral pulses (wrist, ankle) become absent. Skin is grey or mottled. Death is imminent without massive intervention, and even with perfect care, survival is unlikely once this stage is reached.

A severed femoral artery (inner thigh) or brachial artery (inner upper arm) can progress from Class I to Class IV in two to five minutes. This is why hemorrhage control is the FIRST priority in trauma care — before airway, before breathing, before anything else.

The "Platinum 10 Minutes" refers to the first ten minutes after a major hemorrhage. Within this window, hemorrhagic shock is usually reversible if bleeding is controlled. After this window, even if bleeding stops, the cascade of organ damage — particularly to the kidneys and gut lining — becomes increasingly irreversible. Cellular death releases toxins that cause further clotting dysfunction, creating a vicious cycle. The lesson is absolute: stop the bleeding NOW, not after you have assessed the scene, not after you have found the trauma kit, not after you have calmed down. NOW.

The Pathophysiology of Fractures

Bones break when applied force exceeds their tensile or compressive strength. The type of force determines the fracture pattern: bending forces produce transverse fractures, twisting forces produce spiral fractures, crushing forces produce comminuted (multi-fragment) fractures. For field management, the critical distinction is not the fracture pattern but the relationship between the fracture and the skin.

Fracture types relevant to field management:

  • Closed (simple) fracture: The bone is broken, but the skin over the fracture site remains intact. Managed with immobilization (splinting) and pain control. Infection risk is low.
  • Open (compound) fracture: The broken bone end penetrates through the skin, or the skin over the fracture site is broken by the same force that fractured the bone. HIGH infection risk — the bone is now exposed to environmental bacteria. Requires wound management (irrigation, dressing) in addition to splinting. Do NOT push protruding bone back under the skin — this drives surface bacteria into the wound.
  • Femur fracture: This is always an EMERGENCY regardless of whether it is open or closed. The femoral artery runs directly adjacent to the femoral shaft. Displaced bone ends can lacerate this artery, converting a fracture into a fatal hemorrhage. Additionally, femur fractures bleed 1,000 to 2,000 mL internally into the thigh compartment even without arterial damage — enough to cause Class II or Class III shock from the fracture alone.

The muscle spasm problem is central to understanding why traction splinting exists. After a long bone fracture (femur, tibia, humerus), the surrounding muscles go into involuntary spasm. This spasm pulls the broken bone ends past each other, a condition called overriding. Overriding causes three compounding problems: (a) extreme pain that worsens with every involuntary muscle contraction, (b) increased internal bleeding as the sharp bone ends saw back and forth against surrounding tissue with each movement, and (c) progressive nerve and blood vessel damage from the jagged bone edges. Traction — the application of steady, firm pulling force along the axis of the limb — counteracts the muscle spasm, separates the bone ends, and aligns them roughly in their anatomical position. This reduces pain dramatically, decreases bleeding, and protects adjacent structures.

Wound Healing Physiology

Clean wounds heal through four overlapping phases: hemostasis (minutes to hours after injury, blood clots form), inflammation (day 0 to day 4, white blood cells clear debris and bacteria), proliferation (day 4 to day 21, new tissue fills the wound), and remodeling (day 21 to 2 years, scar tissue matures and strengthens). Disrupting any phase — particularly by introducing infection during the inflammation phase — can halt healing entirely and convert a survivable wound into a fatal systemic infection.

Contaminated wounds (containing dirt, plant material, fecal matter, rust, or animal tissue) require thorough irrigation and debridement BEFORE any attempt at closure. Irrigation means physically flushing the wound with pressurized clean water to remove debris. Debridement means cutting away dead, devitalized, or heavily contaminated tissue with a sharp blade. Both are painful. Both are necessary.

The "Golden Period" is the window during which a wound can be closed (sutured, stapled, or taped) with an acceptable risk of infection. For most body regions, this window is 6 to 8 hours after injury. Within this period, bacterial colonization of the wound is low enough that closure will succeed if the wound has been properly irrigated. After 8 hours, bacterial counts in the wound tissue exceed the threshold at which the body's immune system can control them if trapped under a closed wound. After 8 hours, leave the wound OPEN. Pack it with clean dressing material. Change the packing every 24 to 48 hours. Allow the wound to heal by secondary intention (from the bottom up). This produces a larger scar but prevents the trapped-infection catastrophe.

Exception: face and scalp wounds receive exceptionally rich blood supply, which enhances local immune function. These wounds can often be closed up to 24 hours post-injury with acceptable infection rates. The rich blood supply also means that scalp wounds bleed profusely — an alarming but rarely life-threatening amount.

Burn Physiology

Burns are classified by the depth of tissue destruction:

  • Superficial (1st degree): The skin is red, dry, and painful. No blisters. Only the outermost layer (epidermis) is damaged. Heals in 3 to 5 days without scarring. Treatment: cool the burn with room-temperature water (NOT ice) for 10 to 20 minutes. Leave the burn open to air. No dressing needed.
  • Partial thickness (2nd degree): The skin blisters, weeps clear fluid, and is VERY painful. The dermis is partially damaged. Heals in 2 to 4 weeks with possible scarring. Treatment: do NOT pop blisters — intact blisters are sterile biological dressings that protect the wound beneath. If blisters have already ruptured, cover with a clean non-adherent dressing. Apply raw honey (a potent antimicrobial validated in burn literature) to the wound surface beneath the dressing.
  • Full thickness (3rd degree): The skin appears white, brown, or charred black. The texture is leathery and hard. The burn is PAINLESS because the nerve endings in the dermis have been destroyed. The full thickness of the skin is dead. This wound will NOT heal on its own — in industrial medicine, skin grafting is required. In a field setting, skin grafting is not possible. Management: prevent infection aggressively (honey, clean dressings changed daily), manage pain at the wound margins (where partial-thickness injury transitions to full-thickness), and accept that healing will be prolonged (months) with severe scarring and contracture.

The Rule of Nines provides a rapid method to estimate burn surface area:

Body Region Percentage of Total Body Surface Area
Head and neck 9%
Each arm 9%
Anterior trunk 18%
Posterior trunk 18%
Each leg 18%
Groin/perineum 1%

Burns exceeding 20% of total body surface area cause systemic inflammatory response and massive fluid shifts from the bloodstream into the burned tissue. Without aggressive fluid replacement (which requires intravenous access not available in zero-tech settings), burns of this magnitude carry very high mortality. Prevention of burn infection — the primary delayed cause of death — is critical: apply honey or clean dressings to ALL burn wounds regardless of size.

Core Principles

The following five principles govern all trauma care decisions. Memorize them. They are the decision framework that guides action when you are standing over a screaming, bleeding person and your hands are shaking.

1. The MARCH Protocol (Tactical Combat Casualty Care order of treatment):

  • M — Massive hemorrhage control (tourniquets, wound packing)
  • A — Airway management (positioning, clearing obstructions)
  • R — Respiration (chest seal for penetrating chest wounds)
  • C — Circulation (assess for shock, position patient, prevent further fluid loss)
  • H — Hypothermia prevention (insulate from ground, cover, warm the core)

This order reflects the speed at which each condition kills. Hemorrhage kills in minutes. Airway obstruction kills in minutes. Respiratory failure kills in minutes to hours. Circulatory shock kills in hours. Hypothermia kills in hours. Always address the fastest killer first. Do NOT skip ahead to splint a fracture while arterial blood is spurting from another wound.

2. The Tourniquet Decision Rule: Apply a tourniquet for ANY bright red, spurting (arterial) bleeding from an extremity that does not stop with 30 seconds of firm direct pressure. A tourniquet applied unnecessarily can be removed later with no permanent damage. A tourniquet not applied when needed results in death. When in doubt, apply it.

3. The Golden Period Rule: Close clean, irrigated wounds within 6 to 8 hours of injury. After 8 hours, do NOT close the wound. Leave it open, pack with clean dressing material, and change the packing every 24 to 48 hours. Closing a contaminated wound traps bacteria beneath the skin, producing abscess, then cellulitis, then sepsis, then death.

4. The Splint-It-Where-It-Lies Rule: For suspected fractures, immobilize the limb in the position found. The splint must include the joint ABOVE and the joint BELOW the fracture site. Do NOT attempt to "set" (reduce) a fracture unless you have specific training and experience — pushing bone fragments blindly can sever nerves and blood vessels, converting a simple fracture into a catastrophic vascular injury.

5. The Hypothermia Prevention Rule: EVERY trauma patient loses body heat rapidly due to blood loss (less circulating warm fluid), shock (peripheral vasoconstriction diverts warm blood from the skin), wet clothing (evaporative cooling), ground contact (conductive cooling), and exposed skin (radiative and convective cooling). Insulate the patient from the ground with a sleeping pad, branches, or dry clothing. Cover with blankets, tarps, or additional clothing. Warm the core by placing warm stones (heated in fire, wrapped in cloth to prevent burns) in the armpits and groin. Hypothermia causes coagulopathy (the blood loses its ability to clot), which causes more bleeding, which causes more hypothermia — this is the "lethal triad" of trauma (hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy). Treat hypothermia as aggressively as you treat the wound itself.

Practical Implementation

Hemorrhage Control

The Windlass Tourniquet

In zero-tech environments, massive blood loss from extremity wounds is the primary cause of preventable death. Simple direct pressure with a hand or cloth is adequate for venous bleeding and minor arterial oozing, but a transected major artery requires mechanical compression against bone — force that exceeds what human hands can sustain. The windlass tourniquet generates this force through mechanical advantage: a rigid stick twisted within a fabric band progressively tightens until the artery is pinched closed against the underlying bone.

Research from the Committee on Tactical Combat Casualty Care (CoTCCC) validates that improvised tourniquets without a mechanical windlass fail to achieve complete arterial occlusion in over 90% of cases, producing a dangerous "venous tourniquet" effect — blocking low-pressure venous return while allowing high-pressure arterial inflow, which actually INCREASES total blood loss.

Step-by-Step Application:

  1. Identify the bleed. Bright red blood spurting in rhythmic pulses indicates arterial bleeding. Dark red blood flowing steadily indicates venous bleeding. Both can be fatal, but arterial bleeding kills in minutes and demands a tourniquet.
  2. Select fabric. Find a strong piece of fabric at least 5 cm (2 inches) wide: a canvas strip, leather belt, folded triangular bandage, or a trouser leg cut and folded. NEVER use thin cordage, wire, paracord, or shoelaces — material narrower than 4 cm cuts into tissue, damages nerves, and fails to occlude the artery before causing unacceptable tissue destruction.
  3. Place HIGH AND TIGHT. Wrap the fabric around the limb ABOVE the wound, as close to the torso as practical. If you cannot identify the exact bleeding source through clothing, place the tourniquet as high on the limb as possible — upper thigh for leg wounds, high in the armpit for arm wounds. A tourniquet placed too low (below the bleeding vessel) is useless.
  4. Tie a half-knot. Tie a simple overhand (half) knot on the top surface of the limb, pulling it snug against the skin.
  5. Place the windlass. Lay a strong, rigid stick (15-20 cm of hardwood, a metal wrench, a thick tool handle, a sturdy bone) across the half-knot.
  6. Secure the windlass. Tie a full square knot OVER the stick, locking it in place against the limb.
  7. Twist. Rotate the stick. Each half-turn mechanically shortens the fabric band, compressing the underlying muscle tissue against the bone and progressively occluding the artery. Twist until bleeding stops COMPLETELY. Not "slows down." STOPS.
  8. Lock the windlass. Secure the ends of the stick to the limb with another strip of cloth, a loop of cordage, or by tucking the stick under the tourniquet band itself, preventing it from unwinding.
  9. Mark the time. Write the time of tourniquet application on the patient's forehead or on a visible area of skin using any available marking material — blood, charcoal, ink, mud. Use the format "TQ" followed by the time (e.g., "TQ 14:30"). This information is critical for anyone who subsequently manages the patient.

Common errors:

  • Tourniquet too narrow: Material less than 4 cm wide concentrates pressure on a small band of tissue, destroying nerves and skin before generating enough deep pressure to occlude the artery. Always use wide material.
  • Tourniquet too loose (venous tourniquet): The most dangerous error. A loosely applied tourniquet blocks low-pressure veins (3-5 mmHg) but not high-pressure arteries (80-120 mmHg). Blood flows IN but cannot flow OUT, causing the limb to swell, pressure to build, and total blood loss to INCREASE. If the limb is swelling and turning purple but still bleeding, the tourniquet is too loose — tighten it further.
  • Tourniquet placed over a joint: The elbow, knee, and wrist have complex bony anatomy that prevents circumferential compression. A tourniquet over a joint cannot generate uniform pressure. Always place the tourniquet on the straight shaft of the limb (upper arm, thigh, forearm, lower leg).
  • Premature removal: Do not remove a tourniquet in the field unless you are certain the bleeding source has been definitively controlled by other means (wound packing, vessel ligation). Removing a tourniquet from an un-repaired artery immediately restarts the hemorrhage.

Time limits: The historical "2-hour rule" (remove tourniquet within 2 hours to prevent limb loss) is now considered overly conservative based on modern military data. Analysis of over 700 tourniquet applications in Iraq and Afghanistan demonstrates that tourniquets in place for 4 to 6 hours still achieve limb salvage rates above 90%. Nevertheless, minimize tourniquet time when possible — tissue distal to the tourniquet receives no blood flow and accumulates metabolic waste. If transport to a person capable of wound repair is possible within hours, keep the tourniquet in place until definitive care.

Wound Packing (Junctional Hemorrhage)

Tourniquets work only on extremities — anatomical locations where a circumferential band can compress an artery against a single underlying bone. They do NOT work on the neck, axilla (armpit), or groin, where major vessels run in areas that cannot be circumferentially compressed. For hemorrhage from these junctional zones, wound packing is the primary intervention.

Procedure:

  1. Expose the wound. Cut away clothing to visualize the bleeding source.
  2. Pack the wound cavity. Take clean cloth (gauze if available, torn clothing if not) and push it DIRECTLY INTO the wound cavity using your fingers. Do not lay cloth on top of the wound — push it IN, filling the cavity from the deepest point outward. The cloth must compress directly against the bleeding vessel.
  3. Pack tightly. Continue adding cloth until the wound cavity is firmly filled and the cloth is under visible compression. Loose packing is useless.
  4. Apply direct pressure. Place the heel of your hand over the packed wound and press HARD — use your full body weight. Hold this pressure for a minimum of 3 full minutes. Time it. Three minutes feels like an eternity. Do not lift your hand to "check if it stopped" — this breaks the forming clot and restarts the bleeding.
  5. Apply a pressure dressing. After 3 minutes of sustained pressure, apply a circumferential bandage over the packed wound, pulled as tight as you can manage. This maintains compression after you remove your hands.
  6. Do not remove the packing. Once packing has achieved hemostasis, leave it in place. Removal of blood-soaked packing tears the clot and restarts hemorrhage. The packing will need to be removed and the wound irrigated within 24 hours to prevent infection, but this is a secondary concern — the patient must survive the first hour before infection becomes relevant.

Fracture Management

The Traction Splint (Femur Fractures)

A fractured femur can sever the femoral artery if the bone shifts. The massive thigh muscles (quadriceps, hamstrings) go into immediate spasm, pulling the jagged bone ends past each other and converting a fracture into a grinding, bleeding internal wound. Traction splinting counteracts the muscle spasm by applying steady longitudinal force to the limb, separating the bone ends, reducing internal bleeding, and dramatically reducing pain.

Step-by-Step Application:

  1. Apply manual traction. Have the strongest available person grasp the patient's ankle firmly with both hands and pull the leg straight along its long axis, steadily and firmly, aligning it with the uninjured leg. This is excruciating for the patient but necessary — warn them, then do it. DO NOT RELEASE THIS PULL until the splint is fully secured and maintaining traction mechanically.
  2. Measure the splints. Find two sturdy, straight branches, planks, or poles. The OUTSIDE splint must reach from the patient's armpit to at least 30 cm past the sole of the foot. The INSIDE splint must reach from the groin to at least 30 cm past the sole of the foot. The extension past the foot is critical — this is where the traction force will be applied.
  3. Pad the splints. Wrap the splints with clothing, moss, dry grass, or any available soft material, concentrating padding at bony prominences: the hip, the knee, the ankle.
  4. Bind the splints to the body. Using cloth strips, rope, or belts, tie the splints to the leg at a minimum of four points: (a) ankle, (b) below the knee, (c) above the knee, (d) upper thigh near the groin. The outside splint is also bound to the torso at the chest/armpit level. NEVER tie a binding directly over the fracture site.
  5. Apply mechanical traction. Tie a figure-of-eight hitch around the patient's ankle and boot (or directly around the ankle with heavy padding if no boot is available). Attach this hitch to the distal end of the splints using a length of cordage. Insert a stick through the cordage between the splint ends and the ankle hitch — this is a Spanish windlass. Twist the stick to shorten the cordage, applying steady traction to the ankle. Twist until the patient reports significant pain reduction and the injured leg appears approximately the same length as the uninjured leg. Secure the twist stick against the splint to prevent unwinding.
  6. The manual traction assistant may now release. The mechanical traction system has taken over.

Monitoring after application: Check the distal pulse (dorsalis pedis pulse on top of the foot, or posterior tibial pulse behind the inner ankle bone) and sensation (ask the patient: "Can you feel me touching your toes?") every 30 minutes. If the pulse disappears or the patient loses sensation, the traction force is excessive or a binding is too tight — loosen the Spanish windlass by a quarter-turn and recheck. Document each check.

Simple Splinting (Arm, Lower Leg, Wrist, Forearm)

For fractures other than the femur, traction is generally unnecessary. The goal is immobilization: prevent the broken bone ends from moving, which reduces pain, prevents further tissue damage, and allows transport.

Materials: Any rigid item that spans the fracture and the joints above and below it — straight sticks, boards, bark slabs, rolled blankets, a rolled newspaper or cardboard, a folded sleeping pad, or a commercial SAM splint if available.

Procedure:

  1. Pad all bony prominences (knuckles, wrist bones, elbow, ankle bones, kneecap) with soft material to prevent pressure sores.
  2. Place the rigid splint material along the injured limb, extending past the joint above AND the joint below the fracture.
  3. Bind the splint to the limb with cloth strips at multiple points, avoiding the fracture site itself.
  4. Check distal circulation immediately after splinting: press the patient's fingernail or toenail firmly for 2 seconds, then release. The nail bed should return from white to pink in less than 2 seconds (capillary refill). If refill is delayed or absent, the splint or bindings are too tight — loosen and recheck.
  5. Recheck distal circulation every 30 minutes. Swelling may develop over hours, causing previously adequate bindings to become constrictive.

Wound Management

Wound Irrigation

Irrigation is the single most important step in wound care. The mechanical action of pressurized water physically removes dirt, debris, bacteria, and devitalized tissue fragments from the wound bed. Without irrigation, no amount of antibiotic treatment (herbal or pharmaceutical) will prevent wound infection.

Procedure:

  1. Prepare clean water. Boil water and allow it to cool to body temperature (warm to the touch, not hot). If boiling is not possible, use the cleanest available water — even untreated stream water under pressure is better than no irrigation at all, because the benefit of mechanical debris removal outweighs the bacterial load of the water.
  2. Generate pressure. An improvised irrigation syringe can be made from a clean plastic bag, water bladder, or animal bladder with a small puncture hole (2-3 mm). Squeeze the container to produce a focused jet of water directed into the wound. Alternatively, a syringe (veterinary or medical) with an 18-gauge needle produces ideal irrigation pressure (approximately 8 psi).
  3. Irrigate copiously. The minimum effective volume is 500 mL of water per 2 cm of wound length. A 6 cm laceration requires at least 1,500 mL (1.5 liters) of irrigation. More is better. Continue irrigating until the wound bed appears clean and pink, free of visible debris.
  4. Irrigate from clean to dirty. Direct the water stream from the cleanest part of the wound toward the most contaminated area, flushing debris OUT of the wound rather than deeper into it.

Wound Closure

Wound closure accelerates healing, reduces infection risk (if performed correctly within the Golden Period), improves cosmetic outcome, and reduces pain. However, closure performed incorrectly — on contaminated wounds, outside the Golden Period, or with inadequate technique — traps bacteria and guarantees infection.

Methods (from simplest/safest to most complex):

  • Butterfly strips (adhesive wound closure strips): The simplest and lowest-risk closure method. Apply strips perpendicular to the wound, pulling the edges together. Best for shallow, clean, low-tension wounds. If commercially made strips are unavailable, improvise from adhesive tape cut into narrow strips.
  • Sutures: For deep wounds with significant tension that will not hold with strips alone. Use any strong, smooth thread — silk, nylon monofilament (fishing line), or clean cotton thread. Sterilize by boiling for 10 minutes. Use a curved needle: a standard sewing needle can be bent into a C-shape by heating the middle third over a flame until red, then bending with pliers, then quenching in water to re-harden. Place simple interrupted sutures at 5 mm intervals, entering the skin 3 mm from the wound edge, passing through the depth of the wound, and exiting 3 mm from the opposite edge. Tie each suture with a square knot snugged down until the wound edges just touch — do not strangulate the tissue by pulling too tight.
  • Staples: If surgical staples are available from salvage, they are faster to apply than sutures and produce comparable outcomes. Follow the same wound selection criteria.

Do NOT close the following wounds under any circumstances:

  • Animal bites (high bacterial load from animal saliva, particularly anaerobic organisms)
  • Human bites (the human mouth contains the highest bacterial diversity of any bite wound)
  • Puncture wounds (narrow, deep track that cannot be adequately irrigated)
  • Wounds more than 8 hours old (bacterial colonization exceeds safe threshold)
  • Visibly contaminated wounds that cannot be adequately irrigated
  • Wounds with significant tissue loss (the edges cannot be approximated without tension)

Leave these wounds open. Pack with clean, damp dressing material. Change the packing every 24 to 48 hours. Allow healing by secondary intention.

Burn Management

Immediate response (all burns):

  1. Remove the patient from the heat source. Extinguish burning clothing. Remove clothing from the burned area unless it is adhered to the burn — pulling adhered fabric tears tissue.
  2. Cool the burn with room-temperature clean water for 10 to 20 minutes. Do NOT use ice, ice water, or snow — extreme cold causes vasoconstriction that worsens tissue damage. Do NOT apply butter, oil, toothpaste, or any folk remedy. Clean water only.
  3. Assess burn depth and area using the classifications and Rule of Nines described in the Theoretical Foundation above.

Superficial (1st degree) burns: No dressing required. The burn will heal in 3 to 5 days. Manage pain with willow bark decoction (see 03. Temperate Antibiotic Kit for preparation of analgesic botanicals).

Partial thickness (2nd degree) burns: If blisters are intact, do not puncture them. Cover with a clean, non-adherent dressing (cloth lightly coated with rendered animal fat or honey to prevent sticking). If blisters have ruptured, gently remove loose skin with clean scissors, apply a thin layer of raw honey to the wound surface, and cover with a clean dressing. Change dressings every 24 to 48 hours. Monitor for signs of infection: increasing pain, spreading redness, cloudy or foul-smelling drainage, fever.

Full thickness (3rd degree) burns: Apply honey to the wound surface. Cover with a clean, non-adherent dressing. Change dressings daily. Elevate the burned area if possible to reduce edema. Begin gentle range-of-motion exercises of burned joints within 48 hours to prevent contracture (permanent shortening of scar tissue that restricts movement). Accept that healing will be prolonged and scarring will be severe.

Fluid replacement for large burns (>20% body surface area): Without intravenous access, oral rehydration is the only option. Prepare an oral rehydration solution: 1 liter of clean water, 6 level teaspoons of sugar, and half a level teaspoon of salt. The patient should drink as much as they can tolerate — the goal is to maintain urine output (any urine production indicates adequate hydration). Expect to need 3 to 5 liters per day for a major burn patient.

Common Failure Modes

1. Tourniquet Too Loose (Venous Tourniquet). The tourniquet blocks low-pressure venous return but not high-pressure arterial inflow. Result: the limb engorges with blood, turns purple, and total blood loss INCREASES rather than decreases. The rescuer believes the tourniquet is working because they see it on the limb, while the patient bleeds to death beneath it. Prevention: tighten until bleeding stops COMPLETELY. If the limb is swelling or still oozing, the tourniquet is too loose. Tighten further. It should hurt — if the patient does not report significant discomfort from the tourniquet, it is probably too loose.

2. Wound Closed Over Contamination. A wound is sutured with dirt, plant material, or devitalized tissue still inside. The sutures seal bacteria into a warm, moist, nutrient-rich environment. Within 48 to 72 hours, an abscess forms. Within 5 to 10 days, cellulitis (spreading tissue infection) develops. Within 7 to 14 days, sepsis (bloodstream infection) follows. Mortality from sepsis without antibiotics exceeds 50%. Prevention: irrigate every wound thoroughly before closure. If in doubt about wound cleanliness, do NOT close it. An open wound that heals slowly is infinitely preferable to a closed wound that kills.

3. Splint Not Including Adjacent Joints. A forearm fracture is splinted from mid-forearm to wrist, leaving the elbow free. Every time the patient moves the elbow, the fracture site moves, causing increased pain, internal bleeding, and potential nerve damage. Prevention: ALWAYS immobilize the joint above AND the joint below the fracture. A forearm fracture splint extends from above the elbow to the hand. A lower leg fracture splint extends from above the knee to past the foot.

4. Failure to Monitor Distal Circulation. A splint or tourniquet cuts off blood flow to the extremity. Without periodic checks, this is not detected until tissue death has occurred — by which point amputation may be the only option. Prevention: check the pulse and sensation distal to every splint and tourniquet every 30 minutes. Document each check. If the pulse disappears or sensation is lost, loosen the device immediately and reassess.

5. Hypothermia Neglect. The trauma patient is left lying on cold ground, in wet clothing, with wounds exposed to air, while the rescuer focuses exclusively on the injury. Within 30 to 60 minutes, core temperature drops below 35 degrees Celsius. Below this threshold, the blood clotting cascade progressively fails (coagulopathy), making hemorrhage control increasingly difficult regardless of mechanical interventions. The lethal triad — hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy — becomes self-reinforcing. Prevention: insulate the patient from the ground immediately (sleeping pad, branches, dry clothing barrier). Remove wet clothing. Cover with every available blanket, tarp, or coat. Place warmed stones (wrapped in cloth) in the armpits and groin. Treat hypothermia as aggressively as the wound itself.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Hemorrhagic shock: Progressive organ failure caused by insufficient blood volume to maintain perfusion. Classified in four stages by percentage of blood volume lost.
  • Tourniquet: A circumferential compressive device applied to an extremity to occlude arterial blood flow and stop hemorrhage.
  • Windlass: A rigid stick inserted through a tourniquet band and twisted to generate mechanical tightening force.
  • Traction: The application of steady pulling force along the long axis of a limb to counteract muscle spasm and align fracture fragments.
  • Splint: A rigid device applied externally to a limb to immobilize a fracture and prevent movement of bone ends.
  • MARCH protocol: The Tactical Combat Casualty Care prioritization sequence: Massive hemorrhage, Airway, Respiration, Circulation, Hypothermia.
  • Debridement: The surgical removal of dead, devitalized, or contaminated tissue from a wound to promote healing and prevent infection.
  • Irrigation: The use of pressurized fluid to mechanically flush debris and bacteria from a wound cavity.
  • Golden Period: The 6-to-8-hour window after injury during which wound closure carries acceptable infection risk.
  • Rule of Nines: A rapid estimation method for burn surface area, dividing the body into regions each representing approximately 9% (or multiples thereof) of total body surface area.
  • Coagulopathy: A pathological failure of the blood clotting cascade, commonly caused by hypothermia, acidosis, or massive blood loss. Results in inability to form clots and uncontrollable hemorrhage.
  • Lethal triad: The self-reinforcing cycle of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy in trauma patients. Each element worsens the other two.
  • Cellulitis: A spreading bacterial infection of the skin and subcutaneous tissue, characterized by expanding redness, warmth, swelling, and pain.
  • Sepsis: A life-threatening systemic inflammatory response to infection, characterized by fever (or hypothermia), rapid pulse, rapid breathing, confusion, and progressive organ failure.
  • Capillary refill: A test of peripheral perfusion: press a fingernail or toenail bed, release, and time the return of pink color. Normal refill occurs in less than 2 seconds. Delayed refill indicates impaired circulation.
