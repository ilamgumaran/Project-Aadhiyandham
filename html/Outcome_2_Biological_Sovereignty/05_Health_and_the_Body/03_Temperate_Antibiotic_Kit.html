Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Pharmaceutical antibiotics are among the most fragile pillars of modern civilization. They require industrial chemistry, sterile manufacturing, global supply chains, and cold storage. In any sustained collapse scenario — whether regional infrastructure failure, pandemic-driven supply disruption, or full civilizational reset — the antibiotic supply will exhaust within months. Penicillin tablets degrade. Amoxicillin expires. The factories that synthesize ciprofloxacin and azithromycin require electricity, purified water, and precursor chemicals sourced from three continents. When those chains break, they do not repair themselves. The settlement that has not developed a renewable, locally-sourced antimicrobial pharmacy will watch people die from infected cuts — the same wounds that killed millions before Alexander Fleming's 1928 discovery.

This module provides that pharmacy. It is not a collection of folk remedies or grandmother's wisdom, though it draws on traditions stretching back thousands of years. It is a pharmacologically grounded toolkit built on compounds whose antimicrobial mechanisms have been characterized in peer-reviewed literature, tested against known pathogens, and assigned minimum inhibitory concentrations in laboratory settings. Allicin from garlic disrupts bacterial cell membranes by reacting with thiol groups in essential enzymes. Thymol from thyme and oregano inserts into lipid bilayers and lyses bacterial cells. Hydrogen peroxide generated by glucose oxidase in raw honey creates a sustained oxidative environment hostile to nearly all bacteria. Berberine from Oregon grape intercalates into bacterial DNA and inhibits cell division. These are real medicines with real mechanisms of action — and they are also limited medicines that cannot replace surgical-grade systemic antibiotics for deep tissue infections, bacteremia, or sepsis.

The settlement pharmacist must understand both the power and the limits of this kit. These compounds can manage the infections that will constitute ninety percent of post-collapse infectious disease: wound infections, skin abscesses, oral infections, gastrointestinal pathogens, fungal skin infections. For that ninety percent, this botanical pharmacy is not a compromise — it is genuinely effective medicine. For the remaining ten percent — the deep systemic infections, the sepsis cases, the pneumonias — these compounds buy time but may not save the patient. That honest reckoning is itself a form of sovereignty: knowing what you can treat and what will kill despite your best efforts.

> CAUTION: Botanical antibiotics are effective for TOPICAL wound care, mild skin and soft tissue infections, and gastrointestinal infections. They are NOT substitutes for systemic antibiotics in life-threatening infections (sepsis, pneumonia, meningitis). If a patient develops high fever (>39 degrees C), confusion, rapid pulse, or spreading redness from a wound (cellulitis), the infection has likely become systemic. Botanical treatments alone may not be sufficient. Escalate to any available pharmaceutical antibiotics and prepare for the possibility that the patient may not survive.

Theoretical Foundation

The Pharmacology of Plant Antimicrobials

Plants evolved antimicrobial compounds for their own defense — thyme produces thymol against fungal pathogens in Mediterranean soils, garlic generates allicin against soil bacteria. Over hundreds of millions of years of coevolution with microbial pathogens, plants developed chemical weapons that happen to be effective against many of the same organisms that infect human tissues. Understanding the pharmacology of each compound transforms these plants from folk remedies into a rational therapeutic system.

*Allicin (from garlic, Allium sativum):*

Allicin (diallyl thiosulfinate) is produced when garlic cells are crushed — the enzyme alliinase converts alliin to allicin. This reaction requires approximately ten minutes to reach peak concentration.

  • Mechanism: Allicin reacts with thiol groups (-SH) in bacterial enzymes, forming mixed disulfides that disrupt cell metabolism. This multi-target mechanism means resistance development is slow compared to single-target pharmaceutical antibiotics.
  • Spectrum: Effective against gram-positive (S. aureus, Streptococcus, Enterococcus), gram-negative (E. coli, Salmonella, Klebsiella), and fungi (Candida, Aspergillus). Limited activity against Pseudomonas.
  • Key research: Ankri and Mirelman (Microbes and Infection, 1999) — MIC of allicin against S. aureus: 8-32 micrograms/mL, readily achievable in topical applications from fresh garlic.
  • Critical preparation detail: Allicin is unstable — degrades within 24-48 hours and is destroyed by heat. Garlic must be freshly crushed and used immediately. Allow ten minutes after crushing for full enzymatic conversion.
  • Dosage: Topical — fresh crushed poultice on wound for 15-30 minutes (longer causes chemical burns). Internal — 2-4 fresh cloves per day, crushed and swallowed with water.

*Thymol and Carvacrol (from thyme, Thymus vulgaris, and oregano, Origanum vulgare):*

Thymol and carvacrol are monoterpene phenols — the primary antimicrobials in thyme and oregano. Oregano essential oil contains 60-80% carvacrol and thymol combined, making it the most concentrated botanical antimicrobial in temperate zones. Thymol is the active ingredient in Listerine mouthwash, first formulated in 1879 as a surgical antiseptic.

  • Mechanism: Thymol inserts into bacterial cell membranes, increasing permeability and causing cell lysis. It also denatures bacterial proteins. Carvacrol operates identically.
  • Spectrum: Broad-spectrum — S. aureus, E. coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Candida, Listeria, Helicobacter pylori. One of the few botanical antimicrobials effective against Pseudomonas.
  • Preparation: Infusion — 20g dried thyme in 200 mL boiling water, steep 10 minutes, strain. Decoction (stronger) — boil 10 minutes. Oregano oil (steam-distilled): dilute 1 drop in 10 mL carrier oil for topical use. Never apply undiluted essential oil to skin.

Honey (raw, unprocessed, any floral source):

Honey is a complete wound management system documented in the Edwin Smith Papyrus (circa 1500 BCE). Modern randomized controlled trials confirm its efficacy for wound healing, burn treatment, and infection management.

  • Three antimicrobial mechanisms operating simultaneously:
  1. Osmotic dehydration: Sugar concentration exceeds 80% — draws water from bacteria via osmosis, dehydrating and killing them. Same principle as salt curing.
  2. Hydrogen peroxide generation: Glucose oxidase (from bee saliva) converts glucose to gluconic acid + H2O2 when honey is diluted by wound exudate. Low concentration (~1 mmol/L) but sustained over hours — continuous antiseptic activity.
  3. Low pH: Honey pH 3.2-4.5, hostile to most pathogenic bacteria.
  • Wound-healing properties: Anti-inflammatory, promotes angiogenesis and tissue granulation. Wounds heal faster even without infection.
  • Any raw honey works. Manuka honey has additional non-peroxide antimicrobial activity (methylglyoxal), but ANY raw, unprocessed honey is effective medicine through the three mechanisms above.
  • Application: 3 mm layer on clean wound, cover with cloth, change every 24-48 hours. Also effective as burn dressing, oral rinse (diluted 1:4), and cough suppressant.
  • Storage: Raw honey never spoils — edible honey has been found in 3,000-year-old Egyptian tombs. Crystallized honey is not spoiled; warm gently below 40 degrees C to reliquify.

*Berberine (from Oregon grape, Mahonia aquifolium, and goldenseal, Hydrastis canadensis):*

Berberine is an isoquinoline alkaloid — the yellow pigment in goldenseal and Oregon grape roots. It appears in three independent medical traditions (Native American, Traditional Chinese Medicine, Ayurveda), a convergence suggesting genuine efficacy.

  • Mechanism: Berberine intercalates into bacterial DNA, inhibiting replication. It also inhibits bacterial FtsZ protein (cell division) and efflux pumps (the machinery bacteria use to expel antibiotics), making it a synergist with other antimicrobials.
  • Spectrum: E. coli, S. aureus, Streptococcus, Vibrio cholerae, Shigella, and the protozoan Giardia lamblia. Anti-Giardia activity is particularly valuable — giardiasis from contaminated water will be common post-collapse.
  • Preparation: Decoction — simmer 30g dried root in 500 mL water for 20 minutes, strain (liquid should be deep yellow). Tincture — 1:5 in 50% alcohol, 4-6 weeks.
  • Application: Internal for diarrheal infections (50-100 mL 3x daily). Topical for wound washing. Eyewash for bacterial conjunctivitis (dilute 1:1 with sterile water).
  • Sustainability: Goldenseal is overharvested. Oregon grape (Mahonia aquifolium) is a sustainable alternative. Cultivate rather than wild-harvest.

*Plantain (Plantago major — NOT the banana relative):*

Plantago major is one of the most ubiquitous plants in the temperate world, found on every inhabited continent in disturbed soils, roadsides, lawns, fields, and waste ground. The Anglo-Saxons called it "waybread" (waegbrade) — the plant of the path — and it was one of the nine sacred herbs in the Old English "Nine Herbs Charm." Its ubiquity makes it the most reliably available emergency wound treatment in any temperate environment.

  • Active compounds: Aucubin (antimicrobial iridoid glycoside), allantoin (promotes cell proliferation and wound healing — also used in modern pharmaceutical wound creams), mucilage (soothing, anti-inflammatory demulcent), and tannins (astringent, promotes hemostasis — stops minor bleeding).
  • The spit poultice: This is the primary emergency application. Harvest several fresh plantain leaves. Chew them to a paste (salivary amylase partially activates some compounds and the mechanical disruption releases cellular contents). Apply the paste directly to wounds, insect stings, bee stings, or minor burns. Cover with a whole plantain leaf as a natural bandage. This is a legitimate emergency wound dressing validated by the plant's pharmacological profile.
  • Infusion for internal use: Steep 15-20g fresh or dried leaves in 200 mL boiling water for 10 minutes. Drink for sore throat, cough, or mild gastrointestinal inflammation. The mucilage content makes it soothing for irritated mucous membranes.

*Salicin (from willow bark, Salix spp.):*

Salicin is not an antibiotic — it is included because pain management and fever reduction are critical during infection treatment. Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) was derived from salicin isolated from willow bark.

  • Mechanism: Salicin is metabolized to salicylic acid in the liver, inhibiting COX enzymes and reducing prostaglandin synthesis. Effects: analgesia, anti-inflammatory, antipyretic.
  • Preparation: Decoction of inner bark (cambium, from young branches) — simmer 20g in 500 mL water for 15 minutes.
  • Dosage: 100-200 mL three times daily (~50-100 mg salicin per dose). Onset slower than aspirin (1-2 hours) due to hepatic conversion.
  • CAUTION: Inhibits platelet aggregation — do NOT give with active bleeding or bleeding disorders. Do NOT give to children under 12 (Reye's syndrome risk). Do NOT give to patients with aspirin allergy.

Tincture and Extract Preparation Science

Stable extracts transform a seasonal garden into a year-round pharmacy. The five extraction methods:

  • Tincture (alcohol extract): Alcohol extracts both water-soluble and fat-soluble compounds. Ratio: 1:5 dried herb to alcohol by weight (1:2 for fresh herbs). Alcohol: 40-60% ethanol (vodka, brandy, distilled grain alcohol). Extraction: 4-6 weeks in sealed glass jar, shaken daily. Shelf life: 3-5 years in dark glass.
  • Infusion: Boiling water over leaves/flowers, steep 10-15 minutes covered, strain. Water-soluble compounds only. Shelf life: 24-48 hours. Make fresh daily.
  • Decoction: Simmer roots/bark in water 10-30 minutes, strain. Stronger than infusions. Same 24-48 hour shelf life.
  • Oil infusion: Dried herbs in olive/sunflower oil, 2-4 weeks in warm location. Strain. Shelf life: 6-12 months. Use only dried herbs (fresh herbs introduce water, causing spoilage).
  • Honey infusion: Dried herbs submerged in raw honey, 2-4 weeks. Shelf life: indefinite.

Core Principles

  1. The Topical-First Rule. Botanical antibiotics are most effective when applied directly to the infection site — wound surface, skin lesion, oral cavity, gastrointestinal tract. Systemic absorption from oral doses is variable, often poor, and almost never sufficient for deep tissue infections. A garlic poultice on a wound delivers allicin directly to the bacteria. Swallowed garlic cloves deliver a fraction of the allicin to the bloodstream after hepatic metabolism. Always prefer topical application for skin and wound infections.
  2. The Freshness Imperative. Allicin degrades within hours. Thymol volatilizes from dried herbs over months. Infusions and decoctions spoil within days. The botanical pharmacy is fundamentally a LIVING pharmacy — garden beds of garlic, thyme, oregano, and plantain, actively growing and available for harvest. Dried stocks and tinctures are the backup, not the primary supply. Every settlement must maintain dedicated medicinal garden beds with the same priority given to food crops.
  3. The Escalation Ladder. Treat infections aggressively and early. A minor wound infection left untreated for three days becomes cellulitis; cellulitis left untreated becomes sepsis; sepsis kills. The escalation protocol is: (1) clean wound plus honey dressing, (2) garlic poultice (15 minutes) followed by honey dressing, (3) thyme or oregano wash plus honey dressing plus berberine decoction internally, (4) pharmaceutical antibiotics if any remain available, (5) surgical drainage if abscess has formed. Move to the next step if there is no improvement within 48 hours.
  4. The Honey Rule. When in doubt about which botanical antimicrobial to use, use honey. It is the most versatile (wound dressing, burn treatment, cough suppressant, oral rinse), the most stable (never spoils), the most evidence-supported (randomized controlled trials, not just in vitro studies), and the safest (no dosage toxicity, no drug interactions, safe for children over 12 months). Every settlement must maintain beehives. Honey is not optional — it is the single most important medicine in the post-collapse pharmacy.
  5. The Limit of Botanical Medicine. Botanical antibiotics can manage localized infections. They cannot reliably treat systemic infections — bacteremia, sepsis, pneumonia, meningitis, pyelonephritis. If a patient develops signs of systemic infection (fever above 39 degrees C sustained for more than 24 hours, confusion or altered mental status, rapid heart rate above 100 beats per minute at rest, spreading redness extending more than 2 cm from a wound edge), botanical treatments alone may fail. This is an honest limitation, not a failure of the system. Acknowledge it. Continue treatment. Prepare the community for the possible outcome.

Building a Botanical Press

To keep medicinal herbs potent through winter, they must be dried quickly and thoroughly to prevent mold while retaining maximum active compound concentration. A botanical press accelerates drying under controlled pressure.

Materials Needed:

  • Two flat pieces of hardwood, each approximately 30 cm by 40 cm (oak, maple, or any dense hardwood)
  • Corrugated cardboard (multiple sheets, cut to size)
  • Unbleached paper or clean cotton cloth (multiple sheets)
  • Four long bolts with wingnuts, one for each corner
  • Drill with a bit matching the bolt diameter

Construction and Use:

  1. Drill a hole in each corner of both boards, ensuring alignment so the bolts pass through cleanly.
  2. Assemble the sandwich in this order, bottom to top: lower board, cardboard sheet, paper or cloth, a single layer of medicinal leaves or flowers (not overlapping), paper or cloth, cardboard sheet. Repeat the cardboard-paper-herb-paper-cardboard layers for multiple batches.
  3. Place the upper board on top and insert the bolts through all four corners. Hand-tighten the wingnuts.
  4. Tighten the wingnuts by one quarter-turn each day as moisture leaves the plant material. The cardboard wicks moisture away from the herbs; the paper prevents the herbs from sticking to the cardboard.
  5. Drying time: 5-10 days depending on ambient humidity and plant water content. Herbs are fully dry when they crumble between the fingers rather than bending.
  6. Store dried herbs immediately in sealed containers (glass jars with tight lids, ceramic pots with fitted covers) away from light and moisture. Label with plant name and date of drying.

Result: Paper-thin, thoroughly dried herbs that retain 80-90% of their volatile medicinal compounds. Properly dried and stored herbs remain potent for 6-12 months.

Preparation Protocols

Fresh Poultice (garlic, plantain):

Crush or chew fresh plant material to a paste. Apply directly to wound. For garlic, limit to 15-30 minutes (chemical burn risk). For plantain, leave 1-4 hours or until dry.

Infusion (thyme, plantain leaf):

20g dried herb (or 40g fresh) in a heat-proof vessel. Pour 200 mL boiling water over herbs. Cover, steep 10-15 minutes, strain through cloth. Use immediately for wound washing, gargling, or drinking. Discard after 24 hours.

Decoction (berberine root, willow bark):

20-30g dried root or bark in 500 mL cold water. Bring to gentle simmer (not rolling boil). Maintain 15-20 minutes. Strain. Use immediately or store covered for up to 48 hours.

Tincture (long-term preservation):

1:5 ratio dried herb to alcohol by weight (e.g., 100g dried thyme to 500 mL vodka). Submerge herb in clean glass jar, seal tightly, store in dark location. Shake daily for 4-6 weeks. Strain, pressing to extract all liquid. Store in dark glass bottles labeled with contents, ratio, and date.

Honey Infusion:

Fill jar one-third with dried herbs. Pour raw honey to fill. Stir out air pockets, seal, invert daily for 2-4 weeks. Use on wounds, for sore throat, or diluted for irrigation.

Oil Infusion:

Jar half-full of dried herbs, covered with olive or sunflower oil. Warm location 2-4 weeks, shaking daily. Alternative: double-boiler method at 60-70 degrees C for 4-6 hours. Strain. Use only dried herbs to prevent spoilage.

Wound Care Protocol

This is the complete wound care protocol integrating botanical antimicrobials. Memorize it. Practice it. Every adult in the settlement should be able to execute these seven steps.

  1. Stop bleeding. Apply direct pressure with the cleanest available cloth. Maintain pressure for a minimum of 10 minutes without lifting the cloth to check. For arterial bleeding (bright red, pulsing), apply a tourniquet proximal to the wound if direct pressure fails (see Module 04, Advanced Trauma Care).
  2. Irrigate with clean water. Use boiled and cooled water or, in an emergency, the cleanest available water. The goal is mechanical removal of debris, dirt, and bacteria. Pour water directly into and over the wound. Use at least 500 mL for any wound larger than 2 cm. Irrigation pressure matters — squeeze water from a punctured water skin or use a syringe if available. Gentle rinsing is insufficient for contaminated wounds.
  3. Debride. Remove visible debris, dead tissue, and foreign material from the wound using clean fingers or, preferably, clean tools (tweezers, knife tip). Dead tissue (grey, white, or black in color, with no bleeding when cut) is a bacterial growth medium. Remove it. This step is painful — administer willow bark decoction (100-200 mL) thirty minutes before debridement if possible.
  4. Apply antimicrobial. First choice: spread a 3 mm layer of raw honey directly onto the wound surface. Second choice (for visibly infected wounds with pus or redness): apply a fresh garlic poultice for 15 minutes, remove, irrigate with thyme decoction, then apply honey. Third choice: irrigate with berberine decoction, then apply honey. The honey serves as both antimicrobial and wound-healing agent.
  5. Cover with clean dressing. Use the cleanest available cloth — boiled cotton or linen is ideal. The dressing prevents further contamination and holds the honey in contact with the wound. Secure with strips of cloth tied (not too tightly) around the limb or torso.
  6. Change dressing every 24-48 hours. Remove the old dressing gently (soak with warm water if it has adhered to the wound). Inspect the wound. Re-irrigate with clean water or thyme infusion. Reapply honey. Apply fresh dressing. Each dressing change is an opportunity to assess whether the infection is improving or worsening.
  7. Monitor for systemic infection. At every dressing change, assess for: expanding redness beyond 2 cm from the wound edge (indicates cellulitis — the infection is spreading through subcutaneous tissue), red streaks radiating from the wound toward the heart (indicates lymphangitis — bacteria entering the lymphatic system), increasing pain despite treatment, fever above 38.5 degrees C, confusion or lethargy, rapid pulse above 100 at rest. Any of these signs means the infection is winning. Escalate: increase antimicrobial intensity, administer pharmaceutical antibiotics if any are available, and prepare for the possibility that surgical drainage (incision of abscess) may be necessary.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Garlic Burns. Allicin is a sulfur-containing compound that causes chemical burns on prolonged skin contact. A garlic poultice left on skin for more than 30 minutes will cause blistering — creating a new wound while treating the original one. Prevention: strict 15-30 minute time limit. Protect surrounding healthy skin with a ring of oil or petroleum jelly (if available) or a cloth with a hole cut for the wound. Remove the poultice promptly. If burning occurs, irrigate with cool water and apply honey.
  2. Tincture Spoilage. A tincture prepared with insufficient alcohol concentration (below 30% ethanol) does not inhibit microbial growth in the liquid itself. The "tincture" becomes a bacterial culture medium. Prevention: use minimum 40% alcohol (standard vodka is 40%). If the available alcohol is weaker (wine at 12-15%, beer at 4-6%), it is not suitable for tincture-making. Distill it to higher concentration first or use a different preservation method (drying, honey infusion).
  3. Delayed Treatment Escalation. The most dangerous failure mode. A wound infection is treated with garlic and honey for five days while the redness expands, the patient develops fever, and the infection progresses to cellulitis or sepsis. The herbalist, committed to botanical treatment, does not escalate. The patient dies from a treatable localized infection that became an untreatable systemic one. Prevention: the 48-hour rule. If a wound infection shows no improvement after 48 hours of botanical treatment (redness not decreasing, pus still present, pain not diminishing), escalate immediately to the next level on the escalation ladder. Do not wait for signs of systemic infection — by then, the window for effective intervention may have closed.
  4. Misidentification of Plants. Using the wrong plant species — potentially toxic or simply ineffective. Wild carrot (Daucus carota) looks similar to poison hemlock (Conium maculatum). Plantain (Plantago major) is harmless; foxglove (Digitalis purpurea, which contains cardiac glycosides) grows in similar environments. Prevention: maintain a cultivated botanical pharmacy rather than relying on wild-foraging for medicine. Plants grown in dedicated garden beds are known with certainty. If wild-foraging is necessary, the rule is absolute: if there is any doubt about identification, do not use the plant.
  5. Over-Reliance on a Single Remedy. Using only garlic for every infection, or only honey for every wound, ignoring the full toolkit. Single-compound therapy increases the risk of resistance development and fails to exploit the synergistic potential of multiple antimicrobial mechanisms. Prevention: use combinations. A wound treated with garlic (allicin — attacks thiol groups), then honey (H2O2 — oxidative damage, osmotic stress, low pH), and irrigated with thyme decoction (thymol — membrane disruption) faces three distinct antimicrobial mechanisms simultaneously. This combinatorial approach mirrors the logic of multi-drug pharmaceutical regimens.
  6. Heat Destruction of Active Compounds. Boiling garlic destroys allicin. Overheating honey above 40 degrees C denatures glucose oxidase, eliminating hydrogen peroxide production. Storing thyme tincture in direct sunlight accelerates thymol degradation. Prevention: understand each compound's stability. Garlic must be raw. Honey must never be heated above body temperature. Tinctures must be stored in dark containers. Dried herbs must be kept sealed and dry.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Allicin: Diallyl thiosulfinate, the primary antimicrobial compound in garlic, produced by enzymatic conversion of alliin when garlic cells are crushed. Unstable; degrades within hours.
  • Thymol: A monoterpene phenol found in thyme and oregano essential oils. Disrupts bacterial cell membranes and denatures proteins. The active ingredient in Listerine.
  • Berberine: An isoquinoline alkaloid from Oregon grape, goldenseal, and related plants. Intercalates into bacterial DNA and inhibits cell division.
  • Salicin: A glycoside from willow bark, metabolized to salicylic acid in the liver. The botanical precursor of aspirin. Analgesic, anti-inflammatory, antipyretic.
  • Tincture: A plant extract prepared in alcohol (40-60% ethanol). Preserves both water-soluble and fat-soluble compounds. Shelf life: 3-5 years.
  • Decoction: A water extract prepared by simmering plant material (roots, bark) for 10-30 minutes. Stronger than an infusion but perishable within 48 hours.
  • Infusion: A water extract prepared by steeping plant material (leaves, flowers) in boiling water for 10-15 minutes. Equivalent to medicinal tea. Perishable within 48 hours.
  • Poultice: A paste of crushed plant material applied directly to the skin or a wound. The most direct method of delivering botanical antimicrobials.
  • MIC (Minimum Inhibitory Concentration): The lowest concentration of an antimicrobial substance that prevents visible growth of a bacterium in laboratory conditions. The standard measure of antimicrobial potency.
  • Cellulitis: A spreading bacterial infection of the skin and subcutaneous tissue. Characterized by expanding redness, warmth, swelling, and pain. Indicates that a localized infection is becoming invasive. Requires aggressive treatment escalation.
  • Sepsis: A life-threatening systemic inflammatory response to infection. Bacteria have entered the bloodstream. Characterized by fever, rapid heart rate, rapid breathing, confusion. Mortality without modern medical intervention: 40-80%.
  • Debridement: The removal of dead, damaged, or contaminated tissue from a wound to promote healing and reduce bacterial load. Essential for managing infected wounds.

  • David Hoffmann, Medical Herbalism: The Science and Practice of Herbal Medicine (2003) — the most rigorous botanical medicine textbook available, with pharmacological detail and clinical protocols
  • Matthew Wood, The Earthwise Herbal, Volumes 1 and 2 — comprehensive botanical medicine reference covering Old World and New World plants
  • Ankri, S. and Mirelman, D., "Antimicrobial properties of allicin from garlic," Microbes and Infection 1(2): 125-129 (1999) — foundational paper on allicin mechanism and MIC values
  • Molan, P.C., "The antibacterial activity of honey," Bee World 73(1): 5-28 (1992) — comprehensive review of honey's antimicrobial mechanisms and clinical evidence
  • Burt, S., "Essential oils: their antibacterial properties and potential applications in foods," International Journal of Food Microbiology 94(3): 223-253 (2004) — thymol and carvacrol pharmacology
  • Tillhon, M. et al., "Berberine: new perspectives for old remedies," Biochemical Pharmacology 84(10): 1260-1267 (2012) — berberine mechanisms and clinical applications
