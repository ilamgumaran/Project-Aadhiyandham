Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Over forty percent of modern pharmaceutical drugs derive directly from plant compounds. Aspirin originates from willow bark salicin. Morphine comes from the opium poppy. Quinine, the antimalarial that enabled European colonization of the tropics, comes from cinchona bark. Digoxin, the cardiac glycoside still used to treat heart failure, comes from foxglove. The relationship between plants and human medicine is not peripheral or "alternative" --- it is foundational. What we call modern pharmacology is, in most cases, the isolation and standardized dosing of compounds that herbalists had been using for centuries or millennia before the first pharmaceutical laboratory existed.

The ancient healing traditions --- Ayurveda (India, 3000+ years), Traditional Chinese Medicine (2000+ years), Greco-Roman herbalism (Dioscorides, Galen), and the Indigenous pharmacopeias of every inhabited continent --- represent the largest body of empirical medical observation in human history. These systems did not operate on placebo or superstition alone. They operated on observable clinical outcomes, refined across millions of patient encounters over thousands of years. The plants that survived this selection pressure --- ginger for nausea, willow for pain, garlic for infection --- survived because they contain real pharmacological compounds that produce real physiological effects. What modern pharmacology added was not the discovery of these effects, but the isolation of the responsible molecules and the quantification of dose-response relationships.

This module provides the pharmacology for the fifteen most important medicinal plants available in temperate climates. For each plant, the active compound, mechanism of action, preparation method, dosage, and contraindications are specified. It also covers the physical medicine practices --- yoga, hydrotherapy, therapeutic fasting --- that support immune function and recovery without any botanical input at all. The framework used throughout is pharmacological, not mystical. Every recommendation is grounded in identified biochemical compounds with known mechanisms. Where a traditional practice lacks an identified mechanism, it is noted as such.

> CAUTION: Plant medicines contain real pharmacological compounds with real side effects and interactions. Willow bark (salicin) can cause gastric bleeding and should not be given to children under 12 or patients with bleeding disorders. Comfrey (pyrrolizidine alkaloids) is hepatotoxic with prolonged internal use. Pennyroyal is an abortifacient --- NEVER give to pregnant women. Correct identification of plants is critical --- misidentification can be fatal (e.g., confusing water hemlock with wild carrot). Cultivate your medicinal plants rather than wild-foraging to ensure correct identification.

Theoretical Foundation

Pharmacognosy: The Science of Plant Medicine

Pharmacognosy is the branch of pharmacology that studies drugs derived from natural sources --- primarily plants, but also fungi, marine organisms, and animal products. The term derives from the Greek pharmakon (drug) and gnosis (knowledge). It is the oldest branch of pharmacy, predating synthetic chemistry by millennia, and it remains one of the most productive sources of novel drug leads in modern pharmaceutical research.

Why do plants produce compounds that affect human physiology? The answer lies in the secondary metabolite hypothesis. Plants produce primary metabolites (sugars, amino acids, lipids) for growth, and secondary metabolites for competitive advantage: repelling herbivores, killing pathogens, attracting pollinators, inhibiting competitors. These secondary metabolites --- alkaloids, terpenoids, phenolics, glycosides --- are the pharmacologically active compounds. They affect humans because the biochemical pathways they target (enzyme systems, ion channels, receptor proteins) are conserved across the animal kingdom.

Major classes of plant pharmacological compounds:

Alkaloids are nitrogen-containing compounds, often the most pharmacologically potent plant substances. Examples: berberine (antimicrobial), morphine (opioid analgesic), quinine (antimalarial), caffeine (CNS stimulant). Alkaloids frequently have narrow therapeutic windows and must be dosed with extreme care.

Terpenoids are built from isoprene units and constitute the largest class of plant secondary metabolites. The volatile oils that give herbs their aromas are primarily terpenoids: thymol (membrane-disrupting antimicrobial), menthol (smooth muscle relaxant, TRPM8 cold receptor agonist), camphor (topical analgesic), eugenol (COX inhibitor, dental analgesic). Generally less toxic than alkaloids but can cause contact dermatitis at high concentrations.

Phenolics contain hydroxyl groups attached to an aromatic ring. Key examples: salicin (willow bark, COX inhibition), curcumin (turmeric, NF-kB inhibition), tannins (protein precipitation, astringent/antimicrobial), rosmarinic acid (GABAergic anxiolytic). Phenolics tend to have broader therapeutic windows than alkaloids.

Glycosides are compounds in which a sugar molecule is bound to a pharmacologically active aglycone group. Digoxin (foxglove) inhibits the sodium-potassium ATPase pump --- still used for heart failure but has an extremely narrow therapeutic window. Amygdalin (stone fruit pits) releases hydrogen cyanide upon hydrolysis. Glycosides demand particular caution because the sugar component can mask potency.

The dose-response relationship is the single most important pharmacological concept for field medicine. Paracelsus stated it in the sixteenth century: "the dose makes the poison." Every medicinal plant has a therapeutic window --- a range of doses within which the desired effect is achieved. Below this range, the plant has no clinical effect. Above it, the plant becomes toxic. Willow bark at 20g per 500mL decoction reduces fever and pain. At three times that dose taken repeatedly, it can cause gastric hemorrhage. Understanding and respecting the therapeutic window is what separates medicine from poisoning.

The Fifteen-Plant Temperate Pharmacy

The following fifteen plants constitute a functional pharmacy for a temperate-climate settlement. They are organized by primary therapeutic category. For each, the botanical name, active compounds, mechanism of action, standard preparation, dosage, and contraindications are specified.

Analgesics and Anti-inflammatories

1. White Willow (Salix alba)

Active compound: salicin, metabolized in the liver to salicylic acid.

Mechanism: inhibits COX-1/COX-2, reducing prostaglandin synthesis --- lowering fever, reducing inflammation, relieving pain. Same mechanism as aspirin, though slower onset (30-60 minutes) due to the metabolic conversion step.

Preparation: decoction --- simmer 20g dried inner bark in 500mL water for 15 minutes.

Dosage: one cup (250mL) up to three times daily.

Contraindications: children under 12 (Reye's syndrome risk), bleeding disorders, gastric ulcers, salicylate allergy. Stop immediately if patient reports stomach pain or dark stools.

2. Meadowsweet (Filipendula ulmaria)

Active compounds: salicylates (salicin, salicylaldehyde) combined with tannins and mucilage.

Mechanism: same COX inhibition as willow, but tannins and mucilage provide gastroprotective effects --- significantly gentler on the stomach. Aspirin takes its name from meadowsweet's old botanical name (Spiraea).

Preparation: infusion --- steep 4-6g dried flowering tops in 250mL hot water for 10 minutes.

Dosage: one cup three times daily.

Contraindications: same salicylate precautions as willow, though GI risk is lower.

3. Turmeric (Curcuma longa)

Active compound: curcumin (diferuloylmethane).

Mechanism: inhibits the NF-kB signaling pathway (master regulator of inflammatory gene expression), COX-2, and lipoxygenase. Critical problem: extremely poor oral bioavailability. Piperine (black pepper) inhibits glucuronidation of curcumin, increasing bioavailability by ~2000% (Shoba et al., 1998). Must always be combined with black pepper and a fat source.

Preparation: "Golden Milk" --- 1 tsp turmeric, pinch of black pepper, 1 tbsp fat in warm water or milk.

Dosage: 1-2 servings daily for chronic inflammation or joint pain.

Contraindications: may thin blood (avoid combining with willow bark); may stimulate gallbladder contraction (avoid with gallstones).

Antimicrobials

4. Garlic (Allium sativum)

Active compound: allicin (diallyl thiosulfinate), produced when alliinase acts on alliin upon crushing raw garlic.

Mechanism: allicin reacts with thiol groups in microbial enzymes, disrupting function. Broad-spectrum: effective against gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, some fungi, and some viruses. Cooking destroys allicin.

Preparation: crush 2-3 raw cloves, allow to sit 10 minutes (maximizes alliinase activity), consume raw or in honey.

Dosage: 2-3 raw cloves, 2-3 times daily during acute infection.

Contraindications: may increase bleeding risk; GI upset common. Cross-reference: Module 03.

5. Thyme (Thymus vulgaris)

Active compound: thymol (monoterpenoid phenol).

Mechanism: disrupts microbial cell membranes by intercalating into the lipid bilayer. Also acts as bronchial antispasmodic --- particularly effective for productive coughs with suspected bacterial involvement.

Preparation: infusion (2 tsp dried thyme in 250mL hot water, 10 min) or steam inhalation (large handful in boiling water, inhale under towel for 10 min).

Dosage: 3-4 cups daily during respiratory infection.

Contraindications: thyme essential oil (concentrated) should never be ingested --- can cause seizures. Use only the herb internally. Cross-reference: Module 03.

6. Oregano (Origanum vulgare)

Active compound: carvacrol (isomer of thymol), along with thymol.

Mechanism: same membrane disruption as thymol; carvacrol is one of the strongest antimicrobial compounds in temperate-climate plants. Also anti-inflammatory.

Preparation: infusion or steam inhalation (same method as thyme). Also effective as topical wound wash.

Dosage: 2-3 cups strong infusion daily.

Contraindications: concentrated oregano oil is caustic to mucous membranes --- never apply undiluted. Avoid high doses during pregnancy.

7. Oregon Grape (Mahonia aquifolium)

Active compound: berberine (isoquinoline alkaloid), concentrated in root bark.

Mechanism: intercalates into bacterial DNA, inhibiting replication. Also inhibits FtsZ, disrupting cell division. Active against Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, E. coli, and Candida.

Preparation: decoction --- simmer 10g dried root bark in 500mL water for 20 minutes. Liquid should be intensely yellow.

Dosage: 125mL three times daily for GI or urinary infections. Also effective as topical wound wash.

Contraindications: can lower blood sugar; avoid during pregnancy. Do not combine with other strong botanicals without 4-hour spacing. Cross-reference: Module 03.

Gastrointestinal

8. Ginger (Zingiber officinale)

Active compounds: gingerols (fresh) and shogaols (dried, formed by dehydration of gingerols).

Mechanism: 5-HT3 receptor antagonism --- the same mechanism as ondansetron (Zofran). Blocks serotonin receptors in the gut and chemoreceptor trigger zone that initiate the vomiting reflex. Also prokinetic (accelerates gastric emptying).

Preparation: slice or grate 10-15g fresh root into 500mL hot water, steep 10-15 minutes. Or 1/2 tsp dried powder in warm water.

Dosage: sip throughout the day; up to 4g dried equivalent per day.

Contraindications: above 5g/day may be uterotonic --- avoid in early pregnancy. May increase bleeding risk at very high doses.

9. Peppermint (Mentha x piperita)

Active compound: menthol (cyclic monoterpene alcohol).

Mechanism: calcium channel blocker in GI smooth muscle, causing relaxation and relief of intestinal spasm. Also activates TRPM8 cold receptors (cooling sensation). Same basic mechanism as pharmaceutical antispasmodics for IBS.

Preparation: infusion --- 1 tbsp dried leaves in 250mL hot water, 10 minutes. Cover while steeping to retain volatile menthol.

Dosage: 3-4 cups daily for GI cramping.

Contraindications: may worsen gastroesophageal reflux (relaxes lower esophageal sphincter). Avoid in infants (menthol can cause respiratory distress).

10. Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla)

Active compounds: alpha-bisabolol (anti-inflammatory, antispasmodic) and apigenin (flavone, binds benzodiazepine receptors).

Mechanism: bisabolol reduces GI inflammation and spasm. Apigenin produces mild anxiolytic and sedative effects --- making chamomile effective for stress-related GI complaints ("nervous stomach").

Preparation: infusion --- 2-3 tsp dried flowers in 250mL hot water, 10 minutes.

Dosage: 3-4 cups daily. One of the safest medicinal plants, suitable for children and elderly.

Contraindications: allergic reactions possible in patients with Asteraceae (ragweed/daisy family) allergies.

Respiratory

11. Holy Basil / Tulsi (Ocimum tenuiflorum)

Active compounds: eugenol (phenylpropanoid), ursolic acid (triterpenoid), rosmarinic acid.

Mechanism: eugenol inhibits cyclooxygenase (anti-inflammatory in airways). Ursolic acid enhances natural killer cell and T-lymphocyte activity. Classified as an adaptogen --- normalizes immune function under stress, modulating both over- and under-active responses.

Preparation: infusion (2 tsp dried or large handful fresh leaves in 250mL hot water, 10 min). Highly effective as steam inhalation for acute congestion.

Dosage: 3-4 cups daily during illness; 1-2 cups as daily tonic.

Contraindications: mild anticoagulant effect. Mild hypoglycemic action --- monitor diabetic patients.

12. Elderberry (Sambucus nigra)

Active compounds: anthocyanins (cyanidin-3-glucoside, cyanidin-3-sambubioside) and flavonols.

Mechanism: anthocyanins inhibit neuraminidase --- the enzyme influenza viruses use to exit infected cells (Zakay-Rones et al., 2004). Same target as oseltamivir (Tamiflu) and zanamivir (Relenza). Also upregulates cytokine production.

Preparation: berries MUST be cooked (raw contain cyanogenic glycosides). Simmer 100g dried berries in 500mL water for 30-45 min until reduced by half. Strain and sweeten with honey.

Dosage: 1 tbsp syrup 3-4 times daily at onset of influenza symptoms.

Contraindications: raw berries and all parts other than ripe berries and flowers are toxic. Do not use leaves, bark, or unripe berries.

Nervous System and Adaptogens

13. Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

Active compounds: valerenic acid and valepotriates.

Mechanism: valerenic acid inhibits GABA transaminase (the enzyme that degrades GABA) and acts as a positive allosteric modulator of GABA-A receptors. Net effect: increased GABAergic inhibitory tone --- same general mechanism as benzodiazepines, though much milder.

Preparation: decoction of root (3-5g dried root in 250mL water, 15 min). Alternatively, tincture (macerate in strong alcohol 4-6 weeks).

Dosage: one cup decoction or 3-5mL tincture, 30-60 minutes before bedtime.

Contraindications: causes drowsiness. May potentiate other sedatives. Paradoxical stimulation in some individuals.

14. Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis)

Active compounds: rosmarinic acid, citral, citronellal.

Mechanism: rosmarinic acid has GABAergic activity (milder than valerian), producing anxiolytic effects. Citral and citronellal demonstrate antiviral activity against herpes simplex virus (HSV-1/HSV-2), interfering with viral attachment.

Preparation: infusion (2-4 tsp dried leaves in 250mL hot water, 10 min). For cold sores, apply strong infusion as topical compress.

Dosage: 3-4 cups daily for anxiety; topically 3-4 times daily for herpetic lesions.

Contraindications: may interact with thyroid medications. Generally one of the safest medicinal herbs.

Wound Healing

15. Plantain (Plantago major)

Active compounds: aucubin (iridoid glycoside) and allantoin.

Mechanism: aucubin provides antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory activity. Allantoin stimulates epithelial cell proliferation, accelerating wound healing --- the same compound found in commercial wound care products. The combination makes plantain one of the most effective field wound-care plants.

Preparation: fresh poultice --- crush or chew leaves until cell walls break and juice is released, apply mash directly to wound. Dried leaves can be rehydrated.

Dosage: apply fresh poultice, change every 4-6 hours.

Contraindications: none significant. One of the safest topical medicinal plants. Cross-reference: Module 04.

Physical Medicine: Yoga, Hydrotherapy, and Massage

The Lymphatic Pump

The lymphatic system transports lymph --- white blood cells, cellular debris, and metabolic waste --- through a network of vessels and nodes. Unlike the cardiovascular system, it has no central pump. Lymph moves only through: skeletal muscle contraction, respiratory pressure changes (diaphragmatic breathing creates negative intrathoracic pressure), and gravity (inversions drain fluid from extremities toward the thorax).

A bedridden patient who is not moving, not breathing deeply, and lying flat has minimal lymphatic circulation --- immune cells are not being distributed. The following interventions address this:

Deep diaphragmatic breathing (pranayama): When the diaphragm descends during inhalation, it creates negative pressure in the thoracic cavity. This pressure differential draws lymph from the abdominal cavity upward through the thoracic duct and into the venous system at the subclavian veins. Instructing a sick patient to perform 5 minutes of slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing every waking hour directly improves lymphatic drainage and immune cell circulation.

Inversions: The simplest inversion --- legs elevated against a wall (Viparita Karani) --- uses gravity to drain accumulated lymphatic fluid from the lower extremities. This is particularly relevant for patients with lower extremity edema, post-surgical swelling, or prolonged immobility. Hold for 5-15 minutes, 2-3 times daily.

Gentle movement: Even slow walking or gentle stretching activates the skeletal muscle pump. A patient who can walk should walk, even briefly, multiple times daily. Evidence demonstrates increased lymphocyte circulation after yoga practice (Rao et al., 2017), supporting the clinical relevance of these interventions.

Hydrotherapy

Water, at varying temperatures, is a powerful therapeutic tool that requires no botanical knowledge at all.

Cold water immersion causes peripheral vasoconstriction --- blood vessels near the skin surface constrict, redirecting blood to the core organs. This triggers redistribution of immune cells from peripheral tissues into the central circulation, effectively mobilizing the immune system. Brief cold exposure (2-5 minutes in cold water or cold stream) followed by vigorous drying and warming is a daily practice that may enhance immune surveillance. This was the foundation of Sebastian Kneipp's hydrotherapy system in 1890s Germany and echoes the cold-water traditions of Scandinavian, Russian, and Japanese cultures.

Contrast therapy (alternating hot and cold application) creates a vascular pumping action. Hot application causes vasodilation; cold causes vasoconstriction. Alternating between the two (3 minutes hot, 1 minute cold, repeated 3-4 times, always ending on cold) pumps blood and lymph through tissues, accelerating delivery of immune cells and removal of metabolic waste. This is particularly useful for localized injuries and infections.

Fever management: The critical mistake in fever management is applying ice or very cold water to a febrile patient. Extreme cold triggers shivering, which is the body's thermogenesis response --- it raises core temperature, which is the opposite of the intended effect. The correct approach is tepid sponging: apply cloths soaked in lukewarm water (not cold, not hot) to the forehead, neck, axillae (armpits), and groin. These areas have major blood vessels close to the surface, and tepid water promotes evaporative cooling without triggering the shivering reflex. Note: mild to moderate fever (below 39.5C / 103F) is itself an immune mechanism --- elevated body temperature inhibits microbial replication and accelerates immune cell activity. Do not aggressively treat fever unless it exceeds 39.5C or the patient is in significant distress.

Therapeutic Fasting

Digestion is metabolically expensive, consuming approximately 20% of the body's basal metabolic rate. When the body is fighting an acute infection, redirecting that metabolic energy from digestion to immune function can accelerate recovery. This is the biological basis of the universal observation that sick animals (and sick humans) lose their appetite --- it is an evolved response, not a pathology.

Autophagy, the process by which cells break down and recycle damaged components, is significantly upregulated during fasting. Yoshinori Ohsumi received the 2016 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for elucidating the mechanisms of autophagy. During fasting, cells clear damaged organelles, misfolded proteins, and intracellular pathogens. This "cellular housekeeping" supports immune function and recovery.

Protocol: for an acute febrile illness in an otherwise healthy adult, a 24-48 hour water-only fast (with adequate hydration maintained) followed by gradual reintroduction starting with bone broth or vegetable broth, then soft foods, then normal diet over 2-3 days.

CONTRAINDICATION: Do NOT fast pregnant women, children under 16, malnourished individuals, diabetics, or patients who are already debilitated. These populations require continued caloric intake even during illness. For these patients, provide light, easily digestible foods (broth, porridge, stewed fruit).

Core Principles

  1. The Pharmacological Principle: Every plant recommendation in this module is based on identified biochemical compounds with known mechanisms of action. "Traditional use" alone is insufficient --- the mechanism must be understood so that dosing, contraindications, and interactions can be predicted. A plant whose mechanism is not understood should be used with extreme caution and only when no better-understood alternative exists.
  2. The Cultivation Imperative: Medicinal plants must be CULTIVATED in dedicated garden beds (Zone 1 of the settlement), not wild-foraged. Cultivation ensures: (a) correct identification, eliminating misidentification risk; (b) reliable supply independent of seasonal availability; (c) known growing conditions, which affect potency --- soil composition, sunlight, and water stress all influence secondary metabolite concentrations; (d) conservation of wild populations, which may already be under pressure from habitat loss.
  3. The Dose-Response Awareness: Plant medicines have therapeutic windows. Underdosing produces no clinical effect and wastes material. Overdosing produces toxicity. There is no such thing as a "natural" substance that is inherently safe at any dose. Willow bark overdose causes gastric bleeding. Comfrey overuse causes liver damage. Foxglove overdose causes fatal cardiac arrhythmia. Start with the minimum effective dose and titrate upward based on patient response.
  4. The Physical Medicine Foundation: Before reaching for any botanical remedy, ensure the patient is: hydrated, rested, warm (or appropriately cooled in fever), breathing deeply, and has been gently mobilized (walked, stretched). These physical interventions --- hydration, rest, thermoregulation, lymphatic activation --- are the foundation of recovery. Botanical medicine supplements them. A dehydrated patient given the finest herbal preparation will not recover until hydration is corrected.
  5. The Prevention Hierarchy: The best medicine is prevention. Adequate nutrition (Module 04), clean water (Module 03.02), good sleep (Module 05.02), physical activity, and basic hygiene prevent the vast majority of conditions this module addresses. Botanical medicine is a second line of defense, not a first. A settlement with clean water, adequate calories, and good sanitation will need this module rarely. A settlement without those things will find this module insufficient.

Practical Implementation

The Medicinal Garden Plan

A dedicated medicinal garden bed of approximately 4m x 8m (32 square meters) can accommodate all fifteen plants. Organize by water and sun requirements:

Full sun, well-drained section (southern exposure, 4m x 3m):

Thyme (30cm spacing), oregano (30cm spacing), chamomile (25cm spacing), lemon balm (40cm spacing --- will spread aggressively, consider containment). These Mediterranean-origin herbs thrive in lean, well-drained soil and full sun. Overwatering and over-fertilizing reduce essential oil concentration and thus medicinal potency.

Partial shade, moist section (northern or eastern exposure, 4m x 3m):

Peppermint (40cm spacing, MUST be contained --- invasive), valerian (50cm spacing --- tall, place at back), plantain (will self-seed in any disturbed soil), lemon balm overflow. These plants tolerate or prefer partial shade and consistent moisture.

Dedicated beds for perennial shrubs/trees (edges, 4m x 2m):

Oregon grape (1m spacing, evergreen shrub, partial shade), elderberry (2m spacing, grows to 3-4m height, full sun, place where it will not shade other beds), white willow (plant near water source --- willows are riparian; one mature tree provides years of bark). Meadowsweet prefers moist, slightly acidic soil alongside the willow.

Protected/warm microclimate or indoor overwintering (containers if needed):

Ginger and turmeric are tropical rhizomes that require warm soil (above 15C) and will not survive frost. In temperate climates, grow in containers that can be brought indoors or in the warmest, most sheltered microclimate available. Holy basil (tulsi) is also frost-sensitive and should be treated as an annual or overwintered indoors. Garlic is hardy and can be planted in any open ground in autumn for summer harvest.

Perennial vs. annual planning: Thyme, oregano, lemon balm, valerian, Oregon grape, elderberry, willow, meadowsweet, peppermint, and plantain are perennial in temperate climates --- plant once and maintain. Chamomile self-seeds readily and behaves as a perennial. Garlic is planted annually (autumn) but requires minimal effort. Ginger, turmeric, and tulsi require annual replanting or indoor overwintering.

The Ayurvedic Seasonal Framework

The Ayurvedic concept of seasonal dietary adaptation (Ritucharya) aligns with thermoregulation physiology. The body's caloric requirements and optimal macronutrient ratios shift with seasonal temperature changes, and ignoring these shifts degrades health over time.

Winter (cold months): Basal metabolic rate increases to maintain core temperature via thermogenesis. The body requires higher caloric intake, particularly fats and proteins, which have higher specific dynamic action (the metabolic heat generated during digestion). Root vegetables, animal fats, bone broths, and fermented foods are appropriate. Eating raw, cold foods in winter forces the body to expend additional energy warming the food to core temperature before digestion can proceed --- this is metabolically wasteful in a calorie-constrained environment.

Summer (warm months): Thermoregulation shifts to heat dissipation. The body requires more water, electrolytes, and lighter foods that do not generate excessive digestive heat. Fruits, leafy greens, cucumbers, and diluted fermented beverages are appropriate. Heavy, fatty meals in extreme heat increase the thermal burden and risk heat illness.

Transition seasons (spring/autumn): Gradual dietary transitions between the winter and summer patterns. Spring is traditionally a period of lighter eating after winter caloric surplus --- the biological basis of fasting traditions across cultures.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Misidentification Poisoning: A settlement member wild-forages a plant believed to be wild carrot (Daucus carota) but actually harvests water hemlock (Cicuta maculata). Water hemlock contains cicutoxin, a potent CNS toxin that causes violent seizures, status epilepticus, and death within hours. There is no field-treatable antidote. The roots are the most toxic part and have been mistaken for wild parsnip. Prevention: CULTIVATE medicinal plants in dedicated beds. Do not forage for medicine unless the forager has years of verified identification experience with the specific plant in question.
  2. Salicin Overdose: A patient with headache and fever is given willow bark decoction at double concentration and dosed every two hours. After 24 hours, the patient develops severe epigastric pain and passes dark, tarry stools (melena) --- a sign of upper gastrointestinal hemorrhage. Prevention: limit willow bark to 3 standard doses per day (20g bark per 500mL, one cup per dose). Stop immediately at the first sign of stomach pain or GI bleeding. Do not administer to patients with known gastric ulcers. Consider meadowsweet as a gentler alternative.
  3. Botanical Interaction Effects: A patient receiving berberine (Oregon grape) for a GI infection is simultaneously given high-dose willow bark for fever. Both compounds are metabolized by hepatic cytochrome P450 enzymes. Concurrent use may alter the metabolism of either compound, leading to unpredictable blood levels. Prevention: do not combine multiple strong botanical medicines simultaneously. Space different preparations by at least 4 hours. Use one botanical at a time when possible and monitor response before adding a second.
  4. Pregnancy Contraindications Ignored: A pregnant settlement member in early pregnancy is given pennyroyal tea for nausea, or large doses of ginger, or blue/black cohosh for general wellness. These plants have uterotonic or abortifacient properties and may induce miscarriage. Prevention: maintain a clearly labeled "PREGNANCY CONTRAINDICATED" list in the settlement pharmacy, prominently posted. All medicinal preparations given to pregnant women must be approved by the settlement's designated birth attendant. Cross-reference: Module 07 - Midwifery and Childbirth.
  5. Placebo Over-Reliance and Delayed Escalation: A patient with a wound infection is given plantain poultices and garlic washes. The patient reports feeling better (placebo effect, or temporary pain relief from the anti-inflammatory compounds), but the wound continues to worsen --- spreading erythema, increasing purulence, fever. The treatment team continues botanical therapy because the patient "says they feel fine." The infection progresses to cellulitis, then sepsis. Prevention: monitor objectively, not subjectively. Track temperature (twice daily), pulse rate, wound dimensions (measure and record redness spread), and mental status. If there is no objective improvement within 48 hours, escalate --- increase antimicrobial intensity, consider surgical wound debridement (Module 04), or implement the full antibiotic protocol from Module 03.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

Pharmacognosy --- the branch of pharmacology that studies drugs of natural (primarily plant) origin.

Alkaloid --- a class of nitrogen-containing plant compounds, often pharmacologically potent with narrow therapeutic windows (e.g., berberine, morphine, caffeine).

Terpenoid --- a large class of compounds built from isoprene units; includes volatile essential oils (thymol, menthol, eugenol, camphor).

Phenolic --- a compound containing hydroxyl groups on an aromatic ring; includes salicin, curcumin, tannins, and rosmarinic acid.

Glycoside --- a compound in which a sugar molecule is bound to a pharmacologically active aglycone group (e.g., digoxin, amygdalin).

Salicin --- a phenolic glycoside found in willow bark, metabolized to salicylic acid; the natural precursor of aspirin.

Curcumin --- the primary anti-inflammatory phenolic compound in turmeric; requires piperine for adequate oral bioavailability.

Adaptogen --- a plant compound that modulates the stress response, normalizing physiological function under both hypo- and hyper-stimulation conditions (e.g., tulsi, ashwagandha).

Galactagogue --- a substance that promotes lactation; relevant to postpartum care (e.g., fenugreek, fennel).

Immunomodulator --- a compound that regulates (up or down) immune system activity, as opposed to simple immunostimulation.

Expectorant --- a compound that promotes the clearance of mucus from the respiratory tract, either by increasing mucus hydration or stimulating ciliary action.

Tincture --- an alcohol-based extraction of plant material; produces a concentrated, shelf-stable medicinal preparation.

Autophagy --- the cellular process of self-digestion and recycling of damaged components, upregulated during fasting and caloric restriction.

Therapeutic window --- the range of doses between the minimum effective dose and the minimum toxic dose; the safe and effective dosing range for any pharmacological agent.
