Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> Caution — the fire that keeps you alive is the fire most likely to kill you: The settlement invites fire indoors every single day — to cook, to boil water, to keep from freezing. Two of its dangers are covered elsewhere: carbon monoxide (the silent gas) and wildfire (the threat from outside). This module covers the third and most common: the structure catching fire from its own hearth. A thatched, timber, or cob shelter with an open flame inside is, by construction, a controlled fire a metre from a large amount of fuel. It stays controlled only by discipline — and when it fails, a wattle-and-daub home with a thatch roof can become fully involved in minutes, faster than sleeping occupants can wake and find the door in the dark and smoke.

1. Why This Is Important

Before electricity, the house fire was among the most common causes of catastrophic loss — of homes, of stored food and seed, of the winter's fuel, and of lives, especially children and the elderly who could not escape quickly. The settlement rebuilds exactly the conditions that made pre-industrial house fires routine: open flames and hot embers inside combustible buildings, at night, with people asleep, no fire brigade, and no way to replace a home and a winter's stores before the cold comes. A single careless night can erase a season of work and kill a family.

The good news is that house-fire risk is almost entirely designed and disciplined away, cheaply, using materials and habits the settlement already has. This module is the design and the discipline.

2. Expected Outcome

A hearth built so that ordinary use cannot ignite the structure; a shelter every occupant can escape from in the dark within seconds; the means to extinguish a small fire kept within arm's reach of every flame; and a household drilled so that a fire at night produces an orderly escape, not a fatal scramble. The whole system costs a few stones, a screen, a bucket of sand, and a rehearsed habit.

3. The Hearth: Contain the Fire by Construction

Most house fires start at the hearth or chimney. Build these out and the fire has nowhere to jump:

  • The non-combustible surround. The fire sits on and against stone or packed earth, never directly on or against timber, cob-over-timber, or anything that can char. Extend the non-combustible zone well beyond the flame in every direction — a generous hearthstone floor and back wall — so a rolling ember or spitting log lands on rock, not fuel.
  • Clearance from combustibles. Keep a wide gap between the flame and any wall, thatch, timber, drying clothes, fuel pile, or thatched ceiling. Radiant heat alone ignites nearby wood over time (pyrolysis lowers its ignition point with repeated heating — an apparently safe beam can char and finally catch after a season of warm evenings). Nothing combustible stored above or beside the hearth, ever; the drying rack over the fire is a classic ignition point.
  • The chimney/smoke hole — and the creosote trap. A working chimney or smoke hole is required for carbon monoxide anyway; for fire it carries a second duty. Smoke deposits creosote/soot, which is itself flammable and causes chimney fires that spread into the roof. Clean the chimney/flue regularly (a settlement-scale chore, seasonally at least), and build the flue of non-combustible material with clearance from the roof timbers and thatch it passes through.
  • Spark control at the roof. Where a flue exits near thatch, or embers can rise, a spark screen (mesh, perforated metal, or a fine clay/stone cap) keeps rising embers from lodging in the roof — the same logic as the wildfire ember defense, turned inward.
  • Contain the embers. A spark screen or guard in front of an open hearth stops spitting embers reaching the floor and stops a child or sleeper rolling into the fire. Bank or fully extinguish the fire before sleep unless it is a properly contained, screened, thermal-mass fire built for overnight burning (Thermal Mass Cooking designs are safer here than an open hearth precisely because they enclose the flame).

4. Egress: Everyone Out, in the Dark, in Seconds

A fire's lethal weapon is usually not flame but smoke — which blinds, disorients, and asphyxiates faster than burns, and rises to fill a room from the top down. Survival is about getting out before the smoke wins, which means designing and rehearsing escape:

  • Two ways out. Every sleeping space needs a second exit — a door, a low window, a removable panel — because the fire may be between the sleepers and the only door. A single-exit shelter with an internal hearth is a trap by design.
  • Escape in the dark. People must be able to reach an exit without light and without sight — smoke is opaque and the fire may be the only light. Keep exits unobstructed and known by feel; sleep with the path clear. Get low under smoke, where the air lasts longest.
  • The rehearsed drill. Once is enough to save lives: everyone practices leaving the shelter, in the dark, by both exits, to a fixed outside gathering point — the same rally-point logic used everywhere in this manual. Assign who wakes and carries the children and the infirm, who cannot escape unaided. In a real fire there is no time to invent this.
  • Sleep discipline in the heating season. The most dangerous fire is the one that starts while everyone sleeps. Bank or extinguish open flames before sleep; never leave a young child or the infirm alone with a live open hearth.

5. Extinguishing: Kill It Small

A fire caught in its first seconds is a chore; the same fire two minutes later has the building. The whole game is speed, and speed requires the means to be already there:

  • Keep it at every flame. A bucket of sand or dry earth and a vessel of water kept within arm's reach of every hearth and cook-fire. Sand/earth smothers and is safe on more fire types; water is abundant and fast on ordinary wood fire.
  • Know the exception: fat and oil fires. A cooking-fat fire (rendering, frying) must not be doused with water — water flashes to steam and hurls burning fat outward, spreading the fire and burning the cook. Smother it: sand/earth, or a lid/wet cloth to cut off air. This is a Law-tier distinction worth teaching every cook explicitly.
  • Fuel and stores placement. Keep the fuel pile, the lamp oil, the rendered fat, and — above all — the seed stock and food reserve away from the hearth and, ideally, in a separate structure. A house fire should never be able to take the settlement's seed and winter food with it; separating them is cheap insurance against a single fire becoming a starvation event.
  • Fire spacing between buildings. As the settlement grows, gap the buildings so one burning structure cannot ignite the next (the defensible-space logic at settlement scale). Clustered thatch roofs turn one hearth accident into a settlement fire.

7. Common Failure Modes

  • Combustibles above/beside the fire. A drying rack, fuel pile, or thatch too close to the flame. [Tier L] Consequence: radiant heat or an ember ignites the structure; a thatch home is fully involved in minutes. Prevention: wide clearance; nothing combustible above the hearth, ever.
  • The single exit. An internal hearth with only one way out. [Tier L] Consequence: fire between sleepers and the door → no escape → deaths. Prevention: two exits from every sleeping space, rehearsed in the dark.
  • Uncleaned chimney. Creosote builds in the flue. [Tier L] Consequence: a chimney fire spreads into the roof. Prevention: seasonal flue cleaning; non-combustible flue with clearance.
  • Water on a fat fire. Dousing burning cooking fat. [Tier L] Consequence: a steam explosion of burning fat spreads the fire and burns the cook. Prevention: smother fat fires with sand/earth or a lid; teach every cook the exception.
  • Seed and stores by the hearth. Keeping the food reserve and seed in the heated home. [Tier C] Consequence: one house fire becomes a starvation event by taking the winter food and next year's seed. Prevention: store seed and reserve separately, away from any flame.
  • The unbanked overnight fire. Leaving an open flame live and unattended overnight. [Tier L] Consequence: the fire that starts while everyone sleeps is the classic fatal one. Prevention: bank or extinguish before sleep; use enclosed thermal-mass designs for overnight heat.

8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Bank the fire: Reduce an overnight fire to safely contained embers (or extinguish it) before sleep.
  • Creosote: Flammable soot deposited in flues by woodsmoke; the cause of chimney fires; removed by regular cleaning.
  • Egress: A way out; every sleeping space needs two, usable in the dark under smoke.
  • Fire spacing: Gaps between buildings that stop one burning structure igniting the next.
  • Hearth surround: The non-combustible stone/earth floor and back wall that contains the fire and catches stray embers.
  • The fat-fire exception: The Law-tier rule that burning fat/oil is smothered, never doused with water.

9. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

9.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: Build the hearth with a stone/earth surround and clearance from all combustibles; give every sleeping space two exits; keep water and sand at every flame.
  • Execution: Clean flues seasonally; bank fires before sleep; store seed and food reserve separately; drill the dark-escape to a rally point.
  • Review: Re-walk the escape routes after any change to the shelter's layout or fuel storage; re-drill with children periodically.

9.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier

  • [Tier L — Law] Single exit / combustibles over the hearth / water on a fat fire → structure fire or death with almost no warning time. These are non-negotiable design and habit rules; teach the fat-fire exception explicitly.
  • [Tier L — Law] Skip flue cleaning → chimney fire into the roof. Seasonal cleaning is a fixed chore, not an optional one.
  • [Tier C — Craft] Exact clearances and hearth size → calibrate to your fuel, flame size, and building material; when unsure, more clearance.

9.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: Fire discipline is a cultural habit that decays in good times — a settlement that has not had a fire in a decade grows careless, and the eleventh year burns. Keep the drills, the flue cleaning, and the fat-fire rule alive across generations as living practice, not folklore.

11. Further Study

  • NFPA and pre-industrial fire history: the near-universality of the house fire before modern codes, and the simple measures (hearth surrounds, chimney cleaning, spacing) that cut it.
  • Historical: the recurring "great fires" of dense-built towns (London 1666 and many others) — fire spacing and non-combustible construction as the lessons paid for in ash.
  • Cooking-fire burn epidemiology in low-income settings (WHO) — the ongoing real-world toll of open indoor cooking fires and the interventions that reduce it.
