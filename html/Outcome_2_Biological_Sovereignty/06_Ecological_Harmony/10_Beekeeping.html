Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> Caution — the gentle livestock with two real dangers: Bees are low-labor and generous, but two things must be respected. First, stings can kill the small fraction of people who are severely allergic (anaphylaxis) — the settlement must know who they are before a hive is opened. Second, over-harvesting honey starves the colony through winter: the bees made that honey to survive on, and a keeper who takes too much kills the hive that would have fed the settlement for years. Take the surplus, leave the living. Do that, and a hive is one of the highest-return, lowest-effort assets a settlement can keep.

1. Why This Is Important

A beehive gives three things, and they run in ascending order of value — the least obvious being the greatest:

  • Wax — candles and lighting, waterproofing, sealing food-storage vessels, leatherwork, and lost-wax metal casting.
  • Honey — a dense, storable, nearly imperishable source of sugar and energy; the settlement's rare sweetener and a concentrated calorie store; and a proven wound antimicrobial (the medicinal honey of the med kit — its osmotic pull, acidity, and peroxide kill bacteria in wounds).
  • Pollination — the giant, and the reason to keep bees even if you never took a drop of honey. Honeybees dramatically raise the yield of fruit, vegetable, and seed crops across the entire settlement. A hive is a yield multiplier for the whole food system — its value is spread invisibly across every pollinated bed and orchard, which is exactly why it is so easy to undervalue. In a settlement whose survival rides on first-year and every-year harvests, reliable pollination is not a luxury.

For a few days of building and a light seasonal touch, bees repay the settlement in sweetener, medicine, light, and — above all — a fatter harvest.

2. Expected Outcome

One or more healthy, self-sustaining bee colonies sited to pollinate the settlement's crops; a sustainable honey and wax harvest that never compromises the colony's winter survival; and the safety knowledge (who is allergic, how to work calm bees) to keep the hive a benefit and not a hazard.

3. The Hive: Keep It Simple and Sustainable

  • Top-bar hive (the settlement sweet spot). A long, simple box in which bees build natural comb hanging from removable top bars. It is easy to build from local timber, needs no manufactured frames or foundation, lets you inspect and harvest comb bar by bar without destroying the colony, and suits low-tech beekeeping perfectly. This is the recommended default.
  • Fixed-comb (log gum / skep). The oldest form — a hollow log or woven straw dome. Dead simple to make, but traditional harvest often meant destroying the comb (and sometimes the colony). Workable, but the top-bar's non-destructive harvest is a real advantage for a settlement that wants the colony to last.
  • Framed (Langstroth-type). Highest yield and easiest management, but needs precise frames and more materials/skill — a later refinement, not a starting point.

4. Getting and Keeping Bees

  • Catch a swarm — bees are free. In late spring, colonies naturally swarm (split and send out a queen with workers looking for a home). A swarm trap (a baited empty hive) or catching a settled swarm populates a hive at no cost. Splitting a strong colony makes more hives.
  • Forage and water. Bees need diverse flowering plants across the season and a nearby clean water source; a settlement rich in diverse crops, orchards, and wildflowers feeds its bees and is fed by them in return — another closed loop.
  • Seasonal rhythm. Spring: the colony builds up; leave it to grow. Summer: the nectar flow — comb and honey fill; this is when surplus builds. Late summer/autumn: harvest the surplus, then stop — the colony needs its remaining stores to survive winter. Winter: the bees cluster and live on their honey; a keeper who left them enough does nothing but protect the hive from damp and pests.

5. Harvest: Take the Surplus, Leave the Living

The one rule that separates a lasting apiary from a dead one: the bees made the honey to overwinter on; harvest only the genuine surplus and always leave the colony enough to survive to spring. How much "enough" is depends on your climate's winter length — a Craft-tier judgment calibrated locally, and when unsure, take less. Harvest comb from the honey-storage end, crush-and-strain or drain the honey, and render the wax (melt and filter) for candles and sealing. Greedy harvesting is not thrift; it is killing next year's pollination and honey for this year's jar.

6. Safety: Stings and the Allergic

  • Know who is allergic — before opening a hive. Most stings are painful but harmless; in a severely allergic person a sting can cause anaphylaxis and death within minutes. The settlement must know who is at risk, keep them away from hive work, and understand that this is a genuine, if rare, lethal hazard. (A field settlement has little to treat anaphylaxis with, which makes avoidance the whole defense for the allergic.)
  • Work calm bees. Cool smoke calms a colony (it masks alarm signals); work in good weather, unhurried, with a simple veil over the face and covered skin. Calm, slow beekeeping is rarely stung.
  • Site hives thoughtfully. Place hives away from paths, doorways, animal pens, and high-traffic areas, with the entrance facing away from where people walk, so ordinary settlement life and the bees' flight paths do not collide.

7. Common Failure Modes

  • Over-harvesting honey. Taking the colony's winter stores. [Tier L] Consequence: the colony starves over winter and dies — losing the honey, the wax, and the pollination for years. Prevention: harvest only true surplus; leave ample winter stores; when unsure, take less.
  • Opening a hive around an allergic person. [Tier L] Consequence: anaphylaxis, potentially fatal, with little a settlement can do. Prevention: know who is allergic; keep them clear; site hives away from traffic.
  • Feeding honey to an infant. [Tier L] Consequence: infant botulism (honey carries C. botulinum spores) — see Food Toxins and Deficiency Diseases. Prevention: never give honey to a child under 12 months.
  • Poor siting / rough handling. Hives by the path, worked in bad weather without smoke. [Tier C] Consequence: repeated stings, a defensive colony, people avoiding the area. Prevention: site away from traffic; work calm bees with smoke.
  • Neglecting forage/water. A hive with nothing to forage. [Tier C] Consequence: a weak colony and little honey or pollination. Prevention: diverse season-long flowering and nearby water.

8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Anaphylaxis: A severe, potentially fatal allergic reaction to stings; the reason to know who is allergic.
  • Nectar flow: The season when plants yield abundant nectar and the colony builds surplus honey.
  • Overwintering stores: The honey a colony needs to survive winter; never harvested away.
  • Rendering wax: Melting and filtering comb wax for candles, sealing, and casting.
  • Swarm: A queen and workers leaving to found a new colony; caught free in spring to populate a hive.
  • Top-bar hive: A simple horizontal hive where bees build natural comb on removable bars; the low-tech settlement standard.

9. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

9.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: Build a top-bar hive; site it away from traffic near diverse forage and water; identify who in the community is sting-allergic.
  • Execution: Populate it by catching a spring swarm; let the colony build; harvest only surplus honey and wax in late summer; leave ample winter stores.
  • Review: Judge winter-store adequacy against your climate each autumn (take less when unsure); split strong colonies to expand.

9.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier

  • [Tier L — Law] Over-harvest honey → the colony starves over winter and dies. Leave the winter stores; the honey is the bees' first.
  • [Tier L — Law] Hive work near an allergic person / honey to an infant → anaphylaxis / infant botulism. Know who is allergic; no honey under one year.
  • [Tier C — Craft] How much honey is "surplus" → calibrate to winter length; when unsure, take less.

9.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: A calm, knowledgeable beekeeper makes beekeeping look effortless and safe; that calm is a taught skill. Keep it two-deep, and make sure the allergy knowledge and the leave-the-winter-stores rule are common knowledge, not one keeper's secret.
