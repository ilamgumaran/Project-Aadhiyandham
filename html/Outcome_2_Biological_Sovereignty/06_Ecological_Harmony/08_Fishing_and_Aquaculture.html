Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> Caution — the protein most settlements underuse: Water yields protein more efficiently than almost any land system — a well-managed pond can out-produce the same area of pasture, and a passive fish trap gathers food while the settlement sleeps. But water also carries the parasites and the fecal-oral diseases the sanitation modules fight, so fish is a gift that must be cooked and ponds that must be sited clean. Treat the water as both larder and hazard, and it becomes one of the settlement's richest and most reliable food sources.

1. Why This Is Important

The Emergency Nutrition module names fishing as a bridge food; this module makes it a pillar. Fish and other aquatic animals supply complete protein and, critically, fat — the scarcest macronutrient in a foraging or early-settlement diet and the one whose absence causes rabbit starvation. Two things make water-protein exceptional for a settlement:

  • Passive capture. A weir or basket trap, once built, harvests continuously with almost no labor — unlike hunting, which spends calories to gain them. This is protein that arrives while you do other work.
  • Cultivation, not just capture. A stocked pond is farmed protein: it turns scraps, insects, and pond weed into fish, closes nutrient loops with the garden, and yields reliably year after year. Integrated fish-farming fed dense populations for millennia (Chinese carp polyculture, rice-fish systems, European monastery stew ponds) precisely because it is so efficient. A settlement with reliable water and no fish pond is leaving a large, sustainable food source unused.

2. Expected Outcome

Reliable aquatic protein from two complementary systems: wild capture (traps, nets, lines) sized to a sustainable share of the local water's production, and a cultivated pond that farms fish on waste and pond growth and feeds its fertile water back to the garden. Plus the discipline to cook the catch and keep the ponds clean, so the protein does not carry disease.

3. Wild Capture — Let the Water Work

  • Traps and weirs (the highest-value method). A fish weir (a low barrier of stone or stakes that funnels fish into a holding pen or basket as they move with the current or tide) and basket traps (woven funnels a fish swims into but cannot leave) are the settlement's best fishing tools because they are passive — built once, checked daily, working continuously. They need only cordage and withies. Tidal weirs on a coast and fixed traps on a stream can feed a settlement with a fraction of the effort of active fishing.
  • Nets. Woven from plant-fiber cordage; seine nets (drawn through water), gill nets (fish entangle), and cast nets. High yield, more skill and labor than traps.
  • Lines and spearing. Hook-and-line (hooks from bone, thorn, or metal) and spearing/gigging in shallows or by night light — lower yield, useful for larger or specific fish.
  • Beyond fish: shellfish, crustaceans, eels, frogs, and waterfowl are all part of the water's yield; shellfish beds in particular are nearly free protein where they exist (but see the toxin caution below).

4. Aquaculture — Farming the Pond

The cultivated pond is the sustainable heart of water-protein. Its logic is closing loops:

  • Siting and water. A pond needs reliable inflow and a way to hold water (a clay-sealed basin or a dammed low spot — see water and site modules). Crucially, it must sit where its water is clean going in — never downstream of the latrine or manure, or the fish concentrate the settlement's own pathogens.
  • Stocking and polyculture. The classic efficiency trick, perfected in Chinese carp culture, is polyculture: stock several fish species that feed at different levels — surface (eats floating plants/insects), midwater (eats plankton), and bottom (eats detritus and pond-floor growth) — so the pond's entire food web becomes fish flesh with no wasted layer. Hardy, fast-growing, plant-eating fish (carps, tilapia in warm climates) convert vegetation and scraps to protein most efficiently; carnivorous fish need feeding and are less efficient.
  • Feeding on waste and growth. A good pond feeds itself and the settlement's byproducts: kitchen scraps, crop waste, insects, manure-grown plankton, and pond weed all become fish. This is the same nutrient-cycling logic as the compost heap, run in water.
  • The garden loop. Fish-pond water is rich in fish waste — a potent fertilizer. Using pond water to irrigate the garden, and pond weed and mud as fertilizer, turns the pond into an engine that feeds the crops while the crops' waste feeds the pond. The rice-fish system runs both in the same flooded field: the fish eat pests and weeds and fertilize the rice, and both are harvested. Integrated aquaculture is one of the highest-yielding sustainable food systems known.
  • Harvest sustainably. Harvest the mature fish and leave breeding stock; a pond managed this way yields indefinitely.

5. Two Hazards to Respect

  • Cook the fish — parasites. Raw and undercooked freshwater fish can carry serious parasites (flukes, tapeworms) that infect people; some are debilitating or deadly. Cook fish thoroughly (or properly preserve it — hot-smoking, adequate salt-curing, and thorough drying reduce risk, but raw and lightly-treated fish is the dangerous case). This is a Law-tier habit, the aquatic sibling of the manual's meat and water rules.
  • Shellfish and algal toxins. Filter-feeding shellfish can concentrate natural toxins during algal blooms (the "red tide"), which cooking does not destroy and which can paralyze and kill. Learn the local seasons and warning signs; when in doubt, do not harvest shellfish during warm-season blooms or discolored water.

6. Preserving the Catch

A good trap or harvest yields more than can be eaten fresh, so the surplus goes to the preservation system: drying (the oldest method — thin fillets dried hard), cold- and hot-smoking, and salt-curing (which is why salt supply and fish supply are linked). Preserved fish is dense, storable protein and fat for the gap season — historically, dried and salted fish provisioned entire regions and navies.

7. Common Failure Modes

  • Pond sited below the waste stream. Placing a fish pond downstream of latrine or manure runoff. [Tier L] Consequence: the fish concentrate fecal pathogens; eating them spreads disease through the settlement. Prevention: clean water in; the 30 m / downhill-of-water sanitation logic applies to ponds too.
  • Eating fish raw/undercooked. [Tier L] Consequence: parasitic infection (flukes, tapeworm), some severe. Prevention: cook thoroughly; treat lightly-cured raw fish as risky.
  • Shellfish in bloom season. Harvesting filter-feeders during an algal bloom. [Tier L] Consequence: paralytic shellfish poisoning, which cooking will not fix and which kills. Prevention: know the local bloom seasons; avoid discolored water and warm-season shellfish where blooms occur.
  • Overfishing the wild source. Taking too much from a stream or bed. [Tier C] Consequence: the population collapses and the free food source is gone for years. Prevention: harvest a sustainable share, leave breeding stock, respect spawning seasons — the ecological-harmony restraint applied to water.
  • Monoculture pond. Stocking one fish that uses only one food layer. [Tier C] Consequence: wasted pond productivity and a fragile single-species stock. Prevention: polyculture across feeding levels.

8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Basket trap: A woven funnel a fish enters but cannot exit; the settlement's low-labor workhorse.
  • Fish weir: A fixed barrier that funnels moving fish into a trap; passive, high-yield capture.
  • Integrated aquaculture: Coupling the fish pond to crops (rice-fish, pond-water irrigation) so each feeds the other; among the highest-yielding sustainable systems.
  • Paralytic shellfish poisoning: A lethal, cook-proof toxin filter-feeders concentrate during algal blooms.
  • Polyculture (pond): Stocking fish that feed at different water levels so the whole food web becomes protein.
  • Stew pond: A held pond kept stocked as a standing protein reserve (the monastery model).

9. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

9.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: Assess the local water — stream, coast, or a site for a pond; identify hardy plant-eating fish to stock.
  • Execution: Build passive traps first (fastest return); site and seal a pond with clean inflow; stock a polyculture; feed it scraps and pond growth; loop its water to the garden; cook or preserve every catch.
  • Review: Track wild-capture against the population's health (are catches shrinking? back off); expand the pond system as it proves out.

9.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier

  • [Tier L — Law] Dirty-sited pond / raw fish / bloom-season shellfish → disease, parasites, or fatal poisoning. Clean water in, cook the catch, respect bloom seasons.
  • [Tier C — Craft] Stocking, feeding, and harvest rates → calibrate to your water, climate, and species; polyculture and sustainable offtake are the tuning.

9.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: Trap-building, net-making, and pond management are distinct crafts; keep each two-deep. A settlement that loses its pond-keeper loses a food system, not just a skill.
