Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> Caution — the fertilizer people throw away: The humanure system handles the hard half of closing the nutrient loop — feces, with its pathogens and its 55 °C, 12-month discipline. This module is the easy half, and the one settlements most often waste: urine, which from a healthy person is nearly sterile and carries the majority of the nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium the body excretes. Diverting it is simple, using it is safe, and the payoff is large. The only real errors are burning plants by applying it too strong, and forgetting that the same nitrogen that feeds a garden becomes the nitrate that poisons a well if it reaches the water.

1. Why This Is Important

Human excreta split into two very different resources. Feces carry most of the pathogens and demand the full thermophilic composting treatment before use. Urine is the opposite: from a healthy person it is essentially sterile as it leaves the body, and it carries the lion's share of the plant nutrients — nitrogen (as urea), phosphorus, and potassium, the same N-P-K a settlement otherwise struggles to supply. One adult's urine over a year contains enough nitrogen to fertilize a meaningful patch of grain or vegetables. Wasting it — letting it soak away or contaminate water — throws away the cheapest, safest fertilizer the settlement produces and worsens the phosphorus bottleneck the food system fights.

Diverting urine also improves the humanure system: separating the liquid keeps the feces drier, which composts better and smells less. So urine diversion is a double win — better compost and a free fertilizer — for the cost of a simple seat.

2. Expected Outcome

A urine-diverting collection system that captures clean urine separately from feces; safe storage and dilution; and application that feeds the soil and charges the biochar without burning crops or contaminating water — turning a wasted stream into a major, safe source of settlement fertility.

3. Diversion: Capture It Clean at the Source

  • The urine-diverting seat. A simple divider in the toilet seat/pedestal channels urine forward into its own collection vessel while feces fall separately into the composting chamber. It captures clean urine and keeps the compost drier in one stroke.
  • The urinal. For men especially, a simple urinal (a funnel to a container) captures large volumes cleanly and easily and is the simplest possible collector.
  • Keep it out of the water. Collection and storage vessels must not leak toward any water source — urine's nitrogen is exactly the nitrate that causes blue-baby poisoning if it reaches a well, so the 30 m / downhill-of-water rule applies to urine storage as to latrines.

4. Storage: Aging Makes It Safer

Fresh urine can be used immediately, but storing (aging) it improves it. As urea breaks down, the urine turns alkaline (ammonia raises the pH), which sanitizes it further — killing off the few pathogens that might be present and making even urine from a not-perfectly-healthy person safer to use after some weeks of storage. Store it covered (to reduce nitrogen loss as ammonia gas and to keep it from breeding insects) in sealed vessels. Aged, stored urine is the default; fresh is fine for immediate soil or compost use.

5. Application: Feed the Soil, Not the Leaf, and Never the Water

Urine is strong — concentrated nitrogen and salts — so how it is applied matters:

  • Dilute for growing plants. Applied undiluted around growing plants, urine's nitrogen and salt will burn them. Dilute it substantially with water (a common rule of thumb is roughly one part urine to ten parts water for actively growing crops, less dilute for hungry establishment) and apply to the soil, watering it in.
  • Apply to soil, not to edible parts. As a hygiene margin, water urine into the soil around plants rather than onto leaves and fruit eaten raw; and, like humanure, favor it on grain and cooked/above-ground crops over root vegetables eaten raw, especially in the early years.
  • Undiluted into the compost and onto biochar. The compost heap and the biochar charging process want concentrated urine: it adds the nitrogen that balances a carbon-heavy compost and, crucially, charging raw biochar in urine for two-plus weeks is exactly how the biochar module says to make biochar soil-ready. Urine is the settlement's ready nitrogen source for both.
  • Watch the salt and the total load. Over-applying urine season after season builds up salt and can over-nitrogen the soil (and risk nitrate leaching to groundwater); rotate application and don't exceed what the crop can take up.

6. The Illness Exception

The healthy-person "nearly sterile" rule has an exception worth knowing: some diseases do shed in urine (typhoid, leptospirosis, and in some regions schistosomiasis). So during known illness, or a settlement-wide epidemic, treat the urine of the sick with more caution — age it longer before use, or divert it to the thermophilic system — rather than applying it fresh to food crops. In normal health, this is a non-issue.

7. Common Failure Modes

  • Applying it too strong. Undiluted urine on growing plants. [Tier L (for the crop)] Consequence: nitrogen/salt burn kills or stunts the plants you meant to feed. Prevention: dilute for growing crops (~1:10); apply concentrated only to compost/biochar.
  • Letting it reach water. Leaky storage or application near a water source. [Tier L] Consequence: nitrate contamination of the well — the blue-baby hazard. Prevention: the 30 m / downhill rule for urine too; water it into soil away from sources.
  • Fresh urine from the sick on food crops. Ignoring the illness exception. [Tier C→L] Consequence: the few urine-shed diseases spread to food. Prevention: age or divert the urine of the ill; normal-health urine is safe.
  • Salt/nitrogen build-up. Over-applying to the same ground indefinitely. [Tier C] Consequence: salted, over-nitrogen soil and leaching. Prevention: rotate application; match the load to crop uptake.
  • Wasting it entirely. Not diverting at all. [Tier C] Consequence: the settlement throws away its cheapest fertilizer and worsens the nutrient bottleneck. Prevention: a simple diverting seat or urinal — the easiest win in the whole nutrient loop.

8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Aging (urine): Storing urine so it turns alkaline and self-sanitizes before use.
  • Biochar charging: Pre-loading raw biochar with nutrients (urine is ideal) before soil use — the link between this module and Terra Preta.
  • Nitrogen burn: Damage to plants from too-concentrated nitrogen/salt; why urine is diluted for growing crops.
  • Urea: The main nitrogen compound in urine; breaks down to ammonia, raising pH and sanitizing stored urine.
  • Urine diversion: Separating urine from feces at the source, capturing clean fertilizer and keeping the compost drier.

9. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

9.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: Fit a urine-diverting seat and/or urinal; set up covered storage vessels sited away from water.
  • Execution: Age urine when convenient; dilute ~1:10 for growing crops and water into the soil; use it concentrated in the compost and to charge biochar; observe the illness exception.
  • Review: Watch for nitrogen burn (back off dilution) and salt build-up (rotate application); keep storage away from water.

9.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier

  • [Tier L — Law] Urine (nitrogen) reaching a water source → nitrate contamination / blue-baby risk. Store and apply away from water, downhill.
  • [Tier L — Craft-at-the-crop] Undiluted on growing plants → nitrogen burn. Dilute for crops; concentrated only for compost/biochar.
  • [Tier C — Craft] Dilution ratio and application rate → calibrate to crop, soil, and season; watch for burn and salt.

9.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: Urine diversion is almost too simple to teach, which is why its reasons (why dilute, why keep it from water, why it charges biochar) are what must pass down — a settlement that keeps the habit but forgets the reasons will eventually burn a crop or foul a well.
