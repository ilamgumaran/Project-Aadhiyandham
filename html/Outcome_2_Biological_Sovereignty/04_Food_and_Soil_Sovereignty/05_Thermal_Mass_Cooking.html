Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

Cooking is non-negotiable infrastructure. It is not a comfort — it is a biological requirement. Heat neutralizes pathogens (Salmonella, E. coli, Campylobacter, parasitic cysts), denatures anti-nutritional compounds (lectins in beans, oxalates in greens, trypsin inhibitors in grains), and makes macronutrients bioavailable through starch gelatinization, protein denaturation, and collagen breakdown. A settlement that cannot cook reliably will suffer chronic gastrointestinal disease, caloric inefficiency, and eventually malnutrition — even with adequate raw food stores.

But cooking has a cost measured in fuel. An open three-stone fire — the default cooking method for billions of people and the inevitable first instinct after collapse — consumes 5 to 10 kg of firewood per meal for a family of five. At two meals per day, a 50-person settlement burns through 50 to 100 kg of wood daily, or 18 to 36 tonnes per year. This rate of extraction will deforest the surrounding hectares within months, destroying the settlement's windbreak, acoustic buffer, wildlife habitat, and future timber supply. Fuel-efficient cooking technology is therefore not a luxury or an optimization — it is critical infrastructure for long-term settlement survival.

The quantitative case is decisive. Data from the Aprovecho Research Center (Oregon, USA), spanning four decades of empirical testing from the 1980s through the 2020s, establishes the following thermal efficiencies: open three-stone fire, 10-15%; well-built rocket stove, 35-55%; insulated cob earth oven operating in retained-heat mode, 70-80%. A rocket stove reduces fuel consumption by 70-90% compared to an open fire. An earth oven, once fired, requires zero ongoing fuel — it bakes and slow-cooks for hours on stored thermal energy alone. Combined with a hay box (retained-heat cooker), the settlement's daily wood consumption drops from 50-100 kg to 5-15 kg, a rate that 1-2 hectares of managed coppice woodland can sustain indefinitely.

> DANGER: Rocket stoves and earth ovens involve high temperatures (540 degrees C and above in the combustion chamber, 300 degrees C and above in the oven cavity). Burns from these systems are severe and can be life-threatening without medical infrastructure. All cooking installations must have a cleared area (1 m minimum) around them, a fire-resistant ground surface (stone, packed earth — never dry grass or wood chips), and water or sand immediately available for emergency extinguishing. Children must be supervised at all times near hot cooking infrastructure. Never move a hot rocket stove. Never open an earth oven door while standing directly in front of it — superheated steam can escape violently.

Theoretical Foundation

The Thermodynamics of Cooking

Heat transfer occurs by three mechanisms, and understanding which mechanism dominates a given cooking system is essential to building it correctly.

Conduction is heat transfer through direct physical contact. Efficient but requires intimate contact — an air gap of even a few millimeters dramatically reduces conductive transfer.

Convection is heat transfer by the movement of hot fluids (gases or liquids). This is the dominant mechanism in a rocket stove: hot combustion gases are channeled directly across the pot's bottom and sides.

Radiation is heat transfer by infrared electromagnetic waves emitted by hot surfaces. An open fire heats primarily by radiation, which radiates in all directions — only a small fraction strikes the pot. An earth oven heats by radiation from hot thermal mass walls surrounding the food on all sides.

Thermal efficiency is defined as the ratio of energy absorbed by the food to the total energy contained in the fuel:

  • Open three-stone fire: 10-15% (Aprovecho Research Center)
  • Rocket stove with insulated riser: 35-55% (Aprovecho Research Center)
  • Insulated earth oven in retained-heat mode: 70-80% (empirical testing, multiple sources)

Specific heat capacity is the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 degree C. This determines how much fuel is needed and how much heat a thermal mass can store:

  • Water: 4,186 J/kg per degree C (very high — this is why boiling water consumes so much fuel)
  • Cob/clay: approximately 900 J/kg per degree C (absorbs heat well, releases it slowly — ideal thermal mass)
  • Air: approximately 1,000 J/kg per degree C (but low density means low total thermal storage per volume)
  • Stone/brick: approximately 800-1,000 J/kg per degree C (dense, durable, good thermal mass)

Worked example — boiling water. Energy required to bring 5 litres of water from 20 degrees C to 100 degrees C: Q = m x c x delta-T = 5 x 4,186 x 80 = 1,674,400 J, approximately 1.67 MJ. Energy content of 1 kg of dry hardwood: approximately 18-20 MJ. At 10% efficiency (open fire): 1.67 / (20 x 0.10) = 0.84 kg of wood required. At 45% efficiency (rocket stove): 1.67 / (20 x 0.45) = 0.19 kg of wood required. This is a 4.4-fold reduction in fuel consumption for the single most common cooking task.

The Rocket Stove: Combustion Physics

The rocket stove's J-tube geometry achieves near-complete combustion through a simple but precisely engineered sequence of physical processes.

Primary combustion occurs in the feed tube. As dry wood is heated above 250-350 degrees C, it undergoes pyrolysis: complex organic molecules break down into volatile gases (methane, carbon monoxide, hydrogen, hydrocarbons) and solid charcoal. On an open fire, many of these volatiles escape unburned — visible as smoke, representing wasted energy and toxic particulate matter.

Secondary combustion occurs in the insulated riser. When the interior reaches 540-650 degrees C, the volatile gases themselves ignite and burn completely. This eliminates smoke, extracts remaining energy from fuel gases, and produces only carbon dioxide and water vapor as exhaust. The insulation around the riser makes this possible — without it, heat loss through the walls keeps interior temperature below the secondary combustion threshold.

The draft mechanism. Hot air is less dense than cool air and rises (buoyancy). The insulated riser creates a strong convective updraft that pulls cool air through the feed tube. This self-sustaining draft means no bellows or fan is needed. The hotter the riser, the stronger the draft — the system is self-reinforcing.

The chimney-to-feed-tube ratio. The riser height should be 2.5 to 3.5 times the feed tube length for optimal draft. Too short: insufficient draft, poor combustion, smoke. Too tall: excessive draft can pull flames through the system too quickly, reducing heat transfer time at the pot.

The pot gap. The gap between the pot bottom and the riser top must have a cross-sectional area equal to the cross-sectional area of the riser. Too small: the draft is choked, causing smoke to back up through the feed tube. Too large: hot gases escape around the pot without transferring their energy, reducing efficiency dramatically.

The Earth Oven: Thermal Mass Physics

A cob earth oven operates on a fundamentally different principle from a rocket stove. Rather than channeling active combustion gases, it stores energy in a massive thermal reservoir and releases it slowly.

The operating cycle: (1) Build a fire inside the oven and maintain it for 1-3 hours. (2) Remove the fire and embers. (3) Seal the oven with a fitted door. (4) Cook using the stored radiant heat from the walls, floor, and dome.

Heat storage capacity. The energy stored in the thermal mass is given by Q = m x c x delta-T. A 200 kg cob oven heated from ambient (20 degrees C) to a working temperature of 350 degrees C stores: Q = 200 x 900 x 330 = 59,400,000 J = 59.4 MJ. For reference, this is equivalent to the energy in approximately 3 kg of dry hardwood — but because the oven captures and retains heat at 70-80% efficiency, it represents the usable output of roughly 4-5 kg of wood burned during the firing phase.

Cascading heat use. This 59.4 MJ of stored energy is sufficient to bake bread at 200-250 degrees C for 3-4 hours, then slow-cook stews at 100-150 degrees C for another 6-10 hours, then dry herbs at 50-80 degrees C for several more hours, and finally radiate gentle warmth into an adjacent shelter space. One firing, multiple uses, zero additional fuel.

The insulation layer. Thermal conductivity determines how quickly heat escapes through a material. Dense cob has a thermal conductivity of approximately 0.6 W/m per K — it conducts heat readily (desirable for the inner layer, which must absorb and radiate heat inward). Straw-clay insulation has a thermal conductivity of approximately 0.1 W/m per K — it resists heat flow (essential for the outer layer, which must prevent the stored energy from escaping). This six-fold difference in conductivity is why the two-layer construction is critical.

The door seal. A well-fitted door — a wooden plank (soaked in water before each use to prevent charring) or a flat stone slab with a clay gasket — seals the oven cavity and traps both heat and moisture. Steam retention is important for bread baking, producing a crisp crust.

Historical Cooking Technology

Fuel-efficient cooking is not a modern invention. Civilizations have independently developed thermal mass and convective cooking systems for millennia:

  • Roman hypocaust (1st century BCE onward): ducted hot-air heating under floors and through wall cavities — the same convective channeling principle that drives rocket stove efficiency.
  • Tandoor oven (Central and South Asia, 3,000+ years): a vertical clay cylinder that functions as a thermal mass oven, reaching 480 degrees C and baking flatbread in 60-90 seconds.
  • Adobe hornos (Pueblo peoples, Southwestern North America): earth ovens in continuous use for over 1,000 years, demonstrating the indefinite durability of properly constructed thermal mass cooking infrastructure.
  • Japanese kamado (3rd century CE onward): a highly efficient ceramic stove design that channels combustion gases around a recessed pot, achieving thermal efficiencies comparable to modern rocket stoves.
  • Aprovecho Research Center (Oregon, USA, 1980s-present): Dr. Larry Winiarski's systematic research on the rocket stove represents the foundational modern work on fuel-efficient biomass cooking. The Aprovecho testing protocols remain the global standard for evaluating cookstove performance.

Core Principles

  1. The Insulation Imperative. The riser of a rocket stove MUST be insulated. An uninsulated metal or masonry riser loses heat through its walls, reducing the internal temperature below the 540 degrees C threshold required for secondary combustion. The result is smoke, soot, low efficiency, and creosote deposits — essentially an elaborate campfire. Effective insulation materials in order of performance: wood ash (best — lightweight, heat-resistant to 1,000+ degrees C, readily available), pumice, perlite, vermiculite, or dry straw-clay mix. Minimum insulation thickness: 10-15 cm around the entire riser.
  2. The Cross-Section Rule. In a rocket stove, the feed tube, riser, and pot gap must all have the same cross-sectional area. If any section is constricted, the draft is choked and the system smokes. If any section is oversized, gas velocity drops, residence time changes, and efficiency falls. Standard residential cooking size: 12 cm x 12 cm (approximately 144 sq cm) cross-section, suitable for pots up to 30 cm diameter. Scale up to 15 cm x 15 cm for institutional cooking (10+ people per pot).
  3. The Thermal Mass vs. Insulation Distinction. These materials serve OPPOSITE functions and must never be confused or substituted. Thermal mass (dense cob, stone, firebrick) ABSORBS and STORES heat — it forms the inner layer of an earth oven. Insulation (straw-clay, wood ash, pumice) RESISTS heat transfer — it forms the outer layer. Placing insulation on the interior of an oven means no heat storage and rapid cooling. Placing dense thermal mass on the exterior means stored heat conducts outward and is lost. The correct sequence is always: cavity, then thermal mass, then insulation, then optional weather shell.
  4. The Moisture Rule. Fuel wood must be dry, below 20% moisture content. Wet wood wastes enormous energy evaporating its water content before combustion temperatures can be reached. The latent heat of vaporization of water is 2,260 kJ/kg — energy that produces steam instead of useful cooking heat. Wet wood also produces heavy visible smoke, low flame temperature, incomplete combustion, and creosote deposits that clog and degrade the stove. All firewood must be split, stacked under a rain-proof cover with open sides for air circulation, and dried for a minimum of 6 months before use.
  5. The Retained-Heat Principle. After the initial firing, an earth oven requires no additional fuel. The thermal mass walls release their stored energy as radiant heat for hours, with temperature declining gradually and predictably. This means a single firing can serve cascading thermal needs: bake bread at 250 degrees C, then roast vegetables at 180 degrees C, then slow-cook stews at 100 degrees C, then dry herbs at 60 degrees C, then warm an adjacent sleeping space at 30-40 degrees C. Planning the cooking schedule around this temperature cascade maximizes the return on every kilogram of fuel burned.

Practical Implementation

The Rocket Stove: Detailed Construction

Materials required: Mud-bricks or stacked stone for the outer shell. High-clay soil and sand for mortar. Wood ash or pumice for insulation (approximately 30-40 litres). A flat stone or scavenged metal plate for the fuel shelf. Three stones or metal brackets for pot supports. Total construction time with prepared materials: 2-4 hours.

Dimensions (standard residential size, suitable for 5-10 people):

  • Feed tube: 12 cm x 12 cm internal cross-section, 30 cm long, oriented horizontally or at a slight upward angle (5-10 degrees). The slight upward angle helps retain embers in the combustion zone.
  • Riser: 12 cm x 12 cm internal cross-section, 75-90 cm tall (2.5 to 3 times the feed tube length).
  • Insulation gap: 10-15 cm of wood ash or pumice packed continuously around the entire riser. No gaps or settling allowed — top up insulation as it compresses.
  • Outer shell: mud-brick, salvaged metal drum (with bottom removed), or dry-stacked stone. Must contain the insulation and protect it from weather.
  • Fuel shelf: a flat stone or metal plate at the bottom of the feed tube, positioned 3-4 cm above the base. Fuel rests on this shelf; ash falls through the gap below. This allows continuous feeding without ash buildup choking the draft.
  • Ash cleanout: a removable stone plug or small door at the base below the fuel shelf for periodic ash removal.
  • Pot supports: three equally spaced stones or metal brackets on top of the riser, creating a gap between the pot bottom and the riser top. The total gap area must equal the riser cross-section (144 sq cm). For a 30 cm diameter pot, this means a gap height of approximately 1.5 cm around the circumference.

Airflow: Cool air enters the feed tube opening (left side). Combustion occurs where the feed tube meets the riser base. Hot gases rise through the insulated riser (center, upward arrows). Gases exit through the pot gap, transferring heat to the pot bottom and sides.

Firing procedure: Place a small bundle of tinder (dry grass, bark shavings, small splints) at the junction of the feed tube and riser. Light it. Feed small dry twigs (finger-width, 1-2 cm diameter) into the feed tube. Within 2-3 minutes, the riser should be drawing strongly and the flame should be pulled horizontally along the feed tube and up the riser. Visible smoke should cease within 3-5 minutes as the riser reaches secondary combustion temperature. If smoke persists, check insulation integrity and pot gap sizing.

The Cob Earth Oven: Detailed Construction

Materials required: Stone or rubble for the plinth (approximately 0.5 cubic metres). Glass bottles or wood ash for the insulation base. Firebricks or flat sandstone for the hearth floor (approximately 0.5 sq m). Clay soil and sharp sand for cob (approximately 200 kg mixed). Straw and clay for insulation coat (approximately 100 kg mixed). Wet sand for the form (approximately 80 kg, reusable). Newspaper or large leaves for the release layer. Total construction time: 3-5 days of active work, plus 2-4 weeks of drying.

Dimensions and ratios:

  • Plinth: stone and rubble foundation, 0.8-1.0 m tall (waist height for comfortable loading and unloading), 1.2 m x 1.2 m footprint. Must be level, solid, and capable of supporting 200-400 kg.
  • Insulation base: glass bottles laid horizontally in clay mortar (necks alternating), or 10 cm of compacted pure wood ash. This layer prevents heat from conducting downward into the stone plinth.
  • Hearth floor: flat firebricks or dense sandstone tiles, tightly fitted with minimal gaps, laid directly on the insulation base. Total hearth area: approximately 0.5 sq m.
  • Oven cavity: internal dome diameter 60-80 cm. Dome height = 63% of diameter (the empirically determined optimal ratio for heat circulation). For a 70 cm diameter oven: dome height = 44 cm.
  • Inner thermal mass layer: 10 cm of dense cob. Mix ratio: 1 part clay to 3 parts sharp sand by volume. This layer must be dense and heavy — it is the heat storage reservoir. Total weight of this layer: approximately 120-160 kg.
  • Outer insulation layer: 10-15 cm of straw-clay. Mix ratio: 1 part clay to 4 parts chopped straw by volume. This layer must be lightweight and fibrous — it traps the stored heat inside. Apply only after the inner layer has dried to leather-hard (3-7 days).
  • Door opening: width = 50-60% of dome diameter. Height = 63% of dome height (the "golden door ratio," empirically determined for optimal balance between draft during firing and heat retention during baking). For a 70 cm diameter, 44 cm tall oven: door width approximately 38 cm, door height approximately 28 cm.
  • Total finished weight: 200-400 kg depending on dimensions and wall thickness.

Construction sequence:

  1. Build the plinth. Allow mortar to set for 2-3 days.
  2. Lay the insulation base (bottles or ash) on top of the plinth.
  3. Lay the hearth floor (firebricks or sandstone) tightly on the insulation base.
  4. Build the sand form: pile damp sand on the hearth floor in a dome shape matching the target cavity dimensions. Smooth the surface. Cover with wet newspaper or large overlapping leaves (this is the release layer that prevents cob from bonding to sand).
  5. Apply the inner thermal mass layer: pack 10 cm of dense cob (1:3 clay:sand) over the form. Start from the base and work upward. Leave the door opening. Allow to dry to leather-hard (3-7 days).
  6. Apply the outer insulation layer: pack 10-15 cm of straw-clay (1:4 clay:straw) over the thermal mass layer. Allow to dry completely (1-2 weeks).
  7. Excavate: reach through the door opening and scoop out all sand and newspaper, leaving the hollow dome cavity. The sand is reusable.
  8. Drying: allow the entire structure to dry for 2-4 weeks in shade before first firing. Do NOT accelerate drying with sun exposure or artificial heat — rapid drying causes deep structural cracks.

First firing and curing: The first 3-4 firings must be small and brief — just enough to warm the interior slightly. Gradually increase fire size over successive firings. The curing process drives out remaining moisture slowly, preventing thermal shock cracks. The oven is fully cured when the interior surface appears uniformly light grey and sounds hollow when tapped.

The Hay Box (Retained-Heat Cooker)

The hay box is a zero-fuel cooking method that extends the output of a rocket stove by 70-80%, and it requires no special materials or construction skills.

Principle: Bring food to a full rolling boil on the rocket stove (5-10 minutes of active fuel use). Immediately transfer the sealed pot — lid tightly fitted — into a heavily insulated container. The insulation prevents heat loss; the food continues cooking at near-boiling temperature for hours without any additional fuel input.

Construction: Any box, basket, or pit large enough to hold the cooking pot, packed with minimum 15 cm of insulation on all sides. Effective materials: hay, straw, wool, crumpled newspaper, old clothing, dry leaves, or wood shavings. The pot must be surrounded completely — heat escapes through any gap.

Operation: Bring food to a full, vigorous, rolling boil. Maintain the boil for 2-5 minutes to ensure the entire contents reach 100 degrees C. Transfer immediately to the hay box — do not let the pot cool even slightly. Pack insulation tightly around the pot. Do not open for minimum 4 hours (longer is better for beans and tough grains).

Ideal applications: Stews, soups, beans (require 4-8 hours in hay box after boiling), whole grains (rice, wheat berries, barley — 2-4 hours), tough root vegetables, porridge, bone broth.

Energy savings: A dish that requires 3 hours of simmering on a stove uses fuel continuously for 3 hours. With a hay box, the same dish uses fuel for only 5-10 minutes of boiling, then cooks by retained heat. This represents a 70-80% reduction in fuel consumption for slow-cooked dishes.

Historical precedent: Hay box cooking has been practiced in Scandinavia for centuries ("Norwegian cooking") and was widely promoted during both World Wars as a fuel conservation measure.

Fuel Management for a 50-Person Settlement

Daily cooking fuel requirement with rocket stoves: 5-10 kg of dry wood per day, compared to 50-100 kg with open three-stone fires. With hay box integration for all slow-cooked dishes, the lower end of this range is achievable.

Annual fuel requirement: 2-4 tonnes of dry wood per year for a 50-person settlement using rocket stoves and hay boxes.

Sustainable harvest rate from managed woodland: A properly managed coppice rotation (cutting trees at ground level and allowing regrowth from the stump) yields 2-5 tonnes of dry wood per hectare per year, indefinitely, without killing the trees. Species such as willow, hazel, alder, and ash coppice especially well.

Therefore: 1-2 hectares of managed coppice woodland can provide all cooking fuel for 50 people in perpetuity. This is a manageable land allocation that simultaneously provides wildlife habitat, windbreak, and construction material.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Smoking Rocket Stove. The stove produces heavy, visible smoke that persists well beyond the initial 2-3 minute lighting period. Primary cause: the riser is not insulated, or insulation has settled and left gaps, preventing the interior from reaching secondary combustion temperature (540 degrees C). Secondary cause: the pot gap is too narrow, choking the draft and causing smoke to back up through the feed tube. Tertiary cause: fuel wood is wet (above 20% moisture content). Diagnosis: remove the pot and observe if smoke clears — if yes, the pot gap is too narrow. If smoke persists with no pot, the riser insulation is inadequate. Prevention: insulate the riser thoroughly, verify pot gap cross-section equals riser cross-section, use only dry fuel.
  2. Earth Oven Structural Cracks. Deep cracks appear during the drying phase or during the first firings. Primary cause: the cob dried too rapidly (direct sun, wind, artificial heat). Secondary cause: the clay content of the cob mix is too high relative to sand — clay shrinks as it dries, and excess clay produces excessive shrinkage stress. Prevention: dry the oven slowly over 2-4 weeks in shade. Use a mix of at minimum 1 part clay to 3 parts sharp sand. If cracks appear, they can be filled with fresh cob pressed firmly into the crack, then re-dried.
  3. Bread Burns on Bottom, Raw on Top. Uneven heat distribution in the earth oven after firing. Cause: the fire was not maintained long enough to fully heat the thermal mass through its entire thickness, or the oven was loaded too quickly after removing the embers. The hearth floor (receiving direct fire contact) is much hotter than the upper dome (heated by radiation and convection). Prevention: fire for a full 1-2 hours until the entire interior — including the dome apex — appears uniformly white or light grey. After removing embers, close the door and wait 15-20 minutes for the thermal mass to equalize before loading bread. The equalization period allows heat to conduct from the hotter hearth into the cooler dome.
  4. Fuel Wood Too Wet. Produces persistent smoke, low flame temperatures, poor combustion efficiency, and black creosote deposits on the riser interior. Creosote accumulation is both a fire hazard (chimney fires) and an efficiency problem (insulating deposits reduce heat transfer). Prevention: all firewood must be split (increasing surface area for drying), stacked in a single row under a rain-proof roof with open sides for maximum air circulation, and dried for a minimum of 6 months. Hardwoods may require 12 months. Test: strike two pieces together — dry wood produces a clear, ringing sound; wet wood produces a dull thud.
  5. Hay Box Food Safety Failure. Food temperature drops below 60 degrees C for extended periods, entering the bacterial growth danger zone (4-60 degrees C) where pathogens multiply rapidly. This risk is real and must be managed. Prevention: bring food to a FULL rolling boil (not a simmer — a vigorous, bubbling boil at 100 degrees C) before transferring. Use a pot with a tight-fitting lid. Ensure insulation packing is at least 15 cm on all sides with no gaps. Consume hay box food within 8-12 hours of insertion. If in doubt about temperature, re-boil before serving. Never hay-box raw or partially cooked meat.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Thermal mass: A material that absorbs, stores, and slowly releases heat energy. Dense cob, stone, and firebrick are effective thermal mass materials.
  • Insulation: A material that resists heat transfer, slowing the movement of thermal energy. Straw-clay, wood ash, pumice, and air gaps are effective insulation materials.
  • Convection: Heat transfer by the movement of heated fluids (gases or liquids).
  • Radiation: Heat transfer by infrared electromagnetic waves emitted from hot surfaces.
  • Conduction: Heat transfer through direct physical contact between materials.
  • Specific heat capacity: The energy (in joules) required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a material by 1 degree C.
  • Secondary combustion: The ignition and burning of volatile gases released during pyrolysis, occurring at temperatures above 540 degrees C in an insulated environment. Responsible for the near-zero smoke output of a functioning rocket stove.
  • Cob: A building material made from clay, sand, straw, and water. Dense cob (high sand content) serves as thermal mass; straw-clay (high straw content) serves as insulation.
  • Rocket stove: A fuel-efficient biomass cooking device using an insulated J-tube geometry to achieve secondary combustion and directed convective heat transfer.
  • J-tube: The characteristic shape of a rocket stove combustion chamber: a horizontal feed tube joined to a vertical insulated riser, forming a J or L shape.
  • Riser: The vertical insulated chimney of a rocket stove in which secondary combustion occurs and convective draft is generated.
  • Retained heat: The principle of storing thermal energy in a mass (oven walls, hay box insulation) and using it for cooking after the heat source has been removed.
  • Hay box: An insulated container used to continue cooking food by retained heat after it has been brought to a boil, requiring zero additional fuel.
  • Thermal efficiency: The ratio of useful heat energy absorbed by the food to the total chemical energy contained in the fuel consumed.
