Alignment: Outcome 2: Absolute Biological Sovereignty

> Caution — the invisible keystone: Modern life made salt so cheap and constant that it disappeared from awareness. Remove the supply chain and it reappears as one of the most strategically important substances a settlement can possess. Salt is not a seasoning; it is a metabolic necessity the body cannot make, and it is the required reagent for the food preservation that lets a harvest become a winter's survival. A settlement that grows abundant food but cannot salt-cure or ferment it will watch that food rot, and starve beside full fields. History priced salt as treasure and paid soldiers in it (the root of "salary") for exactly this reason. Know where your salt comes from before you need it.

1. Why This Is Important

Salt sits at the intersection of three survival systems, and its absence breaks all three:

  • Human metabolism. Sodium is essential to nerve, muscle, and fluid balance; the body loses it constantly in sweat and waste and cannot synthesize it. Severe deprivation causes weakness, cramps, and eventually death — and hard-laboring people in hot climates lose far more, which is why salt hunger is real and ancient.
  • Food preservation — the load-bearing use. The preservation module is built on salt: salt-curing meat needs roughly 10% salt by the weight of the meat, and safe lacto-fermentation needs 2–3% salt by weight — these are Law-tier ratios, and below them the food spoils or grows botulism. A winter's stored protein therefore requires a large standing quantity of salt. This is the use that turns salt from a nutrient into infrastructure.
  • Livestock. Animals need salt too (salt licks); deprived stock decline and fail to thrive.

The catch is that the industrial world hid how hard salt is to obtain without a supply chain — and how sharply availability depends on where you are. A coastal or brine-spring settlement has an easy, permanent source; an inland settlement far from either faces a genuine constraint that reaches all the way back into site selection and the trade network. This module is how to get salt from the land, and what to do when the land is stingy with it.

2. Expected Outcome

A reliable salt supply matched to the settlement's location: a working solar salt-pan or boiling operation where brine is available; a purification method for rock salt where it is mined; an honest assessment of demand (metabolic + preservation + livestock); dry storage that keeps the salt usable; and, for inland sites, salt secured as a planned trade good rather than an afterthought.

3. Know Your Demand First

Salt demand is dominated by preservation, not by the salt-shaker. A rough reckoning:

  • Metabolic: on the order of a few grams per person per day (more for hard laborers in heat) — small in bulk.
  • Preservation: this is the giant. Salting a winter's meat at 10% of its weight, plus fermenting vegetables at 2–3%, adds up to many kilograms per person per year for a settlement that stores much of its food by salt. Estimate the mass of meat and vegetables you intend to preserve, take the percentages, and the number will surprise a newcomer.
  • Livestock: modest per animal but continuous.

Do this arithmetic before choosing a production method, because it decides scale: a coastal settlement can evaporate almost unlimited seawater, but an inland one boiling a weak brine over precious fuel must know whether it is producing enough to preserve the harvest — the constraint that most often forces salt into the trade ledger.

4. Sources and Methods

4.1. Seawater — Solar Evaporation (the cheapest, if you have sun and coast)

Seawater is ~3.5% salt; the sun and wind do the work for free where the climate allows.

  • The salt-pan sequence. Lead seawater through a series of shallow basins (clay-lined or rock). In the first, larger ponds the water concentrates as it evaporates; less-soluble minerals (gypsum) drop out first. The concentrated brine is then moved to shallow crystallizing pans where, as the last water leaves, salt crystallizes and is raked up.
  • Climate dependence — the hard limit. Solar salt-making needs a hot, dry, sunny, windy season with little rain; it is a summer/dry-season industry and fails in wet or cold climates. This is a Craft-tier match to your climate, and where the climate refuses, boiling is the fallback.
  • Purity. Skim off the first-precipitating minerals and take the cleaner middle-and-late salt; a final dissolve-and-recrystallize (below) purifies further if needed.

4.2. Brine Springs and Seawater — Boiling (works anywhere, costs fuel)

Where the sun won't do it, heat will — at a price.

  • Boil brine (seawater or, far better, a strong natural brine/salt spring) down until the water is gone and salt remains. Salt springs were the basis of great inland salt industries precisely because their brine is far stronger than seawater, so far less fuel is needed per kilogram of salt.
  • The fuel reckoning. Boiling seawater to dryness is enormously fuel-hungry (you evaporate ~30 kg of water per kg of salt) and can deforest a settlement that relies on it at scale — weigh it against the fuel budget. Concentrate first by any free means (solar pre-concentration, freezing off fresh ice in winter, trickling brine over brushwood "graduation" stacks in wind) so you boil only the final strong brine.

4.3. Rock Salt (Halite) — Mining and Refining

Where geological salt deposits exist (field mineralogy can flag them), salt can be dug or quarried directly, or dissolved out of the ground with water and the brine then evaporated. Raw rock salt often carries clay and other minerals; dissolve it in water, let the insolubles settle or filter them out, then evaporate the clean brine to recrystallize pure salt. Purity matters for preservation — dirty salt carries off-flavors and unreliable strength.

4.4. Plant-Ash "Salt" — the Inland Fallback (and its limits)

Some plants of salty ground (glasswort/samphire, certain grasses and reeds) concentrate salts; burning them and leaching the ash yields a salty residue that has been used as a salt substitute inland for millennia.

  • The honest limit: plant-ash salt is largely potassium salts, not sodium chloride — it can season and partly ferment, but it is a poor substitute for the sodium the body needs and for reliable salt-curing of meat, where sodium chloride's specific water-drawing action is what preserves. Treat it as a supplement and a flavoring, not a full replacement for true salt. (This is also, usefully, the potash chemistry the soap-makers use.)

5. The Inland Problem: Salt as Strategy

A settlement far from sea, brine spring, or salt rock faces a real, permanent constraint — and pretending otherwise is how a larder fails. The honest responses:

  • Weigh salt access in site selection. Proximity to a coast, a known brine spring, or a salt deposit is a genuine survival asset, on the list with water and soil. It is easy to overlook because modern salt is invisible.
  • Make salt a planned trade good. Salt was one of history's great trade commodities exactly because inland demand met coastal supply. An inland settlement should secure salt through the network, trading what it has for the salt it cannot make — and a coastal settlement's surplus salt is one of the most valuable things it can offer that network.
  • Stretch what you have. Reuse curing brines, recover salt from spent brines, and prioritize the salt you do have for the preservation that most needs it.

6. Storage

Salt is hygroscopic — it pulls water from the air, clumps, and can dissolve away in a humid store. Keep it dry and covered, in sealed ceramic or well-closed containers, off damp floors, exactly as the pantry keeps grain dry. Well-kept dry salt lasts essentially forever, which makes it an ideal thing to stockpile in good years against lean ones — a stable, imperishable insurance the settlement can bank.

8. Common Failure Modes

  • No salt plan. Assuming salt will be there, then facing the first harvest with nothing to preserve it. [Tier L] Consequence: the harvest rots; the settlement starves beside full fields, or must eat everything before it spoils and then has nothing for winter. Prevention: secure a salt source (or trade supply) before the first preservation season; count demand honestly.
  • Under-salting to stretch supply. Curing meat or fermenting below the Law-tier ratios because salt is scarce. [Tier L] Consequence: spoilage and botulism — the preservation module's exact failure. Prevention: never cut the ratio; if salt is short, preserve less food properly rather than more food dangerously, and get more salt.
  • Boiling away the forest. Relying on boiling weak seawater at scale. [Tier C] Consequence: fuel exhaustion / deforestation. Prevention: solar-concentrate first; boil only strong brine; prefer salt springs and solar pans.
  • Trusting plant-ash salt as a full substitute. Curing meat with potassium-heavy plant salt. [Tier L] Consequence: unreliable preservation and sodium deficiency. Prevention: use plant-ash salt only to season/supplement; get true sodium-chloride salt for curing and metabolism.
  • Damp storage. Leaving salt exposed to humidity. [Tier C] Consequence: it clumps or dissolves away — a hard-won resource lost. Prevention: dry, sealed, covered storage.

9. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Brine: Salt-saturated or salt-rich water; the intermediate from which salt is crystallized. Salt springs yield stronger brine than seawater.
  • Crystallizing pan: The final shallow basin where salt precipitates from strong brine.
  • Graduation (of brine): Pre-concentrating brine by trickling it over stacks in wind/sun to cut boiling fuel.
  • Halite: Rock salt; geological sodium-chloride deposits, mined and refined by dissolving and recrystallizing.
  • Hygroscopic: Water-attracting; why salt clumps and must be stored dry.
  • Plant-ash salt: A potassium-rich salt substitute from burning salt-tolerant plants; seasons and partly ferments but does not replace true curing salt.
  • Solar salt-pan: A cascade of shallow basins that concentrates and then crystallizes salt using sun and wind; needs a hot, dry season.

10. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

10.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: Identify your best source (coast → solar/boil; brine spring → boil; salt rock → mine/refine; inland → trade). Estimate annual demand from your preservation plan.
  • Execution: Build a solar salt-pan cascade where climate allows, or a fuel-efficient boiling setup on concentrated brine; purify by dissolve-filter-recrystallize where needed; store bone-dry.
  • Review: Track output against the preservation calendar; if short, secure trade salt before the curing season, not during it.

10.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier

  • [Tier L — Law] Enter a preservation season without adequate salt → the harvest cannot be safely stored → winter famine. Salt supply is preservation infrastructure; plan it like water.
  • [Tier L — Law] Under-salt to stretch scarce supply → spoilage/botulism. Preserve less, properly; never dilute the cure ratio.
  • [Tier C — Craft] Production method → match to climate (solar needs hot/dry) and fuel budget (boiling is costly); calibrate to your site.

10.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: Salt-making is a seasonal craft easily lost in a settlement that trades for salt in good decades and then finds the route cut in a bad one. Keep the local method alive — the salt-pan, the boiling, the refining — as living practice, so the settlement is never one broken trade route from an unpreservable harvest.
