Alignment: Outcome 4: Psychological Centeredness

1. Why This Is Important

Every human group larger than a family unit will develop a leadership structure. If that structure is not deliberately designed, it will emerge organically around whoever is loudest, most physically imposing, or most charismatic. History is unambiguous on this point: from the French Revolution's Committee of Public Safety to the factional collapses of intentional communes in the 1970s, unmanaged power vacuums are filled by authoritarians. In a post-collapse refugia of 15-50 people, a single charismatic individual consolidating decision-making authority can redirect food, labor, and housing to serve a personal agenda within weeks. The community does not need a president. It needs structural safeguards that make consolidation of power physically difficult.

2. Expected Outcome

After implementing these protocols, the community will have:

  • No single individual holding decision-making authority over more than one critical domain (food, water, shelter, security, health) for longer than one seasonal rotation (90 days).
  • A documented and practiced process for rotating facilitation roles.
  • At least three independent checks on any resource-allocation decision.
  • Observable reduction in deference behavior (measured via Social Cohesion Metrics, Module 04).

4. Assembly Instructions (Step-by-Step)

4.1. Establish the Domain Separation Principle

Identify the 4-6 critical domains of community operation. A typical set:

  1. Food (agriculture, foraging, storage, rationing)
  2. Water (collection, purification, distribution)
  3. Shelter (construction, maintenance, allocation)
  4. Health (medical care, sanitation, mental health)
  5. Security (perimeter, conflict de-escalation, external relations)
  6. Education (skill transmission, apprenticeships, children)

Each domain is led by a Steward, not a chief. A Steward facilitates decisions within that domain but cannot unilaterally allocate resources. No individual may serve as Steward of more than one domain simultaneously.

4.2. Implement Mandatory Role Rotation

Every 90 days (one season), Steward roles rotate. The outgoing Steward must spend the final two weeks training their successor. The rotation order is determined by a pre-agreed sequence, not by election or volunteering. Elections create popularity contests; volunteering selects for ambition. A deterministic rotation (e.g., alphabetical by first name, then cycling) removes both failure modes.

Critical rule: No individual may serve as Steward of the same domain twice in a row. A minimum gap of one full rotation cycle (270 days) must pass before a person can return to a domain they previously stewarded.

4.3. Institute the Three-Voice Rule

Any decision that affects resource allocation (food rationing, water usage limits, labor assignments) requires verbal agreement from three parties:

  1. The domain Steward proposing the decision.
  2. A randomly selected community member (drawn by lot at each meeting).
  3. A designated "Dissent Voice" whose explicit role is to argue against the proposal for a fixed period (5 minutes) before the vote.

The Dissent Voice role rotates at each meeting. This structurally prevents rubber-stamping.

4.4. Create the Anti-Accumulation Norm

Post the following principle on the community notice board and recite it at each seasonal rotation ceremony:

> "No person shall hold both the knowledge and the authority. Those who know how to fix the water filter shall not also decide who receives water. Those who grow the food shall not also decide who eats."

This separates technical expertise from political authority. A person can be the community's best healer and still not be the Health Steward, because the Steward's job is facilitation, not expertise.

4.5. Establish the Recall Protocol

If any three community members (minimum) formally raise concern that a Steward is accumulating disproportionate influence, a community circle is convened within 48 hours. The Steward in question must step down during the review. The circle uses the same E-Prime and Active Mirroring protocols from Module 04 (Conflict Resolution). If the circle reaches consensus that the concern is valid, the Steward is replaced immediately and barred from that domain for two full rotation cycles.

6. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Steward: A temporary facilitator of a community domain. Not a leader. A Steward coordinates and communicates but does not command.
  • Domain Separation: The principle that no individual simultaneously controls more than one critical area of community life.
  • Three-Voice Rule: A structural requirement that resource-allocation decisions require agreement from the Steward, a randomly selected member, and a Dissent Voice.
  • Dissent Voice: A rotating role whose explicit duty is to argue against a proposal for a set time, ensuring critical scrutiny regardless of social pressure.
  • Recall Protocol: A community-initiated process to review and potentially remove a Steward who is consolidating inappropriate influence.
  • Anti-Accumulation Norm: The principle that technical expertise and political authority must not reside in the same person for the same domain.

7. Scientific Validation & Research Context

  • Dunbar's Number and Flat Hierarchies: Robin Dunbar's research (1992, 2010) demonstrates that human groups below approximately 150 individuals can maintain social cohesion without formal hierarchical structures, but only if deliberate norms prevent dominance consolidation.
  • The Iron Law of Oligarchy: Robert Michels (1911) observed that all organizations, no matter how democratic in intent, tend toward oligarchy unless structural counter-mechanisms are embedded from the outset. Rotation and domain separation directly counter this tendency.
  • Ostrom's Design Principles: Elinor Ostrom's Nobel Prize-winning research on commons governance (1990) identified that successful self-governing communities require clearly defined boundaries of authority, collective-choice arrangements where most affected individuals participate, and graduated sanctions. The Steward model and Three-Voice Rule implement these principles.
  • Milgram Obedience Studies: Stanley Milgram's experiments (1963) demonstrated that humans will defer to perceived authority even when it contradicts their own judgment. The Anti-Accumulation Norm and mandatory rotation reduce the formation of perceived permanent authority.

8. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

8.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  1. Week 1: Gather the full community. Collectively identify and name the 4-6 critical domains. Write them on the notice board.
  2. Week 1: Assign initial Stewards by lot (drawing names from a hat). Record the assignments and the date in the Decision Log.
  3. Week 2: Hold a practice Three-Voice meeting. Choose a low-stakes decision (e.g., meal schedule for the week). Run the full protocol: Steward proposes, Random Lot member weighs in, Dissent Voice argues against for 5 minutes. Vote.
  4. Week 3-4: Conduct daily domain check-ins (10 minutes maximum). Each Steward reports one sentence on their domain's status. No discussion unless a Three-Voice decision is needed.
  5. Day 90: Execute the first rotation. The outgoing Steward walks the incoming Steward through ongoing projects, open decisions, and known problems. Log the transition.

8.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes

  • "But she is the only one who knows how...": This is the Strongman trap. If only one person holds a critical skill, the Apprenticeship Mandate (Module 04, Conflict Resolution) must be invoked immediately. No skill monopoly can be allowed to persist, because it inevitably becomes political leverage.
  • Rotation fatigue: If community members complain that rotation is inefficient ("Why change stewards when things are working?"), remind them that the cost of one bad consolidation event dwarfs the inefficiency of transition. Smooth is slow; slow is safe.
  • The shadow leader: Sometimes a former Steward continues to exert influence informally ("Everyone still asks David about water even though Maria is the Steward now"). Address this directly in a community circle. David must be publicly reminded that his role is to support Maria, not to lead from the shadows.

8.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

Over a ten-year horizon, the rotation system produces a community in which every adult has served as Steward of multiple domains. This creates deep cross-functional understanding, eliminates knowledge silos, and builds empathy for the constraints each domain faces. Document every rotation, every Three-Voice decision, and every Recall event in the Decision Log. This log becomes the community's institutional memory and its most powerful defense against future consolidation attempts.
