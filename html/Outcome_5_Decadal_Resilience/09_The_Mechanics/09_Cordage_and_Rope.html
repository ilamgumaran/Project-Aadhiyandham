Alignment: Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

Rope is the connective tissue of every mechanical and structural system a post-collapse settlement will build. Without scavenged nylon paracord or steel cables, you cannot bind timber framing, secure traction splints, build suspension bridges, rig lifting tackle, string the harnesses on a floor loom, or lash a travois for overland transport. Every joint, every hoist, every net, every snare, every bow string, and every lashing that holds your built environment together depends on cordage. A community without rope is a community without infrastructure.

The physics of why twisted fiber works are elegant. A single plant fiber, even a strong one like hemp bast, is thin, short, and fragile in isolation. But when you twist dozens of fibers together in a helix, each fiber presses against its neighbors under lateral compression. Friction between these compressed surfaces prevents any single fiber from slipping out. The helical geometry distributes an axial load across many fibers simultaneously, so the assembly is far stronger than the sum of its parts. This is the same principle that makes steel cable work, and it was discovered independently by every civilization on earth thousands of years before the underlying mechanics were understood.

Every pre-industrial civilization depended on locally produced rope, and the scale of that dependence is staggering. The British Royal Navy consumed over 1,000 tonnes of hemp rope per year at its peak, driving entire agricultural economies in Russia and the Baltic states. Polynesian navigators lashed their ocean-going double-hulled canoes with coconut coir cordage that survived months of saltwater immersion. Egyptian tomb paintings from 2500 BCE depict organized rope walks producing cordage for pyramid construction. The skill you are about to learn is arguably the oldest continuously practiced manufacturing process in human history.

> CAUTION: Rope under tension stores significant energy. A snapping rope can cause severe lacerations or eye injury. Never stand in the bight of a loaded rope. When processing nettles or other irritant plants, wear gloves and long sleeves.

Theoretical Foundation

Physics of Twisted Fiber

The fundamental mechanism of rope is helical geometry under friction. When you twist fibers around a central axis, each fiber follows a helical path. Under axial load, this helix attempts to tighten, pressing fibers against each other laterally. The resulting friction prevents individual fibers from sliding past one another, distributing the load across the entire cross-section.

The optimal twist angle for maximizing tensile strength is approximately 15-20 degrees from the rope axis. Below this angle, lateral compression is insufficient to generate adequate friction between fibers, and they slide past each other under load. Above this angle, the fibers carry too much of their load in the lateral direction rather than the axial direction, reducing the overall tensile strength of the assembly. This optimal range was determined empirically by rope makers long before the underlying mechanics were understood.

Lay direction follows a universal convention. If you hold a rope vertically and the helical strands spiral upward to the right, it has a Z-twist (right-hand lay). If they spiral upward to the left, it has an S-twist (left-hand lay). The critical principle: each successive level of the rope hierarchy must alternate twist direction. If your fibers are given a Z-twist to form yarns, the yarns must be given an S-twist to form strands, and the strands must be given a Z-twist to form the finished rope. This alternation is what prevents the structure from unwinding under load.

The hierarchy of rope construction follows a strict progression:

  • Fibers are twisted together to form yarns
  • Yarns are twisted together (opposite direction) to form strands
  • Strands are laid together (opposite direction again) to form rope
  • Ropes can be laid together to form cable (for the heaviest applications)

The most common construction is three-strand hawser-laid rope, used universally for general-purpose work. Four-strand rope (shroud-laid) is rounder and more resistant to crushing but slightly weaker per unit weight. Braided construction, where strands are interwoven rather than twisted, does not rotate under load (useful for pulleys) but is far more difficult to produce by hand and nearly impossible to splice in the field.

For load calculations, you must distinguish breaking strength from working load. The breaking strength is the force at which the rope catastrophically fails. The working load is the maximum force you should routinely apply, and it must include a safety factor:

  • General material handling: 5:1 safety factor (minimum)
  • Lifting heavy objects where failure causes property damage: 8:1
  • Any application involving human life (climbing, suspension bridges, safety lines): 10:1

A rope with 500 kg breaking strength has a working load of only 100 kg for general use, or 50 kg if a person could be injured by its failure. Never assume a rope is as strong as its original rating; use, weather, and age reduce actual breaking strength.

Science of Natural Fibers

All plant-based cordage fibers are composed of cellulose microfibrils arranged in crystalline bundles (see 05_Textiles_and_Leather.md for detailed cellulose science). The three categories of plant fiber relevant to rope making are bast fibers, leaf fibers, and seed fibers.

Bast fibers are extracted from the inner bark of dicotyledonous plants. They are the strongest and longest natural fibers available, making them the preferred choice for all serious cordage. Leaf fibers (sisal, yucca, agave) are extracted from the structural veins of monocot leaves. They are coarser and less flexible but widely available in tropical and subtropical regions. Seed fibers (cotton, kapok) are short, fine, and comparatively weak, suitable only for light cordage and textiles.

The following table compares tensile strength across the major cordage fibers:

Fiber Type Tensile Strength (MPa) Fiber Length Wet Strength Retention
Hemp Bast 550-900 1-2 m 90%+
Flax Bast 345-1035 30-90 cm 90%+
Nettle Bast 650 50-80 cm 85-90%
Ramie Bast 400-938 5-15 cm (bundles) 95%+
Jute Bast 393-773 1-4 m 60-70%
Sisal Leaf 468-640 60-120 cm 85%
Cotton Seed 287-597 2-3 cm 80-90%

Wet strength retention determines which fibers are appropriate for marine, bridge, and outdoor use. Hemp retains over 90% of its dry strength when wet, which is why it dominated naval rope for centuries. Jute loses 30-40% when wet and rots quickly, making it suitable only for temporary dry-use cordage. Cotton actually gains a small amount of strength when wet, but its short fiber length makes it a poor rope material regardless.

UV degradation is a serious concern for natural rope. Ultraviolet radiation breaks the chemical bonds in cellulose polymer chains, causing fibers to become brittle and lose tensile strength progressively. Unprotected hemp rope loses roughly 50% of its tensile strength after 6 months of continuous sunlight exposure. The degradation is cumulative and irreversible. Store rope out of direct sunlight when not in use and inspect for brittleness before each loaded use. UV degradation affects the outer surface first; a rope may appear sound at the core while its outer fibers are severely weakened.

Fiber length is the single most important predictor of rope quality. Longer fibers mean fewer splice points per unit length, and splice points are always the weakest part of any cordage. Hemp bast fibers at 1-2 meters dramatically outperform cotton seed fibers at 2-3 centimeters. This is why bast fibers are always preferred for load-bearing rope.

Historical Precedent

The oldest known cordage is indirect: perforated shell beads from Blombos Cave, South Africa, dating to approximately 75,000 years ago, imply string to thread them on. The oldest direct evidence is impressions of twisted plant fibers on clay fragments from Dolni Vestonice, Czech Republic, approximately 28,000 years old. By the time Otzi the Iceman died in the Alps around 3300 BCE, he carried sophisticated cordage made from grass and lime bast fiber, including a net with uniform mesh spacing.

Egyptian tomb paintings from the Fifth Dynasty (approximately 2500 BCE) depict organized rope-making operations using the same reverse-wrap principle described in this module. The Great Pyramid alone required hundreds of kilometers of rope for sledge hauling and lever operations.

The Inuit of the Arctic produced cordage from braided caribou sinew and split baleen (the keratinous filter plates from bowhead whale mouths). Sinew cordage has exceptional tensile strength for its diameter and was essential for harpoon lines, dog sled lashing, and bow strings in an environment lacking plant fibers of adequate length.

Polynesian navigators built ocean-going double-hulled canoes lashed entirely with coconut coir rope. Coir, the fiber from coconut husks, has modest tensile strength but exceptional saltwater resistance. These lashed hulls were deliberately flexible, absorbing wave energy rather than fighting it.

The British Royal Navy's appetite for hemp rope shaped global agriculture and trade for centuries. A single 74-gun ship of the line required approximately 30 tonnes of rope at commissioning, with continuous replacement thereafter. At peak strength, the Royal Navy consumed over 1,000 tonnes of hemp rope per year, almost entirely sourced from Russia and the Baltic states. Napoleon's Continental Blockade of 1807 exposed the strategic vulnerability of depending on foreign rope fiber.

The rope walk, a structure 300 meters or longer where rope makers could twist and lay long continuous lengths, was a standard feature of every port and naval yard in the pre-industrial world. The Chatham Dockyard rope walk, built in 1786, stretched 347 meters and operated until 1984. Japanese rice straw shimenawa, the sacred ropes hung at Shinto shrines, demonstrate rope making at monumental scale: the shimenawa at Izumo Taisha weighs approximately 5 tonnes and requires an entire community effort to produce and install.

In the Americas, Indigenous peoples produced cordage from a wide range of plant and animal materials. Dogbane (Apocynum cannabinum) was the most widely used plant fiber across temperate North America, found in archaeological contexts spanning thousands of years. The Ancestral Puebloan peoples of the Southwest used yucca leaf fiber for sandals, nets, and structural lashing. These traditions confirm that effective cordage can be produced from whatever fiber resources are locally available.

The lesson of this history is clear: rope making is not optional infrastructure. It is foundational.

Core Principles

  1. Fiber Length Determines Rope Strength. Long bast fibers are always preferred over short seed or grass fibers. Every splice point is a potential failure zone. The fewer splices per meter, the stronger and more reliable the finished rope. Prioritize hemp, nettle, dogbane, and other long-fiber plants for any load-bearing cordage.
  2. Opposing Twist Locks Structure. The fundamental reverse-wrap principle: twist individual strands in one direction, then wrap them together in the opposite direction. The two opposing torques fight each other, preventing the cord from unwinding. If you twist and wrap in the same direction, the cord will unravel the moment you release tension.
  3. Stagger All Splices. Never let two strand joins align at the same point along the rope. If both strands run out simultaneously, you have a single cross-section where the entire load passes through unsecured new material. Always ensure one strand extends well past the splice point of the other.
  4. Diameter Determines Load. Doubling the diameter of a rope quadruples its cross-sectional area and therefore its breaking strength (assuming consistent fiber density and construction). A 12mm rope is four times as strong as a 6mm rope of the same material and construction. Plan your rope diameter based on the intended load, applying appropriate safety factors.
  5. Inspect Before Every Use. Natural rope degrades. UV exposure makes fibers brittle. Moisture encourages mildew and rot. Abrasion severs surface fibers. Internal rot can weaken a rope that appears sound on the outside. Before every use under significant load, inspect the full length by flexing the rope and looking for stiffness, discoloration, broken surface fibers, or musty odor. Retire any rope that shows signs of degradation.

Practical Implementation

Fiber Identification and Harvesting

The strongest natural ropes are not made from grass; they are made from bast fibers, the inner bark of specific trees and plants.

Trees: The inner bark of Willow, Basswood (Linden), Cedar, and Hickory provide exceptionally strong, long fibers.

  • Basswood (Linden) is the preferred tree species in eastern North America: its bast fibers are long, flexible, and separate cleanly after retting.
  • Willow bark is harvested in spring when the sap is running, as the bark peels easily during this window.
  • Cedar bark fibers are naturally rot-resistant, making them suitable for outdoor cordage exposed to weather.
  • Hickory bark is extremely strong but stiff, best used for heavy lashing rather than flexible rope.

Plants: Stinging Nettle, Dogbane, Milkweed, and Hemp are the gold standards for herbaceous cordage.

  • Dogbane (Apocynum cannabinum) produces fibers of exceptional fineness and strength, yielding cordage suitable for fishing line and bow strings.
  • Stinging nettle produces strong fibers but requires gloves during harvest and processing due to formic acid in the trichomes.
  • Milkweed yields usable fiber but is weaker than dogbane or nettle; adequate for light cordage and twine.
  • Hemp, where available, produces the finest general-purpose cordage fiber in the temperate world.

Seasonal harvesting windows: Herbaceous bast fiber plants (nettle, dogbane, milkweed, hemp) are best harvested in late summer to early autumn, after the plant has reached full height and begun to dry but before winter decay sets in. The stalks should be brown and dry at the base. Tree bark is best harvested in late spring when sap flow loosens the bark from the cambium, allowing clean separation. Bark harvested in winter adheres tightly and tears rather than peeling.

Fiber yield estimates: A single mature dogbane stalk yields approximately 1-2 grams of processed fiber, enough for roughly 30 cm of 3mm two-ply cordage. A dense nettle patch of 10 square meters can yield 200-500 grams of processed fiber per harvest. Plan your fiber garden accordingly: you will need substantial plantings to support ongoing rope production.

Identification features: Dogbane has opposite leaves, reddish stems, and milky sap when broken. Stinging nettle has opposite, toothed leaves with visible stinging hairs on stems and leaf undersides. Milkweed has opposite leaves, milky sap, and distinctive seed pods. These three are frequently confused by beginners; positive identification before processing is essential.

Retting and Processing

Retting is the controlled decomposition of the soft tissue surrounding bast fibers, allowing the fibers to be separated cleanly from the woody core and outer bark.

Water retting (5-14 days): Submerge bundled stalks in still or slow-moving water (a pond, stream eddy, or large trough). Bacterial action breaks down the pectin binding fibers to the woody core. Water retting produces the cleanest fibers with the least mechanical effort, but it is foul-smelling and contaminates the water source. Use a dedicated retting pond away from drinking water.

Dew retting (3-6 weeks): Spread stalks in a thin layer on grass and leave them exposed to dew, rain, and microbial action. Turn the stalks periodically for even decomposition. Dew retting is slower but requires no infrastructure and does not contaminate water. The resulting fibers are slightly coarser than water-retted material but fully adequate for cordage.

The processing sequence after retting follows a standard progression. Breaking: bend the dried, retted stalks sharply over a breaking board to fracture the woody core into short segments. Scutching: scrape the broken woody material (shives) away from the fibers using a wooden blade drawn along the length of the fiber bundle. Hackling: draw the fiber bundle through a series of progressively finer combs (hackles) to separate individual fibers and remove remaining shive particles. This sequence is identical to flax processing for linen textiles (see 05_Textiles_and_Leather.md).

Vigorously rub the dry bast fibers over a smooth log to further separate individual threads and make them soft and pliable. This buffing step is essential for producing uniform cordage.

Fiber storage: Store processed fibers dry, bundled loosely, and out of direct sunlight. A dry attic or covered shelf is ideal. Well-stored bast fibers remain usable for years.

The Reverse Wrap

You cannot simply twist fibers together in one direction; they will immediately untwist when you let go. The reverse wrap uses opposing tension to lock the fibers in place permanently.

  1. The Bite: Take a bundle of processed fibers (about half the thickness of a pencil). Pinch the bundle tightly in the middle with your left hand.
  2. The Twist: With your right hand, grab the top half of the bundle and twist it tightly away from your body (clockwise) until it begins to kink.
  3. The Wrap: Take that tightly twisted top half and wrap it toward your body (counter-clockwise) over the bottom half.
  4. The Lock: Switch your left hand's pinch to the new intersection you just created.
  5. Repeat: Now take the new top half, twist it away from you, and wrap it toward you over the bottom half. The two opposing twists fight each other, creating a permanent, locked cord.

Two-person thigh-rolling method: For faster production of heavier cordage, one person sits with fibers laid across their bare thigh. They roll both strands simultaneously away from their body using the flat of their palm, then wrap them together on the return stroke. A second person sits opposite, feeding new fibers into the strands as they shorten. This method roughly doubles production speed compared to solo finger-twisting and produces more consistent results for rope above 4mm diameter.

Drop-spindle method: For very fine cordage (under 2mm), attach the starting fiber to a small weighted spindle (a stick through a clay or stone disc). Spin the spindle to apply consistent twist to a single strand, then ply two finished singles together with opposite twist. This method produces the most uniform fine cordage but is slow for heavy rope.

Production rates: An experienced maker produces approximately 3-5 meters of 3mm two-ply cordage per hour by hand using the finger-twist method. The thigh-rolling method increases this to 5-8 meters per hour. A beginner should expect roughly 1-2 meters per hour until muscle memory develops. Large-diameter rope (12mm+) requires laying equipment and multiple people working simultaneously.

Splicing and Joining

When you reach the end of your fiber bundle, you must splice in new material.

  1. The Taper: Ensure the ends of your current fibers are staggered (one strand is longer than the other). Never let both strands run out at the same place, or the rope will snap there.
  2. The Lay-in: Take a new bundle of fibers. Lay it directly over the short strand, overlapping them by at least 5 cm (2 inches).
  3. The Continue: Twist the old short strand and the new bundle together as if they were one single piece, and continue the reverse wrap. The tension will pull the new fibers into the matrix flawlessly.

The eye splice (permanent loop): Unlay 15-20 cm of the rope end into its component strands. Bend the rope back on itself to form the desired loop size. Tuck each strand under a corresponding strand of the standing part, working against the lay direction. Make three complete rounds of tucks for security. An eye splice retains approximately 90-95% of the rope's breaking strength, making it far superior to any knot for creating a permanent loop.

The short splice (joining two rope ends): Unlay 15-20 cm of each rope end. Marry the two ends together so that the strands of one rope alternate with the strands of the other. Tuck each strand over and under the strands of the opposing rope for at least three full rounds. A short splice retains approximately 85% of breaking strength but doubles the rope diameter at the splice point, making it unsuitable for rope that must pass through a pulley.

The back splice (preventing unraveling): Unlay 10 cm of the rope end. Form a crown knot by passing each strand over its neighbor and tucking it under the next. Then tuck each strand back into the standing part for three rounds, as in a short splice. This permanently prevents the rope end from unraveling without reducing working diameter.

Knot strength reduction table:

Knot/Bend Retained Strength Primary Use
Eye splice 90-95% Permanent loops
Short splice 85% Joining rope ends
Bowline 60-75% Non-slip end loop
Figure-eight 75-80% Stopper, end loop
Clove hitch 60-65% Binding to post or beam
Sheet bend 55-60% Joining ropes of unequal size
Overhand knot 45-50% Stopper (avoid under load)

Any knot weakens a rope by creating a sharp bend where fibers on the outside of the curve bear disproportionate load. Splices are always preferred over knots for permanent connections.

Three-Strand Rope Laying

Graduating from two-ply cordage to three-strand rope requires coordination between multiple people and a longer working space.

The concept of the rope walk: three people each twist a single strand simultaneously using a hand crank or simple spinning hook. A fourth person, the "top man," stands at the opposite end holding a conical wooden separator (the "top") with three grooves. As the three strand-twisters apply Z-twist to their individual strands, the top man allows the strands to come together, applying S-twist as they converge. The opposing twist directions lock the three strands into a stable hawser-laid rope.

Makeshift rope walk: Drive a sturdy post into the ground and attach three iron hooks (bent nails, or three-pronged wooden forks) to a rotating handle. Tie one end of each strand to a hook. Three people walk backward, each paying out and twisting a single strand. The person at the post controls the rate of lay by adjusting how quickly they allow the handle to rotate. A minimum clear lane of 50 meters is needed for practical rope production; historical rope walks were 300+ meters long to produce ship-length cordage.

Lay length: Approximately 3 turns per 30 cm (per foot) produces an optimal balance of flexibility and strength for general-purpose hawser-laid rope. Too few turns per unit length and the rope is loose, spongy, and weak. Too many turns and the rope becomes stiff, prone to kinking, and difficult to splice. You can check lay tightness by feel: a properly laid rope should have a firm, round cross-section with no gaps between strands, but should still flex smoothly around a post without kinking.

Diameter guidelines for common tasks:

  • Light lashing, fishing line, snares: 2-4 mm two-ply cordage
  • General tying, bundling, net making: 5-8 mm two-ply or light three-strand
  • Structural lashing, lifting moderate loads: 10-16 mm three-strand rope
  • Heavy hoisting, bridge cables, anchor lines: 20-30 mm three-strand or cable-laid

The following ASCII diagram shows the structural principle of three-strand Z-laid rope, viewed from the end:


        Strand A (Z-twist)
           \
            \
   Strand C--+--Strand B
  (Z-twist)  |  (Z-twist)
              |
       [S-laid together]

  Fiber (Z) -> Yarn (S) -> Strand (Z) -> Rope (S)
  Each level reverses the twist direction of the level below.

Rope Care and Storage

Proper care dramatically extends the service life of natural cordage.

Coiling: Always coil rope in the direction of its lay to avoid introducing unwanted twists. For storage, the Flemish coil (a flat spiral laid on a surface) keeps rope organized and tangle-free. For transport, coil rope into a standard clockwise coil and secure with a gasket hitch.

Drying: Never store rope wet. Hang it in loose coils in a ventilated, shaded area until completely dry. Residual moisture promotes bacterial and fungal growth that weakens fibers from the inside, often invisibly.

Avoiding sharp bends: The minimum bend radius for natural fiber rope is 8 times the rope diameter. A 12mm rope should never be bent around a pin, thimble, or pulley smaller than 96mm in diameter. Sharp bends concentrate stress on the outer fibers, dramatically reducing service life.

Inspection: Before each use under significant load, run the full length of the rope through your hands. Feel for stiff sections (UV damage), soft spots (internal rot), or visible broken surface fibers (abrasion damage). Flex the rope sharply and look for dust falling from the interior, a sign of advanced fiber degradation. Unlay a short section and inspect the interior fibers: they should be flexible and consistent in color. Dark, brittle, or powdery interior fibers indicate advanced degradation invisible from outside. Retire any rope that shows these signs from load-bearing service; it may still serve for non-critical lashing.

Service life expectations: Natural fiber rope under regular outdoor use has a service life of 1-3 years depending on fiber type, UV exposure, and moisture conditions. Rope kept indoors and dry can last 5-10 years. Maintain a rope log for critical applications, recording date of manufacture, material, and diameter. Replace rope on schedule rather than waiting for visible failure.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Cord untwists immediately.

Cause: You twisted the individual strands and wrapped them together in the same direction. Both torques add rather than oppose, so the cord has no locking mechanism.

Prevention: Always twist individual strands away from your body and wrap them together toward your body (or vice versa). The wrap direction must be opposite the twist direction.

  1. Weak splice point.

Cause: Insufficient overlap between old and new fibers, or two splices aligned at the same cross-section of the rope.

Prevention: Maintain at least 5 cm overlap on every splice. Always stagger splices so that no two strand joins occur within 10 cm of each other.

  1. Rope snaps under expected load.

Cause: UV degradation, internal rot, or abrasion damage has weakened the rope below its original rated strength. Natural rope can lose 50% or more of its strength from environmental exposure while appearing intact on the surface.

Prevention: Inspect before every loaded use. Store out of sunlight when not in service. Apply appropriate safety factors. Replace rope on a schedule based on usage intensity.

  1. Fiber exhaustion.

Cause: Over-harvesting a single patch of cordage plants without allowing recovery time.

Prevention: Rotate harvesting across multiple patches. For herbaceous plants, harvest no more than one-third of any patch in a single season. Actively cultivate cordage plants (hemp, nettle, dogbane) as a crop rather than relying solely on wild harvest.

  1. Uneven diameter.

Cause: Inconsistent fiber feeding during the twist, with some sections receiving more material than others.

Prevention: Prepare fiber bundles of uniform thickness before beginning to twist. Feed new fibers in at a consistent rate. Periodically measure diameter with a simple gauge (a drilled block of wood) during production.

  1. Mildew and rot.

Cause: Rope stored wet or in a humid, unventilated location.

Prevention: Always dry rope completely before storage. Store in a dry, ventilated area out of direct ground contact. If rope becomes saturated during use, hang it to dry immediately afterward. Avoid storage in basements, cellars, or any space prone to condensation.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Bast fiber: The strong, flexible inner-bark fiber of dicotyledonous plants, the primary material for high-quality cordage.
  • Retting: Controlled decomposition of plant tissue to release bast fibers from the woody core, accomplished by water immersion or dew exposure.
  • Hackling: Drawing fibers through progressively finer combs to separate, align, and clean them before spinning or twisting.
  • Reverse wrap: The fundamental cordage technique in which individual strands are twisted in one direction and wrapped together in the opposite direction, creating a self-locking structure.
  • S-twist: A left-hand twist in which the helix spirals upward to the left (matching the center stroke of the letter S).
  • Z-twist: A right-hand twist in which the helix spirals upward to the right (matching the center stroke of the letter Z).
  • Lay: The direction and tightness with which strands are twisted together to form rope; also, the act of combining strands into finished rope.
  • Strand: An intermediate assembly of twisted yarns that forms one of the main structural elements of a rope.
  • Yarn: A thin assembly of twisted fibers, the smallest structural unit above individual fibers in rope construction.
  • Hawser-laid: Three-strand rope construction, the most common and versatile form of traditional rope.
  • Splice: A method of joining rope by interweaving the component strands, stronger and more permanent than any knot.
  • Eye splice: A splice forming a permanent loop at the end of a rope, retaining 90-95% of breaking strength.
  • Bight: A curve or loop in a rope, particularly the U-shaped section between the two ends. Standing in the bight of a loaded rope is extremely dangerous.
  • Working load: The maximum force that should be routinely applied to a rope, calculated by dividing breaking strength by the appropriate safety factor.
  • Rope walk: A long, narrow lane (50-350 meters) used for twisting and laying multi-strand rope, historically a standard feature of every naval yard and port.
  • Breaking strength: The force at which a rope catastrophically fails under tension, always significantly higher than the working load.
