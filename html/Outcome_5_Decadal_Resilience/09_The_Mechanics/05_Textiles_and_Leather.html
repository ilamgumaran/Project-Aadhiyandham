Alignment: Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

Clothing is survival infrastructure, not comfort. In temperate climates, an unclothed human loses core body temperature to lethal levels within 2-4 hours at 5 degrees Celsius with moderate wind. Wet skin accelerates this timeline dramatically through evaporative cooling. Modern synthetic fabrics such as polyester and nylon degrade under UV radiation and mechanical abrasion within 3-7 years of daily outdoor use. Cotton garments, even well-made ones, last only 1-3 years under the demands of heavy agricultural and construction labor.

Within a single decade of settlement, the community must produce its own textiles from locally sourced fiber or face death from exposure. There is no workaround, no substitute, and no amount of scavenging that postpones this requirement indefinitely.

Textile production is the most labor-intensive manufacturing process in pre-industrial economies. Spinning alone consumed an estimated 5-10 hours per meter of finished cloth in medieval Europe. Historical records consistently show that 4-5 spinners were needed to keep a single weaver supplied with yarn.

This staggering labor demand is precisely why the spinning wheel and loom were among the first machines automated by water power during the medieval period, and why this module connects directly to Module 02 (Mechanical Power). A settlement that does not mechanize spinning within the first five years will find textile production consuming a crippling share of its total labor budget.

Leather provides what textiles cannot: abrasion resistance for gloves, boots, and aprons; water resistance for bags, covers, and weatherproofing; and structural rigidity for harnesses, belts, drive belts, and machinery components. No woven fabric matches leather for durability under mechanical stress.

Together, textiles and leather form the two pillars of material self-sufficiency that clothe and equip the community indefinitely. Neither is optional, and both require distinct raw materials, skills, and processing chains that must be established in the first years of settlement.

> CAUTION: Lye solutions used in fiber processing and hide dehairing cause severe chemical burns on contact with skin and can cause permanent blindness if splashed in the eyes. Retting produces foul-smelling anaerobic gases including hydrogen sulfide. Brain tanning involves handling raw animal tissue that may harbor parasites and pathogens. Always wear leather gloves and eye protection when handling lye. Keep vinegar (an acid neutralizer) accessible at all times. Work in well-ventilated areas. Wash hands thoroughly after handling raw hides.

Theoretical Foundation

The Science of Natural Fibers

Plant fibers, known as bast fibers, include flax, hemp, nettle, and ramie. These are cellulose-based fibers extracted from the phloem (inner bark) of plant stems. Cellulose is a linear polymer of glucose units bonded in long chains, and it possesses a tensile strength of 400-800 MPa, which is comparable to structural steel on a per-cross-section basis.

Linen, derived from flax, is the strongest common plant fiber and has been cultivated for textile use for at least 8,000 years. Hemp fiber is coarser but grows faster and in poorer soils, making it an excellent secondary crop. Nettle fiber, often overlooked, produces a fine, strong thread and grows wild across most temperate regions, providing a zero-cultivation backup source.

Animal fibers include wool, mohair, alpaca, and silk. These are protein-based: wool and mohair are composed of keratin (the same protein in human hair and fingernails), while silk is composed of fibroin.

Wool possesses a unique and critical property: the microscopic scales on each fiber interlock when subjected to heat, moisture, and agitation. This process, called felting, creates a dense, wind-resistant, water-resistant fabric without any spinning or weaving at all. Felting is the fastest path from raw fiber to functional textile, and settlements should exploit it for hats, boot liners, blankets, and insulation while building weaving capacity.

Wool retains approximately 80% of its insulating value when wet, compared to cotton which loses roughly 90% of its insulating capacity. The crimped structure of wool fibers traps air pockets that constitute approximately 80% of a wool fabric's total volume.

These trapped air pockets are the actual insulating medium, not the fiber itself. This is why wool has been the primary cold-weather textile for over 10,000 years across every northern culture, and why it must be the settlement's priority animal fiber.

Cotton requires a long, hot growing season of 150-200 frost-free days and significant water inputs. It is unlikely to be viable for most temperate settlements. The modern world's dependence on cotton is an industrial-era anomaly driven by plantation economics and mechanical ginning. Pre-industrial northern populations used wool and linen exclusively. The settlement should not waste time or land attempting cotton cultivation unless located in a subtropical climate.

The Chemistry of Leather Tanning

Raw animal hide is composed primarily of collagen, a structural protein arranged in a triple-helix configuration, embedded in a water-containing matrix of fats, blood vessels, and cellular material. Untreated, aerobic and anaerobic bacteria decompose the collagen within days at room temperature, producing a putrid, unusable mass.

The purpose of tanning is to cross-link the collagen fibers, rendering them resistant to microbial decomposition, water absorption, and thermal denaturation. Three tanning methods are relevant to the settlement:

  • Vegetable tanning uses tannin molecules, which are polyphenolic compounds extracted from oak bark, sumac leaves, chestnut wood, or hemlock bark. These tannin molecules form hydrogen bonds with the collagen fibers, stabilizing the protein matrix against decomposition. Vegetable tanning takes 1-12 months depending on hide thickness and tannin concentration, but it produces the most durable, rigid leather. This is the method for boot soles, belts, harnesses, and structural applications.
  • Brain tanning uses emulsified lipids from animal brains, which penetrate the collagen fiber matrix and coat individual fibers. The tanned hide is then finished by smoking over smoldering punk wood, which introduces formaldehyde-type aldehydes that permanently cross-link the collagen through covalent bonding. This aldehyde cross-linking is what makes the tanning permanent: without smoking, brain-tanned leather will stiffen and lose its suppleness when wetted and dried. Brain tanning produces soft, flexible "buckskin" ideal for clothing, gloves, and moccasins.
  • Alum tanning uses aluminum salts (potassium aluminum sulfate) to cross-link collagen quickly, often in a matter of days. It produces stiff, white leather known as tawed leather. Alum-tanned leather is less durable than vegetable-tanned and less flexible than brain-tanned, but its speed makes it useful for items needed urgently or for lining and light-duty applications.

Each method has distinct advantages, and a settlement should practice all three to match tanning technique to end use.

The historical scale of these processes should not be underestimated. The Roman leather industry supplied sandals, body armor, tents, water skins, and equipment for over 400,000 soldiers simultaneously. Medieval European towns had entire quarters dedicated to tanning, invariably located downwind and downstream due to the overwhelming smell of retting hides and liming vats.

The processes described in this module are not primitive crafts; they are the same techniques that scaled to civilizational output for millennia.

The Economics of Pre-Industrial Textile Production

Medieval English cloth production records provide reliable quantitative data. Spinning 1 kg of wool into yarn required 40-60 hours of labor with a drop spindle, or 10-15 hours with a spinning wheel. Weaving that yarn into finished cloth on a floor loom required an additional 8-12 hours.

These figures represent skilled labor; beginners should expect rates 2-3 times slower during the first year of practice.

The labor ratio between spinning and weaving is the central fact of pre-industrial textile economics: 4-5 spinners were required to keep a single weaver continuously supplied with yarn. Spinning was always the bottleneck. The great wheel, the treadle wheel, and later the spinning jenny were all invented specifically to address this constraint.

Annual cloth requirement per person is approximately 5-8 square meters of fabric for basic clothing consisting of 2-3 garments replaced annually, plus contributions to bedding and repair. For a community of 50 people, this translates to 250-400 square meters per year.

At drop-spindle production rates, manufacturing 400 square meters of cloth requires approximately 20,000-30,000 person-hours per year, equivalent to nearly 15 full-time workers doing nothing but spinning and weaving. This is why mechanization through water-powered spinning and weaving frames is designated a high priority in Module 02.

Core Principles

  1. The Fiber Sovereignty Principle -- The community must establish at least two fiber sources within Year 1: one plant-based (flax or hemp) and one animal-based (sheep wool or equivalent). Dependence on a single fiber source is fragile. Disease can devastate a flock in weeks. Drought can fail a flax crop entirely. Maintaining two independent fiber chains ensures that the loss of one does not halt textile production.
  2. The Spinning Bottleneck Principle -- Spinning is the rate-limiting step in textile production, and every strategy must address this constraint. Every community member who can hold a drop spindle should spin during idle moments: council meetings, storytelling sessions, nightwatch duty, convalescence. Mechanizing spinning through a water-powered spinning frame should be a priority as soon as the line-shaft system described in Module 02 is operational.
  3. The Leather-First Principle -- Leather production should begin immediately, from Day 1, using the hide of any animal processed for meat. A single deer hide, brain-tanned and smoked, produces enough material for one pair of boots or two pairs of work gloves. A cow hide yields enough for several pairs of boots, a full apron, and multiple belts. Never waste a hide. The community cannot afford to discard material that takes months to replace.
  4. The Seasonal Integration Principle -- Textile production is inherently seasonal and must be integrated with the agricultural calendar. Plant fiber crops are sown in spring and harvested in late summer. Retting occurs in autumn when water temperatures are still warm enough for bacterial action. Fiber processing and spinning occupy the long winter months when outdoor agricultural work is impossible. Weaving resumes in spring as daylight extends. This calendar aligns textile labor with agricultural downtime, minimizing conflict between the two systems.
  5. The Durability Hierarchy Principle -- Invest the most skilled labor and the finest materials in items that receive the most mechanical wear: boots, work gloves, tool belts, pack straps, and harness components. Undergarments and bedding can tolerate coarser weave and lower thread counts. Match material quality to functional demand. A pair of boots made from poorly tanned leather fails in weeks; the same hide, properly vegetable-tanned, lasts years.

Practical Implementation

Fiber Processing: Flax to Linen

Harvest: Pull flax plants by the roots (do not cut them, as cutting wastes fiber length) when the stems turn yellow-brown, approximately 90-120 days after spring planting. Bundle into sheaves of approximately 20-30 stems and stand upright to dry for 1-2 weeks.

Expected yield: approximately 150-250 kg of dried stems per hectare, depending on soil quality and rainfall. A community of 50 people will need approximately 2-4 hectares of flax to meet basic linen requirements, assuming supplemental wool production is also established.

Retting: Submerge bundled stems in slow-moving or still water for 7-14 days. Weight the bundles with stones to keep them fully submerged. Dew retting on grass is an alternative method taking 2-4 weeks, where bundles are spread on dewy grass and turned every 2-3 days.

During water retting, anaerobic bacteria dissolve the pectin binding the bast fibers to the woody core. Retting is complete when the outer bark separates easily when rubbed between the fingers. Daily checking from Day 7 is essential to prevent over-retting.

Breaking: Use a flax brake, which consists of heavy hinged wooden bars that crush the stems when the upper jaw is brought down repeatedly. Work in batches of 10-20 stems. Strike each bundle 8-12 times, rotating the stems between strikes.

The woody core (called shive) shatters into small fragments while the flexible bast fibers survive intact due to their superior tensile strength.

Scutching: Hold a handful of broken stems vertically against a wooden board and beat them with a wooden scutching knife, a flat paddle-shaped tool approximately 40 cm long. This action knocks away all remaining broken shive fragments, leaving only clean bast fibers.

Expected yield: approximately 15-20% of original stem weight remains as usable fiber. Collect the tow (short fibers and shive fragments) separately for coarse use. Do not discard tow; it has value as stuffing, tinder, caulking material, and rough cordage.

Hackling: Draw the scutched fibers through progressively finer hackle combs. A coarse hackle has approximately 2 nails per centimeter, a medium hackle 4 per centimeter, and a fine hackle 6 per centimeter.

Hackling aligns fibers parallel to each other and separates quality grades. Long fibers that pass through all combs are "line" fiber, suitable for fine thread. Short fibers that are combed out are "tow," used for coarse cord, stuffing, caulking, or tinder. A well-processed hectare of flax yields approximately 25-40 kg of line fiber, enough for roughly 50-80 square meters of linen cloth.

Fiber Processing: Wool

Shearing: Sheep are shorn once per year in late spring, after the last frost risk has passed. A single adult sheep yields 2-5 kg of raw fleece (greasy wool) depending on breed. The fleece is removed in one piece by cutting close to the skin with hand shears or spring shears.

Sorting and skirting: Spread the fleece on a clean surface and remove the edges (skirting), which contain the dirtiest, most matted wool.

Sort the remaining fleece by quality: the finest, longest fibers come from the shoulders and sides; coarser, shorter fibers come from the belly and legs. Fine fiber is reserved for clothing; coarse fiber is used for blankets, rugs, or felting.

Washing (scouring): Raw wool contains 30-50% of its weight in lanolin (wool grease), dirt, and sweat. Soak the sorted wool in warm water (not hot, which causes premature felting) with a small amount of wood-ash lye or soap.

Gently agitate, drain, and repeat 2-3 times until the water runs mostly clear. Spread the clean wool on a rack to dry thoroughly before carding. Save the lanolin-rich wash water; lanolin can be skimmed off and used as a waterproofing agent for finished wool garments.

Carding: Pull tufts of clean wool between two carding paddles (flat boards studded with bent wire teeth or teasel heads). This aligns the fibers into a loose, fluffy roll called a rolag, ready for spinning. Carding is faster than hackling and produces a softer, loftier yarn than hackled fiber.

Spinning: Drop Spindle to Spinning Wheel

The drop spindle is the simplest spinning tool and requires no infrastructure to build. Carve a straight shaft from hardwood, 25-30 cm long. Fashion a whorl (a disc or sphere) 7-10 cm in diameter and fix it near the bottom of the shaft. Total weight should be 30-60 g. Heavier spindles produce thicker yarn; lighter ones produce finer thread.

Tie a leader string around the shaft above the whorl, loop it over the top notch, and attach drafted fibers to the leader. Give the spindle a sharp clockwise spin and let it hang freely. The twist travels up through the leader into the drafted fibers, binding them into yarn. When the spindle nears the floor, stop, wind the new yarn onto the shaft, re-hook, and continue.

Spinning rate: A beginner can expect to produce approximately 10-20 meters of yarn per hour. An experienced spinner achieves 40-80 meters per hour on a drop spindle. These rates underscore why spinning is the bottleneck: producing enough warp yarn for a single meter of 60 cm wide cloth requires approximately 500-700 meters of thread.

The great wheel (walking wheel) is a larger, hand-driven wheel that spins the spindle via a belt drive. The large wheel (60-90 cm diameter) drives a small spindle whorl through a cord or leather belt, multiplying rotational speed. It is simpler to construct than a treadle wheel and serves as an effective intermediate step. The spinner walks backward while drafting fiber, then reverses to wind on. Production rate: approximately 2-3 times faster than a drop spindle.

The treadle spinning wheel adds a foot-powered treadle and crankshaft that drives the wheel, plus a flyer mechanism that allows continuous spinning without stopping to wind on. The flyer rotates faster than the bobbin, automatically winding yarn as it is spun. This increases production 3-5 times over a drop spindle.

Construction requires competent woodworking skills, a leather or cord drive belt, and metal hooks for the flyer. Building a treadle wheel should be a priority once basic woodworking and metalworking capacity is established.

Weaving: The Floor Loom

A floor loom for producing 60 cm wide cloth requires a frame approximately 1.0 meters wide by 1.5 meters deep, constructed from heavy hardwood to resist the tension of hundreds of warp threads.

A minimum of 2 harnesses produces plain weave (the simplest over-under pattern). Adding a third and fourth harness enables twill weave patterns, which produce denser, more durable, and more water-resistant fabric. Twill is the preferred weave for work clothing and outerwear due to its superior wind resistance and drape.

Warp setup: Calculate the total warp thread count by multiplying the desired ends per centimeter by the fabric width. For sturdy linen cloth, 8-12 ends per centimeter is standard. For a 60 cm width at 10 ends per centimeter, the loom requires 600 individual warp threads.

Each warp thread must be cut to the desired cloth length plus approximately 50 cm for loom waste and tie-on. Thread each warp end through a heddle on the appropriate harness, then through a dent in the reed (the beater comb that spaces the warp threads evenly).

Warping the loom is the most tedious and error-prone step in the entire process; expect 4-8 hours for a full warp setup. A single crossed or skipped thread can create a visible defect in the finished cloth, so careful attention during warping saves time during weaving.

Weaving sequence: Press a foot treadle to raise one harness, creating the shed (the V-shaped opening between raised and lowered warp threads). Pass the shuttle carrying the weft thread through the shed from one side to the other. Pull the beater forward firmly to pack the new weft thread tightly against the growing cloth face.

Release the treadle, press the opposite one to reverse which threads are raised, and pass the shuttle back. Each complete cycle adds one weft row. Maintain consistent tension and beat force for even cloth.

Weaving rate: An experienced weaver on a properly set-up floor loom can produce approximately 1-2 meters of cloth per day, depending on thread count and fabric width. A narrow warp (30 cm) for belting or straps can be woven faster. Wide warps for blankets (over 1 meter) require a fly shuttle or a second person to catch and return the shuttle.

Felting: Fabric Without a Loom

Felting produces dense, warm, wind-resistant fabric from wool without any spinning or weaving. It is the fastest method to convert raw fiber into functional textile and should be used extensively while loom capacity is being established.

Wet felting process: Lay out clean, carded wool in thin, overlapping layers on a flat surface, alternating the fiber direction 90 degrees between layers. Build up 3-5 layers. Sprinkle with hot soapy water (wood-ash soap dissolved in water at approximately 60 degrees Celsius).

Press and roll the wool firmly and repeatedly, adding more hot water as needed. The combination of heat, moisture, alkalinity, and mechanical agitation causes the keratin scales on the wool fibers to interlock irreversibly. Continue rolling and pressing for 30-60 minutes until the fabric is dense, uniform, and does not pull apart. Rinse in cold water and press flat to dry.

Applications: Felted wool is ideal for hats, boot insoles, blanket liners, saddle pads, insulation panels, and gaskets. A thick felt panel (1-2 cm) provides thermal insulation comparable to modern synthetic equivalents.

Leather Tanning: Brain Tanning Protocol

Step 1 -- Fleshing: Stretch the fresh hide over a fleshing beam (a smooth, debarked log set at an angle) or lace it into a wooden frame with cordage threaded through holes punched around the perimeter.

Using a dull blade, bone scraper, or the back of a drawknife, scrape off all fat, membrane, blood vessels, and adhering flesh from the underside. Work from the center outward in overlapping strokes. The hide should be uniformly thin and translucent, free of any tissue that would block tanning solution penetration.

Step 2 -- Dehairing: Prepare a wood ash lye solution by leaching hardwood ash through water to achieve a pH of 12-13. Submerge the fleshed hide in this solution for 3-7 days, stirring daily. The alkaline solution dissolves the hair roots and swells the collagen matrix.

When the hair pulls out easily with gentle finger pressure, remove the hide and scrape off all loosened hair with a dull blade. Rinse the hide thoroughly in clean water to remove residual lye before proceeding.

Step 3 -- Brain solution: Boil one animal brain (or an equivalent volume of rendered fat mixed with egg yolks) in 2-3 liters of water. Mash thoroughly until fully emulsified into a smooth, creamy liquid with no lumps.

A useful rule of thumb: every animal has enough brain to tan its own hide. Larger hides from cattle may require supplementing with additional fat or eggs. Allow the solution to cool until warm but not hot to the touch.

Step 4 -- Working: Soak the dehaired hide in the warm brain solution for 24 hours, kneading it periodically to ensure full penetration of the lipid emulsion into the collagen matrix. Remove and wring thoroughly by twisting around a post or stout branch.

Then begin working the hide: stretch, pull, twist, and rub it over a staking post or taut cable continuously as it dries. This is the critical step. If the hide dries without being continuously worked, the collagen fibers bond to each other and the leather becomes stiff and board-like. Working keeps the fibers separated and individually coated with the lipid emulsion.

Expect 4-8 hours of continuous effort for a deer-sized hide. Multiple workers can share the task in shifts, but the hide must never be left to dry unattended.

Step 5 -- Smoking: Sew the soft, worked hide into a bag or cone shape and suspend it over a smoldering fire of punk wood (rotten, moisture-saturated wood that smolders without producing flame). Smoke for 4-8 hours per side, rotating periodically for even coverage. The dense, cool smoke introduces aldehyde compounds that permanently cross-link the collagen fibers through covalent bonds.

After smoking, the hide can be wetted and dried repeatedly without stiffening. The color changes from pale white to golden-brown, indicating successful aldehyde penetration.

Result: Soft, golden-brown buckskin suitable for clothing, moccasins, gloves, bags, and light-duty applications. One deer hide yields approximately 0.5-0.8 square meters of usable buckskin.

Vegetable Tanning Protocol

Vegetable tanning produces heavy, rigid leather suitable for boot soles, belts, harnesses, drive belts, knife sheaths, and structural components where stiffness and abrasion resistance are more important than flexibility.

Prepare a tanning liquor by soaking crushed oak bark (or sumac, chestnut, or hemlock bark) in water for 2-4 weeks. The water will turn deep brown as tannins leach out. Begin with a weak solution and prepare several vats of increasing concentration.

Submerge the fleshed and dehaired hide in the weakest tanning solution first. Over the following weeks and months, transfer the hide to progressively stronger tanning liquors as the outer layers absorb tannin and the solution must penetrate deeper into the hide.

Duration varies significantly by hide thickness and intended use: 1-6 months for light leather suitable for bags, book covers, and light strapping; 6-12 months for heavy sole leather that must resist years of ground abrasion and flexing. The tanning is complete when a cut cross-section shows uniform brown color throughout with no pale center remaining.

The finished leather is firm, brown, and possesses excellent resistance to both abrasion and water penetration. It can be further treated with tallow, beeswax, or a mixture of both (dubbed) to improve water resistance and flexibility.

Vegetable-tanned leather improves with age, developing a rich patina, and can last decades under heavy use when properly maintained. It is the preferred material for any component subject to sustained mechanical stress.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Over-Retting -- Flax left too long in water becomes weak and brittle as bacteria attack the bast fibers themselves rather than just the pectin binding them.

Prevention: check daily from Day 7 onward. Pull a test stem and rub it between your fingers. If the bark separates easily but the fiber is still strong and flexible, retting is complete. If the fiber feels mushy or breaks easily, the batch has been over-retted and fiber quality is compromised.

  1. Under-Spinning -- Yarn that is not twisted tightly enough will fall apart under the tension of the loom warp.

Prevention: test yarn strength by pulling firmly between both hands. Properly spun yarn should stretch slightly under tension before breaking. If it simply unravels or separates into individual fibers, increase twist by adding more spindle rotations per draft length. Warp yarn must always be stronger than weft yarn.

  1. Brain-Tan Stiffness -- The hide was not worked continuously during drying, causing the collagen fibers to bond to each other and produce board-stiff leather. Once fully stiffened, the hide cannot be recovered without re-soaking in brain solution and re-working from scratch.

Prevention: begin working the hide the moment it starts to feel dry on the surface and continue without interruption until it is completely dry throughout. This may require 4-8 hours of sustained physical effort. Assign shifts among multiple workers.

  1. Smoke Taint -- The hide was smoked over open flame or hot-burning wood rather than smoldering punk wood. This produces soot deposits that blacken and weaken the leather, and excessive heat that can partially cook and shrink the collagen.

Prevention: use only thoroughly rotten punk wood that smolders without flame. The smoke should be dense and cool. If you can hold your hand in the smoke stream comfortably, the temperature is correct.

  1. Warp Thread Breakage -- Weak or unevenly spun warp threads snap during weaving, requiring the loom to be stopped and the broken thread repaired by knotting and re-threading, which is time-consuming and weakens the finished cloth at that point.

Prevention: use only the strongest, most evenly spun yarn for warp threads. Test every warp thread by suspending a 500 g weight from it. Any thread that breaks under this load is not suitable for warp use; downgrade it to weft or cordage.

  1. Lye Burns -- Chemical burns from handling concentrated wood-ash lye solution during fiber processing or hide dehairing. Lye burns may not be immediately painful but cause deep tissue damage that becomes apparent hours later.

Prevention: always wear leather gloves and eye protection when handling lye solutions. Keep a container of vinegar (acid neutralizer) within arm's reach at all times. If lye contacts skin, flush immediately with vinegar, then rinse with large quantities of clean water. If lye contacts eyes, flush with clean water for a minimum of 15 minutes and seek medical attention.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Bast fiber -- plant fiber extracted from the phloem (inner bark) of stems; sources include flax, hemp, nettle, and ramie
  • Retting -- controlled microbial decomposition that separates bast fibers from the woody stem core by dissolving the pectin binding them
  • Hackling -- combing fibers through progressively finer nail-beds to align them parallel and separate quality grades
  • Carding -- pulling wool fibers between toothed paddles to align them into a soft rolag ready for spinning
  • Warp -- the longitudinal threads held under tension on the loom frame; these must be the strongest yarn in the fabric
  • Weft -- the lateral threads woven through the warp, carried back and forth by the shuttle
  • Shed -- the V-shaped opening between raised and lowered warp threads through which the shuttle passes
  • Tannin -- polyphenolic compounds extracted from tree bark that cross-link collagen in animal hides to produce leather
  • Collagen -- the structural protein composing the bulk of animal hide; tanning stabilizes collagen against microbial decomposition
  • Fleshing -- removing fat, membrane, blood vessels, and adhering flesh from the underside of a raw hide before tanning
  • Buckskin -- soft, golden-brown, smoke-finished brain-tanned leather suitable for clothing and light-duty applications
  • Saponification -- chemical reaction between fat and alkali producing soap; related to the lipid chemistry employed in brain tanning
  • Drop spindle -- the simplest spinning tool: a weighted shaft that converts loose fiber into yarn through the application of twist
  • Treadle wheel -- a foot-powered spinning wheel with a flyer mechanism enabling continuous spinning; 3-5 times faster than a drop spindle
  • Felting -- the matting of wool fibers through the combined action of heat, moisture, and agitation; creates fabric without spinning or weaving
  • Tow -- short, broken fibers separated out during hackling; used for coarse cordage, caulking, or stuffing
