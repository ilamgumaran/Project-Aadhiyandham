Alignment: Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

> Caution — light is fire indoors: Every lamp and candle is an open flame the settlement carries into a combustible building, so all of the hearth-safety discipline applies to it — an unattended candle has burned down as many homes as any chimney. And light has a second, subtler cost: the wrong light at night wrecks the sleep the settlement's health depends on. Made and used well, though, light is what extends the useful day, lets fine work and reading happen after dark, and turns a shelter from a place to sleep into a place to live.

1. Why This Is Important

When the grid dies, the night gets very long. Without made light, useful activity collapses to daylight hours — no reading, no fine craft, no gathering, no archive work, no comfort after dusk through a long winter when daylight is short. Light is one of the quiet foundations of a civilized life rather than a merely surviving one, which is why every settled culture invested in making it. The settlement can make good light from materials it already produces — animal fat, plant oil, and beeswax — with simple technology, and this module is how.

It also sits at a real tension the manual must resolve honestly: the circadian-health module calls for dim, warm, low light after dark to protect sleep — and the flame-based light this module makes is, conveniently, exactly that: warm, low-intensity, low to the ground. Made light and healthy light are, for a low-tech settlement, the same thing — as long as the flame stays dim and is put out before sleep.

2. Expected Outcome

A reliable supply of made light matched to need: quick rushlights for everyday moving-about, oil lamps for steady work light, and candles for portable and stored light — all from local fat, oil, and wax, used with the fire-safety and circadian disciplines that keep light a benefit.

3. The Fuels

Light comes from burning a fat, oil, or wax drawn up a wick; the fuel determines the method.

  • Animal fat (tallow) and plant oil — the everyday fuels, byproducts the settlement already has (livestock, small stock, oilseeds, fish oil). Cheap and abundant; tallow candles and oil lamps smoke and smell more than wax but are the workhorse.
  • Beeswax — the premium fuel from the hive: clean, bright, low-smoke, pleasant-smelling, and it holds its shape as a firm candle. Reserve it for the best candles.

4. The Forms of Light

4.1. Rushlights — the near-free everyday light

The cheapest light there is: strip most of the skin from a dried rush (a common wetland plant), leaving a strip to support the spongy pith, dip the pith in melted fat/tallow, and let it set. It burns like a slender taper held in a simple pinch-holder — minutes to an hour of soft light per rush. Rushlights lit the poor of the world for centuries because they cost only fat and a walk to the marsh; they are the settlement's default "just need to see" light.

4.2. Oil lamps — steady work light

The oldest lamp: a vessel of oil or melted fat with a fiber wick drawn up over the edge or through a spout; the wick draws fuel by capillary action and burns at the tip. From a stone or pottery saucer to a spouted lamp, this gives steady, adjustable, refillable light for as long as there is oil — ideal for a work table, and easily made warm and dim by keeping the flame small. A simple wick-in-a-jar of fat is the crudest version and works.

4.3. Candles — portable and stored light

Solid fuel around a wick, so the light is portable, storable, and needs no vessel of liquid.

  • Dipping: repeatedly dip a hanging wick into melted tallow or wax, letting each coat cool, until the candle builds to thickness. Simple, no mold, the traditional method.
  • Molding: pour melted fat/wax around a centered wick in a mold (metal, wood, or clay) — faster for making many uniform candles.
  • The wick matters: a plant-fiber wick, ideally lightly treated (traditionally soaked in a salt/borax-type solution) so it burns evenly and doesn't need constant trimming. A bad wick is why a candle gutters, smokes, and drowns in its own melt.

5. Using Light Well: Safety and Sleep

  • Fire safety first. Every flame is governed by the hearth-safety rules: stable non-tip holders, clearance from anything combustible (thatch, cloth, bedding, drying racks), never left burning unattended, and always extinguished before sleep. The candle knocked over or left burning by a sleeper is a classic house-fire origin.
  • Ventilation. Open-flame lamps consume oxygen and can produce soot and some carbon monoxide in a sealed room; the same "never seal all ventilation with a flame burning" rule applies — usually a non-issue with a small lamp in a normally-ventilated space, but worth knowing.
  • Sleep discipline — a hidden gift. Keep evening light dim, warm, and low — which flame-light naturally is — and it doubles as the circadian protection the health module prescribes: enough to see and work by, too dim and too warm-toned to wreck melatonin and sleep. Bright, blue-white light after dark is the enemy of sleep; the settlement's warm flames are, by happy accident, exactly right. Use the least light that does the job.

6. Common Failure Modes

  • Unattended flame. A lamp or candle left burning, especially at sleep. [Tier L] Consequence: house fire — one of the commonest ignition sources historically. Prevention: extinguish every flame before sleep and when leaving; stable holders; clearance from combustibles.
  • Burning the food fat for light carelessly. Fat and oil are also food (and the settlement needs dietary fat). [Tier C] Consequence: lighting competes with nutrition if fat is scarce. Prevention: budget fat between food and light; prefer rushlights (little fat) and reserve precious fat for eating in lean times.
  • Bright light at night. Using as much light as possible after dark. [Tier C] Consequence: disrupted sleep and the health costs that follow (the circadian module's whole concern). Prevention: dim, warm, low, and minimal after dark.
  • Bad wick. Untreated or wrong-size wick. [Tier C] Consequence: guttering, smoking, drowning flame, wasted fuel. Prevention: proper fiber wick, sized to the candle, lightly treated for an even burn.

7. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Dipping vs. molding: The two ways to make candles — repeated dipping of a wick, or pouring fuel into a mold.
  • Guttering: A flame burning unevenly and dripping, usually from a bad wick or a draft.
  • Rushlight: A dried rush pith dipped in fat; the cheapest everyday light.
  • Tallow: Rendered animal fat; the workhorse candle and lamp fuel.
  • Wick: The fiber that draws liquid fuel up to the flame by capillary action; its quality governs how cleanly a lamp or candle burns.

8. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

8.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: Render tallow / collect oil / reserve some beeswax; make or gather wick fiber; gather rushes.
  • Execution: Start with rushlights (fastest); make simple oil lamps for work light; dip or mold candles for portable/stored light; use warm dim light after dark and put every flame out before sleep.
  • Review: Track fat/oil use against the food budget; refine wicks for a clean burn.

8.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier

  • [Tier L — Law] Leave a flame burning unattended / at sleep → house fire. Extinguish before sleep; stable holders; clearance.
  • [Tier C — Craft] Fuel choice and how much light → balance fat-for-food against fat-for-light; keep night light dim and warm for sleep.

8.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: Candle- and lamp-making are simple, pleasant crafts worth teaching widely; the safety habit (out before sleep) and the sleep habit (dim and warm) matter more than the technique and should be universal.
