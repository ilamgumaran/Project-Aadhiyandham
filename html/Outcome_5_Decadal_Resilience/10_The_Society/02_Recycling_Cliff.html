Alignment: Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

Every post-collapse settlement inherits a decaying inventory of industrial materials. The tools in your hands, the roofing over your shelter, the wire in your salvaged electrical systems — all of it was manufactured in factories that no longer exist, using supply chains that have disintegrated. This inherited wealth is enormous, but it is finite and it is dying. From the moment industrial civilization ceased maintaining its output, the Second Law of Thermodynamics began its patient, irreversible work on every manufactured object within your reach.

Entropy is relentless and predictable. Steel rusts. Rubber cracks. Plastics embrittle and shatter. Concrete spalls. Lubricants oxidize. Coatings weather away. None of this happens overnight, but none of it stops, either. The rate of decay depends on climate, exposure, and material composition, but the direction is always the same: toward disorder, toward failure, toward loss. You can slow it. You cannot stop it. The mathematics of corrosion and polymer degradation give you a window — measured in years, not generations — before the material inheritance of the old world becomes useless. That window is your most precious and most wasted resource.

The transition from scavenging to manufacturing is the single most critical governance decision a settlement will face. Get the timing right and your community crosses a bridge from dependence on decaying relics to genuine material sovereignty. Get it wrong — start too late, invest too little, or succumb to the psychological comfort of scavenging — and your settlement falls off the recycling cliff into a regression that may take generations to reverse. This module gives you the theory, the tools, and the timeline to ensure you cross that bridge.

> CAUTION: Scavenging operations involve structural collapse risk in degraded buildings, chemical exposure from corroding batteries and industrial chemicals, and tetanus risk from rusted metal. Never scavenge alone. Wear eye protection, heavy gloves, and closed-toe boots. Test structural integrity before entering any building.

Theoretical Foundation

The Thermodynamics of Decay

The Second Law of Thermodynamics states that the total entropy of an isolated system always increases over time. Every manufactured object represents a temporary, low-entropy arrangement of matter — steel refined from ore, copper drawn into wire, polymers synthesized from petroleum. Without continuous energy input for maintenance and repair, these arrangements spontaneously revert toward higher-entropy states. This is not a tendency or a risk; it is a physical law.

The driving force behind corrosion is quantified by Gibbs free energy. When the change in Gibbs free energy (delta-G) for a reaction is negative, the reaction proceeds spontaneously. Iron rusting is strongly exothermic and spontaneous:

4Fe + 3O2 + 6H2O -> 4Fe(OH)3 (delta-G < 0)

The iron hydroxide product (rust) occupies roughly 6x the volume of the original iron, which is why rusting steel swells, cracks coatings, and spalls concrete from within. This reaction requires only iron, oxygen, and water — three things that are everywhere. You cannot remove them from the environment. You can only erect barriers between them.

Corrosion rates for carbon steel in temperate humid environments range from 0.1 to 0.5 mm/yr under normal atmospheric exposure. Salt spray accelerates this to 0.5-1.0 mm/yr. Galvanic coupling — where dissimilar metals are in electrical contact — can accelerate corrosion of the less noble metal by 5-10x. A steel bolt in an aluminum frame, or a copper pipe joined to iron, creates a galvanic cell that destroys the more reactive metal preferentially.

Polymer degradation follows a different but equally relentless path. Ultraviolet photolysis breaks carbon-carbon bonds in the polymer backbone. Polyethylene, polypropylene, and PVC all embrittle within 2-10 years of direct sun exposure, depending on UV stabilizer content and latitude. Rubber suffers ozone cracking — atmospheric ozone attacks the sulfur crosslinks that give rubber its elasticity, and tires, hoses, and gaskets degrade within 5-15 years of outdoor exposure regardless of mechanical use. Concrete, often assumed to be permanent, undergoes carbonation as atmospheric CO2 reacts with calcium hydroxide in the cement matrix. The carbonation front advances at approximately 1 mm/yr; when it reaches the rebar (typically at 20-40 mm depth), the steel reinforcement begins to corrode, and the concrete cracks from within.

The Cliff Curve

The "recycling cliff" is not a metaphor for linear degradation. It describes a specific nonlinear phenomenon: the point at which degradation accelerates catastrophically because protective barriers have failed.

Most industrial materials are protected by coatings or passivation layers. Galvanized steel is coated with a zinc layer 40-85 micrometers thick. Paint systems add 100-300 micrometers of polymer barrier. Aluminum forms a natural aluminum oxide passivation layer only 5-10 nanometers thick but extraordinarily durable. Stainless steel depends on a chromium oxide passivation layer. These barriers slow the underlying corrosion rate by factors of 10 to 100.

But the barriers themselves degrade. Zinc coating is consumed by corrosion at 1-3 micrometers/yr in rural atmospheres, faster in marine or industrial environments. Paint weathers under UV, cracks with thermal cycling, and is abraded by wind-borne particles. Once the protective layer is fully consumed or breached, the underlying base metal is exposed. Corrosion rate then jumps from the protected rate (0.01-0.05 mm/yr) to the unprotected rate (0.1-0.5 mm/yr or higher) — an increase of 10-50x. This transition from slow to rapid degradation is the cliff.

The cliff curve is best modeled as a sigmoid: a long plateau of slow degradation while coatings hold, followed by a steep drop as coatings fail and base-metal corrosion accelerates, then a terminal phase of total material loss. Different materials hit the cliff at different times:

Material Cliff Onset (years) Notes
Carbon steel (uncoated) 5-15 Fastest decline; tools and structural steel
Galvanized steel 15-30 Zinc coating thickness determines window
Aluminum 20-40 Passivation layer eventually fails in acidic/salt conditions
Copper 50-100 Patina is partially protective; very long window
Rubber (outdoor) 5-15 Ozone and UV; no protective layer
Plastics (UV-exposed) 2-10 Stabilizer depletion, then rapid embrittlement
Glass 1000+ Effectively permanent; prioritize salvage
Concrete (unreinforced) 50-100 Carbonation only; slow
Concrete (reinforced) 30-50 Rebar corrosion is the failure mode
Treated lumber 10-25 Preservative leaching, fungal attack

The strategic implication is clear: you do not have a single cliff. You have a cascade of cliffs, with plastics and rubber falling first, uncoated steel next, galvanized steel and aluminum later, and copper last. Your transition plan must account for this cascade.

The Scavenger's Dilemma

The economics of scavenging follow a predictable curve of diminishing returns. In the first years after collapse, the nearest buildings and vehicles yield abundant, high-quality materials with minimal effort. But each successive scavenging trip must travel farther, search harder, and accept lower-quality finds. Meanwhile, the buildings themselves become more dangerous as roofs sag, floors weaken, and chemical containers corrode.

This creates a game-theory problem analogous to the tragedy of the commons. The opportunity cost of scavenging rises continuously: every hour spent pulling copper wire from a collapsing building is an hour not spent constructing a forge, training an apprentice, or surveying ore deposits. But scavenging produces immediate, tangible results — you return with a bag of useful metal. Manufacturing investment produces nothing visible for months or years. The first usable bloom of iron from a new bloomery may take six months of construction and experimentation to achieve.

This cognitive bias — the preference for immediate tangible reward over delayed but greater benefit — is the scavenger's trap. It is the same bias that prevents people from saving for retirement in the old world. In the post-collapse context, it is potentially fatal. A settlement that delays its manufacturing transition by three years because scavenging still "works" may find itself crossing the cliff with no bridge on the other side. The scavenger's dilemma must be understood and explicitly countered through governance, planning, and community education.

Historical Precedent

Post-Roman Britain (5th-6th century CE) provides the most thoroughly documented example of a society living on inherited infrastructure it could use but not reproduce. When the Roman legions withdrew around 410 CE, the Romano-British population inherited roads, aqueducts, hypocaust heating systems, wheel-thrown pottery, glazed tiles, and high-quality steel tools. Within two generations, the archaeological record shows a stark regression. Pottery declined from professional wheel-thrown forms to crude hand-formed vessels. Metalwork shifted from newly smelted steel to reworked Roman iron — smiths were cutting up Roman objects to forge new ones because they had lost (or never possessed) smelting knowledge. By 500 CE, most Roman buildings were quarries, not habitations. The villa system collapsed not because the buildings fell down, but because no one could repair the roof tiles, replace the lead plumbing, or maintain the hypocaust flues.

The lesson is precise: using inherited infrastructure without understanding how to reproduce it leads to inevitable regression. The regression is not gradual — it follows a cliff curve as accumulated failures reach critical mass.

A more recent parallel exists in post-Soviet rural Russia during the 1990s. When centralized supply chains collapsed, rural communities dependent on factory-produced tools, fertilizers, and equipment experienced severe material shortages. However, communities that had maintained traditional workshop skills — blacksmithing, woodworking, basic metalworking — adapted within 2-3 years. Those entirely dependent on factory supply chains suffered a decade or more of material poverty. The variable was not resources but manufacturing knowledge.

Core Principles

The following five principles govern your approach to the recycling cliff. They are listed in order of priority.

  1. The Cliff Is Predictable. Corrosion rates are measurable. Coating thicknesses can be estimated. An annual materials audit makes degradation visible and quantifiable before it becomes a crisis. You do not need to be surprised by the cliff — you need to be watching for it.
  2. Manufacturing Before the Cliff. Your bloomery and forge must be operational at least 2 years before the first critical material reaches its cliff point. Two years provides margin for the learning curve, failed experiments, and seasonal interruptions that inevitably delay early manufacturing. If your uncoated carbon steel tools show 2 mm of pitting after 3 years, your cliff is approximately Year 8. Your forge must be producing replacement tools by Year 6.
  3. Prioritized Scavenging. Not all scavenged materials are equal. Harvest what you cannot manufacture first, and what degrades fastest second. Alloy steels (spring steel, tool steel) contain elements like vanadium, tungsten, and chromium that you cannot add from local ores. Copper requires smelting temperatures above 1085 degrees C — difficult without advanced furnaces. Zinc (from galvanized sheet) is essential for brass production. Glass is infinitely recyclable by remelting. Prioritize these over common mild steel, which a bloomery can eventually replace.
  4. Preservation Extends the Window. Every year you delay the cliff is a year gained for manufacturing development. Oil coating, charcoal paint, wax sealing, sheltered storage, and sacrificial anodes are cheap interventions that buy expensive time. A simple roof over your steel stockpile extends its useful life 3-5x compared to outdoor exposure.
  5. The Scavenger-to-Maker Transition. The settlement must deliberately shift its identity from a community of scavengers to a community of manufacturers. This is a cultural transition as much as a technical one. It requires governance decisions to allocate labor, dedicate resources, and accept short-term productivity losses for long-term survival. It cannot happen by accident — it must be planned, scheduled, and led.

Practical Implementation

This section translates the theoretical foundation into concrete procedures. Each subsection provides step-by-step methods that can be implemented immediately with no specialized equipment.

The Annual Materials Audit

The audit is the settlement's early warning system. Without it, you are blind to the cliff until you step off it.

Categorize every metallic tool, structural component, and critical material into three groups each spring:

  1. Stable: Intact protective coating, no visible corrosion, full structural integrity. Galvanized surfaces still bright, paint unbroken, rubber still flexible. Estimated remaining life exceeds 5 years.
  2. Degrading: Surface corrosion visible, protective coating compromised in places, early signs of embrittlement. Pitting depth less than 20% of cross-section. Estimated remaining life 2-5 years.
  3. Critical Failure: Deep pitting (greater than 50% of cross-section), structural cracking, rubber crumbled, plastic shattered. Tool unsafe to use at full load or within 1 year of failure.

Use the following standardized audit form for each critical item:

Item Material Condition (1-5) Est. Years Remaining Replacement Strategy
Felling axe Carbon steel 3 4 Forge from leaf spring
Roofing sheets Galvanized steel 4 12 Manufacture (bloomery)
Water pipe Copper 5 40+ Scavenge additional
Wheelbarrow tire Rubber 2 2 Eliminate (solid wheel)
Storage buckets HDPE plastic 2 3 Replace with ceramic/wood
Saw blade Tool steel 3 5 Scavenge replacements

Condition scale: 5 = new/excellent, 4 = good with minor wear, 3 = functional but visibly degraded, 2 = degraded and approaching failure, 1 = failed or unsafe.

Replacement strategy options: Scavenge (obtain from ruins), Manufacture (produce from raw materials), Substitute (replace with different material/design), Eliminate (redesign process to not require this item).

Track year-over-year trends. When a tool drops from 4 to 3 in one year, the cliff is accelerating. The cliff predictor: when more than 30% of your critical tools (axes, saws, hammers, knives, agricultural implements) enter the "Degrading" category simultaneously, your manufacturing capability must already be operational. If it is not, you are behind schedule and must treat manufacturing investment as an emergency.

The High-Value Scavenging Priorities

Think of scavenging as building material "batteries" — concentrated reserves of specific materials harvested and stored for future use. Not all batteries are equally valuable. Prioritize materials you cannot manufacture locally, followed by those that degrade fastest in situ.

The Copper Battery: Copper is critical for electrical applications, cookware, and eventually brass production (when alloyed with zinc). Sources: electrical transformers (the largest concentrated copper finds), heavy industrial wiring, plumbing pipe, motor windings from appliances and vehicles, roofing flashing. Copper's long natural lifespan (50-100 yr) means it is worth traveling farther to obtain. Strip and coil recovered copper wire for compact storage.

The Carbon Steel Battery: High-carbon and alloy steels are the raw material for edge tools — knives, axes, chisels, plowshares. Sources: vehicle leaf springs (excellent high-carbon steel, 0.5-0.6% C), industrial saw blades, railroad spikes (medium carbon), files and rasps (very high carbon, 1.0-1.3% C), ball bearings (chrome steel). These can be forged into new tools by a competent smith without smelting. This is your bridge material — the steel that keeps you functional while your bloomery comes online.

The Zinc Battery: Zinc is the most strategically undervalued scavenging target. Sources: galvanized roofing sheets (zinc coating can be recovered by heating to 420 degrees C, above zinc's melting point), zinc die-cast components (carburetors, door handles, faucets), zinc-carbon batteries. Zinc is essential for brass production (copper + zinc), galvanizing new iron, and sacrificial anode protection. A settlement with zinc reserves can protect its iron tools and produce brass fittings for decades.

The Glass Battery: Glass is infinitely recyclable by remelting at approximately 1500 degrees C (achievable in a well-designed furnace with bellows). Sources: windows (flat glass), bottles (soda-lime glass), laboratory glassware (borosilicate, higher value). Glass does not degrade meaningfully over human timescales. Harvest it systematically: it is heavy but permanent. Store flat glass vertically to prevent breakage.

The Bearing Battery: Sealed ball bearings from vehicles, appliances, electric motors, and industrial equipment are precision-manufactured components that a post-collapse settlement cannot reproduce for decades, if ever. They are small, easy to store, and transform the efficiency of any rotating mechanism — wheels, lathes, windmills, water wheels. Scavenge every bearing you find. Clean, re-grease with animal fat, and store sealed against moisture.

The Fastener Battery: Stainless steel bolts, brass fittings, bronze valves, and copper rivets are corrosion-resistant fasteners that outlast their carbon steel equivalents by 5-10x. Sources: marine hardware, plumbing fittings, electrical panel hardware. Sort by size and type. A well-organized fastener battery saves hundreds of forge-hours that would otherwise be spent manufacturing replacements.

Prioritization logic: Harvest what you cannot manufacture first (bearings, alloy steels, copper, glass). Harvest what degrades fastest second (uncoated carbon steel, rubber components). Harvest what you can eventually manufacture last (mild steel, simple iron).

Preservation Techniques

Every preservation technique buys time on the cliff curve. The goal is not permanent protection — it is extending the window long enough for your manufacturing capability to mature.

Oil Bath: Submerge high-carbon tools (knives, chisels, axes, saws) in rendered animal fat, linseed oil, or any available drying oil when not in use. The oil film excludes oxygen and moisture from the steel surface. Linseed oil is preferred because it polymerizes into a semi-hard film; animal fat remains liquid and must be reapplied after each use. For long-term storage, coat tools thickly and wrap in oiled cloth. See Foundational Chemistry module for linseed oil processing.

Charcoal Paint: Mix finely crushed charcoal with pine resin (or any available natural resin) at a ratio of approximately 2 parts charcoal to 1 part resin by volume. Heat gently to combine. Apply to structural steel, iron fittings, and any metal not regularly handled. The carbon layer blocks oxygen and the resin binds it to the surface. Reapply annually or when the coating cracks. This is a simplified version of the coal-tar coatings used historically on bridges and ships.

Wax Coating: Beeswax or rendered tallow, applied warm to precision tools (measuring instruments, thread taps, drill bits, small bearings), provides a moisture barrier superior to oil for items in long-term storage. Melt the wax, dip or brush onto the clean dry tool, allow to cool. The wax film is self-sealing — minor scratches close under finger warmth.

Desiccant Storage: Place tools in sealed containers (clay pots with wax-sealed lids, or intact plastic tubs) with a desiccant to absorb residual moisture. Effective desiccants: charcoal (dried at 200+ degrees C to activate), quicklime (CaO — highly hygroscopic, produces heat when wet, handle with care), or wood ash (less effective but readily available). Replace desiccant annually. This method is especially important for spring steel and tool steel, which are highly sensitive to pitting corrosion. Cross-reference lime production in the Foundational Chemistry module.

Sheltered Storage: The single most cost-effective preservation measure is a roof. Metal tools stored under a simple open-sided shelter last 3-5x longer than identical tools left outdoors. Rain is the primary corrosion accelerant for atmospheric exposure. A shelter eliminates direct rainfall and reduces dew formation. Build dedicated tool storage as early infrastructure — before you build a smithy, before you build a kiln. The storage shed protects the materials you need to build everything else.

Sacrificial Anode Protection: When zinc is available, attach a piece of zinc to iron structures (fences, water tanks, buried pipe) using direct metal contact or a wire. The zinc corrodes preferentially, sacrificing itself to protect the iron. This is the same electrochemical principle used in marine and pipeline corrosion protection worldwide. A 1 kg zinc anode can protect approximately 5-10 square meters of iron surface for 2-5 years depending on environment. Replace the zinc anode when it is consumed to approximately 20% of original mass.

The Transition Plan

The transition from scavenging to manufacturing is a multi-year process that must be planned from Year 0, even though the most intensive work begins later. The five phases below assume a temperate environment with accessible iron ore within 20 km.

Phase 1: Pure Scavenging and Preservation (Year 0-2).

All material needs met by salvage. Focus: build material batteries (copper, steel, zinc, glass, bearings, fasteners), construct sheltered storage, begin oil/charcoal preservation program, conduct first annual materials audit, identify local ore deposits and clay sources. Governance action: appoint a Materials Officer responsible for the audit and preservation.

Phase 2: Forge Establishment (Year 2-4).

Construct a charcoal-fired forge using scavenged firebrick or locally made refractory clay. Begin basic metalworking: re-forging leaf springs into tools, straightening and sharpening edge tools, forge-welding broken tools. Train 2-3 apprentice smiths. The forge is your bridge — it extends the useful life of scavenged steel by reshaping, hardening, and repairing it. Continue scavenging, but begin shifting labor allocation: 70% subsistence/scavenging, 30% manufacturing development.

Phase 3: Bloomery Construction and Ore Survey (Year 4-7).

Construct a bloomery furnace for primary iron smelting from local ore. This is the critical manufacturing capability — the ability to produce new iron from raw materials. Conduct systematic ore survey within 20 km radius. Experiment with local ore: different ores require different furnace conditions. Expect 3-5 failed smelts before producing usable blooms. Labor allocation shifts to 50% subsistence, 30% manufacturing, 20% scavenging.

Phase 4: Primary Manufacturing (Year 7-12).

Bloomery producing regular iron blooms. Forge converting blooms to tools, hardware, and structural elements. Begin replacing scavenged stock with locally manufactured equivalents. Develop secondary manufacturing: charcoal kilns (fuel supply), ceramic crucibles (for higher-temperature work), lime kilns (for mortar and preservation chemicals). Labor allocation: 50% subsistence, 40% manufacturing, 10% scavenging (targeted only — copper, bearings, specialty items).

Phase 5: Material Sovereignty (Year 12+).

The settlement manufactures the majority of its metal tools and structural components from local ore and fuel. Scavenging continues only for materials that cannot be locally produced (copper, zinc, glass, bearings). The cliff is no longer a threat because the settlement produces faster than entropy consumes. This is material sovereignty — not the absence of scavenging, but the independence from it.

Common Failure Modes

The following failure modes have been observed historically or can be predicted from the theoretical framework. Each is preventable with awareness and planning.

1. The Scavenging Trap. The community never transitions to manufacturing because scavenging continues to feel productive and sufficient. Each year, the settlement says "we will start the forge next year" while material quality silently declines.

Prevention: Set hard calendar deadlines for each transition phase. The Materials Officer reports annually to the full community on cliff proximity. Make the manufacturing transition a governance commitment, not an aspiration.

2. Cliff Blindness. No audit is conducted. Degradation is noticed only when a critical tool fails during use — an axe head snaps during felling, a water pipe bursts, a roofing sheet collapses. By the time failure is visible, the cliff has already been passed.

Prevention: Conduct the annual materials audit every spring without exception. Record results in a permanent physical ledger. Compare year-over-year. The audit makes the invisible visible.

3. Hoarding Without Preservation. Materials are scavenged and stockpiled but stored improperly — piled outdoors, left in damp buildings, or mixed with dissimilar metals creating galvanic cells. The stockpile degrades faster than it would have in its original location.

Prevention: Build sheltered, dry, organized storage before large-scale scavenging begins. Oil-coat metals, separate dissimilar metals, elevate stock off damp ground. A stockpile without preservation is not an asset; it is a waste of scavenging labor.

4. Single-Source Dependency. All tools of one type come from the same scavenging source or the same batch of material. A single metallurgical defect, a single corrosion event, or a single storage failure destroys the entire inventory of that tool type.

Prevention: Diversify sources. Maintain at least 3 independent reserves of every critical tool category (cutting, striking, fastening, measuring). Store reserves in separate locations. Redundancy is not waste; it is insurance.

5. Specialist Bottleneck. Only one person knows how to operate the forge or bloomery. Their death, disability, or departure collapses the entire manufacturing program and potentially the settlement's long-term survival.

Prevention: Every master smith must have at least 2 apprentices at all times. No critical skill should be held by only one person. Document techniques, temperatures, ore sources, and charcoal ratios in a permanent physical journal. Cross-train aggressively.

6. Premature Abandonment of Scavenging. The settlement shifts too enthusiastically to manufacturing and stops scavenging before manufacturing output is reliable. Early bloomery iron is inconsistent — some blooms produce good wrought iron, others are full of slag. The settlement needs scavenged steel as backup during the transition.

Prevention: Maintain targeted scavenging operations through Phase 4. Do not stop scavenging until manufacturing has produced at least one full annual cycle of replacement tools that have been field-tested and proven durable.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

The following terms are used throughout this module and across the Project Aadhiyandham library. Master these definitions to communicate precisely about material degradation and transition planning.

  1. Recycling Cliff: The nonlinear acceleration point where material degradation rate increases dramatically due to protective coating failure, leading to rapid loss of usability.
  2. Entropy: The thermodynamic quantity measuring disorder in a system; in this context, the irreversible tendency of manufactured materials to degrade toward simpler, less useful forms.
  3. Corrosion: The electrochemical degradation of metals through reaction with oxygen, water, and other environmental agents; the primary mechanism of the recycling cliff for metals.
  4. Galvanic Coupling: The accelerated corrosion that occurs when two dissimilar metals are in electrical contact, creating an electrochemical cell where the more reactive metal corrodes preferentially.
  5. Passivation: The formation of a thin, stable oxide layer on a metal surface (e.g., chromium oxide on stainless steel, aluminum oxide on aluminum) that dramatically slows further corrosion.
  6. Scavenger's Dilemma: The cognitive and economic trap where the immediate tangible rewards of scavenging delay investment in manufacturing, even when manufacturing is the only long-term survival strategy.
  7. Materials Audit: The systematic annual assessment of all critical tools and materials, categorizing each by condition and estimating remaining useful life to predict cliff timing.
  8. Cliff Predictor: The threshold indicator (more than 30% of critical tools entering "Degrading" status) that signals manufacturing capability must already be operational.
  9. Material Sovereignty: The state in which a settlement manufactures the majority of its critical materials from local resources, independent of scavenged industrial stock.
  10. Bloomery: A simple shaft furnace for smelting iron from ore using charcoal fuel, producing a spongy mass of wrought iron (bloom) that can be forged into tools and hardware.
  11. High-Value Battery: A concentrated, preserved reserve of a specific scavenged material type (copper, carbon steel, zinc, glass, bearings, fasteners) stockpiled for long-term use.
  12. Sacrificial Anode: A piece of more reactive metal (typically zinc) attached to a less reactive metal structure (typically iron) to corrode preferentially, protecting the structure.
  13. Transition Plan: The five-phase, multi-year schedule for shifting a settlement from total scavenging dependence to material sovereignty through progressive manufacturing development.
  14. Degradation Timeline: The estimated lifespan of each material category under local environmental conditions, used to predict cliff timing and prioritize scavenging and manufacturing.
  15. Preservation Window: The period of time that can be added to a material's useful life through active preservation techniques (oil coating, sheltered storage, sacrificial anodes, desiccant storage).
