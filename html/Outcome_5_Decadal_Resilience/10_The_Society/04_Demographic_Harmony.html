Alignment: Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

The fundamental law of biology is unforgiving: if a species has surplus food, clean water, and no predation, its population expands exponentially. Every successful civilization has confronted this law, and the majority have failed. The Maya Classic Period collapse, Easter Island's deforestation spiral, and the Irish Potato Famine share a common root — populations that exceeded the carrying capacity of their land. A post-collapse refugia that masters food production, water purification, and disease prevention will face the same pressure within a generation. The very success of the settlement becomes the engine of its destruction if population growth is not addressed with the same rigor applied to soil management or water infrastructure.

A post-collapse settlement must deliberately match its population to its carrying capacity through continuous ecological calculation. This is not a one-time exercise. Carrying capacity shifts with soil depletion, climate variation, forest succession, and aquifer drawdown. The community that calculates its limit in Year 3 and never revisits the question will be blindsided by drought in Year 15. The carrying capacity audit must become as routine as spring planting — an annual accounting between people and land.

This requires both ecological mathematics and culturally embedded fertility management. The mathematics alone are insufficient: a community that knows its limit but lacks the cultural tools to stay within it will overshoot just as surely as one that never calculated. Fertility management must be woven into daily life — taught during coming-of-age education, supported by trained herbalists, discussed openly between partners, and monitored through annual census. The goal is not population control imposed from above, but demographic literacy shared by all.

> DANGER: Botanical contraceptives and emmenagogues carry real medical risk. Pennyroyal (Mentha pulegium) is hepatotoxic — liver failure and death have been documented at doses only slightly above the purported effective dose. Poison Hemlock (Conium maculatum) is visually similar to Wild Carrot (Daucus carota) and is rapidly lethal. Never use botanical fertility management without positive plant identification training and herbalist guidance. All fertility decisions are individual and consensual — coercion is never acceptable.

Theoretical Foundation

The Mathematics of Population Growth

Population growth follows the exponential equation N(t) = N(0) x e^(rt), where N(0) is the starting population, r is the annual growth rate, and t is time in years. The doubling time for any population is calculated as ln(2)/r, approximately 0.693/r. Consider the implications at various growth rates:

Annual Growth Rate Doubling Time (years) Population after 100 years (from 50)
1.0% 69 135
1.5% 46 224
2.0% 35 365
2.5% 28 607
3.0% 23 985

A settlement of 50 people with a growth rate of 2.5% — a modest pre-industrial rate, well below the biological maximum — doubles in just 28 years. Within a century, that settlement grows from 50 to 100 to 200 to 400 and beyond. Even a seemingly conservative 1.5% growth rate produces a fourfold increase in a century. No fixed land base can absorb these increases without catastrophic resource degradation. The mathematics are relentless: exponential growth always overwhelms linear resource constraints given sufficient time.

Thomas Malthus articulated this in his Essay on the Principle of Population (1798): population grows geometrically (2, 4, 8, 16) while food supply grows arithmetically (2, 3, 4, 5). The Malthusian check follows — when population exceeds food supply, mortality rises through famine, disease, or conflict until balance is restored. Malthus was criticized for underestimating technological innovation, but his mathematics remain sound for any community operating without fossil fuels and industrial agriculture. A refugia running on muscle power and hand tools lives within Malthusian constraints whether it acknowledges them or not.

The Club of Rome's 1972 study, The Limits to Growth (Meadows et al.), modeled this dynamic at global scale using system dynamics simulation, demonstrating that infinite growth on a finite resource base results in systemic collapse. The same mathematics apply, with even greater force, to a 500-acre micro-valley. The smaller the system, the faster overshoot becomes visible — and the less room for error.

The demographic transition model describes a pattern observed across industrializing societies: pre-industrial populations have high birth AND high death rates, producing near-equilibrium. As societies develop medicine and sanitation, death rates fall first — but birth rates lag behind by decades. This lag produces explosive growth. A successful refugia faces exactly this danger. If the settlement lowers infant mortality through clean water and basic medicine without simultaneously establishing cultural mechanisms to lower birth rates, population will surge beyond carrying capacity within a generation. The refugia must achieve its own demographic transition deliberately, rather than waiting for the slow cultural shift that took industrialized nations 50-100 years.

Carrying Capacity and Liebig's Law

Carrying capacity (K) is the maximum population a given land area can support indefinitely without degrading the resource base. The critical word is "indefinitely." Short-term overshoot is possible — a community can temporarily exceed K by drawing down grain stores, depleting soil fertility, or clear-cutting forest. But overshoot borrows against the future. Resource degradation lowers K itself, deepening the overshoot and accelerating degradation — a positive feedback loop that ends in collapse. This pattern destroyed Easter Island's agricultural base, Lebanon's cedar forests, and the American Dust Bowl's topsoil.

Liebig's Law of the Minimum states that growth is limited by the scarcest essential resource, not the total of all resources. A valley with abundant water, rich soil, and vast forests is limited by whichever resource is scarcest relative to demand. The community must identify its limiting factor through systematic audit:

  • Water: liters per day of stream flow in the driest month, divided by minimum per-person requirement (10 L/day for drinking, cooking, and basic hygiene)
  • Calories: arable land area in hectares, multiplied by achievable yield, converted to kilocalories, divided by 730,000 kcal per person per year (2,000 kcal/day)
  • Protein: animal husbandry capacity plus legume/grain production, measured in grams per person per day (minimum 50g)
  • Fuel wood: heating, cooking, and manufacturing needs compared to sustainable coppice harvest (typically 5-10 cubic meters per hectare per year)
  • Building materials: timber, stone, clay, and thatch availability for housing and infrastructure maintenance

Each of these resources has its own renewal rate, its own seasonal variation, and its own degradation pattern. Water fluctuates with rainfall; calories depend on soil fertility that can be built or destroyed; fuel wood depends on forest management decisions made decades earlier. The audit must account for these dynamics — a static snapshot is insufficient.

The lowest of these calculations determines the true carrying capacity. A valley that can feed 300 people but only water 180 in a drought year has a carrying capacity of 180.

The 25% buffer principle follows directly: never exceed 75% of calculated K. If the audit yields K = 200, the population target is 150. This buffer absorbs drought years, crop failures, pest outbreaks, and refugee intake. A community at 100% of K has zero margin — the first bad harvest becomes a famine. The buffer is the difference between resilience and fragility.

Historical Demography

Pre-industrial fertility management was universal, not a modern invention. The notion that pre-modern peoples bred without restraint is a myth contradicted by extensive ethnographic evidence.

On Tikopia, a volcanic island of 4.6 square kilometers in the Solomon Islands, population was maintained at approximately 1,200 for over three millennia through celibacy, coitus interruptus, infanticide, and sea voyaging (voluntary exile). Tikopia's chiefs explicitly recognized the connection between population and food supply. Diamond documents this case in Collapse (2005) as a rare society that solved its population problem.

The !Kung San of the Kalahari Desert maintained near-stable population for millennia through extended breastfeeding (4+ years per child) and high mobility. The combination of lactational amenorrhea and the caloric demands of nomadic life produced birth intervals of 4-5 years — an evolved cultural-biological equilibrium.

Medieval Europe practiced the "European marriage pattern" identified by John Hajnal (1965): women married late (ages 22-25), a significant fraction never married at all, and households were nuclear rather than extended. This pattern kept fertility well below the biological maximum and produced growth rates of 0.5-1.0% per year — far more manageable than the 2-3% rates seen in populations without cultural restraints.

Tokugawa Japan (1600-1868) maintained population stability at approximately 30 million for 250 years through mabiki (literally "thinning"), delayed marriage, primogeniture inheritance rules, and herbal abortifacients. This stability coincided with one of the most culturally productive periods in Japanese history, demonstrating that demographic equilibrium and civilizational flourishing are not opposed.

These are not exotic or aberrant practices. They are the historical norm. Every stable pre-industrial society managed its fertility through some combination of delayed marriage, extended breastfeeding, herbal contraception, abstinence rules, and social customs governing family formation. The societies that did not manage their fertility — or whose management systems broke down — are the ones that appear in the archaeological record as collapse events. The question is not whether a post-collapse community will practice fertility management, but whether it will do so proactively and humanely, or reactively through the Malthusian checks of famine, epidemic, and conflict.

The Minimum Viable Population

The danger of population decline is as real as population growth. Below a certain size, a population becomes genetically unviable. The 50/500 rule (Franklin 1980) establishes two thresholds: 50 individuals minimum to avoid inbreeding depression in the short term (3-5 generations), and 500 for long-term genetic diversity.

For human settlements, a founding population of 150-200 provides adequate genetic diversity if supplemented by occasional inter-settlement exchange. The annual demographic census detects both dangerous growth and dangerous shrinkage.

A population that falls below 50 effective breeding adults faces measurable inbreeding depression within three generations: increased infant mortality, reduced immune function, and accumulation of deleterious recessive alleles. Historical examples include the Habsburg dynasty's genetic deterioration and isolated island populations with elevated rates of rare genetic disorders.

The solution is diplomatic exchange — the Genetic Diversity Exchange protocols detailed in the Inter-Settlement Diplomacy module. Periodic marriage exchange between settlements (even one or two individuals per generation) dramatically increases effective population size and prevents the genetic bottleneck that dooms isolated small populations.

Core Principles

  1. Know Your Carrying Capacity. Calculate the maximum sustainable population before the community reaches it. The carrying capacity audit is non-negotiable — it must be conducted by Year 3 of the settlement and repeated annually thereafter. A community that does not know its limit cannot stay within it.
  2. The 25% Buffer. Never allow the population to exceed 75% of the calculated carrying capacity. This margin absorbs drought, crop failure, disease outbreak, and refugee intake. Operating without a buffer is operating without insurance — it works until it does not, and when it fails, people die.
  3. Cultural Fertility Management. Birth spacing must be culturally normal, not emergency policy. If fertility management is only discussed during crisis, it is already too late. The knowledge must be taught to every adolescent, practiced by every couple, and supported by trained herbalists and midwives as routine.
  4. Individual Autonomy. All fertility decisions are personal and consensual. The community educates, supports, and provides resources — it never coerces. Any system that imposes reproductive rules from above destroys the trust and social cohesion the settlement depends on. Coercive population control is both morally wrong and strategically counterproductive.
  5. Demographic Monitoring. An annual population census with age-sex structure analysis provides the data foundation for all demographic planning. Without data, the community is guessing. The census converts demographic intuition into demographic knowledge.

Practical Implementation

The Carrying Capacity Audit

The audit is conducted annually, beginning in Year 3 when agricultural yields have stabilized enough for reliable data. Years 1 and 2 are establishment years — crop yields fluctuate as the community learns its soil, climate, and pest dynamics. By Year 3, enough harvest data exists for conservative projections. Do not delay beyond Year 3 — a community that waits until Year 10 may discover it has already overshot.

Step 1 — Water. Measure stream flow during the driest month of the year in liters per day. This is the hydrological bottleneck. Divide by 10 liters per person per day (the minimum for drinking, cooking, and basic hygiene — irrigation water is separate). Result: maximum population supportable by water supply.

Step 2 — Calories. Measure total arable land in hectares. Multiply by expected yield using conservative estimates based on actual performance, not optimistic projections:

Crop Type Expected Yield (kg/ha) Calories per kg Calories per ha
Potatoes 15,000 770 11,550,000
Wheat 2,000 3,400 6,800,000
Mixed garden 5,000 1,200 6,000,000
Dry beans 1,500 3,370 5,055,000
Maize 3,000 3,650 10,950,000

Divide total caloric output by 730,000 kcal per person per year (2,000 kcal/day). Result: maximum population supportable by food production.

Step 3 — Fuel wood. Estimate total fuel wood demand for heating (climate-dependent — 3-8 cubic meters per household per year), cooking (1-2 cubic meters per household per year), and manufacturing (charcoal for smithing, lime burning, pottery). Compare to sustainable coppice harvest of managed woodland, typically 5-10 cubic meters per hectare per year depending on species and climate. Result: maximum population supportable by fuel supply.

Step 4 — Take the minimum. The lowest of the three calculations is the carrying capacity. Liebig's Law applies — the scarcest resource determines the limit.

Step 5 — Apply the 25% buffer. Multiply the minimum by 0.75. This is the maximum sustainable population target.

Example calculation: A settlement with a driest-month stream flow of 2,400 L/day (supports 240 people), 15 hectares of mixed garden producing 90,000,000 kcal/year (supports 123 people), and 20 hectares of managed coppice producing 120 cubic meters/year against a demand of 0.8 cubic meters/person/year (supports 150 people). The minimum is 123 (calories are the limiting factor). Apply the buffer: 123 x 0.75 = 92. Maximum sustainable population: 92 people.

This calculation must be recorded in the community's permanent records and updated each year. If a new water source is developed, arable land expanded, or a drought reduces stream flow, the carrying capacity changes and the target changes with it. The audit is never finished — it is an ongoing conversation between the community and its land.

Fertility Awareness Methods

Lactational Amenorrhea Method (LAM). Frequent, exclusive breastfeeding suppresses the hormones required for ovulation. If a mother breastfeeds on-demand — at least every 4 hours during the day and every 6 hours at night — and the infant is under 6 months old, and the mother has not yet resumed menstruation, the probability of pregnancy is less than 2%.

This was established by the Bellagio Consensus of 1988, which confirmed LAM efficacy at 98% when all three criteria are met simultaneously:

  • Exclusive breastfeeding (no supplemental food or water for the infant)
  • Amenorrhea (the mother's menstrual period has not returned)
  • Infant under 6 months of age

All three criteria must be met. When any one ceases to apply, LAM is no longer reliable and the couple must transition to another method. LAM naturally spaces children by 12-18 months at minimum, protecting the mother's iron reserves, allowing full physical recovery between pregnancies, and slowing population growth at the baseline level.

Symptothermal Method. The female body provides clear, observable signs of fertility that can be tracked without any technology beyond a thermometer.

Basal body temperature rises 0.2-0.5 degrees Celsius after ovulation due to progesterone release. This temperature shift, measured each morning before rising at the same time each day, identifies the post-ovulatory infertile phase. The temperature must be measured orally or vaginally with a thermometer capable of reading to 0.1 degree Celsius resolution. Production of mercury-in-glass or alcohol-in-glass thermometers is cross-referenced in the glassmaking module.

Cervical mucus changes provide a complementary signal: during the fertile window, mucus becomes clear, stretchy, and slippery (the "egg white" consistency described by the Billings method), while during infertile phases it is scant, sticky, or absent.

Calendar calculation adds a third layer of prediction based on cycle length history. A woman who has recorded 6 or more cycles can calculate her fertile window using the shortest and longest cycle lengths observed.

Method Typical-Use Efficacy Perfect-Use Efficacy Requirements
LAM 95-98% 99.5% Exclusive breastfeeding, amenorrhea, <6 months
Symptothermal (combined) 95% 99.6% Thermometer, daily charting, 3-month training
Calendar only 87% 95% 6+ months cycle records
Cervical mucus only 90% 97% Training in Billings method

The combined symptothermal method achieves 95-99% efficacy with proper training.

The training protocol requires a 3-month apprenticeship with an experienced practitioner before relying on the method. During training, the couple tracks all three indicators daily while using an additional method as backup. After three months, the couple reviews their charts with the practitioner to confirm accurate identification of fertile and infertile phases.

Botanical Fertility Management

Humanity has managed fertility botanically for millennia. The evidence base is ethnobotanical rather than clinical-trial quality, meaning efficacy rates are uncertain and safety margins less defined than for modern pharmaceuticals. These methods are necessary but must be used with full awareness of their limitations.

Wild Carrot Seed (Daucus carota). Also known as Queen Anne's Lace. Documented use in Appalachian, Indian, and Chinese folk medicine as a post-coital anti-fertility agent. Seeds are chewed (approximately one teaspoon) within 8 hours of intercourse and continued daily for 7 days. The proposed mechanism is disruption of progesterone synthesis, preventing implantation. Efficacy is not clinically established but consistent traditional use across multiple unconnected cultures constitutes meaningful evidence.

Critical identification warning: Wild Carrot (Daucus carota) is visually similar to Poison Hemlock (Conium maculatum). Misidentification is lethal — Hemlock contains coniine, a piperidine alkaloid that causes ascending paralysis and respiratory failure within hours. There is no antidote.

The distinguishing features are:

  • Stem: Queen Anne's Lace has a hairy stem. Poison Hemlock has a smooth stem with purple-red spots or blotches.
  • Flower: Queen Anne's Lace typically has a single dark purple-red floret at the center of the flower head. Hemlock has no dark central floret.
  • Smell: Crush a leaf and smell it. Queen Anne's Lace smells like carrot. Hemlock has a musty, mousy, unpleasant odor.
  • Root: Queen Anne's Lace has a white taproot that smells like carrot. Hemlock root has no carrot scent.

All four tests should be applied. Any uncertainty means the plant is not used. Formal identification training with live specimens is required before any member harvests Wild Carrot.

Emmenagogues. Plants that stimulate uterine contractions and induce menstruation. These are used when a menstrual cycle is late and pregnancy is suspected but not desired.

  • Mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris): dried leaves brewed as strong tea. Used historically across European and Chinese herbal traditions. Acts as a uterine stimulant. Moderate risk profile but should not be used during confirmed pregnancy past the earliest stages.
  • Pennyroyal (Mentha pulegium): EXTREME CAUTION. The essential oil contains pulegone, which is hepatotoxic. Liver failure and death documented at approximately 10 ml of essential oil — only slightly above the purported effective dose. The therapeutic window is dangerously narrow. Pennyroyal tea (dried leaves, not essential oil) is less concentrated but still carries liver risk. Avoid entirely if any alternative exists. Included here because it appears in historical herbals and members must understand the danger.
  • Neem (Azadirachta indica): documented spermicidal properties in the Indian Ayurvedic tradition. Neem oil applied intravaginally before intercourse has demonstrated spermicidal activity in laboratory studies. Available only in tropical and subtropical climates where the tree grows.
  • Vitamin C (ascorbic acid): high doses from pine needle tea or rosehip infusion have been used as folk emmenagogues. Evidence of efficacy is weak, but risk is low.

All emmenagogue use must be supervised by the community herbalist. Dosage, timing, and contraindications vary by plant, individual, and preparation. The herbalist must maintain a written pharmacopoeia documenting local preparations, observed efficacy, and adverse reactions — building an empirical evidence base over time.

The evidence base for botanical fertility management is ethnobotanical, not clinical. Efficacy rates cannot be stated with pharmaceutical precision. These methods supplement fertility awareness methods — they do not replace them. The symptothermal method combined with LAM remains the most reliable non-surgical fertility management available in a post-collapse setting.

Cultural Integration

Fertility management works only when it is culturally normal rather than taboo. A community where contraception is discussed in whispers will not maintain demographic balance.

Education during coming-of-age. Fertility awareness — both the biology and the practical methods — is taught during the coming-of-age rituals that mark the transition to adulthood (cross-reference Intergenerational Identity module). This is not a clinical lecture but a mentored introduction to adult responsibility.

Young men and young women receive the same education. Fertility management is framed not as restriction but as competence — the same way food preservation or fire management are taught as essential adult skills.

The community herbalist. Every settlement needs at least one trained herbalist who serves as a trusted, confidential advisor on fertility matters. The herbalist maintains the botanical garden where contraceptive and emmenagogue plants are cultivated, conducts plant identification training, prepares standardized preparations, and provides individual consultation.

The herbalist role carries the same social respect as the healer or the teacher.

Seasonal workshops. Twice-yearly workshops (spring and autumn) review symptothermal tracking, share observations about local plant efficacy, and provide a forum for questions. These normalize fertility discussion as routine community business.

Male involvement. Fertility management is not exclusively a female responsibility. Male partners participate in symptothermal tracking, share in daily charting, and engage in joint decision-making about family size. A community where fertility management is "women's business" has placed the entire demographic burden on half the population. Studies consistently show higher method adherence when both partners are trained.

Inter-generational knowledge transfer. Elder women are the primary keepers of fertility knowledge in most traditional societies. Their experience represents decades of practical observation. The settlement must formalize this transfer through apprenticeship with elder herbalists and midwives rather than letting it fade with each generation.

The Annual Demographic Census

The census is conducted each year at a fixed date — midwinter is traditional, when the population is settled and agricultural work is minimal. The protocol is straightforward:

Count every person by age and sex. Record the name, age, and sex of every community member. Record births, deaths, arrivals, and departures since the last census. This produces the age-sex pyramid — the fundamental tool of demographic analysis.

Calculate the dependency ratio. Dependents (children under 15 plus adults over 65) divided by working-age adults (15-64). A ratio above 0.7 indicates significant strain on productive capacity.

Project population forward. Using current birth and death rates, project population 10 and 20 years forward. If projected population exceeds 75% of K within 10 years, intensify fertility education. If it exceeds K within 20 years, bring the issue before the community assembly for consensus decision-making (cross-reference Consensus Models).

Compare projections to carrying capacity. The census and the carrying capacity audit are two halves of the same analysis. One measures the population. The other measures the land. Together they answer the only question that matters: are we in balance?

Record and archive. Every census is recorded in the community's permanent archive. Over time, accumulated records reveal trends invisible in any single year — gradual shifts in age structure, slowly changing birth rates, dependency ratio changes signaling future labor shortages. A community with twenty years of census data can project with confidence. The archive transforms demographic management from crisis response into long-range planning.

Common Failure Modes

1. Carrying capacity overestimate. The audit was conducted during a good year. The calculated K is 200, but in drought the true K drops to 130. The community has 160 people and no buffer.

Prevention: Always use driest-month water flow and worst-year crop yields. The buffer exists because average conditions are not worst-case. Review carrying capacity annually and revise downward when data warrants.

2. Toxic plant misidentification. A community member harvests what they believe is Wild Carrot but has actually collected Poison Hemlock. The result is rapid respiratory failure and death.

Prevention: Formal plant identification training with live specimens. All four identification tests (stem hair, central floret, leaf-crush smell, root smell) must be applied before any harvest. No solo harvesting — a second trained identifier must confirm.

3. Fertility management as taboo. The community avoids discussing contraception out of cultural discomfort, religious objection, or simple embarrassment. Couples lack knowledge of methods. Birth rates rise unchecked. By the time the issue becomes undeniable, the population is already in overshoot.

Prevention: Integrate fertility education into coming-of-age rituals from the settlement's founding. Normalize the topic through seasonal workshops and open discussion. The community herbalist serves as a confidential resource. Frame fertility management as ecological competence, not moral judgment.

4. Coercive population control. Leadership imposes reproductive rules — mandatory contraception, birth permits, or penalties for large families. This destroys trust and produces resistance, evasion, and social fracture.

Prevention: Individual autonomy is inviolable. The community educates and provides resources. Consensus decision-making addresses the structural question (what is our target population?) while leaving the personal question (how many children will I have?) to the individual.

5. Ignoring the minimum. The community focuses on preventing growth and neglects population decline. Emigration, epidemic, or low fertility shrinks the population below genetic viability.

Prevention: The annual census tracks decline as well as growth. If population approaches 50 effective breeding adults, actively pursue inter-settlement exchange. The Genetic Diversity Exchange protocols provide the diplomatic framework.

6. Growth-as-success ideology. The community equates population growth with prosperity and health. A stable population is seen as stagnation. This ideology prevents recognition of overshoot until crisis.

Prevention: Cultural education must explicitly address growth ideology. Tokugawa Japan (250 years of stability and cultural flourishing) and Tikopia (3,000 years of sustainable island management) demonstrate that demographic stability is the precondition for long-term civilizational success, not stagnation.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Carrying Capacity (K): the maximum population a given land area can support indefinitely without degrading the resource base
  • Liebig's Law of the Minimum: the principle that growth is limited by the scarcest essential resource, not the total of all resources
  • Demographic Transition: the shift from high birth/high death rates (pre-industrial equilibrium) to low birth/low death rates (modern equilibrium), with a dangerous intervening period of high growth
  • Malthusian Check: the restoration of population-resource balance through increased mortality (famine, disease, conflict) when population exceeds carrying capacity
  • Exponential Growth: growth at a constant percentage rate, producing a doubling pattern (2, 4, 8, 16) rather than linear increase (2, 3, 4, 5)
  • Doubling Time: the time required for a population to double at a given growth rate, calculated as ln(2)/r or approximately 0.693/r
  • Dependency Ratio: the ratio of non-working-age dependents (children + elderly) to working-age adults; a measure of productive burden
  • Lactational Amenorrhea: the suppression of ovulation caused by frequent, exclusive breastfeeding; the biological basis of the LAM method
  • Symptothermal Method: a fertility awareness method combining basal body temperature, cervical mucus observation, and calendar calculation to identify fertile and infertile phases
  • Emmenagogue: a substance that stimulates uterine contractions and induces menstruation; used historically as a post-conception fertility management tool
  • Minimum Viable Population: the smallest population size that can sustain itself genetically and demographically over the long term
  • 50/500 Rule: the population genetics guideline that 50 individuals are needed to avoid short-term inbreeding depression and 500 for long-term genetic diversity
  • Demographic Census: an annual count of every community member by age and sex, providing the data foundation for population projection and planning
  • Buffer Principle: the practice of maintaining population at 75% or less of calculated carrying capacity to absorb shocks (drought, crop failure, refugee intake)
  • Fertility Awareness: the knowledge and practice of identifying fertile and infertile phases of the menstrual cycle for the purpose of achieving or avoiding pregnancy
