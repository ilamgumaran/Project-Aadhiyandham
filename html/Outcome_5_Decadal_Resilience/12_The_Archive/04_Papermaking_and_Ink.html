Alignment: Outcome 5: Decadal and Generational Resilience

Information storage is the foundation of civilizational continuity. Every technical skill documented in this blueprint — water purification, metallurgy, agriculture, medicine — exists as knowledge first and practice second. Without a reliable medium to record, preserve, and transmit that knowledge across generations, a settlement degrades from engineering to folklore within a single human lifetime. The oral tradition is powerful but lossy: critical quantitative details (dosages, temperatures, ratios, dimensions) erode with each retelling. Writing is the error-correction mechanism that prevents civilizational drift.

Digital and commercial media will fail within a decade of systemic collapse. Hard drives require electricity and functioning read heads. USB flash memory degrades through charge leakage within 5-10 years without refresh cycles. Optical discs scratch and delaminate. Commercial printer paper, manufactured with acidic wood pulp, yellows and crumbles within 20-50 years even under ideal storage conditions. Ballpoint pens dry out. Laser toner flakes off. Every information storage technology of the industrial era depends on supply chains that will not exist. The settlement cannot wait for these media to fail and then scramble to replace them — by then, the knowledge they contained is already lost.

The settlement must therefore manufacture its own durable writing surfaces and permanent inks from locally available materials. Paper and ink are not luxuries — they are infrastructure as critical as clean water or shelter. A sheet of well-made, alkaline-buffered paper written with iron gall ink has a demonstrated archival lifespan exceeding 500 years. This module teaches the complete process: from harvesting plant fibers and processing them into paper, to synthesizing two classes of ink (iron gall for archival permanence, carbon for daily use), to fabricating writing tools from feathers and reeds. Every material is available in a temperate forest environment. Every process is achievable with zero-tech equipment.

> CAUTION: Papermaking involves prolonged water exposure (skin maceration risk with extended contact) and alkaline solutions for fiber processing. Iron gall ink is acidic and corrosive — avoid skin and eye contact. The ink-making process involves boiling liquids and handling rusty metal. Wear eye protection when mixing iron solutions.

Theoretical Foundation

The Science of Paper

Paper is a mat of interlocking cellulose fibers. Cellulose — chemical formula (C6H10O5)n — is the most abundant organic polymer on Earth. It is the structural component of every plant cell wall, from the towering oak to the smallest blade of grass. The "n" in the formula represents the degree of polymerization: in wood, n ranges from 5,000 to 10,000 glucose units per chain; in cotton, n can exceed 15,000. Longer polymer chains mean stronger fibers.

Papermaking works because cellulose fibers, when suspended in water and then drained onto a flat surface, bond to each other through hydrogen bonding as they dry. Each cellulose chain bristles with hydroxyl (-OH) groups. When two wet fibers come into contact and the water evaporates, the hydroxyl groups on adjacent fibers form hydrogen bonds — weak individually, but collectively powerful when millions of bonds form across every fiber junction. This is why paper must be formed from a water suspension: the water mediates the initial fiber contact, and its removal drives the bonding.

The strength of paper comes from two sources: the intrinsic tensile strength of individual cellulose fibers (400-800 MPa, comparable to structural steel — cross-reference the Textiles module for fiber mechanics) and the density of hydrogen bonds between fibers. Longer fibers produce stronger paper because each fiber spans more junctions and participates in more bonds. This is why bast fibers from mulberry, hemp, and flax — with fiber lengths of 15-65 mm — make vastly superior paper to wood pulp, whose fibers measure only 1-3 mm.

Paper acidity is the critical factor in archival longevity. Acid degrades cellulose through hydrolysis: hydrogen ions (H+) break the glycosidic bonds that link glucose units in the cellulose chain, shortening the polymer and weakening the fiber. At pH below 5, this degradation is rapid — paper yellows, becomes brittle, and crumbles within decades. Archival paper must be pH neutral (7.0) or slightly alkaline (7.5-8.5). Adding calcium carbonate (CaCO3, from ground limestone or chalk) to the pulp creates an alkaline buffer that neutralizes acids as they form, extending paper life from decades to centuries.

The Chemistry of Iron Gall Ink

Iron gall ink is the product of a reaction between gallic acid (C7H6O5, a polyphenol extracted from oak galls) and iron(II) sulfate (FeSO4, produced when iron dissolves in acid). Oak galls are hard, round growths on oak tree branches caused by wasp larvae — the tree's defensive response concentrates tannic and gallic acids to extraordinary levels, sometimes 50-70% by dry weight.

The core reaction proceeds in two stages.

Stage 1 — Chelation: Gallic acid chelates iron(II) ions to form a soluble iron(II) gallotannate complex. This is the pale grey-purple liquid you see when the two solutions are first mixed.

Stage 2 — Oxidation: Atmospheric oxygen oxidizes the iron(II) to iron(III), producing an insoluble iron(III) gallotannate precipitate that is deep blue-black.

The simplified reaction:

3 C7H6O5 + FeSO4 --> Fe(C7H5O5)3 + H2SO4 (actual complex is polymeric)

Fe2+ (soluble, pale) --[O2]--> Fe3+ (insoluble, black)

The color change is diagnostic: fresh ink appears pale grey-purple on the page. Over the following hours, it darkens progressively to deep black as oxidation proceeds. A document written in the morning will be noticeably darker by evening. Full darkening takes 24-48 hours.

The permanence mechanism is what makes iron gall ink irreplaceable for archival purposes. Unlike carbon ink, which sits as particles on the paper surface and can be rubbed or washed off, iron gall ink is a corrosive chemical complex. When applied to paper, the iron-tannin complex physically burns into the cellulose fibers — it penetrates the fiber structure and bonds chemically with the hydroxyl groups of cellulose. The ink IS the paper at the molecular level. This is why historical documents written in iron gall ink remain perfectly legible after centuries, while carbon-based inks on the same documents have faded or rubbed away.

The acid danger is the other side of this coin. The reaction produces sulfuric acid (H2SO4) as a byproduct, and excess unreacted iron acts as a catalyst for further acid generation. If the iron-to-tannin ratio is too high, the excess acid will literally burn holes through the paper over years. The finished ink should have a pH of 2.5-3.5 — acidic enough to be reactive and permanent, but balanced enough that the acid load does not overwhelm the paper's buffering capacity. If your ink has a strong, sharp metallic smell and looks rusty brown instead of dark purple, it is too iron-heavy. Add more gall extract to bind the excess iron.

Historical Precedent

The technologies in this module are not theoretical — they have been proven across millennia of continuous use.

Papermaking origins: Chinese papermaking is traditionally attributed to Cai Lun, a court official of the Han Dynasty, in 105 CE, though archaeological evidence from Fangmatan and Xuanquanzhi pushes the invention back to the 2nd century BCE. Early Chinese paper used mulberry bark, hemp, and old rags — the same materials recommended in this module.

Japanese washi: Made from kozo (mulberry) bark, washi represents the pinnacle of handmade paper. Some washi specimens have survived over 1,000 years in temple archives, with no significant degradation. The secret is long mulberry fibers (up to 25 mm), alkaline processing with wood ash, and the nagashi-zuki forming technique that layers fibers in multiple directions for superior strength.

Westward spread: Islamic scholars carried papermaking westward from Samarkand (captured from Chinese papermakers in 751 CE) through Baghdad, Cairo, and across North Africa to Spain. The first European paper mill was established in Xativa, Spain, around 1056 CE. Fabriano, Italy (1276) introduced watermarking and gelatin sizing — innovations that improved paper quality and enabled identification of the paper's origin. By the 14th century, paper had largely replaced parchment for everyday documents across Europe.

Iron gall ink was used continuously from approximately the 5th century CE through the 19th century — a span of 1,400 years. Key surviving documents include:

  • The Dead Sea Scrolls (2,000+ years old, written on animal skin with carbon ink) — demonstrating the longevity of carbon ink on parchment
  • The Magna Carta (1215, iron gall ink on vellum) — legible after 800 years
  • Leonardo da Vinci's notebooks (iron gall ink on handmade paper, late 15th century) — perfectly readable after 500 years
  • Bach's musical manuscripts (iron gall ink, 18th century) — still performed from the originals

These are not exceptional cases — they are the expected performance of properly made materials.

Alternative Inks

Not every writing task requires archival permanence. Iron gall ink is slow to prepare, requires specific materials, and its acidity limits the paper it can be used on. For everyday writing — notes, drafts, practice, calculations — simpler inks are appropriate.

Carbon ink is made from lampblack (soot) mixed with water and a binder such as gum arabic or egg white. It is the oldest ink technology (predating iron gall by thousands of years) and the simplest to manufacture. Carbon ink is pH neutral and will not damage paper. Its weakness is that carbon particles sit on the paper surface rather than bonding into it — the ink can smudge when rubbed, wash off if wetted, and flake away over time. Carbon ink is best for everyday writing; iron gall ink is reserved for archival documents.

Walnut ink, made by boiling the green or black hulls of walnuts, produces a warm brown ink that is moderately permanent and pH neutral. It is a good compromise between carbon ink's safety and iron gall's permanence, suitable for documents that need moderate longevity (5-50 years) without the acid risk.

Berry and plant inks (from elderberry, pokeberry, beetroot, etc.) are fugitive — they fade within weeks to months under light exposure. They are not suitable for any purpose that requires permanence. Do not use them for records.

Core Principles

  1. Fiber Length Determines Paper Quality. Long bast fibers from mulberry (15-25 mm), hemp (15-25 mm), and flax (25-65 mm) produce the strongest, most durable paper. Short fibers from wood pulp (1-3 mm) produce weak, brittle sheets. When choosing fiber sources, always prefer bast fibers and textile rags over wood.
  2. Acid Is the Enemy of Paper. Lignin (the brown, rigid polymer in wood) generates acid as it degrades. Excess iron in ink generates acid. Acidic environments accelerate cellulose hydrolysis. Maintain alkaline or neutral pH throughout paper production — add calcium carbonate (ground limestone, chalk) to the pulp at 2-3% by weight. This single step extends paper life from decades to centuries.
  3. Iron Gall for Archives, Carbon for Daily Use. Match ink permanence to document importance. The community's legal records, medical knowledge, engineering specifications, and agricultural calendars must be written in iron gall ink on buffered paper. Practice exercises, personal notes, and temporary records can use carbon ink on any available paper.
  4. The Ratio Determines the Outcome. In iron gall ink, the iron-to-tannin ratio must be carefully balanced. Too much iron produces excess acid that burns through paper over years. Too little iron produces pale, fugitive ink that fades. The target pH of finished ink is 2.5-3.5. Always test ink on a scrap sheet before committing to an archival document.
  5. Dry Storage Is Non-Negotiable. Paper and ink must be stored below 60% relative humidity. Moisture enables mold growth (which digests cellulose) and accelerates acid degradation. Store finished paper and written documents in sealed containers with desiccants (dry rice, charcoal, or quicklime in a porous bag). Elevate storage above ground level to avoid rising damp.

Practical Implementation

Papermaking — Step by Step

1. Fiber Sourcing

Fiber quality is ranked by fiber length and cellulose purity:

Source Fiber Length Quality Notes
Mulberry bark (kozo) 15-25 mm Excellent Japanese washi tradition; finest paper
Flax / linen rags 25-65 mm Excellent Cross-reference Textiles module
Hemp 15-25 mm Very good Fast-growing; strong fibers
Cotton rags 15-40 mm Good Recycled clothing; "rag paper"
Softwood bark 1-3 mm Poor Short fibers; avoid if possible

For bast fibers: harvest inner bark from mulberry, basswood, or hemp stalks. Strip the outer bark, then peel the soft inner bark in long strips. For rag paper: collect worn-out 100% cotton or linen clothing. Cut into 2-3 cm squares. Both sources produce excellent paper.

2. Cooking and Retting

Raw plant fibers contain lignin, pectin, and other non-cellulose materials that must be removed before papermaking. Lignin is the brown, rigid polymer that gives wood its stiffness — if left in the pulp, it generates acid as it degrades, dramatically shortening the paper's lifespan. This is why modern newsprint (which retains most of its lignin) yellows and crumbles within years, while properly processed rag paper lasts centuries.

Preparing the lye: Pour water slowly through a bucket of hardwood ash and collect the runoff. This yields a solution of potassium hydroxide (KOH) at approximately pH 12. Oak, hickory, and maple ash produce the strongest lye. Softwood ash (pine, spruce) is weaker but usable — double the quantity.

The cook: Soak the raw bark strips or rag scraps in water mixed with wood ash lye. Use approximately 15-20 g of dry wood ash per liter of water. Heat the fiber-lye mixture to near-boiling (90-100 degrees C) and maintain for 2-4 hours, stirring occasionally. The alkaline solution dissolves lignin and pectins, leaving nearly pure cellulose.

Signs of completion: The fibers will soften dramatically and change color from brown to pale cream or white. When a strand can be pulled apart easily between two fingers, cooking is complete.

Rinsing: Rinse thoroughly in clean running water — at least three complete water changes. Squeeze and agitate the fibers between rinses. Residual alkali left in the fibers will interfere with ink adhesion and can make the finished paper slippery. The rinse water should run clear and feel neutral (not slippery) to the touch.

3. Beating

The rinsed fibers must be beaten until they separate into individual strands suspended in water. Place a handful of cooked fiber on a flat stone or wooden board. Pound with a wooden mallet — heavy, flat-faced blows, not chopping. Continue for 30-60 minutes per batch.

Beat until fibers are fully separated and individually suspended: test by lifting a handful of pulp from water. The fibers should be translucent and separate freely, not clumpy or stringy. Underbeaten pulp produces weak, uneven sheets. Overbeaten pulp (rare with manual methods) produces dense, inflexible paper.

A trip-hammer powered by a water wheel reduces the labor from hours to minutes and produces more consistent results (cross-reference Mechanical Power module). This is a high-priority mechanical upgrade once the settlement has a functioning water wheel.

4. Vat Preparation

Fill a vat (any large, shallow container — a wooden trough, a halved barrel) with water to a depth of 15-20 cm. Add beaten pulp until the water is cloudy but translucent — approximately 1-2% fiber by weight. Stir gently to distribute fibers evenly. The consistency should resemble thin, cloudy soup.

Add alkaline buffer at this stage: stir in ground limestone or chalk (calcium carbonate, CaCO3) at 2-3% of the dry fiber weight. This buffers against future acid degradation, extending the finished paper's lifespan from decades to centuries.

5. Sheet Forming with the Mould and Deckle

The mould is a wooden frame with a fine mesh stretched across it. The deckle is a matching empty frame that sits on top to contain the pulp and define the sheet edges. Together, these two frames are the most important tools in the papermaking workshop. Guard them carefully — a damaged mesh means no paper production until it is repaired.

Construction: Build the frame from straight-grained hardwood strips, 25 x 35 cm (roughly A4 size). The wood must be smooth and flat — any warping produces uneven sheets. Join the corners with mortise-and-tenon or half-lap joints and seal with waterproof glue or tight lashing.

The mesh: Stretch fine woven fabric or metal screen across the mould frame. Target mesh density: 20-30 threads per cm. Finer mesh produces smoother paper but drains more slowly. An old window screen works well. Tightly stretched pantyhose over a picture frame is a functional emergency substitute for initial experiments. Attach the mesh with small tacks or by wrapping it around the frame edges and securing with cord.

The pull: Stir the vat to resuspend settled fibers. Hold the mould (mesh side up) with the deckle clamped on top. Dip the frame into the vat at a 45-degree angle, submerge fully, then lift out horizontally in a smooth, confident motion. Hesitation produces uneven sheets.

Give the mould a slight shake — forward-back, then side-to-side — to interlock the fibers and even out the sheet. This shaking step is critical: it causes fibers to cross over each other in random directions, producing paper with roughly equal strength in all directions. Without it, fibers tend to align in one direction, and the paper tears easily along that axis.

Allow water to drain through the mesh for 15-30 seconds. The sheet should appear uniform in thickness and translucency when held against the light.

Couching: Remove the deckle. Flip the mould face-down onto a damp felt or smooth cloth. Press gently and lift the mould away — the wet sheet transfers to the felt. Stack multiple sheets separated by felts. If the sheet sticks to the mould rather than releasing to the felt, the felt is too dry — dampen it further.

6. Pressing and Drying

Stack the couched sheets (still on their felts) and press with heavy weight — at minimum 50 kg, ideally more. A simple lever press (a plank with rocks on one end) works. Press for 2-4 hours to expel excess water.

Separate the pressed sheets from their felts carefully. Hang on a line or lay flat on smooth boards to dry for 24-48 hours, depending on humidity. Avoid direct sunlight, which can cause uneven drying and warping.

7. Sizing

Unsized paper ("waterleaf") absorbs ink like a sponge — writing bleeds and spreads, making the text illegible. Sizing seals the paper surface to control ink absorption.

Starch sizing: Boil rice or wheat flour in water (1 part flour to 10 parts water) until a thin, smooth paste forms. Strain through cloth. Brush or dip the dried paper through this solution. Dry again. This produces a slightly stiff, smooth writing surface.

Gelatin sizing: Dissolve animal hide glue or gelatin in warm water (1 part gelatin to 20 parts water). Dip dried paper through the solution. Dry again. Gelatin sizing is the traditional European method (used at Fabriano from the 13th century onward) and produces excellent ink holdout.

Iron Gall Ink — Step by Step

Gall Extract

Collect 30 g of oak galls (approximately 8-12 galls, depending on size). Crush them thoroughly with a rock or hammer — break into fragments no larger than 5 mm. Place the crushed galls in a ceramic or glass container (not metal — the tannins will react). Pour 200 mL of boiling water over the crushed galls. Cover and steep for 24 hours, stirring occasionally. The liquid will turn dark amber-brown. Strain through cloth to remove solid fragments. This is your gall extract.

Iron Solution

Take 10 g of rusty iron nails or scrap iron. Place in a glass or ceramic jar with 100 mL of vinegar (acetic acid, approximately 5% concentration). The acid slowly dissolves the iron:

Fe + 2 CH3COOH --> Fe(CH3COO)2 + H2 (iron acetate + hydrogen gas)

Cover loosely (hydrogen gas must escape) and steep for 2-4 weeks, stirring daily. The liquid will turn dark amber as iron dissolves. When the vinegar tastes strongly metallic and the nails have visibly corroded, the solution is ready. Strain to remove solid fragments.

Mixing

Combine 2 parts gall extract with 1 part iron solution (e.g., 100 mL gall extract + 50 mL iron solution). An immediate color change occurs — the liquid turns deep purplish-black as iron gallotannate forms. Stir slowly and observe: the reaction is visible and dramatic.

Add 5 g of gum arabic (if available) dissolved in a small amount of warm water. If gum arabic is unavailable, use one of these substitutes:

  • Pine resin dissolved in warm water
  • Strained egg white (beaten and settled)
  • Boiled flaxseed mucilage

The binder thickens the ink for smooth flow from pen to paper and helps the ink adhere during the initial drying period before oxidation locks it in place.

Testing

Test immediately: dip a quill or reed pen and write on a scrap of paper. The ink should appear pale grey-purple when wet. Over the following hours, it should darken progressively to deep black. Evaluate after 24 hours:

  • If too pale: add more gall extract (insufficient tannin).
  • If rusty-brown instead of black: too much iron — add more gall extract to bind the excess iron, or dilute with water.
  • If the ink does not flow smoothly: add more binder (gum arabic or egg white).

Shelf life: store in a sealed glass or ceramic container. The ink remains usable for 1-3 months. Discard when mold appears on the surface or the ink develops a foul smell. Do not store in metal containers — the acid will corrode them.

Carbon Ink — Simple Recipe

Carbon ink is the simplest permanent ink and requires no specialized materials.

Collecting lampblack: Hold the bottom of a ceramic plate or metal pot 2-3 cm above a burning candle, oil lamp, or resinous wood flame. Soot (nearly pure carbon) deposits on the cool surface. Scrape off with a knife. Collect approximately 5 g (about 1 teaspoon by volume — lampblack is very light).

Mixing: Combine 1 part soot with 3 parts water in a small container. Add gum arabic or strained egg white as a binder — approximately 5-10% by total volume. Grind the mixture thoroughly with a pestle against a smooth stone for 10-15 minutes. Carbon particles must be fully dispersed — lumpy ink clogs pens and writes unevenly.

Result: A stable, pH-neutral black ink suitable for everyday writing. It sits on the paper surface rather than bonding into the fibers, so it can smudge if rubbed and will wash off if wetted. It is not archival. Use for notes, drafts, practice, and any document that does not require multi-generational permanence.

Storage: Carbon ink settles over time. Store in a sealed container and stir or shake before each use. It does not spoil in the same way as iron gall ink (no mold risk, since there is no organic tannin component), but the binder may degrade after 6-12 months. Refresh by adding a small amount of new binder.

Writing Tools

The quill pen: Select a large flight feather from the leading edge of the wing — goose, turkey, or crow feathers work well. The five outermost primary feathers are strongest. Strip the barbs from the lower 5-7 cm of the shaft. Cure the barrel by plunging it into hot sand (approximately 150 degrees C) for 10 seconds — this hardens the keratin and makes it easier to cut. Cut the nib: slice the tip at a 45-degree angle with a sharp knife. Make a 3-5 mm slit up the center of the cut face for ink flow. Shape the writing tip to desired width — broader for headings, narrower for text. A well-cut quill writes smoothly for 1-2 pages before the nib needs re-cutting.

The reed pen: Cut a section of dry reed or bamboo, approximately 20 cm long and 8-12 mm in diameter. Shape the writing end identically to a quill: 45-degree cut, 3-5 mm center slit. Reed pens are stiffer than quills and produce bolder lines. They wear faster but are easier to replace.

Pen maintenance: Re-cut the nib with a sharp knife when it becomes worn, split beyond the intended slit, or misshapen. A pen knife (the original meaning of the term) should be kept razor-sharp specifically for this purpose. After each writing session, rinse the pen tip in clean water to prevent dried ink from clogging the slit — this is especially important with iron gall ink, which solidifies as it oxidizes.

Ink wells and containers: Use ceramic, glass, or fired clay vessels to hold ink during writing. Do not use metal containers for iron gall ink — the acid will corrode them and alter the ink chemistry. A small, stable vessel with a narrow opening minimizes evaporation and the risk of spills. Cover when not in active use.

Common Failure Modes

1. Ink burning through paper.

Cause: too much iron in the ink produces excess sulfuric acid. The pH drops below 2, and the acid hydrolyzes the cellulose fibers directly under the ink lines. Over months to years, the inked text eats entirely through the paper, leaving lace-like holes where words once were.

Prevention: Maintain a 2:1 ratio of gall extract to iron solution. Test ink pH if possible (target 2.5-3.5). If ink appears rusty-brown and smells sharply metallic, add more gall extract before use. Use alkaline-buffered paper for archival documents.

2. Paper too weak or brittle.

Cause: fibers not beaten long enough. Clumpy, poorly separated pulp produces sheets with large gaps between fibers and few hydrogen bonds. The resulting paper tears easily and has an uneven, rough surface.

Prevention: Beat pulp until fibers are translucent and fully separated when lifted from water. Test a small sheet before committing a full batch — if it tears easily when dry, beat the pulp longer.

3. Ink bleeding and spreading on paper.

Cause: paper not sized. Unsized paper (waterleaf) absorbs liquid freely — ink wicks along fibers and spreads outward from the pen stroke, producing fuzzy, illegible text.

Prevention: Size all paper intended for writing with starch or gelatin solution. Test by placing a drop of water on the paper surface — it should bead and sit, not soak in immediately.

4. Mold on stored paper or documents.

Cause: humidity above 60% relative humidity. Mold spores are ubiquitous and will colonize any cellulose surface that remains damp. Mold digests cellulose, producing brown stains and structural damage.

Prevention: Store paper and documents below 60% RH. Use desiccants (dry rice, charcoal, quicklime in porous bags) in storage containers. Ensure air circulation. If mold appears, isolate affected documents, brush off dry mold outdoors, and move to drier storage immediately.

5. Ink fading over time.

Cause: insufficient iron or tannin concentration — the ink is too dilute to form a durable iron gallotannate complex. Alternatively, using berry or plant-based inks that are inherently fugitive (they decompose under UV light within weeks to months).

Prevention: Use the specified concentrations (30 g galls per 200 mL water; 10 g iron per 100 mL vinegar). Test ink darkness after 24 hours — if it has not darkened to near-black, the ink is too weak. Never use berry or plant inks for any document requiring permanence.

6. Paper yellowing and embrittlement in storage.

Cause: acid degradation. Sources of acid include residual lignin in the pulp (from insufficiently cooked wood-based fiber), excess iron from iron gall ink, and atmospheric pollutants. The acid hydrolyzes cellulose chains, shortening the polymer, reducing fiber strength, and producing chromophores (colored compounds) that cause yellowing.

Prevention: Remove lignin by thorough cooking in alkali (step 2 of papermaking). Add calcium carbonate buffering to pulp (2-3% by weight). Balance iron gall ink properly. Store documents in dry, dark conditions away from heat sources.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

Term Definition
Cellulose The primary structural polymer of plant cell walls; chemical formula (C6H10O5)n; the raw material of all paper
Hydrogen bonding Weak electrostatic attraction between hydroxyl (-OH) groups on adjacent cellulose fibers; the mechanism that holds paper together
Bast fiber Long, strong cellulose fibers from the inner bark (phloem) of plants such as mulberry, hemp, flax, and basswood
Pulp Cellulose fibers suspended in water after beating; the intermediate material from which paper sheets are formed
Mould A flat wooden frame with fine mesh stretched across it; the tool used to form paper sheets from pulp
Deckle An empty frame placed on top of the mould to contain pulp and define sheet edges during forming
Sizing A surface treatment (starch, gelatin) applied to paper to control ink absorption and prevent bleeding
Iron gall ink A permanent writing ink made from the reaction of gallic acid (from oak galls) with iron(II) sulfate; bonds chemically into cellulose fibers
Gallic acid A polyphenol compound (C7H6O5) concentrated in oak galls; one of the two reactants in iron gall ink
Iron sulfate FeSO4 or iron(II) acetate produced by dissolving iron in acid; the second reactant in iron gall ink
Carbon ink A simple ink made from soot (lampblack) suspended in water with a binder; pH neutral but not chemically bonded to paper
Lampblack Nearly pure carbon soot collected from incomplete combustion of oil, wax, or resinous wood; the pigment in carbon ink
Gum arabic A natural polysaccharide gum from acacia trees; used as a binder in both iron gall and carbon inks to improve flow and adhesion
Alkaline buffering The addition of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) to paper pulp to neutralize acids and prevent long-term degradation
Archival Designed or suitable for long-term preservation (decades to centuries); implies acid-free materials and permanent inks
