> SAFE: Site layout planning is observational. When clearing land for zones, exercise standard tool safety with axes and saws. Ensure latrine placement is finalized before any digging to avoid contamination of water sources.

Theoretical Foundation

Settlement layout is not an aesthetic exercise. It is an engineering problem whose currency is calories, whose constraints are gravity and prevailing energy flows, and whose failure mode is starvation or disease. The theoretical foundations draw from permaculture science, historical military engineering, landscape hydrology, and human biomechanics.

Permaculture Zone Theory (Bill Mollison, 1978)

Bill Mollison and David Holmgren formalized the zones-and-sectors model in the late 1970s as the spatial backbone of permaculture design. The central insight is deceptively simple: place elements according to the frequency with which they must be visited. Zone 0 (the dwelling) is visited constantly — it is the gravitational center of the settlement. Zone 5 (wilderness) is visited rarely or never — it is the outermost boundary where human intervention ceases.

Each zone radiates outward from the center in concentric, roughly circular bands (deformed by topography in practice). The optimization principle is to minimize the total distance traveled per day by placing high-frequency-of-use elements closest to the center. This is not a suggestion; it is a mathematical imperative.

Consider the arithmetic. A person visiting a Zone 1 element (20 meters from center) 10 times per day walks:

20m × 2 (round trip) × 10 visits = 400 meters/day

If the same element were misplaced in Zone 3 (200 meters from center):

200m × 2 × 10 visits = 4,000 meters/day

That is a 10× increase in walking distance — and therefore a 10× increase in caloric expenditure on movement alone. Across a settlement of 30 people, each making similar errors, the aggregate waste becomes a significant fraction of total daily food production. Layout is not aesthetics; it is survival arithmetic.

Sector Analysis

While zones organize elements by frequency of human interaction (radiating outward from the center), sectors organize external energies that flow through the site from the outside in. These energies — sunlight, wind, water flow, fire risk, wildlife movement — arrive from predictable compass directions and must be mapped before any permanent element is placed.

Sectors are drawn as pie-slices radiating from the Zone 0 center outward. Each slice represents the path of one external energy across the site. The designer's task is to decide, for each sector, whether to block, channel, or harvest the energy it carries:

  • The cold-wind sector (direction of prevailing winter wind) should be blocked by Zone 4 forest, earth berms, or dense windbreak plantings.
  • The sun sector (south in the Northern Hemisphere, north in the Southern) should be left open and unshaded for Zone 2/3 agriculture and passive solar heating of Zone 0.
  • The fire-risk sector (typically the direction of hot, dry summer wind) requires firebreaks, cleared buffer zones, and fire-resistant species.
  • The water sector (direction of surface and subsurface water flow) should be harvested with swales, dams, and keyline earthworks.

Failure to map sectors before building is one of the most common and costly settlement errors. A dwelling oriented without regard to the cold-wind sector will hemorrhage heat all winter. A garden placed in the shade sector of a hill will never produce adequate yields.

The Keyline System (P.A. Yeomans, 1954)

Percival Alfred Yeomans, an Australian mining engineer turned farmer, developed the keyline system in the 1950s after observing that water distribution across a landscape is inherently uneven. Ridges shed water; valleys concentrate it. The result is that ridge soils are chronically dry while valley soils are waterlogged — neither condition is optimal for cultivation.

The critical concept is the keypoint: the point on a primary valley where the ridge transitions from convex (shedding) to concave (collecting). Above the keypoint, water disperses across the landscape. Below the keypoint, water concentrates into defined drainage lines. The keypoint is therefore the highest point at which water can still be economically collected and stored.

Keyline design works by running cultivation lines (plowing, swales, or planting rows) slightly off-contour from the keypoint outward. These lines drift gently from the valley toward the ridge, carrying water laterally across the slope rather than allowing it to concentrate in the valley bottom. The effect is to redistribute water from wet areas to dry areas, evening out soil moisture across the entire landscape. This prevents both erosion (by slowing water flow) and drought stress (by distributing water to ridge soils that would otherwise be parched).

For a post-infrastructure settlement, keyline principles determine where to place swales, where to orient garden beds, and where to site water storage. The keypoint is the ideal location for a dam or water tank — it is the highest point where gravity will still deliver water to the maximum land area below.

Caloric Geometry

The human body is a heat engine with measurable fuel costs for every activity. Walking on level ground costs approximately 3.3 joules per kilogram of body mass per meter traveled (Margaria's equation, validated by decades of exercise physiology). For a 70-kilogram person:

Energy cost per meter = 3.3 J/kg/m × 70 kg = 231 J/m

A round trip to a water source 500 meters from the kitchen:

231 J/m × 1,000 m (round trip) = 231,000 J = 55.2 kcal

At 6 water-fetching trips per day:

55.2 kcal × 6 = 331 kcal/day

That is 331 kilocalories per day spent by a single person merely walking to and from water — the caloric equivalent of roughly 400 grams of boiled potato, or nearly one-sixth of a typical subsistence-level daily intake of 2,000 kcal. Now multiply by 50 settlement members who must also visit the water point, and the daily caloric waste on water-fetching alone approaches 16,550 kcal — the energy content of approximately 8 kilograms of grain.

This is why layout is not a matter of preference. It is caloric engineering. Every unnecessary meter between high-frequency facilities and the settlement center is a direct subtraction from the food supply. The difference between a well-laid-out settlement and a poorly-laid-out one can be the difference between caloric surplus and caloric deficit — between thriving and slow starvation.

Historical Precedent: The Roman Castrum

The Roman military camp (castrum) is perhaps the most successful standardized settlement layout in history. A trained legion could establish a fully functional camp in 3-4 hours, anywhere in the Empire, because the layout was identical every time:

  • Principia (central command tent/building) = Zone 0
  • Officer quarters and barracks (immediate surroundings) = Zone 1
  • Stables, workshops, granary (support infrastructure) = Zone 2
  • Training ground and parade area (open space) = Zone 3
  • Defensive perimeter (ditch, rampart, palisade) = Zone 4

This standardization meant that every soldier, transferred to any unit at any posting in the Empire, knew exactly where the latrine was, where the water point was, where the medical station was, and where to report in an emergency. No time was wasted learning the layout. No energy was wasted on confusion.

The Aadhiyandham site layout serves the same function: a predictable, optimized template that any member of the settlement can internalize once and apply forever. When a new member arrives, they already know where everything is. When a new settlement is founded, the layout is already designed.

The Energy Audit

Before finalizing any layout, the settlement must perform a quantitative energy audit: calculate the total daily walking distances for all settlement members to all daily-use facilities (water, latrine, kitchen, garden, livestock, workshop, lookout). Rearrange elements until total daily walking is minimized.

This is a physical instantiation of the assignment problem in operations research — assigning facilities to locations such that total transport cost is minimized. In an academic setting, this is solved with the Hungarian algorithm or linear programming. In a field setting, it is solved with stakes, twine, a measuring tape, and arithmetic. The method is different; the mathematical structure is identical.

The audit should be repeated whenever a new permanent element is added to the settlement. A blacksmith forge placed without an energy audit might seem convenient near the timber supply (Zone 4) but will impose enormous walking costs on the smith who must return to Zone 0/1 for meals, water, and sleep multiple times daily. The forge belongs in Zone 2 — close enough for daily access, far enough to manage fire risk and noise.

Core Principles

The following five principles govern all site layout decisions. They are non-negotiable and listed in order of priority.

  1. The Frequency-Distance Rule. Elements visited most frequently must be closest to the settlement center — no exceptions. If an element is visited 10 times per day, it belongs in Zone 1. If it is visited once per week, it belongs in Zone 3 or 4. Violating this rule imposes a caloric tax on every member of the settlement, every day, for the life of the settlement. The most common violation is placing the water source far from the kitchen because "the spring is where the spring is." The correct response is to pipe or channel the water to Zone 1, not to accept the distance.
  2. The Sector Awareness Mandate. Map sun, wind, water flow, and fire risk sectors before placing any permanent element. A building oriented without sector awareness may need to be demolished and rebuilt — an enormous waste of labor and materials. Spend two full days on sector mapping before driving a single stake. Use compass bearings, observe tree growth lean (indicating prevailing wind), note where morning and evening shadows fall in each season, and identify the direction of highest fire risk.
  3. The Gravity-Water Axis. Water source above, sanitation below. The water-to-waste flow must always follow gravity and must never allow waste to contaminate the water source. This is the single most critical public health constraint in settlement layout. The latrine must be downslope and downstream of the water source. Greywater from the kitchen must flow away from, not toward, the water intake point. Violation of this principle has killed more settlements throughout history than any military enemy.
  4. The Keyline Integration. Orient cultivation and water harvesting along keyline contours for maximum water distribution across the landscape. Cultivation rows running straight downhill cause erosion. Rows running exactly on contour cause waterlogging in valleys and drought on ridges. Keyline orientation — slightly off-contour, drifting from valley to ridge — distributes water evenly and builds soil across the entire site.
  5. The Expandable Core. Design Zone 0-1 for the initial group but layout Zone 2-4 boundaries for three times the initial population. Growth must be anticipated in the layout from day one. A settlement of 15 people that lays out its zones for exactly 15 people will hit a carrying capacity ceiling the moment the 16th member arrives. Reserve space. Mark expansion boundaries. Plan access paths to future zones. The cost of planning for expansion is zero; the cost of retrofitting a constrained layout is enormous.

Practical Implementation

Zone Specification Table

Zone Distance from Center Visit Frequency Key Elements Design Priority

Zone 1 0–20 m Multiple times daily Kitchen, water filter (slow sand), herb garden, composting latrine, plant nursery, medical station, tool storage, firewood rack (3-day supply) Minimize walking distance, all-weather path surfaces, lighting for night access
Zone 2 20–100 m Daily Main vegetable beds, small orchard (fruit and nut), poultry housing, greenhouse or cold frame, compost bays, rainwater cistern Soil fertility, irrigation access, pest exclusion fencing
Zone 3 100–500 m Every 2–3 days Staple crops (grain, potato, corn, legumes), large livestock pasture, hay meadow, field-scale composting, grain drying and threshing area Bulk production, rotational grazing layout, vehicle/cart access paths
Zone 4 500 m – 2 km Weekly to monthly Managed woodland (coppice rotations for fuel and construction timber), foraging areas, charcoal production kiln, wild bee habitat, hunting blinds Sustainable harvest rotations, firebreak maintenance, trail marking
Zone 5 > 2 km Seasonal or never Wilderness observation, ecological reserve, seed source for wild species, watershed protection forest No extraction permitted, observation-only access, biodiversity preservation

Note on Zone shapes: Zones are rarely perfect circles. Topography, water flow, and sector energies deform them into irregular shapes. A steep slope compresses zones vertically (shorter walking distances uphill/downhill correspond to greater energy expenditure). A water source on one side of the site pulls Zone 1 toward it. Accept irregular zone shapes; enforce the frequency-distance principle within those shapes.

Sector Mapping Protocol

Perform this protocol before placing any permanent element. Required tools: compass, notebook, pencil, 8 stakes or flags.

Step 1. Stand at the proposed Zone 0 center point. This should be the highest defensible ground with proximity to water — the location determined by the site selection process (see 01_Rationale_and_Importance.md).

Step 2. Using a compass, mark the bearing of each of the following and drive a stake or flag in each direction:

  • Summer sunrise bearing (approximately ENE at 40°N latitude)
  • Winter sunrise bearing (approximately ESE at 40°N latitude)
  • Summer sunset bearing (approximately WNW at 40°N latitude)
  • Winter sunset bearing (approximately WSW at 40°N latitude)
  • Prevailing cold/winter wind direction (ask long-term local residents; observe tree growth lean — trees lean away from prevailing wind; note lichen growth on the windward side of rocks)
  • Prevailing warm/wet wind direction (the direction from which rain typically arrives)
  • Primary water flow direction (downslope toward the nearest watercourse)
  • Fire risk direction (typically the direction of the hottest, driest seasonal wind)

Step 3. On your site plan (a hand-drawn map is sufficient), draw each sector as a pie-slice radiating outward from the Zone 0 center. Label each slice with the energy it carries and its seasonal variation.

Step 4. Apply sector-specific design responses:

Sector Energy Design Response
Cold-wind sector Heat loss, wind chill Place Zone 4 forest or dense windbreak plantings; orient Zone 0 with minimal openings on this face
Sun sector Solar gain, photosynthesis Keep open and unshaded; orient Zone 0 glazing and thermal mass toward this sector; place Zone 2/3 agriculture here
Fire-risk sector Wildfire approach Maintain cleared firebreak (minimum 30 m wide); use fire-resistant species; no fuel storage on this side
Water-flow sector Surface runoff, groundwater Place swales, keyline earthworks, and water storage; orient Zone 2 irrigation to receive this flow
Wet-wind sector Rainfall, moisture Place rainwater catchment and cisterns; orient roof drainage to harvest this sector

The Gravity-Water Layout

DOWNSLOPE (lowest elevation)

Critical rule: Sanitation infrastructure must ALWAYS be downslope and downwind of water sources and habitation areas. There are no circumstances under which this rule may be relaxed. If the only flat ground for a latrine is upslope of the water source, the latrine location is wrong — find another location, even if it requires more walking distance. The Frequency-Distance Rule is subordinate to the Gravity-Water Axis when the two conflict. A latrine that is 200 meters away but downslope is infinitely better than a latrine that is 20 meters away but upslope of the water intake.

Minimum separation distances:

  • Latrine to water source: minimum 30 meters horizontal distance AND downslope
  • Latrine to kitchen/food preparation: minimum 30 meters AND downwind
  • Greywater discharge to water intake: minimum 50 meters downstream
  • Livestock enclosure to water intake: minimum 50 meters AND downslope

The First-Week Layout Protocol

When arriving at a new site, follow this seven-day protocol to establish the initial layout. Do not skip days or reorder steps. Each day builds on the previous.

Day 1: Water and Temporary Camp.

Identify the primary water source (spring, stream, or groundwater seep). Establish a temporary camp at a safe distance (minimum 50 meters) from the water source, on higher ground if possible. Do not dig, build, or clear anything permanent. Observe the site. Note wind direction, sun path, slope, drainage patterns. Boil all water before drinking.

Day 2: Zone 0-1 Boundaries.

Pace out and stake the Zone 0 center (where the permanent dwelling will be built) and the Zone 1 perimeter (20-meter radius). Use twine between stakes to visualize the boundary. Verify that the Zone 0 position satisfies: (a) proximity to water source, (b) defensible ground, (c) solar exposure in the sun sector, (d) shelter from the cold-wind sector.

Day 3: Latrine Establishment.

Dig the initial composting latrine in its permanent position — downslope of the water source, downslope of Zone 0/1, downwind of habitation. This is the first permanent installation because sanitation cannot wait and because its position constrains all other layout decisions. A latrine in the wrong place will need to be relocated — an unpleasant and labor-intensive task.

Day 4: Kitchen and Water Path.

Establish the kitchen area within Zone 1 with a cleared, level work surface. Clear and improve the path between the kitchen and the water source. If the water source is more than 50 meters away, begin planning a gravity-fed pipe or channel. The kitchen-to-water path will be the most-traveled route in the settlement; it must be reliable in all weather conditions.

Day 5: Zone 0 Clearing.

Clear and level the Zone 0 area for temporary shelter (tarp, tent, or lean-to). The permanent structure will come later (see Module 05), but a dry, wind-protected sleeping area is essential from the first week. Orient the shelter opening away from the cold-wind sector and toward the sun sector.

Day 6: Zone 2 Preparation.

If the season permits planting, begin Zone 2 bed preparation. Clear vegetation, mark bed boundaries, and begin soil improvement (composting, mulching). Even if planting is months away, bed preparation now saves critical time when the planting window opens. Prioritize beds in the sun sector.

Day 7: Sector Map and Layout Finalization.

Complete the full sector mapping protocol (see above). Draw the permanent layout plan on paper, including all zone boundaries, sector overlays, water flow paths, and expansion areas for Zone 2-4. This plan becomes the settlement's master document. All future construction and planting decisions reference this plan.

Common Failure Modes

These are the five most frequent and most consequential errors in settlement layout. Each has caused settlement failures historically, and each is entirely preventable through proper planning.

  1. Latrine upslope of water source. Fecal contamination of the water supply is the single most common settlement-killing error in human history. Fecal pathogens (Vibrio cholerae, Salmonella typhi, Entamoeba histolytica, hepatitis A virus) travel with water flow. A latrine upslope of a spring or stream intake will eventually — not "might," but "will" — contaminate the drinking water. The resulting dysentery or cholera outbreak can incapacitate or kill a majority of the settlement within weeks. There is no acceptable reason to place sanitation upslope of water. None.
  2. Kitchen far from water source. Water is the most frequently transported substance in any settlement. Cooking, cleaning, drinking, and food preservation all require water at the kitchen. If the kitchen is 200 meters from the water source, each round trip costs approximately 22 kcal. At 6 trips per day, that is 132 kcal per person per day — the caloric content of roughly 160 grams of boiled potato, wasted on walking. For a settlement of 30 people, the aggregate daily waste is 3,960 kcal. The kitchen must be within 20 meters of the water delivery point (pipe outlet, cistern, or filter).
  3. Ignoring the cold-wind sector. A settlement that does not map and block the prevailing cold winter wind will suffer enormous heat losses from habitation structures. Wind speed of 20 km/h across an unprotected structure can increase heat loss by 50-70% compared to a sheltered structure. This translates directly into increased firewood consumption — potentially doubling the fuel requirement for winter heating. The cold-wind sector must be blocked by Zone 4 forest, earth berms, stone walls, or dense hedgerow plantings before the first winter.
  4. No expansion plan. A settlement that designs its layout for exactly the current population has no margin for growth. When new members arrive, or when births increase the population, there is nowhere to expand Zone 2 gardens or Zone 3 fields without encroaching on other zones. The result is a carrying capacity ceiling that forces painful choices: refuse new members, reduce per-capita food allocation, or demolish and rebuild portions of the settlement. All three options are costly. The solution is to design Zone 2-4 boundaries for three times the initial population from the start.
  5. Aesthetic layout over functional layout. The temptation to arrange a settlement "attractively" — symmetrical paths, decorative plantings, visually balanced structures — is strong and must be resisted. Every meter of unnecessary daily walking imposed by an aesthetic choice is wasted food. A winding path that adds 30 meters to the kitchen-to-water route costs the settlement approximately 600 kcal per day (across 30 people at 6 trips each). That is 219,000 kcal per year — the caloric equivalent of approximately 110 kilograms of wheat. Beauty is a luxury of surplus. Optimize for survival first; add aesthetic elements only where they impose zero caloric cost.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Permaculture Zone: A spatial classification system that organizes settlement elements into concentric bands (Zone 0 through Zone 5) based on the frequency of human interaction required. Elements needing constant attention are placed in inner zones; elements needing minimal attention are placed in outer zones.
  • Sector Analysis: The mapping of external energy flows (sun, wind, water, fire, wildlife) across a site as directional pie-slices radiating from the settlement center. Each sector is managed by blocking, channeling, or harvesting the energy it carries.
  • Keyline: A cultivation or earthwork line oriented slightly off-contour, running from a valley toward a ridge, designed to redistribute water laterally across a slope. Keyline orientation moves water from wet areas to dry areas, evening out soil moisture.
  • Keypoint: The specific point on a primary valley where the ridge profile transitions from convex (water-shedding) to concave (water-collecting). The keypoint is the optimal location for water storage infrastructure because it is the highest point from which gravity will distribute water across the maximum land area.
  • Caloric Geometry: The quantitative analysis of settlement layout in terms of human energy expenditure. Every meter of walking costs a calculable number of calories; layout optimization seeks to minimize total daily caloric expenditure on movement.
  • Gravity-Water Axis: The non-negotiable principle that water collection must be upslope of habitation and sanitation must be downslope, ensuring that the flow of water through the settlement follows gravity and never carries contamination toward the water source.
  • Coppice: A woodland management technique in which trees are cut near the base and allowed to regrow from the stump. Coppiced trees produce poles, fuel wood, and construction material on 5-20 year rotation cycles without requiring replanting. Common coppice species include hazel, willow, chestnut, and oak.
  • Swale: A shallow, level-bottomed trench dug on or near contour to intercept surface water runoff, slow it, and allow it to infiltrate the soil. Swales recharge groundwater, reduce erosion, and create moist planting zones on their downslope berms.
  • Greywater: Wastewater from kitchen sinks, washing, and bathing that does not contain fecal matter. Greywater can be safely used for garden irrigation after basic filtration (grease trap and sand filter). It contains nutrients (phosphorus, nitrogen from soap and food residues) beneficial to plants.
  • Blackwater: Wastewater containing fecal matter and urine from latrines. Blackwater must be treated through composting, anaerobic digestion, or constructed wetlands before any contact with food production areas. Untreated blackwater is the primary vector for waterborne disease.
  • Principia (Roman Camp Center): The central headquarters building of a Roman military camp (castrum), analogous to Zone 0 in the permaculture model. The principia housed the command staff, the standards, and the treasury — the most critical and most frequently accessed elements of the camp.
  • Energy Audit: A quantitative assessment of total daily caloric expenditure on movement within a settlement, calculated by summing the walking distances and trip frequencies for all members to all facilities. Used to compare alternative layouts and identify placement inefficiencies.

  • Holmgren, David. Permaculture: Principles and Pathways Beyond Sustainability. Holmgren Design Services, 2002. — Updates and extends Mollison's framework with 12 design principles; Chapter 7 addresses spatial design.
  • Vegetius (Flavius Vegetius Renatus). De Re Militari (Concerning Military Matters). 4th century CE. — The primary surviving source on Roman military camp (castrum) layout; Book I describes the standardized camp plan that is the historical precedent for zone-based settlement design.
