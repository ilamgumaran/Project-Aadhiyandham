Hydrological Analysis: Watershed Math and Drought Resilience

Alignment: Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

Index > 02 The Site > 05 Hydrological Analysis and Watershed Math

1. Why This Is Important (The Rationale)

A settlement is defined by its water floor—the absolute minimum amount of water available during the peak of a 100-year drought. Relying on "current" flow is a fatal design flaw. This detailed study provides the mathematical framework to calculate carrying capacity based on hydrological reality, ensuring the settlement never outgrows its lifeblood.

1.1. Measuring Supply: The Bucket-and-Stopwatch Method

To determine flow rate: Divert flow into a known volume bucket (V) and time how many seconds (S) it takes to fill.

2. Calculating Your Water Floor

2.1. The Seasonal Decay Factor

Springs fluctuate. To find your Drought Floor, measure the LPM at the end of the dry season and apply the Aadhiyandham Margin of Safety (0.4).

Drought Floor = Dry Season LPM * 0.4

This ensures that even if the spring drops by 60% in a historic drought, the settlement survives.

3. Watershed Math: Carrying Capacity

How many people can your land support?

3.1. Individual Consumption Baseline

  • Survival (Minimum): 15 Liters/Day (Drinking, Cooking, Basic Hygiene).
  • Resilience (Standard): 50 Liters/Day (Includes small-scale garden and livestock).
  • Abundance (Outcome 6): 100 Liters/Day (Full permaculture and mechanical power).

3.2. The Capacity Formula

Total People = (Drought Floor LPM * 1440) / 50 Liters

*1440 is the number of minutes in a day.

4. Reservoir Sizing for Intermittent Sources

If your source is rain-fed or an intermittent stream, you must build a "Buffer" (The Reservoir).

4.1. The 90-Day Rule

A resilient settlement must have 90 days of storage in reserve.

Reservoir Size (Liters) = (Population * 50) * 90

4.2. Silt Trap and Reservoir Design

The Silt Trap catches sediment before it enters the deep reservoir. Duckweed prevents evaporation loss.

5. 🔬 Scientific Validation: Liebig’s Law of the Minimum

Hydrological resilience is the ultimate expression of Liebig's Law. It states that growth is dictated not by total resources available, but by the scarcest resource (the limiting factor). In any refugia, the water floor is the limiting factor. If your soil can support 1,000 people but your water can only support 100, your carrying capacity is 100.

6. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

6.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Phase 1: Measurement: Perform the LPM test every month for one full year. Record the data in the settlement's physical Archive (Module 10).
  • Phase 2: Drought Simulation: Calculate your population limit based on the lowest recorded month.
  • Phase 3: Storage Construction: If the drought floor is below 20 LPM, prioritize building a stone-and-clay lined reservoir before increasing the population.

6.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes

  • Siltation: If the reservoir fills with mud, its capacity drops. Build a "Silt Trap"—a smaller pond before the main reservoir where water slows down and drops its sediment.

6.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • The Water Warden: Appoint a member to track seasonal flows and maintain the silt traps. Knowledge of the water's rhythm is the most valuable intelligence in the refugia.

Vocabulary and Definitions

Aadhiyandham Margin of Safety
A 0.4 multiplier applied to dry-season water flow to account for extreme 100-year drought events.
Drought Floor
The absolute minimum water flow expected during a historic drought.
Liebig’s Law of the Minimum
The principle that growth is limited by the scarcest resource, not the total resources available.
LPM
Liters Per Minute.
Silt Trap
A pre-reservoir basin designed to slow water flow so sediment can settle before entering the main storage.
Watershed
The area of land that drains all the streams and rainfall to a common outlet.
