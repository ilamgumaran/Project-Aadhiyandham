1. Why This Is Important

The existing framework acknowledges climate change over a "300-500 year horizon" but never quantifies what changes would force abandonment versus adaptation. This is the difference between a plan and a wish. A community needs hard numbers — temperature ceilings, precipitation floors, growing-season minimums — so that when conditions shift, they know whether they are within the envelope of adaptation or approaching the boundary of viability.

Without these thresholds, a community may slowly starve over 20 years without recognizing the cause until it is too late to relocate.

2. Expected Outcome

A site-specific climate envelope with quantified thresholds for human survival, crop viability, and water security. A monitoring protocol that provides 5-10 years of advance warning before any threshold is breached.

3. The Human Survival Envelope

3.1. Temperature Thresholds

Parameter Threshold Consequence if Breached
Wet-bulb temperature 35°C (95°F) sustained for 6+ hours Lethal. Human body cannot shed heat via sweating. Healthy adults die within hours. No adaptation possible.
Sustained summer high >45°C (113°F) dry bulb for 3+ consecutive days Heat stroke risk even with shade and water. Outdoor labor impossible.
Winter sustained low <-30°C (-22°F) for 2+ weeks Frostbite risk at any exposed skin in minutes. Firewood consumption exceeds sustainable harvest.
Diurnal swing >30°C (54°F) difference between day and night Material stress (cracking mortar, splitting timber). Crop root damage from freeze-thaw.

3.2. The Wet-Bulb Test (Zero-Tech)

Wet-bulb temperature measures how effectively sweat can cool you. It combines heat AND humidity into one survival metric.

To measure without instruments:

  1. Wrap a wet cloth around the bulb of any liquid thermometer (if scavenged), or:
  2. The body test: If you are sitting in full shade with a breeze, drinking water, and wearing wet clothing, and you are still getting hotter — the wet-bulb temperature has likely exceeded 35°C. Evacuate to a cave, root cellar, or body of water immediately.
  3. The evaporation test: Hang a wet cloth in shade with airflow. If it fails to dry noticeably in 2 hours during peak heat, humidity is dangerously high. Combined with temperatures above 40°C dry bulb, the wet-bulb is approaching lethal thresholds.

3.3. Recording Temperature Without Thermometers

The shadow-length calendar method:

A vertical stick of fixed height (1m) casts shadows whose length correlates with sun angle and therefore season and temperature patterns. Record the noon shadow length on the same date each year:

  • If the summer noon shadow is getting shorter year over year (sun higher in sky), the latitude has not changed — but if paired with phenological indicators showing earlier spring, this confirms warming.
  • If the winter noon shadow is getting longer year over year, winters may be getting colder (or cloud cover is increasing, blocking solar heating).

Biological thermometers:

  • Cricket chirp rate (Dolbear's Law): Count chirps in 15 seconds, add 40 = approximate temperature in °F. Record weekly throughout summer. Multi-year trends reveal warming.
  • Lake/pond ice duration: Record the first date of full freeze and first date of ice-out each year. Shortening ice duration = warming trend.
  • Snow line elevation: Each winter, mark the lowest elevation where snow persists for 24+ hours. Rising snow line = warming.

4.2. The Frost-Free Day Record

How to track frost-free days without a thermometer:

  1. Last spring frost: The morning when the first delicate annual seedlings (beans, squash) survive overnight without wilting or blackening.
  2. First autumn frost: The morning when the first visible ice crystals appear on grass or puddles.
  3. Record both dates annually. The gap between them is the frost-free season.

Warning thresholds:

  • If the frost-free season shortens by more than 10 days over a 5-year period: reassess crop selection. Switch to shorter-season varieties.
  • If the frost-free season shortens by more than 30 days over a 10-year period: the site may be transitioning to a climate zone that cannot support the current staple crops. Begin scouting alternative sites at lower elevation or latitude.

4.3. The Crop Failure Early Warning System

Track these biological indicators annually:

Indicator What It Predicts Warning Threshold
First tree-bud date (same species, same tree each year) Growing season start Shift of >5 days over 3 years = climate change signal
Pollinator arrival date (first bee/butterfly of season) Pollination window alignment If pollinators arrive >7 days after flowers bloom = phenological mismatch; fruit/seed set will decline
Grain fill weight (weigh 100 seeds of the same crop annually) Soil nutrition + climate stress >10% decline in 3 years = combined stress; investigate whether heat, drought, or depletion
Pest emergence date Pest pressure trajectory If pests appear >10 days earlier than historical baseline, warming is accelerating their breeding cycle

5. The Water Security Envelope

5.1. Precipitation Thresholds

Parameter Minimum Consequence if Breached
Annual precipitation 500mm (20 inches) for rain-fed agriculture without irrigation Below this, only irrigated crops survive. Arid-adapted species only.
Driest month precipitation >25mm (1 inch) Below this for 3+ consecutive months = seasonal drought requiring stored water
90-day drought tolerance Per the reservoir calculation in Module 05 If the dry season exceeds 90 days AND spring/stream flow drops below drought floor = water emergency

5.2. The Rain Gauge (Zero-Tech)

Construct a straight-sided, flat-bottomed vessel (a cut section of bamboo, a clay cylinder, or a scavenged tin can). Place it in an open area away from trees.

  • After each rain event, measure the water depth with a marked stick.
  • 1 cm depth in the vessel = 10 mm of rainfall (assuming the vessel opening equals the measurement stick graduation).
  • Record every rain event. Sum monthly. Sum annually.

5.3. Stream Flow Monitoring

At a fixed narrow point in the primary water source:

  1. Mark two points exactly 10 meters apart along the bank.
  2. Drop a floating stick at the upstream mark. Time how many seconds it takes to reach the downstream mark.
  3. Velocity (m/s) = 10 / seconds.
  4. Estimate the cross-sectional area of the stream at the measurement point (width × average depth).
  5. Flow (liters/second) = Velocity × Area × 1000 (converting cubic meters to liters).

Record this measurement at the same date each month. The driest month reading is the drought floor referenced in Module 05.

Warning thresholds:

  • Drought floor declining >15% over 5 years: upstream watershed is degrading (deforestation, aquifer depletion) or precipitation is declining.
  • Drought floor declining >30% over 10 years: water sovereignty is at risk. Investigate cause. If caused by regional precipitation decline, begin planning relocation or supplementary water infrastructure (additional reservoirs, deeper wells).

6. The Climate Envelope Scorecard

At site selection and every 5 years thereafter, score the site against these pass/fail criteria:

Parameter Pass Fail
Wet-bulb ever exceeds 35°C No recorded event Any single event
Frost-free season >90 days <70 days (cannot grow any staple crop)
Annual precipitation >500mm <400mm for 2 consecutive years
Drought floor stream flow Supports >50 L/person/day Falls below 15 L/person/day
Winter sustained cold >-30°C minimum <-40°C sustained for 1+ week
Phenological mismatch <5 day pollinator/bloom gap >10 day gap for 3+ years
Crop seed weight trend Stable or increasing >15% decline over 5 years

Scoring:

  • All Pass: Site is within the climate envelope. Continue operations.
  • 1-2 Fail: Site is under climate stress. Implement mitigation (crop switching, water storage expansion, passive cooling structures). Reassess in 2 years.
  • 3+ Fail: Site is approaching the boundary of viability. Begin active scouting of alternative locations while current site can still sustain the community.

7. Latitude-Specific Design Adjustments

The existing modules assume a generic "temperate" climate. These corrections address the fact that a site at 25° latitude and a site at 55° latitude require fundamentally different designs:

7.1. Solar Angle Corrections

Latitude Winter Solstice Sun Angle Equator-Facing Window Tilt Roof Overhang for Summer Shade
25° (subtropical) 41.5° Near-vertical windows; low angle sun penetrates deep 1.5m overhang needed
35° (warm temperate) 31.5° 60° tilt from horizontal 1.0m overhang
45° (cool temperate) 21.5° 45° tilt 0.6m overhang
55° (boreal) 11.5° 30° tilt; maximize any available winter light 0.3m overhang (summer sun is low-angle but long-duration)

7.2. Heating Season Length

Latitude Range Approximate Heating Season Firewood Requirement (per household)
25-30° 0-2 months 1-2 cords
30-40° 3-5 months 3-5 cords
40-50° 5-7 months 5-8 cords
50-60° 7-10 months 8-12 cords

1 cord = 3.6 cubic meters of stacked firewood. At 50° latitude, a single household burns approximately 25-45 cubic meters of wood per year. This defines the minimum forest area required for sustainable harvest (see Section 02, Topographical Resilience, 1.5 acres/person for timber).

7.3. Hemisphere Corrections

All solar design in the existing modules assumes Northern Hemisphere. For Southern Hemisphere sites:

  • Equator-facing = NORTH-facing (not south-facing).
  • Polaris is not visible; use the Southern Cross to find celestial south.
  • Magnetic declination patterns are different; recalibrate per Module 08 protocol.
  • Seasons are inverted; adjust all phenological observation dates by 6 months.

8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Wet-Bulb Temperature: The lowest temperature to which air can be cooled by evaporating water into it. At 35°C wet-bulb, human thermoregulation fails regardless of fitness, shade, or hydration.
  • Climate Envelope: The set of temperature, precipitation, and seasonal parameters within which a community can sustain itself. Exceeding the envelope requires adaptation, relocation, or death.
  • Phenological Mismatch: A growing temporal gap between interdependent biological events (e.g., flowers blooming before pollinators emerge) caused by differential responses to climate change.
  • Drought Floor: The minimum stream flow observed during the driest period of record, used as the baseline for water infrastructure sizing.

9. Scientific Validation & Research Context

  • Wet-Bulb Lethality Threshold: Sherwood & Huber (2010, PNAS) established 35°C wet-bulb as the absolute upper limit for human survival. More recent research (Raymond et al., 2020, Science Advances) has documented wet-bulb events approaching this threshold in South Asia and the Persian Gulf, confirming the real-world relevance of this metric.
  • Frost-Free Day Trends: NOAA's Climate Extremes Index shows that over the past 50 years, the frost-free season in the continental US has lengthened by approximately 2 weeks, with corresponding shifts in USDA Plant Hardiness Zones. This rate of change (approximately 3 days per decade) calibrates the warning thresholds used in this module.
  • Cricket Thermometry: Dolbear's Law (1897) has been independently validated within ±1°F accuracy for the snowy tree cricket (Oecanthus fultoni) across temperate North America, making it a reliable zero-tech temperature proxy.
  • Phenological Mismatch: Kudo & Ida (2013, Ecology Letters) demonstrated that a 7-day pollinator-bloom mismatch reduces seed set by 30-50% in alpine ecosystems, establishing the empirical basis for the 7-day warning threshold used here.

10. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

10.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Year 1: Install a rain gauge, establish the stream flow measurement point, identify the phenological indicator tree and insect species for your latitude. Record the frost-free season. Measure noon shadow length on both equinoxes and both solstices. This is your baseline.
  • Year 2-5: Continue all measurements. By Year 3, you have enough data to calculate trends. By Year 5, you can complete the first Climate Envelope Scorecard.
  • Year 5+: Compare the scorecard to baseline. Adjust crop selection and water infrastructure as needed. Share your data with connected refugia to build a regional climate picture.

10.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes

  • Cannot measure wet-bulb directly: Use the body test (Section 3.2). If it fails, you are in immediate danger regardless of the precise number.
  • Only 2 years of data: Too few to establish trends. Do NOT make major decisions (relocation, crop abandonment) based on less than 5 years of observations. Weather varies year to year; climate is the trend.
  • Data recorder dies or leaves: This is the most common failure mode. Ensure 2 people maintain independent records. Cross-check annually.

10.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: The climate observer role requires literacy, patience, and discipline. Assign it to someone intrinsically curious about weather. Pair them with a backup observer from the next generation by Year 5.
  • The Weather Stone (Archive): Inscribe the annual summary data (frost-free days, total rainfall, drought floor, phenological dates) onto a permanent stone tablet installed in a public location. Over decades, this becomes the community's most valuable scientific instrument.
