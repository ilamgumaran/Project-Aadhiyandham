Theoretical Foundation

The existing framework acknowledges climate change over a "300-500 year horizon" but never quantifies what changes would force abandonment versus adaptation. This is the difference between a plan and a wish. A community needs hard numbers — temperature ceilings, precipitation floors, growing-season minimums — so that when conditions shift, they know whether they are within the envelope of adaptation or approaching the boundary of viability.

Without these thresholds, a community may slowly starve over 20 years without recognizing the cause until it is too late to relocate.

The climate envelope concept formalized. Every species (including humans) has a multidimensional climate envelope defined by temperature range, precipitation range, seasonality, and extreme event frequency. When conditions move outside this envelope, the population must adapt (change behavior/crops), migrate (relocate), or die. The thresholds in this module define the envelope for a self-sufficient agricultural settlement.

Climate inertia and lag. Even if global emissions stopped today, committed warming of 0.5-1.0 degrees C over the next 50 years is locked in due to ocean thermal inertia. Planning must account for conditions that will be different from today even in the best-case scenario. The thermal mass of the oceans absorbs heat slowly and releases it slowly; the warming already absorbed but not yet expressed at the surface guarantees future change regardless of human action. This means site selection must target conditions viable not only today but under an additional 0.5-1.0 degrees C of warming.

Expected outcome. A site-specific climate envelope with quantified thresholds for human survival, crop viability, and water security. A monitoring protocol that provides 5-10 years of advance warning before any threshold is breached.

Scientific basis. The thresholds in this module draw on established research: Sherwood & Huber (2010, PNAS) established 35 degrees C wet-bulb as the absolute upper limit for human survival. Raymond et al. (2020, Science Advances) documented wet-bulb events approaching this threshold in South Asia and the Persian Gulf. NOAA's Climate Extremes Index shows the frost-free season in the continental US lengthened by approximately 2 weeks over 50 years (approximately 3 days per decade), calibrating the warning thresholds used here. Dolbear's Law (1897) has been independently validated within plus or minus 1 degree F accuracy for the snowy tree cricket across temperate North America, making it a reliable zero-tech temperature proxy. Kudo & Ida (2013, Ecology Letters) demonstrated that a 7-day pollinator-bloom mismatch reduces seed set by 30-50% in alpine ecosystems, establishing the empirical basis for the 7-day warning threshold.

Core Principles

  1. The Envelope, Not the Average. Design for extreme events, not mean conditions. A single wet-bulb 35 degrees C event can kill, regardless of average temperatures. A single 90-day drought can destroy a harvest, regardless of annual precipitation averages. The scorecard in this module tests extremes, not means.
  2. The 5-Year Baseline Rule. No major decisions on less than 5 years of continuous data. Weather varies year to year; climate is the trend. A single cold summer does not mean cooling, and a single dry year does not mean desertification. Only multi-year patterns justify action.
  3. The Early Warning Doctrine. Monitor for threshold approach, not threshold breach. 5 years of warning allows adaptation — crop switching, water infrastructure expansion, scouting alternative sites. 0 years of warning forces crisis — starvation, dehydration, forced migration without preparation.
  4. The Crop Portfolio Strategy. Maintain 3 or more staple crops spanning different climate tolerances. If warming kills the cold-adapted crop, the heat-tolerant one may survive. If drought kills the water-dependent crop, the drought-resistant one carries the community. Monoculture is a bet; polyculture is insurance.
  5. The Relocation Trigger. Define in advance what conditions will trigger site abandonment. Deciding during a crisis — when food stores are depleted and morale is broken — is too late. The Climate Envelope Scorecard provides the trigger: 3 or more failures means active scouting begins while the community can still sustain the effort.

Practical Implementation

The Human Survival Envelope

Temperature Thresholds

Parameter Threshold Consequence if Breached
Wet-bulb temperature 35 degrees C (95 degrees F) sustained for 6+ hours Lethal. Human body cannot shed heat via sweating. Healthy adults die within hours. No adaptation possible.
Sustained summer high >45 degrees C (113 degrees F) dry bulb for 3+ consecutive days Heat stroke risk even with shade and water. Outdoor labor impossible.
Winter sustained low <-30 degrees C (-22 degrees F) for 2+ weeks Frostbite risk at any exposed skin in minutes. Firewood consumption exceeds sustainable harvest.
Diurnal swing >30 degrees C (54 degrees F) difference between day and night Material stress (cracking mortar, splitting timber). Crop root damage from freeze-thaw.

The Wet-Bulb Test (Zero-Tech)

Wet-bulb temperature measures how effectively sweat can cool you. It combines heat AND humidity into one survival metric.

To measure without instruments:

  1. Wrap a wet cloth around the bulb of any liquid thermometer (if scavenged), or:
  2. The body test: If you are sitting in full shade with a breeze, drinking water, and wearing wet clothing, and you are still getting hotter — the wet-bulb temperature has likely exceeded 35 degrees C. Evacuate to a cave, root cellar, or body of water immediately.
  3. The evaporation test: Hang a wet cloth in shade with airflow. If it fails to dry noticeably in 2 hours during peak heat, humidity is dangerously high. Combined with temperatures above 40 degrees C dry bulb, the wet-bulb is approaching lethal thresholds.

Recording Temperature Without Thermometers

The shadow-length calendar method:

A vertical stick of fixed height (1m) casts shadows whose length correlates with sun angle and therefore season and temperature patterns. Record the noon shadow length on the same date each year:

  • If the summer noon shadow is getting shorter year over year (sun higher in sky), the latitude has not changed — but if paired with phenological indicators showing earlier spring, this confirms warming.
  • If the winter noon shadow is getting longer year over year, winters may be getting colder (or cloud cover is increasing, blocking solar heating).

Biological thermometers:

  • Cricket chirp rate (Dolbear's Law): Count chirps in 15 seconds, add 40 = approximate temperature in degrees F. Record weekly throughout summer. Multi-year trends reveal warming.
  • Lake/pond ice duration: Record the first date of full freeze and first date of ice-out each year. Shortening ice duration = warming trend.
  • Snow line elevation: Each winter, mark the lowest elevation where snow persists for 24+ hours. Rising snow line = warming.

The Frost-Free Day Record

How to track frost-free days without a thermometer:

  1. Last spring frost: The morning when the first delicate annual seedlings (beans, squash) survive overnight without wilting or blackening.
  2. First autumn frost: The morning when the first visible ice crystals appear on grass or puddles.
  3. Record both dates annually. The gap between them is the frost-free season.

Warning thresholds:

  • If the frost-free season shortens by more than 10 days over a 5-year period: reassess crop selection. Switch to shorter-season varieties.
  • If the frost-free season shortens by more than 30 days over a 10-year period: the site may be transitioning to a climate zone that cannot support the current staple crops. Begin scouting alternative sites at lower elevation or latitude.

The Rain Gauge (Zero-Tech)

Construct a straight-sided, flat-bottomed vessel (a cut section of bamboo, a clay cylinder, or a scavenged tin can). Place it in an open area away from trees.

  • After each rain event, measure the water depth with a marked stick.
  • 1 cm depth in the vessel = 10 mm of rainfall (assuming the vessel opening equals the measurement stick graduation).
  • Record every rain event. Sum monthly. Sum annually.

Stream Flow Monitoring

At a fixed narrow point in the primary water source:

  1. Mark two points exactly 10 meters apart along the bank.
  2. Drop a floating stick at the upstream mark. Time how many seconds it takes to reach the downstream mark.
  3. Velocity (m/s) = 10 / seconds.
  4. Estimate the cross-sectional area of the stream at the measurement point (width x average depth).
  5. Flow (liters/second) = Velocity x Area x 1000 (converting cubic meters to liters).

Record this measurement at the same date each month. The driest month reading is the drought floor referenced in Module 05.

Warning thresholds:

  • Drought floor declining >15% over 5 years: upstream watershed is degrading (deforestation, aquifer depletion) or precipitation is declining.
  • Drought floor declining >30% over 10 years: water sovereignty is at risk. Investigate cause. If caused by regional precipitation decline, begin planning relocation or supplementary water infrastructure (additional reservoirs, deeper wells).

The Climate Envelope Scorecard

At site selection and every 5 years thereafter, score the site against these pass/fail criteria:

Parameter Pass Fail
Wet-bulb ever exceeds 35 degrees C No recorded event Any single event
Frost-free season >90 days <70 days (cannot grow any staple crop)
Annual precipitation >500mm <400mm for 2 consecutive years
Drought floor stream flow Supports >50 L/person/day Falls below 15 L/person/day
Winter sustained cold >-30 degrees C minimum <-40 degrees C sustained for 1+ week
Phenological mismatch <5 day pollinator/bloom gap >10 day gap for 3+ years
Crop seed weight trend Stable or increasing >15% decline over 5 years

Scoring:

  • All Pass: Site is within the climate envelope. Continue operations.
  • 1-2 Fail: Site is under climate stress. Implement mitigation (crop switching, water storage expansion, passive cooling structures). Reassess in 2 years.
  • 3+ Fail: Site is approaching the boundary of viability. Begin active scouting of alternative locations while current site can still sustain the community.

1 cord = 3.6 cubic meters of stacked firewood. At 50 degrees latitude, a single household burns approximately 25-45 cubic meters of wood per year. This defines the minimum forest area required for sustainable harvest (see Section 02, Topographical Resilience, 1.5 acres/person for timber).

Hemisphere Corrections

All solar design in the existing modules assumes Northern Hemisphere. For Southern Hemisphere sites:

  • Equator-facing = NORTH-facing (not south-facing).
  • Polaris is not visible; use the Southern Cross to find celestial south.
  • Magnetic declination patterns are different; recalibrate per Module 08 protocol.
  • Seasons are inverted; adjust all phenological observation dates by 6 months.

Implementation Timeline

  • Year 1: Install a rain gauge, establish the stream flow measurement point, identify the phenological indicator tree and insect species for your latitude. Record the frost-free season. Measure noon shadow length on both equinoxes and both solstices. This is your baseline.
  • Year 2-5: Continue all measurements. By Year 3, you have enough data to calculate trends. By Year 5, you can complete the first Climate Envelope Scorecard.
  • Year 5+: Compare the scorecard to baseline. Adjust crop selection and water infrastructure as needed. Share your data with connected refugia to build a regional climate picture.

Troubleshooting

  • Cannot measure wet-bulb directly: Use the body test (Section 3.2). If it fails, you are in immediate danger regardless of the precise number.
  • Only 2 years of data: Too few to establish trends. Do NOT make major decisions (relocation, crop abandonment) based on less than 5 years of observations. Weather varies year to year; climate is the trend.
  • Data recorder dies or leaves: This is the most common failure mode. Ensure 2 people maintain independent records. Cross-check annually.

Community Stewardship

  • Apprenticeship: The climate observer role requires literacy, patience, and discipline. Assign it to someone intrinsically curious about weather. Pair them with a backup observer from the next generation by Year 5.
  • The Weather Stone (Archive): Inscribe the annual summary data (frost-free days, total rainfall, drought floor, phenological dates) onto a permanent stone tablet installed in a public location. Over decades, this becomes the community's most valuable scientific instrument.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Normalcy bias. "It has always been this way" is the most dangerous sentence in climate adaptation. Climate is changing; historical baselines are not reliable beyond 20-30 years. The community that assumes stability will be the community that starves.
  2. Insufficient data duration. Making decisions on 2 years of data is guessing, not planning. Minimum 5 years for trend detection, 10 years for confidence. A single anomalous year (volcanic winter, El Nino event) can mimic a long-term trend.
  3. Single-metric focus. Tracking only temperature while ignoring precipitation decline or phenological mismatch. Climate stress is multidimensional. A site can be warm enough but too dry, or wet enough but with pollinators arriving 2 weeks after bloom. The scorecard exists to force multi-metric assessment.
  4. Delayed relocation decision. Community waits until 3 or more scorecard failures before acting — by then, resources for relocation may be depleted. The trigger must be agreed upon in advance, when the community is fed and healthy, not debated during crisis.
  5. Loss of monitoring continuity. Observer dies or leaves, records are lost to fire or flood. Always maintain 2 independent observers recording in 2 separate locations. The stone archive provides a third backup that survives most disasters.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Wet-Bulb Temperature: The lowest temperature to which air can be cooled by evaporating water into it. At 35 degrees C wet-bulb, human thermoregulation fails regardless of fitness, shade, or hydration.
  • Climate Envelope: The set of temperature, precipitation, and seasonal parameters within which a community can sustain itself. Exceeding the envelope requires adaptation, relocation, or death.
  • Phenological Mismatch: A growing temporal gap between interdependent biological events (e.g., flowers blooming before pollinators emerge) caused by differential responses to climate change.
  • Drought Floor: The minimum stream flow observed during the driest period of record, used as the baseline for water infrastructure sizing.
  • Climate Inertia: The tendency of the climate system to continue changing after the forcing (e.g., greenhouse gas emissions) has stopped, due to the thermal mass of oceans and ice sheets. Warming already absorbed but not yet expressed guarantees future change.
  • Return Period (Climatic): The average time interval between occurrences of a given extreme event (e.g., a "100-year flood" has a 1% chance of occurring in any given year). As climate shifts, return periods shorten for heat events and lengthen for cold events.
  • Growing Degree-Day: A unit of heat accumulation calculated as the daily mean temperature minus a base temperature (typically 10 degrees C). Crops require a species-specific number of growing degree-days to mature. Tracking GDD reveals whether the growing season provides enough thermal energy for crop completion.
  • Committed Warming: The additional temperature increase that is inevitable due to greenhouse gases already in the atmosphere and heat already absorbed by the oceans, even if all emissions ceased immediately. Current estimates: 0.5-1.0 degrees C.
  • Relocation Trigger: A pre-defined set of conditions (typically 3 or more scorecard failures) that obligates a community to begin actively scouting and preparing alternative sites. The trigger must be agreed upon while the community is stable, not during crisis.
