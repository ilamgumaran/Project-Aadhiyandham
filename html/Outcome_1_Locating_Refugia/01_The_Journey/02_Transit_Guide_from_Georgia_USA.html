Transit Guide: The Last Mile Strategy

Alignment: Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

1. The Port-to-Foothill Transition

Reaching a port is only the first stage. This guide details the transition to local, resilient mobility.

1.1. The Approach

PORT HUB -> ACQUIRE MULE / TRIKE -> FOLLOW RIVER UPSTREAM -> REACH 100m CONTOUR -> IDENTIFY WIND SHADOW -> ARRIVAL

1.2. How-To: Build a Travois (All-Terrain Sled)

Move 200kg of supplies over mud and rocks without wheels:

  • Design: A simple A-frame structure made of two 4m hardwood poles.
  • Harness: Lash the narrow end to the animal's shoulders; the wide end drags on the ground.
  • Benefit: Impossible to "break an axle"; works in dense forests and rugged slopes.

1.3. Domestic Routes (from Atlanta)

  • Sequatchie (TN): I-75 North to Chattanooga.
  • Pine Mountain (GA): I-85 South to US-27.
  • Ozark (MO): I-24 West to I-44.

1.4. International Hubs

  • Europe: ATL to Venice (VCE) or Barcelona (BCN).
  • Asia: ATL to Tokyo (HND) or Seoul (ICN).
  • Oceania: ATL to Sydney (SYD) then Hobart (HBA).

2. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners: The Journey

2.1. Detailed Action Plan

  • Phase 1: The Blueprint: Read the theoretical archives fully. Translate the concept into a physical drawing using charcoal or pencil before touching any raw materials.
  • Phase 2: The Prototype: Build a 1/10th scale model of the structure or system. Identify where the stress points are. If the model fails, the real thing will kill you.
  • Phase 3: Redundancy: Never rely on a single system for heat, water, or food. If you build a mechanical system, keep manual hand-tools preserved and oiled as a fallback.

2.2. Advanced Troubleshooting

  • The "5 Whys" Method: When a system fails, ask "Why?" five times to reach the root cause. (e.g., The wall collapsed. Why? The wood rotted. Why? Water pooled. Why? No drainage trench. Fix the trench, not just the wall.)
