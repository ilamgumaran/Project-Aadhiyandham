Geometric Trail Engineering: Strategic Pathfinding

Alignment: Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

வடிவியல் பாதை பொறியியல்: மூலோபாய வழி கண்டறிதல்

Index > 01 The Journey > 04 Terrestrial Navigation > 05 Geometric Trail Engineering

A trail is not just a path; it is a piece of infrastructure. If a trail is too steep, it will erode and become impassable for Travois carts. If it is too visible, it compromises the security of the settlement.

1. Why This Is Important (The Rationale)

Geometric Trail Engineering ensures that transit routes are sustainable, energy-efficient, and strategically concealed. Proper alignment prevents erosion and maximizes stealth.

2. The Geometry of the Slope

2.1. The 5% Rule (The Golden Grade)

To move heavy loads using a manually pulled Travois or cart, the trail must maintain a 5% average grade.

  • Calculation: For every 100 meters of horizontal distance, the trail should rise no more than 5 meters.
  • The Limit: Grades exceeding 10% cause rapid fatigue and lead to catastrophic water erosion during storms.

2.2. Engineering Switchbacks

When the terrain is steeper than 10%, you must use "Switchbacks"—a zig-zag pattern that artificially lengthens the trail to reduce the grade.

  1. The Turning Platform: The "apex" of the turn must be a flat, reinforced area to allow a cart to turn without tipping.
  2. The Outslope: The trail surface must be tilted slightly downward toward the outer edge (approx. 2-3%) to allow rainwater to sheet off the trail.

Switchback Geometry

3. Topographical Masking (Stealth Transit)

3.1. Visual Shadowing

  • Ridge Line Avoidance: Never build a trail on the very top of a ridge (the "skyline").
  • The Military Crest: Build the trail 10-20 meters below the ridge line on the interior slope. This hides the traveler from observers in the valley.

The Military Crest

4. 🔬 Scientific Validation: Soil Shear Strength

  • Compaction: Intentional compaction of the trail tread increases the soil's "shear strength."
  • The Half-Rule: A trail's grade should never exceed half the grade of the hillside it is built on.

5. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

5.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Phase 1: Sighting: Use your Clinometer to flag a 5% route up your primary access slope.
  • Phase 2: The Bench Cut: Dig into the hillside to create a flat "bench" for the trail.
  • Phase 3: Armoring: Place flat stones in areas where the trail crosses seasonal water runoff.

Vocabulary and Definitions

Military Crest
A position just below the top of a ridge that allows for observation of the valley while remaining hidden from view.
Switchback
A zig-zag pattern in a road or trail that reduces the steepness of the grade.
Travois
A type of frame used for dragging loads over land.
