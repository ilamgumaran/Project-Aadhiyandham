Alignment: Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

A baseplate compass and a topographic map together form the most reliable navigation system available to a foot traveler. They require no batteries, no satellites, and no infrastructure. They have guided explorers, soldiers, and surveyors across every continent for over 800 years. Mastering this system is not optional for overland transit — it is the core skill that transforms uncertain wandering into precise, repeatable movement toward a known destination.

> SAFE: Map and compass navigation is a low-risk skill to learn and practice. The instruments contain no hazardous materials. Practice in known terrain before depending on these skills in survival conditions.

Theoretical Foundation

How a Magnetic Compass Works

A magnetic compass exploits the fact that Earth generates a planetary magnetic field through convection currents in its liquid iron outer core (the geodynamo). This field extends from the magnetic south pole (near geographic north) to the magnetic north pole (near geographic south), creating invisible field lines that arc from pole to pole.

A magnetized needle, free to rotate on a low-friction pivot, aligns itself with these field lines. The red (north-seeking) end of the needle points toward Earth's magnetic north pole. This gives a consistent directional reference at any point on the planet's surface, day or night, in any weather.

Critical distinction — True North vs. Magnetic North:

The geographic North Pole (True North) and the magnetic north pole are not in the same location. As of 2025, the magnetic north pole is located in the Canadian Arctic, approximately 500 km from the geographic pole, and it moves at roughly 40-55 km per year. The angular difference between True North and Magnetic North at any location is called magnetic declination.

Region Approximate Declination (2025) Direction
Eastern USA (Georgia) 5-7° West Compass reads East of True North
Western USA (Oregon) 14-16° East Compass reads West of True North
UK (London) 0-1° West Nearly zero
Western Australia 1-3° East Nearly zero
Northern Canada 15-25° West Large correction needed
Equatorial Africa 0-5° varies Generally small

To convert a magnetic bearing to a true bearing: In areas of West declination, ADD the declination. In areas of East declination, SUBTRACT the declination. The mnemonic: "Declination West, Compass Best" (compass reads higher than true), "Declination East, Compass Least" (compass reads lower than true).

Many modern baseplate compasses have an adjustable declination screw that mechanically offsets the orienting arrow. If set correctly, all readings are automatically corrected. Set the declination before departure and verify it at each map change.

How Topographic Maps Encode Terrain

A topographic map represents three-dimensional terrain on a two-dimensional surface using contour lines — curves connecting points of equal elevation. The key relationships:

  • Contour interval: The elevation difference between adjacent contour lines (typically 10 m or 20 m on 1:50,000 maps). Every fifth line (the index contour) is drawn thicker and labeled with its elevation.
  • Closely spaced contours = steep terrain. If 5 contour lines (at 10 m interval) cross in 200 m of horizontal distance, the slope is 50/200 = 25% — very steep, requiring hands for scrambling.
  • Widely spaced contours = gentle terrain. If the same 5 lines span 2 km, the slope is 50/2000 = 2.5% — easy walking, suitable for carts.
  • V-shapes pointing uphill = stream valleys. Water flows down the V. This is the most reliable rule for reading drainage patterns.
  • Closed loops = hilltops or depressions. Hilltops have the highest labeled contour inside. Depressions are marked with tick-marks pointing inward (downhill).
  • Saddles/cols: A low point between two peaks, visible as an hourglass shape in the contours. Saddles are natural crossing points for ridgelines.

Map Scale and Distance Measurement

Map scale expresses the ratio between map distance and ground distance:

  • 1:25,000 — 1 cm on map = 250 m on ground. Best for detailed local navigation. 4 cm = 1 km.
  • 1:50,000 — 1 cm on map = 500 m on ground. Standard for regional travel. 2 cm = 1 km.
  • 1:100,000 — 1 cm on map = 1 km on ground. Useful for strategic route planning, too coarse for precision navigation.

To measure distance along a curved route: lay a piece of string along the route on the map, then measure the string against the map's scale bar. Or use the edge of a compass baseplate marked in known increments.

Map distance is horizontal distance. In hilly terrain, the actual walking distance is longer because you travel up and down, not just sideways. A 1 km horizontal distance with 200 m of elevation change has an actual path length of approximately √(1000² + 200²) = 1,020 m — only 2% longer. But at 500 m elevation change over 1 km horizontal, path length becomes √(1000² + 500²) = 1,118 m — 12% longer and vastly more exhausting.

Core Principles

  1. Set-Before-Walk: Never take a single step on a compass bearing without first setting the bearing on the map while stationary. Navigating without a pre-set bearing is guessing. The correct sequence is: plan bearing on map → transfer to compass → walk. Never reverse this.
  2. Red-in-the-Shed: The fundamental compass operating rule. After setting a bearing on the bezel, hold the compass flat and rotate your body until the red (north-seeking) needle sits inside the orienting arrow (the "shed"). The direction-of-travel arrow now points toward your destination. This must become automatic muscle memory.
  3. Constant Declination Correction: Every bearing must be corrected for magnetic declination. An uncorrected 10-degree declination error over a 10 km leg displaces your endpoint by 1.75 km. This is a systemic error — it does not average out, it compounds in the same direction on every leg.
  4. Navigate Point-to-Point: Never try to walk a bearing for more than 1-2 km without an intermediate waypoint. Choose a visible feature (distinctive tree, rock, hilltop) on your bearing line, walk to it, then take the next bearing. This prevents gradual drift and provides continuous position verification.
  5. The Pace Count Discipline: Know your personal pace count. On flat ground, an average adult takes approximately 62-68 double-paces per 100 m (each double-pace = left-right-left). On uphill terrain, this increases to 75-85 per 100 m. On downhill, 58-65. Calibrate your personal count over a measured 100 m distance on flat and sloped ground. Track distance using ranger beads or pocket pebbles.

Practical Implementation

Compass Anatomy

  • Baseplate: The clear rectangular plate. Has a straight edge for drawing lines on the map and a ruler for measuring distances.
  • Magnetic Needle: The rotating needle. The red end points to Magnetic North. The white/silver end points to Magnetic South.
  • Bezel (Rotating Dial): The graduated ring marked 0-360 degrees. You rotate this to set your desired bearing.
  • Orienting Arrow (The "Shed"): A fixed arrow (or parallel lines) inside the bezel. When set correctly, the red needle sits inside this arrow — "red in the shed."
  • Direction-of-Travel Arrow: The arrow printed on the baseplate, pointing away from you. This shows the direction to walk after the needle is aligned.
  • Declination Adjustment Screw: On quality compasses, a small screw that offsets the orienting arrow by the local declination, so all readings are automatically true bearings.

Procedure 1: Setting a Course from Map to Destination

  1. Lay the map flat. Identify your current position (Point A) and destination (Point B).
  2. Place the compass on the map with the long edge of the baseplate connecting A to B. Ensure the direction-of-travel arrow points from A toward B.
  3. Without moving the baseplate, rotate the bezel until the orienting lines inside the bezel are parallel to the map's north-south grid lines, with the orienting arrow pointing toward map North.
  4. Read the bearing at the index mark on the bezel. This is your grid bearing to the destination.
  5. If your compass does not have declination adjustment: apply the magnetic declination correction. (West declination: add. East declination: subtract.)
  6. Pick up the compass. Hold it flat at chest height.
  7. Rotate your entire body until the red needle sits inside the orienting arrow ("red in the shed").
  8. The direction-of-travel arrow now points to your destination. Identify a visible feature on that line (tree, rock, ridge point). Walk to it. Repeat.

Procedure 2: Finding Your Position by Resection

When you are uncertain of your position but can see two or more known landmarks:

  1. Identify Landmark A (a peak, tower, or other feature you can find on the map).
  2. Point the direction-of-travel arrow at Landmark A. Rotate the bezel until "red in the shed." Read the bearing.
  3. Calculate the back-bearing (bearing ± 180°). This is the bearing FROM the landmark TO you.
  4. On the map, place the compass edge on Landmark A. Rotate the entire compass until the orienting lines align with the map's north-south grid. Draw a line from the landmark along the baseplate edge. You are somewhere on this line.
  5. Repeat for Landmark B. Where the two lines cross is your position.
  6. Verification: Repeat for a third landmark. If the three lines form a small triangle (cocked hat < 200 m across), your position is verified. If the triangle is large, one bearing is incorrect — recheck.

Procedure 3: Aiming Off

When navigating to a point on a linear feature (e.g., a bridge on a river), do NOT aim directly at it. Aim 5-10 degrees to one side deliberately. When you reach the river, you will know which direction to turn to find the bridge. Without aiming off, you reach the river and face a 50/50 guess — left or right — which can cost hours.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Declination Error: As detailed above, the most common and most consequential error. Prevention: set declination adjustment; write declination on every map; verify on known landmarks before departure.
  2. Holding the Compass Tilted: A tilted compass allows the needle to touch the capsule wall, causing it to stick or read incorrectly. Liquid-damped compasses are more forgiving, but the baseplate must still be level within ±10 degrees for accurate readings. Prevention: Hold at chest height, check that the bubble (if present) is centered, and that the needle swings freely.
  3. Magnetic Interference: Steel objects deflect the compass needle. Common culprits: belt buckles (2-5° error at 30 cm), smartphones (5-15° at 20 cm), wire fences (3-10° at 3 m), vehicles (10-30° at 5 m), power lines (5-15° at 30 m). Prevention: Clear all metal from your hands and torso. Step at least 10 m from fences, 50 m from vehicles and power lines. Test by taking the same bearing three times from slightly different positions — if readings differ by more than 3°, interference is present.
  4. Following the Wrong Grid Lines: Maps have three types of north: True North, Grid North (map grid lines), and Magnetic North. The difference between True and Grid North is usually < 2° and can be ignored for field navigation. The difference between Grid North and Magnetic North is the critical declination correction. Prevention: Read the map's declination diagram (usually in the margin) and apply the Grid-Magnetic angle, not the Grid-True angle.
  5. Pace Count Drift: On rough terrain, pace count accuracy degrades. Walking through thick brush, climbing over obstacles, or sidestepping hazards all shorten or lengthen strides unpredictably. After 1 km of rough terrain, pace count error may be ±10-15%. Prevention: Recalibrate pace count for each terrain type. Use terrain features (stream crossings, ridge crests) as distance checkpoints whenever possible.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Baseplate Compass: A compass mounted on a transparent rectangular plate with a rotating bezel, designed for map navigation.
  • Bezel: The rotating graduated ring on a compass, marked 0-360 degrees.
  • Bearing: The clockwise angle from North to a target direction, in degrees.
  • Back-Bearing: The reverse of a bearing — exactly 180° opposite. If the bearing to a landmark is 045°, the back-bearing (from the landmark to you) is 225°.
  • Magnetic Declination: The angular difference between True North and Magnetic North at a given location.
  • Grid Bearing: A bearing measured relative to Grid North (the map's north-south lines).
  • Magnetic Bearing: A bearing measured relative to Magnetic North (what the compass actually reads).
  • Resection: Fixing your position by taking bearings to known landmarks and plotting back-bearings on the map.
  • Aiming Off: Deliberately navigating slightly to one side of a target on a linear feature, so you know which way to turn upon reaching the feature.
  • Pace Count: The number of double-paces (e.g., left-right-left = one count) per 100 meters. Used to estimate distance walked.
  • Ranger Beads: A cord with sliding beads, used to track pace counts mechanically. Typically 9 beads for hundreds of meters and 4 beads for kilometers.
  • Handrail: A long linear feature (river, ridge, road) followed to maintain direction without constant compass reference.
  • Catching Feature: A large, unmissable feature that signals you have gone too far past your destination.
  • Index Contour: Every fifth contour line, drawn thicker and labeled with elevation.
