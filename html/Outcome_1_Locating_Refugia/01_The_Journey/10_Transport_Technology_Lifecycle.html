1. Why This Is Important

The existing Journey framework assumes motorized transport is already gone and jumps directly to foot travel with travois. But collapse is not binary — it is a gradient. Vehicles do not stop working on the same day. Fuel depletes unevenly. Electric vehicles outlast gasoline ones in specific ways but fail catastrophically in others. A community that understands the degradation sequence of each transport technology can extract months or years of additional mobility from systems that others abandon prematurely.

Equally, a community that clings to a vehicle past its useful life wastes time maintaining a dead machine when they should be walking.

2. Expected Outcome

A decision framework that tells you: (a) what transport technology to use RIGHT NOW given what still works, (b) when to abandon it, and (c) what replaces it in the next phase.

3. The Transport Degradation Timeline

3.1. Phase 0: Pre-Collapse (Months -6 to 0)

Everything still works but supply chains are failing. This is the acquisition window.

Priority actions:

  • Fuel: Top off all vehicles. Fill every available container with gasoline (add fuel stabilizer if available — extends shelf life from 3 months to 12-18 months). Diesel lasts longer than gasoline without stabilizer (6-12 months vs. 3-6 months).
  • Bicycles: Acquire the most durable bicycles available. Steel-frame touring bikes with wide tires are ideal. Avoid carbon fiber (catastrophic failure without warning) and suspension bikes (complex, unrepairable). Stock spare inner tubes, tire patches, brake pads, and chain links.
  • Maps: Acquire every paper topographical map within your planned route radius. Laminate if possible. Digital maps are worthless once power grids fail.
  • Animal contacts: Identify local horse, mule, and donkey owners. Establish relationships before collapse — post-collapse, animals become the most valuable asset in existence.

3.2. Phase 1: Motorized Transport (Months 0-6)

What still works and for how long:

Vehicle Type Fuel Shelf Life Failure Mode Realistic Range
Gasoline car/truck 3-6 months (unstabilized) Fuel degrades → gum deposits → injector/carburetor clog 300-500 miles on a full tank
Diesel truck 6-12 months Fuel grows algae/bacteria → filter clog → injection pump failure 500-800 miles on a full tank
Electric vehicle (EV) Battery holds charge 1-3 months sitting idle No charging infrastructure → battery depletes → permanently dead 150-300 miles on a full charge
Hybrid Gasoline component: same as above; battery extends range 20-40% Same as gasoline but marginally longer 400-700 miles
Motorcycle/scooter Same fuel constraints Lighter = more efficient; but carries less cargo 200-400 miles; 1-2 people only

The EV Trap: Electric vehicles are silent, powerful, and require zero fuel — but they are absolutely dependent on the electrical grid. Once the grid fails, an EV is a 4,000-pound paperweight. There is no field-expedient way to charge an EV. Solar panels sufficient to charge an EV battery (60-100 kWh) would require 20-30 residential panels and 3-5 days of full sun. This is not portable.

When to abandon motorized transport:

  • Fuel vehicles: When fuel runs out OR when the engine develops a problem that requires a part you cannot fabricate (electronic sensors, fuel injectors, rubber hoses). Do NOT waste weeks trying to repair a modern engine. Strip the vehicle for useful parts (mirrors, springs, wiring, battery acid, glass) and walk.
  • EVs: When the battery drops below 20% charge with no charging source. Do NOT drain it to zero — a fully depleted lithium battery may not recover even if power returns later.

What to salvage from abandoned vehicles:

Component Post-Collapse Use
Side mirrors Heliograph signaling
Leaf springs Knife/tool blade stock (high-carbon steel)
Battery (lead-acid) Acid for leather tanning; lead for casting; battery case for storage
Wiring (copper) Snare wire, lashing, electrical conductor for future use
Tires Cut into sole rubber for sandals/shoe repair; burn for emergency signal (toxic — last resort)
Glass (windshield/windows) Greenhouse panels, water distillation, lens grinding
Seat foam Insulation padding, sleeping mat
Fan belts / hoses Cordage, strapping, tourniquet material
Alternator magnets Permanent magnets for future generator construction
Fuel tank Water storage (after thorough cleaning)

3.3. Phase 2: Bicycle Transport (Months 3-36)

The bicycle is the most efficient human-powered machine ever invented. A human on a bicycle is 3-5x more efficient than walking. A fit cyclist covers 50-80 km/day vs. 15-25 km/day on foot.

Why bicycles outlast vehicles:

  • No fuel requirement.
  • Mechanical simplicity (bearings, chain, brakes — all field-repairable).
  • Tire patches and pump can extend tire life for years.
  • Steel frames can be welded at a forge (Section 09, The Mechanics).
  • Cargo capacity with trailer: 50-100 kg.

Bicycle failure timeline:

Component Expected Lifespan (heavy use) Field Repair? Failure Consequence
Tires/tubes 1,000-3,000 miles Yes (patches, solid inner tubes from cut hose) Flat = walk until patched
Chain 2,000-5,000 miles Yes (spare links, chain tool) Broken = no drivetrain; becomes a scooter
Brake pads 500-2,000 miles Yes (carved hardwood or leather substitute) No brakes = dangerous on hills
Bearings 5,000-15,000 miles Difficult (requires grease, correct size) Grinding = increasing drag; eventual seizure
Cables 3,000-10,000 miles Moderate (paracord substitute for shift cables; braided wire for brake) No shifting = single-speed; no brakes = foot braking
Spokes 5,000+ miles individually Yes (spare spokes + spoke wrench) 2-3 broken spokes = wobbly wheel; 5+ = wheel collapse
Frame Indefinite if steel; 2-5 years if aluminum (fatigue cracks) Steel: weld at forge. Aluminum: No field repair. Frame failure = total loss

When to abandon bicycle transport:

  • When tires are destroyed beyond patching AND no replacement rubber exists. A bicycle with no tires can still roll on dirt with wrapped-cloth rims, but painfully slowly and with rapid rim wear.
  • When bearings seize and cannot be repacked.
  • When the frame cracks (aluminum) or rusts through (steel, after years of exposure).

The bicycle-to-cart conversion: A broken bicycle's wheels, axle, and chain can be repurposed into a human-pulled cart that rolls on bearings — far more efficient than a travois on flat terrain.

3.4. Phase 3: Animal Transport (Months 6-Indefinite)

This is the permanent replacement. Animals reproduce. They fuel themselves on grass. They navigate terrain no vehicle can handle. See Module 12 (Animal-Based Transport) for full details.

Acquisition window: Animals must be secured during Phase 0 or early Phase 1, while roads are still passable and barter is still functional. By Phase 2, anyone with animals will not trade them for anything less than irreplaceable skills or critical supplies.

3.5. Phase 4: Foot + Travois (Permanent Baseline)

If no animals can be acquired, the travois system described in the existing modules becomes the permanent transport mode. The travois is the floor — it always works, requires no maintenance beyond replacing worn poles, and functions on any terrain.

5. Fire-Making Redundancy (The Critical Backup)

The existing modules assume ferrocerium rods for fire. If these are lost, the journey's cooking, water purification, and cold-weather shelter systems all fail. Every traveler must carry three independent fire-making methods:

  1. Ferrocerium rod (primary) — produces 3,000°C sparks, works when wet, lasts 10,000+ strikes.
  2. Flint and steel (secondary) — a piece of high-carbon steel (back of a knife blade, a file, or a vehicle leaf spring) struck against natural flint, chert, or quartz produces sparks that ignite char cloth.
  • Making char cloth: Place small squares of cotton or linen fabric in a sealed tin with one small hole. Place the tin in a fire. Smoke will vent from the hole. When smoke stops, remove from heat and seal the hole. The fabric is now charred carbon that catches a spark instantly.
  1. Bow drill (tertiary) — friction fire from wood alone. Requires practice but uses only materials found on the ground.
  • Components: A hardwood fireboard (cedar, willow, cottonwood), a hardwood spindle (same species), a bow (any curved stick with cordage), and a hand socket (hard stone or shell with a depression).
  • Technique: Place spindle in a notch in the fireboard. Wrap bow string once around spindle. Saw the bow back and forth rapidly. Friction creates a coal in the notch. Transfer coal to a tinder bundle (dry grass, bark fiber) and blow gently to flame.
  • The reality: Bow drill fire-making takes 30-60 seconds for an experienced practitioner and 10-30 minutes for a beginner. Practice before the journey, not during it.

7. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Fuel Stabilizer: A chemical additive that slows the oxidation and evaporation of gasoline, extending its usability from 3 months to 12-18 months. Not required for diesel.
  • Ferrocerium Rod: A metallic rod (mischmetal alloy) that produces extremely hot sparks (3,000°C) when scraped with a hard edge. The primary fire-starting tool in the Aadhiyandham kit.
  • Char Cloth: Cotton or linen fabric that has been carbonized by pyrolysis (heated without oxygen). It catches a single spark and holds a glowing ember, which can then ignite a tinder bundle.
  • Travois: An A-frame cargo sled dragged by a human or animal, consisting of two long poles with cross-bars. Functions on any terrain including mud, rock, and dense forest where wheeled vehicles cannot pass.

8. Scientific Validation & Research Context

  • Gasoline Degradation: The American Petroleum Institute confirms that unstabilized gasoline degrades significantly within 3-6 months due to oxidation and evaporation of volatile compounds, producing gum and varnish deposits that clog fuel systems.
  • Bicycle Efficiency: Wilson & Schmidt ("Bicycling Science," MIT Press, 3rd ed.) demonstrate that cycling is 3-5x more energy-efficient than walking per unit distance, making the bicycle the most efficient human-powered transport mechanism ever measured.
  • Bow Drill Fire-Making: Experimental archaeology (Hough, 1926; Wescott, 1999) confirms that friction fire-making with a bow drill is achievable in 30-90 seconds by practiced individuals across a range of wood species, with cottonwood, willow, and cedar producing the most reliable coal.

9. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

9.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Now (Pre-Collapse): Acquire steel-frame bicycles, spare tubes, patch kits, and a chain tool for every traveling adult. Acquire paper topographical maps. Practice flint-and-steel and bow drill fire-making until reliable (minimum 10 successful fires each method).
  • Phase 0: Top off all fuel. Stabilize if possible. Load vehicles with maximum supplies and drive to destination or as far as fuel allows.
  • Phase 1: When vehicle fails, strip it (see salvage table). Transfer cargo to bicycle trailers or travois. Continue on bicycle if route permits.
  • Phase 2+: When bicycles fail, transition to animal transport (if acquired) or foot + travois.

9.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes

  • Gasoline smells "off" or engine sputters: Fuel has degraded. Drain the tank, filter through cloth to remove varnish particles, and mix with any fresh fuel available. If engine still won't run, strip and walk.
  • Bicycle tire shredded, no patches: Wrap the rim with multiple layers of cloth or leather secured with cord. This provides 10-20 miles of very slow riding on smooth terrain before the wrap shreds. Better than walking with a 50 kg trailer.
  • Cannot make fire with any method: Carry an ember. When a fire is burning, create a slow-burning "fire bundle" (tightly packed dry punk wood or fungus wrapped in bark) that smolders for 6-12 hours. Transport it to the next campsite and blow it back to flame. This is how humans transported fire for 100,000 years before striking tools existed.

9.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: Every adult in the traveling group must be able to make fire with at least 2 of the 3 methods. Every adult must be able to perform basic bicycle repair (patch a tire, reconnect a chain). These are non-negotiable transit skills.
