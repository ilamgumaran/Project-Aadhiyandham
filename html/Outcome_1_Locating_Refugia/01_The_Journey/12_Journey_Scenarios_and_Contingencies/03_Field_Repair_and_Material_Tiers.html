Alignment: Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

> Caution: Practice every repair in this module at home, on real gear, before departure. A repair you have only read about is not a capability; it is a hope. Deliberately break and re-sole an old sandal, split and splint a pole, patch a punctured container. Two evenings of practice buys you the actual skill.

1. Why This Is Important

Everything carried on a long journey is a consumable. Footwear delaminates, straps tear at stitch lines, containers crack at stress points, travois poles split at the drag end, clothing fails at seams and knees. On a 100–300 km journey, the question is not whether equipment fails but which failure you cannot walk away from.

The Essential Packing List builds redundancy into the kit. This module handles the moment redundancy runs out. Its organizing idea is the material tier: every repair is planned three times — once with your kit, once with scavenged human-made materials, and once with the landscape alone. A party that knows all three tiers for its five critical items is functionally unstoppable by equipment failure; a party that knows none is one broken sandal from a medical emergency.

1.1. The Criticality Triage

Not all failures deserve equal attention. Rank by what the failure does to the party:

Class Definition Examples Response budget
A — Movement-critical Failure halts or injures the party Footwear, water containers, travois/cart under essential load, litter/carry system Stop and fix properly, today
B — Capability-critical Failure degrades a core function Fire kit, knife/edge tools, shelter tarp, pack harness Fix at next camp
C — Comfort Failure costs morale or efficiency Clothing tears, worn padding, cook-pot handle Fix on rest day

The classic field error is inverted triage: spending an hour on a torn jacket at noon (Class C) and then improvising a bad footwear fix at dusk (Class A). Class A failures own the schedule.

2. Expected Outcome

For each of the five Class A/B items your party carries: a rehearsed Tier 1 repair, a known Tier 2 substitution, and a understood Tier 3 improvisation. Concretely: the party can re-sole footwear, restore a water container or replace its function, rebuild a travois joint, re-hang a pack, and maintain an edge — indefinitely, from the landscape if necessary.

  Doctrine: descend one tier at a time. Spend Tier 1 materials only on
  Class A repairs; harvest Tier 2/3 for Class B/C even while kit remains.

The doctrine matters more than the lists: your kit is the reserve, not the default. Parties that patch every small failure from the kit arrive at the true emergency with an empty repair pouch. Harvest bark cordage on rest days before you need it; it costs nothing but time you are already spending.

4. The Five Critical Repairs

4.1. Footwear (Class A — the journey-ender)

Footwear failure is the most common Class A event on every long trail. Modes: sole delamination, sole wear-through, upper/strap tear-out.

  • Tier 1: Re-stitch with awl and waxed thread using a saddle stitch (two needles, opposing passes — it does not unravel when one stitch cuts). Delaminated soles: clean, score both faces, apply heated pine resin mixed with a pinch of crushed charcoal (plasticizer), clamp overnight under a weighted flat rock.
  • Tier 2: Cut replacement soles from tire tread (the universal post-industrial shoe sole — sandal-making from tires is a living craft across East Africa; the akala pattern needs only a knife and cord). Lash through four slits, cord crossing over the foot.
  • Tier 3: Rawhide moccasin over a woven grass or bark inner sole; or the Roman-pattern carbatina — a single oval of hide, holes punched around the rim, drawn up around the foot with one cord. Crude, effective, three hours of work.
  • Prevention beats all tiers: feet checked at every rest halt, hot spots taped before they blister, boots dried every night (wet leather loses half its abrasion life).

4.2. Water containers (Class A)

  • Tier 1: Pinhole in a soft container: resin patch over roughened surface. Crack in rigid plastic: drill/melt a small hole at each crack end first (stops propagation), then patch.
  • Tier 2: Any bottle, can, or drum found en route, boiled or flamed before use.
  • Tier 3: This is the hardest Tier 3 item — treat container loss as a route problem, not only a craft problem: re-plan the day's route to hug water (see Riverine Logic) while you solve it. Craft options in rough order of speed: a rawhide or animal-stomach water bag (hours, if hunting/butchering happens anyway); bark containers sealed with resin (a day; birch is best, others serviceable); fired clay (only worth it during a multi-day halt — see Pottery and Ceramics principles at camp scale).
  • Doctrine: water capacity is the one redundancy that is never dead weight. Two half-size containers beat one full-size in every failure scenario.

4.3. Travois and cart (Class A when loaded with essentials)

The Transit Guide covers pole snap (carry a spare; cut green). Add the two other modes:

  • Lashing failure: joints work loose from vibration long before rope breaks. Re-tension all lashings every morning — 90 seconds that prevents the noon collapse. Re-lash with a square lashing finished by frapping turns; wet rawhide, if available, shrinks as it dries into a joint harder than the wood.
  • Drag-end wear: the ground end of a travois pole abrades through in 80–150 km on stone. Rotate poles end-for-end daily (wear spreads evenly); shoe the drag end with a found tin can, tire section, or green hardwood cap lashed over the tip.
  • Cart axle/wheel seizure (if wheeled): grease with animal fat or resin-fat mix at every rest day; carry no cart you cannot convert back to a travois with the materials on it — wheels are a luxury with a failure mode, poles are a system without one.

4.4. Pack and harness (Class B, becomes A in cold or when carrying a child)

  • Strap tear-out at the anchor point: do not sew the strap back to the same torn fabric — it re-fails. Bridge the load past the tear: wrap the strap around a smooth pebble pushed against the pack fabric from inside and cinch the cord behind it (the "stone toggle" — works on any fabric, no sewing, holds full pack weight).
  • Full harness failure: convert the pack to a tumpline (single broad strap across the crown of the head, load high on the back) — cut from any webbing, belt, or plaited bark. Tumplines carried the entire pre-industrial freight economy of the Americas and the Himalaya; heads carry what shoulders cannot.

4.5. Edge tools (Class B)

  • A dull knife is a slow tool; a chipped one is a lost one if pushed. Strop daily on a belt with fine ash or clay slip as compound; regrind chips on any flat river stone (sandstone best), coarse-to-fine, keeping the original bevel angle.
  • Handle split: whip the split with cord soaked in resin; or re-handle entirely — a knife tang set into a split green branch, bound with wet rawhide, outlasts the journey.
  • Total loss of all edges: knapped stone flakes (any glassy or fine-grained stone) will butcher, cut cord, and shape wood. Ugly is irrelevant; sharp is sharp.

5. The Repair Kit Audit (pre-departure)

The packing list already allocates repair weight; audit it against your five critical items, not a generic list:


REPAIR POUCH AUDIT — target < 700 g total
=========================================
[ ] Awl + 2 needles + 20 m waxed thread   (footwear, packs, hide)
[ ] 15 m cordage, 3 mm + 5 m, 6 mm       (lashings, tumpline)
[ ] Resin block 100 g (or knowledge to tap it: Tier 3)
[ ] Patch stock: leather 20x20 cm + canvas 30x30 cm
[ ] 2 m soft wire (Tier-2 multiplier: binds what cord cannot)
[ ] Whetstone chip or knowledge of river-stone grades
[ ] EVERY item above: named person who has USED it

The last checkbox is the audit. A kit no one has used is 700 g of talisman.

7. Common Failure Modes

  • Inverted triage. An hour on a Class C tear, then a rushed Class A bodge at dusk. Prevention: name the class aloud before touching any repair.
  • Kit-first spending. Every minor failure patched from the pouch; the pouch is empty by week two. Prevention: Tier doctrine — kit is the reserve; harvest Tier 2/3 on rest days.
  • First-use repairs. The awl comes out of its wrapper 60 km from the trailhead, in the rain. Prevention: the pre-departure practice sessions (break-and-fix evenings).
  • Repairing instead of re-routing. Two days spent crafting a bark container when the river alternate route made the loss trivial. Prevention: for every Class A failure ask first — does the route solve this cheaper than the craft?
  • Riding the failing part. The fraying strap, the working-loose lashing, visibly degrading for days, addressed only at the break. Prevention: the 90-second morning audit — hands on every lashing, strap, and sole before the first step.

8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Criticality Triage (A/B/C): Ranking failures by their effect on the party — movement-critical, capability-critical, comfort — which sets the response budget.
  • Frapping Turns: The tightening wraps perpendicular to a lashing's main turns; the difference between a joint that holds and one that walks loose by noon.
  • Material Tier: One of three planning levels for any repair — kit (packed), scavenge (human-made, found), land (harvested). Every critical item needs all three thought through.
  • Saddle Stitch: Two-needle opposing stitch that survives individual thread cuts; the only hand stitch worth using on load-bearing seams.
  • Stone Toggle: No-sew anchor repair that spreads a strap load across a broad cone of fabric via a cinched pebble.
  • Tumpline: Head-strap carry system; the universal fallback when shoulder harnesses fail, and the highest-capacity human carry method in pre-industrial use.
