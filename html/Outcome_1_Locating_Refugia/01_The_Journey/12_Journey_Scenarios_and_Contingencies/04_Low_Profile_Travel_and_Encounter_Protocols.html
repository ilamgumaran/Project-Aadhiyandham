Alignment: Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

> Caution: This module is avoidance doctrine, not combat doctrine. Its entire aim is that your party is never worth noticing, never worth following, and never worth confronting. In any scenario where society still functions — which is most scenarios — the same doctrine simply reads as traveling politely and unremarkably, which costs nothing. Nothing here licenses aggression; a party that seeks confrontation has already failed the module.

1. Why This Is Important

Physical terrain is honest: a river is where the map says. Human terrain is not. In a degraded society, the distribution of other people — their needs, fears, and organization — becomes the dominant route hazard, and unlike weather it reacts to you. Every signature your party emits (sound, light, smoke, track, silhouette, rumor) changes the behavior of people you will never see.

The historical record of civilian movement through unstable regions — refugee memoirs, aid-worker field protocols, displacement studies — converges on a stable hierarchy of what actually keeps travelers safe. It is worth stating plainly because it inverts popular assumptions:

  1. Not being seen (route and timing) prevents almost all trouble.
  2. Being seen but not interesting (profile) prevents most of the rest.
  3. Being interesting but not confrontable (encounter management) resolves most of what remains.
  4. Physical defense — the popular obsession — is the last percent, and parties that invest there first usually bought their confrontation with the attention their posture attracted.

This module works down that hierarchy in order. The settlement-side equivalents live in Outcome 3; this module is for the party in motion, which has no perimeter, no stores, and no ground of its own.

2. Expected Outcome

A party that moves through inhabited or contested country emitting the minimum signature its speed allows; that detects other people before being detected in most meetings; that has a rehearsed, role-assigned response to the four encounter types; and that can pass a controlled checkpoint or negotiate transit through claimed territory without escalation. Measured honestly: most journeys applying this doctrine should complete with zero encounters that ever reached the negotiation stage.

3. Layer One — Route and Timing (not being seen)

The Transit Guide establishes the Density Gradient and Contour-Following principles. Extend them with the signature disciplines:

  • The parallel-path rule. Roads, rail lines, and river banks are perfect navigation handrails and terrible places to walk. Travel 100–400 m off the handrail, on its quieter side, paralleling it. You keep the navigation and lose the exposure. (Already implicit in Water-Route Convergence; apply it to every linear feature.)
  • Timing against human rhythm. Human presence on routes is not uniform: it concentrates mid-morning to dusk. The two low-activity windows are first light (civil twilight to +2 hours) and, for short necessary exposures like road crossings or bridge use, the pre-dawn hours. Night movement is a specialist's tool — navigation error and injury rates triple, which is a worse trade than exposure for most parties. Default: move at first light, lie up midday near water, move again to dusk, camp dark.
  • Choke-point protocol. Bridges, passes, dam crossings, and trail junctions are where routes force convergence — with everyone else's routes too. Approach every choke point as an observation problem: halt inside cover at 200–400 m, watch for as long as you can afford (minimum 15 minutes; an hour is better), cross fast and singly-spaced, regroup beyond. Never rest at a choke point.
  • Track discipline. In soft ground, a party of five towing a travois writes its size, direction, and load onto the earth for days. Where following is a plausible threat: walk hard ground and watercourse edges at entry/exit to camps, avoid cutting switchbacks (fresh shortcuts are flags), and never leave camp waste — buried food waste marks a camp as certainly as a signpost (and draws animals that draw attention).

4. Layer Two — Profile (being uninteresting)

If seen — and on a long journey you will be — the goal is a three-second read of "poor, local-ish, organized enough to be a nuisance, carrying nothing worth the trouble."

  • Wealth signature. Bright technical fabrics, matched new gear, visible optics, and clean bulging packs read as supply cache on legs. Dull, worn, mismatched outer layers over whatever quality lies beneath; valuable items (tools, seed stock, the archive) carried deep and split across carriers, never all in one pack.
  • Competence signature — the calibration. Looking helpless invites predation; looking tactical invites challenge. The target is the well-documented middle: unremarkable competence — steady pace, no furtiveness, no staring, gear stowed, hands free. Aid organizations training staff for unstable regions teach exactly this band: neither victim posture nor threat posture.
  • Group signature. Children and elders visibly present generally lower the threat read of your party and raise the social cost of harming it (see Party Composition Playbooks); this is a real asset, and it is honest to arrange the march order so it is visible when being seen is unavoidable. The same march order keeps them nearest the Guardian.
  • Noise and light. Voices carry 300+ m over open ground at dawn; metal-on-metal carries farther. Silence pack rattles with cloth at every repack. After dark in inhabited country: no open flame, no white light; cook at dusk, not after (see camp doctrine below).

5. Layer Three — Encounter Management

Most encounters are with people exactly like you: tired, wary, hoping you are not a problem. Doctrine for the four types, in rising order of structure:

5.1. The Sighting (they have not seen you)

Freeze low, observe, let them pass, resume after a spacing interval, log it (people imply more people — update the day's route read). Do not shadow them to observe more; curiosity is how sightings become meetings.

5.2. The Mutual Sighting (distance, both aware)

The universal grammar of non-aggression at distance: stop, show empty hands briefly, alter course visibly away, keep moving at unchanged pace. Do not approach to explain yourself; do not run (fleeing invites chase instincts and marks you as carrying something). In most recorded refugee-column encounters, both parties want exactly this outcome and will mirror it with relief.

5.3. The Meeting (conversation is unavoidable)

Pre-assigned roles from the Party Profile: one Speaker (calm, plain voice — often the eldest present is the strongest choice), everyone else silent and relaxed; Rear Guard drifted a few steps aside with the party's depth behind them (not as a threat — as unhurried spacing). The Speaker's script is short and boring: local-sounding origin, unremarkable destination ("my brother's place, two days on"), nothing about numbers behind you, supplies, or your true endpoint. Offer nothing, ask little, end it first and politely: "Long day ahead of us. Safe travels to you." Walk a false bearing until out of sight if your true route is sensitive, then correct.

  • The generosity question. If asked for food or water by people in genuine need: a small, immediate, visibly complete gift ("this is what we can spare") from a side pouch — never opened from the main packs — both discharges the human obligation and closes the negotiation. What you must never do is display the depth of your stores. Guarded generosity is safer than refusal: refusal creates a grievance, display creates a target; a bounded gift creates neither. And remember the inverse asset: people met on the road are the richest source of route intelligence available — "How's the bridge at the river? Anyone working the pass?" costs nothing and updates your map.

5.4. The Checkpoint (controlled passage, formal or informal)

Someone controls passage and knows the ground; you do not. The decision is made before you are seen, from your choke-point observation halt:

Two hard rules distilled from decades of humanitarian field practice: never approach a checkpoint at night, and never let the first contact happen at conversational range — hail from distance so both sides make their decisions unpressed. A toll of food or a tool is a cheap fare; the cargo that must never be surrendered (seed stock, archive, medical core) travels deep, split, and boring-looking for exactly this moment.

5.5. Disengagement and the failed encounter

If an encounter turns acquisitive or hostile despite the above: the party's rehearsed break-contact is not a fight, it is a pre-named rally point ("back-bearing 400 m, the split rock") reached by walking — calmly, together, Guardian and dependents first, Rear Guard last — while the Speaker continues to fill the air with compliant, boring words. Groups that stay coherent and unpanicked are almost never pursued; pursuit selects stragglers and scatterers. Cache-and-return beats surrender-of-everything: if seizure looks certain, the earlier layers failed — most parties get to choose again a layer earlier next time, which is why the layers, not the confrontation, are the module.

6. Camps and Caches in Transit

  • The dusk-camp doctrine. Cook and eat at dusk at a first site near water; then move 300–800 m, off-path and uphill, to a second, cold, dark sleeping site chosen for concealment and exits, not comfort. Fires and food smells advertise the first site; you are no longer there. This single habit removes the majority of night-encounter risk for the cost of twenty minutes' walk.
  • Sleeping-site criteria (in order): not visible from any path or handrail; two exits; natural noise perimeter (dry litter, gravel) on likely approaches; tolerable ground. Defensibility in the fortress sense is not a criterion for a party in motion — being unfindable outranks being unassailable, every time.
  • Caches. For staged journeys and scouted routes (see Transit Guide): bury or elevate, waterproof triple-wrapped, at a bearing-and-paces offset from a durable landmark (never at the landmark itself — landmarks attract everyone), recorded in the party's route notes in the same offset code (see Location Recording Without GPS). Split every cache: two half-caches 100 m apart double the survival odds of your supply line for ten extra minutes of digging.

7. Common Failure Modes

  • Tactical cosplay. Equipping and moving like a paramilitary column to feel safe — thereby reading as either a threat to organized groups or a prize (that gear!) to predatory ones. Prevention: the boring-competence profile; hide capability, show normalcy.
  • The friendly overshare. The Speaker, relieved the meeting is going well, names the destination, the party size, the seed stock. Rumor is the fastest traveler on any road. Prevention: script rehearsed aloud; everyone else silent by role, not by improvisation.
  • Choke-point impatience. Crossing the bridge after five minutes' watching because the day is long. The fifteenth minute is the one that shows you the watcher on the far bank. Prevention: the minimum-watch rule, enforced by the Rear Guard, who owns the clock.
  • Night-camp at the cook site. The oldest error; nearly every recorded transit-camp robbery begins with smoke or firelight marking a party that then slept where it cooked. Prevention: dusk-camp doctrine, no exceptions in inhabited country.
  • Winning the argument. Escalating at a barrier or in a meeting over property, pride, or being right. Every field manual from every aid agency says the same thing: you can win the argument or complete the journey. Prevention: the Speaker's only KPI is boredom achieved; the party judges them on nothing else.

8. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Boring Competence: The calibrated middle profile — neither victim nor threat — that field organizations train for unstable regions.
  • Choke Point: Terrain forcing route convergence (bridge, pass, junction); observed from cover before every approach.
  • Dusk-Camp Doctrine: Cooking at one site, sleeping cold at a second concealed site; the highest-value single habit in transit security.
  • Handrail: A linear terrain feature (road, river, ridge) used for navigation. The parallel-path rule keeps its guidance and sheds its exposure.
  • Offset Cache: Supplies hidden at a recorded bearing-and-distance from a durable landmark, never at the landmark itself.
  • Rally Point: The pre-named fallback location a party regroups at if an encounter fails; walked to, never fled to.
  • Signature: Everything a party emits that others can detect — sound, light, smoke, track, silhouette, rumor. Low-profile travel is signature management.
