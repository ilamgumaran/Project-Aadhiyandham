Alignment: Outcome 1: Locating and Connecting Optimal Refugia

> Caution — the asymmetry that should govern the whole decision: Leaving too early costs you an inconvenience — a disrupted life, a false alarm, some wasted preparation, the mild embarrassment of having gone when you did not have to. Leaving too late costs you everything — the roads close, the fuel runs out, the checkpoints go up, the window shuts, and the plan you spent years building becomes a museum of good intentions. These costs are not symmetric, and the human mind is wired to get the trade exactly backwards. This module exists to make the leave/stay decision before the pressure arrives, because in the moment the pressure arrives, your judgment will be the least trustworthy thing you own.

1. Why This Is Important

Under any scenario where departure is triggered rather than chosen at leisure — a deteriorating situation, a regional crisis, a slow slide — the single hardest decision is when. And it is hard not because the information is unavailable but because of a well-documented feature of the human mind: normalcy bias, the tendency to interpret each new bad sign as within the range of normal, and to defer action because acting means accepting that the world has genuinely changed. In disaster after disaster — floods, fires, evacuations, collapses — the recurring finding is that most people wait too long, not because they lacked warning, but because each individual warning was, by itself, deniable.

The Journey Scenarios rationale names the mechanism — plan-continuation bias — and the antidote — the pre-decided decision. This module applies that antidote to the largest decision of all. You cannot trust yourself to judge "is it time?" correctly in the moment, because the moment is exactly when the bias peaks. You can decide, now, in calm, what specific observable conditions would move you — and then commit to honoring them when they arrive, even though every instinct in the moment will argue "not yet."

2. Expected Outcome

A written, group-agreed trigger ladder: a tiered set of specific, observable indicators, each tied to a specific action (from "watch more closely" up to "go now"), decided in advance and reviewed on a cadence. The ladder converts the leave/stay decision from an in-the-moment judgment (which bias corrupts) into a checklist (which it cannot).

3. Why You Cannot Decide This in the Moment


   THE COST ASYMMETRY (why "when in doubt, lean early")
   ===================================================
   LEAVE TOO EARLY            LEAVE TOO LATE
   -----------------          -----------------
   disrupted life             roads closed / checkpoints up
   wasted preparation         fuel/supplies gone
   false-alarm embarrassment  window shut
   ...recoverable             ...often fatal, irreversible

   Rational response to an asymmetric bet: bias toward early.
   Actual human response (normalcy bias): bias toward late.
   The ladder exists to overrule the instinct with the arithmetic.

Three biases conspire to make the in-the-moment decision late:

  • Normalcy bias — each new sign is rationalized as still-normal.
  • Sunk-cost and continuity — leaving means abandoning home, work, possessions, and the story that things will be fine; staying defers that grief.
  • Social proof — "no one else is leaving," which is exactly what everyone else is also observing, producing collective paralysis until the window is gone.

None of these can be defeated by willpower in the moment; they are defeated by a decision made before the moment, by a calmer version of yourself, and honored as a promise.

4. The Trigger Ladder

Build a ladder with (at least) three rungs. Each rung has specific, observable indicators (not vibes) and a committed action. The indicators must be things you can actually see or verify, chosen so that reaching a rung is a fact, not a feeling.


   RUNG 3  GO NOW          hard triggers: the window is closing.
   (execute)              committed action: depart on the plan.
                          e.g. movement restrictions beginning,
                          local violence/looting, supply cutoff,
                          official evacuation, the specific
                          named event your group pre-agreed.

   RUNG 2  PREPARE / STAGE softening indicators cluster. action:
   (30-min-out)          top up supplies, stage packs, confirm
                          route & roles, shorten the trigger-review
                          interval, pre-position caches, tell the
                          group "we are at Rung 2."

   RUNG 1  WATCH          early, ambiguous signs. action: raise
   (heightened)          attention, track the named indicators
                          deliberately, review weekly not yearly.

   RUNG 0  NORMAL          baseline. periodic review (e.g. seasonal).

Design rules that make a ladder actually work:

  • Specific and observable. "Things feel bad" is not a trigger; "the second of these three named events" is. Vague triggers get rationalized away — the whole point is to remove the moment's discretion.
  • Pre-committed actions. Each rung names what you do, agreed now. Rung 3 especially must be a commitment made in calm, because in the moment it will feel premature — that feeling is the bias, and the pre-commitment is how you overrule it.
  • The "one-way door" test. Identify the specific conditions after which departure becomes impossible or far more dangerous (roads controlled, fuel gone, borders closed). Your Rung 3 must trigger before that door, with margin — because the door is exactly what normalcy bias will let you walk up to and through.
  • A designated caller. Name, in advance, who is authorized to declare a rung — and give any core member the power to convene the decision. Diffuse responsibility is how "someone should say something" becomes no one saying anything.
  • Bias toward early, explicitly. Write the cost asymmetry into the ladder so that when the group debates "isn't this premature?", the answer is already on the page: early is recoverable, late is not.

5. Reviewing and Honoring the Ladder

  • Review on a cadence, tightening as you climb. At Rung 0, seasonal review is fine. At Rung 1, weekly. At Rung 2, daily. The review interval itself is an indicator — if you are checking constantly, you are already higher on the ladder than you admitted.
  • Honor the rung when it is reached — this is the whole point. The failure mode is not building the ladder; it is reaching Rung 3 and saying "let's give it a few more days." Those few more days are the window. Pre-agree that reaching a rung triggers its action without re-litigating the decision, precisely because the group in the moment is the group least able to judge.
  • False alarms are successes, not failures. A Rung-2 stage that turns out unnecessary cost you a weekend and proved the system works. Treating false alarms as embarrassments trains the group to raise the bar until the real event slips under it. Celebrate the drill.

6. Common Failure Modes

  • No ladder at all. Leaving the when-to-go decision to in-the-moment judgment. [Tier L] Consequence: normalcy bias defers departure until the window closes; the entire years-long plan is lost at the last decision. Prevention: the written, pre-committed ladder.
  • Vague triggers. "When it gets bad enough." [Tier L] Consequence: "bad enough" is redefined upward with each new sign, so it is never reached until too late. Prevention: specific, observable, pre-named indicators.
  • Triggers set past the one-way door. Rung 3 conditions that only occur after departure is already impossible. [Tier L] Consequence: the trigger fires when you can no longer act on it. Prevention: the one-way-door test; set Rung 3 with margin before the point of no return.
  • Reaching the rung and stalling. Building the ladder, then not honoring it in the moment. [Tier L] Consequence: identical to having no ladder — the plan dies at the final hesitation. Prevention: pre-agree that a reached rung executes without re-litigation; name the caller.
  • Punishing false alarms. Treating a precautionary stage as an embarrassing overreaction. [Tier C] Consequence: the group raises its threshold to avoid future embarrassment, until the real event slips beneath it. Prevention: frame false alarms as successful drills.

7. Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Normalcy bias: The documented tendency to interpret warning signs as within normal range and defer action; the reason in-the-moment departure decisions run late.
  • The cost asymmetry: The fact that leaving early is recoverable while leaving late is often not — the rational basis for biasing toward early.
  • The designated caller: The pre-named person authorized to declare a rung, with any core member empowered to convene the decision.
  • The one-way door: The condition after which departure becomes impossible or far more dangerous; Rung 3 must fire before it, with margin.
  • The trigger ladder: The tiered set of specific indicators and pre-committed actions (watch → prepare → go) that decides departure timing in advance.

8. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

8.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: With the group, in calm, list the specific observable indicators for each rung and the one-way-door conditions for your scenario and region.
  • Execution: Write the ladder, name the caller and the pre-committed actions, set the review cadence, and pre-position what Rung 2 requires.
  • Review: Re-examine the ladder on cadence and after any real-world event that tests it; tighten indicators that proved vague.

8.2. Consequence of Deviation & Precision Tier

  • [Tier L — Law] No pre-committed ladder → normalcy bias defers departure past the window; total plan loss at the final decision. Decide in calm; do not trust the moment.
  • [Tier L — Law] Rung 3 set past the one-way door → the trigger fires when you can no longer act. Test every trigger against the point of no return; keep margin.
  • [Tier C — Craft] Where exactly to set each threshold → calibrate to your scenario, region, and group's mobility; when unsure, set earlier — the asymmetry favors it.

8.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: The discipline of pre-deciding under calm and honoring the decision under pressure is the same discipline that runs the whole scenario method. A group that can honor its own trigger ladder can honor its emergency protocols; one that cannot, cannot.
