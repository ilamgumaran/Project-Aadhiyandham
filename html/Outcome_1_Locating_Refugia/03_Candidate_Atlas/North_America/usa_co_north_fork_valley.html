1. Location Overview

  • Name: North Fork Valley (Paonia / Hotchkiss)
  • Coordinates/Region: Western Slope, Colorado (Delta County)
  • GPS Coordinates: 38.8683° N, 107.5920° W
  • Base Elevation: ~1,600m - 1,800m (5,300 - 6,000 ft) - Note: Exception to 100-300m rule.
  • Primary Biome: Colorado Plateau Shrublands / Mountain Transition

2. Phase I: Placement & Arrival (Pass/Fail Criteria)

  • [x] Elevation: 100m+ above sea level? Yes (Significantly higher).
  • [x] Protection: Shielded by a major mountain range? Yes. Protected by the massive Grand Mesa to the north and West Elk Mountains to the east.
  • [x] Gravity Water: Year-round water sources from higher elevation? Yes. Fed by snowmelt and high-altitude reservoirs flowing down the North Fork of the Gunnison River.
  • Notes: While this breaks the <300m altitude rule, it is included because it is the premier "thermal belt" of the Rockies. The unique topography drains freezing air off the agricultural benches, creating a Mediterranean-like microclimate at high altitude.

3. Phase II: Stabilization & Sustenance

  • Solar Potential: Extreme. Over 300 days of intense, high-altitude sunshine per year. Ideal for passive solar design and winter greenhouse heating.
  • Soil Type: Mineral-rich, well-draining alluvial soils on the benches and mesas.
  • Biomass Availability: Moderate to High. Sagebrush and juniper at lower elevations transitioning rapidly to scrub oak, aspen, and higher-altitude pines/firs.
  • Agricultural Score (8.5/10): Famous for stone fruits (peaches, cherries) and grapes. Excellent for amaranth, quinoa, and potatoes. Water rights/irrigation management is the single limiting factor, requiring highly efficient water systems (swales, drip).

4. Phase III: The Human System

  • Navigability: The agricultural mesas are flat and easily navigable, though the terrain between mesas can be steep and rugged.
  • Climate & Mood: High diurnal swings (hot days, cool nights). The intense, clear light and vast, open views are psychologically uplifting, aligning well with the restorative aesthetic of "Light on Two Sides."
  • Health Score (8/10): Very low humidity reduces fungal diseases and respiratory issues. Medicinal plants are different (e.g., Osha root, desert sage) but potent. High altitude requires physiological adaptation.

5. Phase IV & V: Wisdom & Synthesis

  • Geological Foundations: Exceptional access to sandstone, river rock, and clays. Historically a coal mining region, meaning high carbon resources are available, though reliance on them contradicts the Aadhiyandham philosophy.
  • Isolation vs. Network: Geographically isolated on the Western Slope. Winters can close major mountain passes, creating an enforced, peaceful isolation that protects the valley from external disruption.

6. Final Verdict

  • Recommendation: PROCEED (High-Altitude Alternative)
  • Justification: For a society willing to manage water rigorously, the North Fork Valley offers a nearly perfect physical environment for the "centered null." The combination of extreme solar energy, natural thermal belts, and deep mountain protection makes it a premier high-altitude refugium.

7. Practical Implementation Guide for Beginners

7.1. Step-by-Step Action Plan

  • Preparation: Read this module fully before attempting to build or implement. Gather raw materials locally.
  • Execution: Build a small-scale prototype first. For example, if building a mechanical system or shelter, make a miniature version to test physics and material strength.
  • Review: Test the implementation under stress (wind, water, heavy use) and refine.

7.2. Troubleshooting & Failure Modes

  • System Failure: When a system breaks, apply the "5 Whys" technique to find the root cause (e.g., The gear broke. Why? It was made of soft wood. Why? We lacked hardwood. Why?). Fix the root cause, not just the symptom.
  • Burnout: Decadal survival is a marathon, not a sprint. If the community is exhausted, lower productivity expectations and focus on rest and social cohesion.

7.3. Community & Decadal Flourishing

  • Apprenticeship: No critical skill should be held by only one person. Every master must have an apprentice. Document local modifications to these original instructions in a physical journal.
