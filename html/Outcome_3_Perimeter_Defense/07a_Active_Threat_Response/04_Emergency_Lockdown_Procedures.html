Alignment: Outcome 3: Perimeter Sovereignty and Passive Defense

Lockdown is the last resort. It is triggered only when passive defense has been breached AND de-escalation has failed — meaning the community has already moved through Phases 4-5 of Module 03 (verbal warnings, non-lethal deterrence, withdrawal signals) and the threat continues to advance. Lockdown is not a combat plan. It is a structured retreat into the settlement's most defensible position: the Safe Core. The objective is not to defeat the threat but to outlast it, buying time until the threat withdraws of its own accord, which raiding parties almost always do when they encounter a sealed, fire-resistant position they cannot enter quickly. The community does not need to win a confrontation. It needs to survive one.

The critical insight from historical siege warfare is straightforward: a small group in a prepared position can outlast a much larger force if they have water, food, ventilation, and discipline. A single narrow entrance eliminates numerical advantage. Thick walls eliminate ranged attacks. Fire suppression eliminates the primary weapon used against fixed positions. What remains is a waiting game, and the defenders hold the advantage because they are sheltered and supplied while the attackers are exposed and operating on a clock — they must take the settlement quickly or move on before they exhaust their own supplies or attract attention from other groups.

The 3-minute lockdown target is not arbitrary. Evacuation research (Gwynne et al., 1999) demonstrates that rehearsed groups execute emergency procedures 3-5 times faster than unrehearsed groups. An unrehearsed community will take 10-15 minutes to reach full lockdown. A fast-moving threat can cover the distance from the perimeter to the settlement center in 2-4 minutes. The math is unforgiving: without rehearsal, the threat arrives before the lockdown is complete. Monthly drills are therefore the mechanism that makes the 3-minute target achievable — not an aspiration, but a non-negotiable operational requirement. The first drill will take 8-10 minutes. By the fourth drill, the community should be under 3 minutes. If they are not, the drill frequency must increase until they are.

> DANGER: A sealed Safe Core with inadequate ventilation will become lethal through CO2 accumulation within 4-8 hours depending on occupancy. Before ANY real lockdown, the Safe Core MUST be tested for ventilation adequacy. Test: light a candle in the sealed space at maximum occupancy — if it gutters within 2 hours, ventilation is INADEQUATE. Fix before proceeding. CO2 poisoning causes confusion and loss of consciousness before the victim recognizes the danger. This is not a theoretical concern — it is the single most common cause of death in improvised shelters.

Theoretical Foundation

The Physics of Defensible Positions

A defensive position's effectiveness is determined by the ratio of defender effort to attacker effort required. A position is strong when the attacker must expend enormous effort to breach it while the defender expends very little effort to hold it. The Safe Core is designed to maximize this ratio across every attack vector.

A solid earth or stone wall of 30 cm or greater thickness cannot be breached by hand tools in under 1 hour. Even with improvised battering instruments, rammed earth absorbs kinetic energy through plastic deformation rather than cracking catastrophically. This means an attacker must either breach the wall (slow, loud, exhausting) or find the entrance. If the entrance is a single narrow opening, the attacker's numerical advantage evaporates — only one person can enter at a time, and a single defender inside with a heavy implement can hold that opening indefinitely.

This is the "Thermopylae principle" at miniature scale. At the Battle of Thermopylae (480 BCE), a force of roughly 7,000 Greeks held a narrow coastal pass against a Persian army estimated at 70,000-300,000 for three days, because the geography compressed the attackers' front to a width that negated their numbers. The Safe Core applies the same geometry: one entrance, 80-100 cm wide, defended from inside. The ratio of defenders to attackers needed is inverted from open ground (where numbers dominate) to the narrow entrance (where position dominates).

Fire is the historical primary weapon against fixed positions, not direct assault. From the burning of Roman villas by Gallic raiders to the fire-arrow sieges of Japanese castles, attackers who cannot breach a wall will attempt to burn it. The fire suppression protocol (wet hides, sand buckets, fire-resistant construction) addresses the primary attack vector, not a secondary one. A Safe Core that can resist fire and has a single barricaded entrance is, for practical purposes, impregnable to any force without heavy siege equipment — equipment that raiding parties do not carry.

Ventilation Science for Sealed Spaces

Understanding CO2 dynamics in a sealed space is essential because the Safe Core must balance two competing requirements: sealing against intrusion and maintaining breathable air. The physics are unforgiving.

Human CO2 production averages approximately 200-250 mL/min per adult at rest. Under stress — which is the expected condition during a real lockdown — production rises to 300-350 mL/min due to elevated heart rate and respiration. Children produce proportionally less (roughly 60-75% of adult output), but a community with 30 occupants will include a mix of ages.

Ambient atmospheric CO2 is approximately 400 ppm (parts per million). The physiological progression of CO2 exposure follows a well-documented curve: drowsiness begins at 1,000 ppm; headache and impaired cognition at 2,000-5,000 ppm; dangerous physiological effects at 40,000 ppm (4%); loss of consciousness at 100,000 ppm (10%). For a sealed room of 50 cubic meters with 30 people producing an average of 250 mL/min each, CO2 concentration reaches 1,000 ppm in approximately 20-30 minutes and dangerous levels in 4-8 hours, depending on initial air quality and room geometry.

The ventilation requirement is a minimum of 8-10 litres per second per person of fresh air exchange. For 30 people, this means 240-300 litres per second — a substantial airflow that requires a properly designed ventilation shaft. The shaft must satisfy three constraints simultaneously: (a) large enough cross-section for the required airflow (minimum 15 cm x 15 cm, larger is better), (b) elevated or baffled at the exit so it cannot be blocked from outside without climbing, and (c) screened with metal mesh (aperture less than 5 mm) so that embers and smoke cannot enter.

Medieval castle keeps solved this problem through ventilation shafts disguised as arrow slits or chimney flues. The design served dual purpose — observation and air exchange. The Safe Core should adopt the same principle: the ventilation shaft doubles as a concealed observation point, providing both breathable air and situational awareness.

Emergency Evacuation Psychology

Gwynne et al. (1999) demonstrated in their review of evacuation modelling methodologies that rehearsed occupants evacuate 3-5 times faster than unrehearsed ones. This finding has been replicated across building fire evacuations, ship evacuations, and aircraft emergency exits. The mechanism is procedural memory: when the body has physically performed the sequence multiple times, the conscious mind does not need to process decisions during the emergency. The feet move before the brain fully comprehends the situation.

The "normalcy bias" is the single greatest obstacle to effective emergency response. Research by John Leach (2004) found that in emergencies, 70-80% of people initially freeze rather than act. They wait for social cues confirming that this is "really" an emergency — they look to others for guidance, they rationalize the alarm as a drill or false alarm, they continue their current activity. The lockdown signal (continuous horn) and monthly drills counter normalcy bias by creating an automatic trained response that bypasses the conscious evaluation phase. When the horn sounds, the body moves. This is the purpose of the drill.

Children under stress respond differently depending on age. Children aged 4-8 respond best to physical guidance: the Shepherd physically takes their hand and moves them. Verbal instruction alone ("go to the Safe Core") is ineffective under stress for this age group. Children aged 8-14 can follow rehearsed procedures if they have been drilled, but they require a familiar adult presence to maintain compliance. Teenagers respond similarly to adults and can be assigned junior roles in the lockdown sequence.

The panic myth deserves explicit correction. Research consistently shows that true panic — irrational flight behavior, trampling, complete loss of self-control — is extremely rare in emergencies. Most emergency failures are caused by inaction (normalcy bias), not panic. People do not stampede; they stand still. The drill program combats inaction, not panic. Framing drills as "preventing panic" is both inaccurate and counterproductive — it suggests that the community members are the danger, when in fact the danger is hesitation.

Historical Siege Principles

The medieval European castle keep represents centuries of iterative design for the exact scenario the Safe Core addresses: a last-resort refuge when outer defenses are breached. The keep's defining characteristics were thick walls (2-4 metres of stone), a single entrance above ground level (accessible by removable ladder or drawbridge, eliminating ground-level breach), an internal water supply (well or cistern), and food stores sufficient for weeks to months. The keep was not the castle — it was the castle's inner sanctum, the place where the lord's family and household retreated when the curtain walls fell.

The Safe Core applies these principles at settlement scale with appropriate material substitutions: cob or rammed earth for stone, a heavy timber door with crossbar for a drawbridge, a ventilated shaft for arrow slits, and a 48-hour supply cache for a months-long larder. The scale is smaller, but the principles are identical.

The most important historical lesson is this: most sieges ended not through breach but through starvation or disease inside the keep. The 48-hour supply requirement is the minimum — longer is always better. But 48 hours covers the realistic threat window for a raiding party, which operates under severe time pressure. Raiders cannot sustain a siege; they lack supply lines, they are vulnerable to counterattack from neighboring communities, and every hour spent at one target is an hour not spent at softer targets elsewhere. A raiding party that encounters a sealed, fire-resistant Safe Core with no easy entry will almost certainly move on within 24-48 hours. The community need only wait.

Core Principles

  1. The 3-Minute Standard. Full lockdown — from signal to sealed door with all personnel accounted for — must be achievable in under 3 minutes. This is only achievable through monthly drills. Unrehearsed communities take 10-15 minutes, which is long enough for a fast-moving threat to traverse the entire settlement. The 3-minute standard is the line between a lockdown that works and one that does not.
  2. The Single Signal Principle. The lockdown signal must be unique, unambiguous, and recognizable by every person in the settlement, including children. One signal, one meaning, one response. No interpretation required. No escalation ladder to remember. When the continuous horn sounds three times, everyone moves to the Safe Core. Nothing else in the settlement's signaling system sounds like this.
  3. The Assigned Roles Principle. Every person has one pre-assigned role: Shepherd, Barricader, Supply Runner, Fire Watch, or Rear Guard. Confusion in emergencies arises from people not knowing what to do. Pre-assignment eliminates decision paralysis entirely. Each person knows their role, knows their route, and knows their backup role. There is no deliberation during a lockdown — only execution.
  4. The Non-Combatant Priority. The first people into the Safe Core are children, elderly, and injured. The last are the Rear Guard. This ordering is not chivalry — it is efficiency. Non-combatants move slowly and need physical assistance. Getting them moving first prevents bottlenecks at the Safe Core entrance during the critical final 60 seconds when Barricaders and Rear Guard are falling back.
  5. The Discipline of Waiting. Once inside the Safe Core, the hardest part is waiting. The urge to open the door and check is powerful, especially when sounds from outside are ambiguous. The all-clear protocol — two independent observers from different vantage points must confirm the threat has departed — prevents premature emergence that could expose the community to a threat that is still present, repositioned, or baiting the door.

Practical Implementation

Safe Core Design

The Safe Core is the single most defensible structure in the settlement, designated as the community's emergency shelter. If no suitable structure exists, the first construction priority after basic shelter is a root cellar or earthen bunker that doubles as the Safe Core. Its specifications are non-negotiable:

  • Dimensions: For 30-person capacity, minimum 50 cubic metres of internal volume. This can be achieved with a 4 m x 4 m footprint and 3 m ceiling height, or a larger rectangular shape (e.g., 5 m x 4 m x 2.5 m). Larger is better — occupants under stress need space to avoid physical crowding that accelerates CO2 buildup and psychological distress.
  • Wall construction: Cob or rammed earth, minimum 40 cm thick. Alternatively, stone masonry at 30 cm or greater, or log construction using logs of 20 cm or greater diameter. Earth-sheltered construction (partially underground) is ideal — soil provides thermal mass, fire resistance, and structural strength simultaneously.
  • Entry: Single door, minimum 80 cm wide and 180 cm tall. Constructed of heavy timber, minimum 8 cm thick hardwood. Barred from inside with a heavy timber crossbar resting in iron or hardwood brackets mounted on either side of the door frame. The crossbar must be stored within arm's reach of the door, not across the room.
  • Ventilation shaft: Minimum 15 cm x 15 cm cross-section. Angled upward at 30-45 degrees to prevent rainwater entry. Screened with metal mesh (aperture less than 5 mm) to prevent ember entry. Exit point at 2 metres or more above ground level, behind a concealed baffle that prevents visual detection and cannot be easily blocked from outside.
  • Water: Sealed clay vessels, 20 litres per person (600 litres for 30 people). Distributed across a minimum of 4 vessels to spread the weight load on the floor and provide redundancy if one vessel cracks.
  • Food: 3-day supply of shelf-stable food — hardtack, pemmican, dried fruit, nuts — in sealed containers. Rotate stock every 6 months during scheduled inventory checks.
  • Sanitation: 2 sealed buckets with tight-fitting lids (improvised latrines), lime powder for sanitation and odor control. Adequate for 48 hours with 30 occupants if managed with discipline.
  • Lighting: 2-3 beeswax candles. These serve dual purpose: illumination and ventilation monitoring. A guttering candle is the earliest visible warning that CO2 is rising and ventilation is inadequate.
  • Medical: Basic first aid kit — bandages, antiseptic, tourniquet, splint materials (see Module 05.04 for full kit specifications).
  • Observation: The Safe Core must have a concealed observation point — a peephole in the door, an elevated vent with a sightline to the settlement exterior, or both. Situational awareness during lockdown is critical for timing the all-clear.

Barricade System

Every entry point to the settlement (trails, gates, gaps in the living wall) must have pre-positioned barricade materials that can be deployed in under 30 seconds by a single person:

  • Barrier logs: 2 pre-cut heavy logs per entry point, minimum 15 cm diameter and 2 metres long, stored on wall-mounted brackets that allow them to be dropped into position in under 10 seconds. The logs rest in U-shaped bracket receivers mounted on either side of the entry point. No lifting required — the Barricader releases a pin and gravity does the work.
  • Lashing: Pre-tied rope loops allow the logs to be secured to anchor posts with a single pull. The knots must be pre-set so that the Barricader only needs to pull one free end to cinch the lashing tight.
  • Sand buckets: 10-15 litre capacity, positioned at each entry point and at the Safe Core exterior. Sand smothers fire without water, which is a critical advantage — water is too valuable to use for fire suppression during a lockdown. Minimum 2 buckets per entry point, 4 at the Safe Core.
  • Wet hides: Pre-soaked in a water bucket (refresh daily in dry conditions, every 3 days in humid conditions). Can be thrown over burning debris or draped over the Safe Core door to smother flames. Maintain 2-3 wet hides at each entry point and 4-6 at the Safe Core.

Lockdown Signal

The lockdown signal must satisfy two requirements absolutely: it must be audible from every point in the settlement, and it must be impossible to confuse with any other signal in the community's communication system.

Standard signal: Continuous horn blast of 10 or more seconds, repeated three times with 5-second gaps between blasts. Total duration: approximately 40 seconds. This is the only signal in the settlement's alert system that uses a continuous blast — all other signals (wake-up, meal call, weather warning) use short, distinct patterns. The continuous blast is viscerally different and triggers the trained response without conscious interpretation.

Horn positions: minimum 3 horns distributed at watchpoints around the settlement. Any watchperson can initiate lockdown. Once one horn sounds, all other horn positions repeat the signal to ensure full settlement coverage. The signal propagates across the settlement in under 15 seconds.

Lockdown Roles

Every community member has a pre-assigned role during lockdown. Every person must know their primary role and at least one backup role. Roles are assigned based on physical capability, proximity to key positions, and temperament.

Supply Runners 1-2 adults Grab emergency seed bank, medical kit, and archive box; deliver to Safe Core Items must be stored in grab-and-go containers at pre-known locations. Route to Safe Core memorized.

Cross-training is mandatory. Every Shepherd must know the Barricader procedure. Every Barricader must know the Supply Runner locations. If someone is absent, injured, or unreachable during a lockdown, the nearest person with backup training assumes their role without waiting for instruction.

The Lockdown Sequence


 T+0:00  Continuous horn blast ×3 (lockdown signal activated)
 T+0:30  Shepherds moving non-combatants to Safe Core
 T+1:00  Barricaders dropping entry-point barriers
 T+1:30  Supply Runners arrive at Safe Core with critical items
 T+2:00  Fire Watch in position at Safe Core exterior
 T+2:30  Rear Guard conducts headcount at Safe Core entrance
 T+3:00  Safe Core sealed. All personnel accounted for. Door barred.

Target: Full lockdown in under 3 minutes. The first drill will take 8-10 minutes. This is expected. By the fourth monthly drill, the community should achieve the 3-minute standard. If they do not, increase drill frequency to biweekly until the standard is met.

Night lockdown adaptation: Add 30-60 seconds to the timeline. Shepherds use memorized routes without lights (lights reveal positions). Pre-mark critical paths with tactile markers — a raised rope staked along the ground leading from each sleeping shelter to the Safe Core, detectable by foot in darkness. Night drills must be conducted at least quarterly.

Drill Progression

The drill program follows a deliberate progression that builds competence incrementally:

  • Drill 1 (Month 1): Announced, daytime, walk-through pace. The community leader explains each step as it happens. Time the drill but do not pressure speed. Expected time: 8-10 minutes. Purpose: everyone learns their role and route.
  • Drill 2 (Month 2): Announced, daytime, full speed. No walk-through narration. Each person executes their role from memory. Expected time: 5-7 minutes. Purpose: procedural memory begins forming.
  • Drill 3 (Month 3): Unannounced, daytime. The horn sounds without warning during normal activity. Expected time: 4-6 minutes. Purpose: test normalcy-bias response under realistic conditions.
  • Drill 4 (Month 4): Unannounced, nighttime. Shepherds navigate by tactile markers. No lights. Expected time: 4-7 minutes. Purpose: the hardest scenario. If the community can execute a night lockdown, daytime lockdowns become trivial.
  • Drills 5+ (Monthly, rotating): Vary the scenario each month — during meals, during work parties when some personnel are at distant garden plots, with one Barricader "missing" (testing backup assignments), with a simulated injury requiring carry to the Safe Core. Record times. Celebrate improvements publicly.

Children aged 8 and above should know the lockdown signal and their assigned Shepherd by name. By age 14, adolescents should be training for Barricader or Supply Runner roles with adult supervision. Framing early drills as a timed team challenge rather than a fear exercise builds competence without unnecessary anxiety.

Inside the Safe Core

Once the Safe Core is sealed, the following protocol governs all activity inside:

  1. Headcount. The Rear Guard calls the roster. Every name, every response. If someone is missing, record their name and last known location. Do NOT send a rescue party. Do NOT break lockdown.
  2. Silence. Minimize all noise. Listen to the external environment. Assign one person to the observation point. All others sit or lie down to reduce CO2 production.
  3. Ration. Begin water and food rationing immediately. Assume a minimum 48-hour lockdown. Distribute water in small measured portions (250 mL per person per 6-hour block). No food in the first 6 hours unless medical need dictates otherwise.
  4. Observe. The assigned observer monitors the exterior through the concealed observation point and reports in whispers to the Rear Guard. Rotate the observer every 2 hours to prevent fatigue-induced missed observations.
  5. Monitor ventilation. Watch the candles. If a candle gutters or dims noticeably, ventilation is becoming inadequate. Partially unblock the ventilation shaft or, if necessary, crack the door 2-3 cm with a guard posted. Breathable air overrides perfect security.
  6. Wait. The hardest discipline. Hours may pass with no information. The protocol holds. The door stays sealed until the all-clear.

Post-Lockdown Protocol

The lockdown does not end when the threat appears to have departed. It ends when the all-clear protocol is satisfied.

  • All-clear determination: Requires two independent observers (from different vantage points, ideally the Safe Core observation point and one Rear Guard member who exits through the ventilation shaft or a secondary concealed exit) to independently confirm the threat has departed. One person's assessment is not sufficient — a single observer can be deceived by a threat that has repositioned rather than departed.
  • Emergence sequence: Rear Guard exits first and conducts a perimeter sweep of the immediate Safe Core surroundings. Only after the Rear Guard signals safe do non-combatants emerge. Children and elderly exit last (reversal of entry order) so the exit path is clear.
  • Damage assessment: Systematic check of all structures for fire damage, barrier integrity, and stolen or damaged supplies. Inventory the seed bank, medical stores, water reserves, and archive. Document everything.
  • Debrief: Community discussion within 24 hours while memory is fresh. What triggered the lockdown? Was the threat real or perceived? What worked in the sequence? What failed? Which role had the most difficulty? Adjust assignments, routes, and procedures based on findings.
  • Psychological recovery: Lockdowns — especially real ones — are traumatic events. Even drills can trigger anxiety in trauma survivors. Outcome 4, Module 08 (The Mind) protocols apply. Allow emotional processing time. Do not dismiss fear or distress. Acknowledge that the lockdown was frightening and that the community survived it together.

Common Failure Modes

  1. Ventilation Failure. The Safe Core is sealed with too many people. CO2 rises. Confusion sets in. Occupants lose consciousness without recognizing the danger. Prevention: ventilation testing before any real lockdown, using the candle test at maximum occupancy. If in doubt during a real lockdown, leave the ventilation shaft partially open rather than fully sealed. Accept slightly reduced security for breathable air — a threat outside the door is survivable; asphyxiation inside the Safe Core is not.
  2. Entry Point Left Unbarricaded. One Barricader fails to reach their assigned entry point — they were too far away, injured, or confused. The attacker enters through the undefended point. Prevention: backup assignments. If a Barricader cannot reach their post within 60 seconds of the signal, the nearest person with backup training assumes the role. The drill must include scenarios where one Barricader is "unavailable."
  3. Missing Person Dilemma. Headcount reveals someone is missing. Community pressure mounts to open the Safe Core for a rescue attempt. Prevention: the protocol is absolute. Do not break lockdown for a missing person. The missing individual is likely hiding in a secondary location, has fled into the surrounding terrain, or has already been captured. Opening the Safe Core risks every person inside for the uncertain benefit of one. Rescue is attempted only after the all-clear protocol is satisfied.
  4. Fire Attack on the Safe Core. Attackers set fire to the Safe Core structure itself. Prevention: Safe Core construction must be fire-resistant — earth, stone, or earth-bermed construction, never exposed timber framing. Fire Watch positioned with sand and wet hides at the Safe Core exterior. The ventilation shaft must be baffled so smoke cannot be pumped in, and screened so burning materials cannot be pushed through.
  5. Drill Fatigue. Monthly drills become tedious. The community stops taking them seriously. Attendance drops. Response time degrades back toward 10+ minutes. Prevention: vary drill scenarios — daytime, nighttime, during meals, during work, announced, unannounced. Add a competitive element by timing each drill and publicly celebrating improvements. Rotate which entry point is designated as the "breach point" each drill so Barricaders cannot predict the scenario. Never skip drills, even when morale is low — especially when morale is low, because that is when the community is most vulnerable to normalcy bias in a real emergency.

Vocabulary of the Foundation

  • Lockdown — The activation of the full emergency retreat protocol, triggered by the lockdown signal, moving all personnel and critical supplies to the Safe Core.
  • Safe Core — The single most defensible structure in the settlement, designated as the community shelter during emergencies. Characterized by thick walls, a single barricaded entrance, ventilation, and pre-positioned supplies.
  • Lockdown signal — A unique, unambiguous auditory signal (continuous horn blast repeated three times) that triggers immediate lockdown. No other signal in the settlement sounds like this.
  • Lockdown sequence — The timed, rehearsed series of actions from signal activation (T+0:00) to sealed Safe Core (T+3:00), executed by personnel in their assigned roles.
  • Shepherd — A lockdown role responsible for physically guiding non-combatants (children, elderly, injured) to the Safe Core. Each Shepherd is paired with specific individuals by name.
  • Barricader — A lockdown role responsible for deploying pre-positioned barriers at assigned entry points, then falling back to the Safe Core.
  • Supply Runner — A lockdown role responsible for retrieving critical items (seed bank, medical kit, archive) and delivering them to the Safe Core.
  • Fire Watch — A lockdown role responsible for fire suppression at the Safe Core exterior using sand buckets and wet hides.
  • Rear Guard — The last personnel to enter the Safe Core. Responsible for headcount, roster confirmation, and sealing the entry. Authorized to make the final seal decision.
  • Headcount — The roster call conducted by the Rear Guard immediately after the Safe Core is sealed. Every name called, every response recorded.
  • All-clear protocol — The procedure for ending a lockdown, requiring two independent observers to confirm the threat has departed before the Safe Core is opened.
  • Normalcy bias — The psychological tendency to freeze and wait for social confirmation during an emergency rather than acting immediately. Countered by drill-based procedural memory.
