# Conflict Resolution: Engineered Protocols for Community Equilibrium

**Alignment:** Outcome 4: Psychological Centeredness

Conflict in a small, isolated community is not a pathology to be eliminated. It is a natural
consequence of diverse humans sharing limited space and resources under stress. When thirty
people share water infrastructure, food production, sleeping quarters, and decision-making
authority with no option to leave, friction is inevitable. The goal is not to prevent conflict
but to PROCESS it efficiently before it metastasizes into faction formation, power
concentration, and settlement collapse. Lucy Sargisson and Lyman Tower Sargent's study of
more than 100 intentional communities (*Living in Utopia*, 2004) found that interpersonal
conflict, not resource scarcity, caused more than 70% of community failures. The settlements
that survived were not conflict-free. They had BETTER CONFLICT RESOLUTION PROTOCOLS.

The distinction matters enormously. A community that views conflict as failure will suppress
it, driving grievances underground where they ferment into resentment, passive aggression,
and eventual rupture. A community that views conflict as signal — as useful information about
misaligned expectations, unmet needs, or structural problems — will surface it early, process
it transparently, and emerge stronger.

Every protocol in this module has been tested in real communities: indigenous (Navajo
Peacemaking, Maori family group conferences), religious (Quaker clearness committees,
Hutterite communal arbitration), intentional (kibbutzim, Findhorn, Twin Oaks), and military
(after-action review processes). These systems have demonstrated efficacy across cultures and
centuries. What follows is the neuroscience of conflict, the structured mediation process,
the hierarchy management systems, and the community maintenance practices that transform
conflict from a destructive force into a productive one.

> **SAFE**: All conflict resolution techniques described here are non-coercive verbal and procedural protocols. No physical intervention is described. For situations involving physical threat, see Outcome 3, Module 07a.03 (De-escalation and Non-Lethal Deterrence).

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Neuroscience of Conflict

The human brain does not process social conflict the way it processes a math problem. When
the amygdala detects a social threat — an insult, an accusation, a perceived injustice, a
raised voice — it triggers the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis BEFORE the
prefrontal cortex can evaluate the threat rationally. Daniel Goleman named this the
"amygdala hijack" (*Emotional Intelligence*, 1995), and the physiological cascade it
initiates follows a predictable timeline:

- Adrenaline floods the bloodstream within **12-15 milliseconds** — faster than conscious
  thought
- Cortisol, the longer-acting stress hormone, reaches peak blood concentration within
  **20-30 minutes**
- Full cortisol clearance from the body requires **12-24 hours**

During this entire window, the neurochemical environment actively opposes rational dialogue,
perspective-taking, and empathy.

Heart rate provides a reliable external indicator of this internal state. Dave Grossman's
research on stress physiology (*On Combat*, 2004) mapped the relationship between heart rate
and cognitive capacity in detail:

| Heart Rate (bpm) | Cognitive State | Mediation Viability |
|---|---|---|
| 60-80 (resting) | Full cognitive function — nuanced reasoning, empathy, creative problem-solving | Optimal |
| 115+ (sympathetic activation) | Fine motor control degrades | Marginal — proceed with caution |
| 145+ (high sympathetic) | Complex reasoning degrades — cannot hold multiple perspectives simultaneously | Non-viable — mediation will fail |
| 175+ (maximal activation) | Rational brain essentially offline — only gross motor function and primal threat responses | Dangerous — do not attempt |

The person at 175+ bpm cannot hear your words, cannot consider your perspective, and cannot
agree to anything meaningful.

The implication for conflict resolution protocol design stands clear: NO mediation can
succeed while participants remain in sympathetic activation. The 24-hour cooling-off period
described below represents neurological necessity, not social courtesy. Attempting mediation
while cortisol remains elevated produces escalation, not resolution. A mediator who ignores
this biology — who tries to "talk it out" immediately — will make the conflict worse, not
better. The protocol must respect the body's timeline for returning to baseline.

### Restorative vs. Retributive Justice

Two fundamentally different paradigms exist for responding to harm, and the choice between them determines whether a community's conflict resolution system heals or damages.

Retributive justice follows this logic: "Someone did wrong. Identify the wrongdoer. Assign
punishment proportional to the wrong." The focus centers on the OFFENDER and their
punishment. The outcome: the offender receives punishment, but the harm done to the victim
and community remains unrepaired. The punished person frequently becomes resentful, nursing
a grievance that increases future conflict probability. In a small settlement where the
punished person cannot leave, this dynamic poisons the social environment indefinitely.

Restorative justice follows a different logic entirely: "Harm occurred. Bring together the
affected parties. Understand the harm from all perspectives. Collectively determine how to
repair it." The focus centers on the HARM and its repair. The outcome: the victim's
experience receives acknowledgment, the offender understands the impact of their actions,
and both participate in designing the repair.

Howard Zehr formalized this as a distinct paradigm in *The Little Book of Restorative
Justice* (2002), crystallizing three core questions that drive every restorative process:

1. Who was harmed?
2. What are their needs?
3. Whose obligation is it to address those needs?

**Empirical evidence** strongly favors restorative approaches. Lawrence Sherman and Heather
Strang conducted a Campbell Collaboration systematic review (2007) and found that restorative
justice conferences reduced repeat offending by **14-27%** compared to conventional criminal
justice processing. Victim satisfaction with restorative processes consistently exceeds
**80%**, compared to 30-50% with conventional courts. These numbers become even more
significant in a settlement context, where "repeat offending" means ongoing daily friction
and "victim dissatisfaction" means a community member who has lost faith in the community's
ability to protect them.

**Indigenous precedent** provides centuries of tested implementation:

- **Navajo Peacemaking** (hozhoojiinaat'aanii): Centers on restoring harmony (hozho) rather
  than assigning blame. The peacemaker (naat'aani) does not judge or rule. They facilitate a
  conversation in which all parties speak, receive a hearing, and collectively design the
  restoration of balance. This process has operated continuously for centuries and has been
  formally integrated into the Navajo Nation's modern judicial system — a living
  demonstration that restorative justice scales across time.

- **Maori restorative justice** (New Zealand): Employs marae-based family group conferences,
  where the extended family and community meet to address harm, understand its roots, and
  design collective accountability. New Zealand's government adapted this process into its
  formal youth justice system in 1989, with demonstrated reductions in reoffending that
  outperform punitive alternatives.

### Gottman's Four Horsemen and Community Application

John Gottman at the University of Washington spent decades (1970s-present) studying the communication patterns that predict relationship failure. He identified four patterns — which he named the "Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse" — that predict relationship dissolution with over 90% accuracy:

1. **Criticism**: Attacking the person's CHARACTER rather than addressing a specific behavior. "You never help with anything" versus "The dishes were not done yesterday." Criticism frames the problem as a permanent defect in the other person's identity, which triggers defensive escalation because identity feels non-negotiable.

2. **Contempt**: Communicating superiority, disgust, or mockery — eye-rolling, sarcasm, name-calling, sneering. Contempt stands as the single strongest predictor of relationship dissolution in Gottman's data. It communicates "I hold a position above you" and activates deep threat responses in the recipient because it attacks their social standing within the group.

3. **Defensiveness**: Responding to complaints with counter-complaints or denial rather than acknowledgment. "You left the gate open." "Well, YOU forgot to check the water tanks last week." Defensiveness prevents problem-solving by redirecting every conversation away from the issue at hand and into an escalating exchange of grievances.

4. **Stonewalling**: Withdrawing from interaction entirely — refusing to engage, going silent, physically leaving. Stonewalling appears when a person has become physiologically overwhelmed (Gottman calls this "flooding") and can no longer process the interaction. While it may feel self-protective, it communicates abandonment to the other party and prevents resolution entirely.

In a settlement context, these four patterns operate identically but at community scale:

- **Criticism** generates factions ("He never does his share" becomes a group narrative)
- **Contempt** creates social hierarchy (mockery of a community member's abilities becomes a
  shared attitude)
- **Defensiveness** prevents problem-solving at the community level (every meeting becomes a
  counter-accusation tournament)
- **Stonewalling** isolates individuals from the community (the withdrawn member becomes
  peripheral, then resentful, then disruptive or departed)

The protocols in this module directly counter each horseman:

- **E-Prime** (see Module 02, Linguistic Precision) prevents the first two horsemen by making
  character attacks linguistically impossible — you cannot say "He IS lazy" in E-Prime; you
  must describe observable behavior.
- **Active Mirroring** prevents the third horseman by requiring acknowledgment before
  response.
- **The cooling-off period** channels the impulse to stonewall into a structured pause with a
  scheduled resumption, converting withdrawal from abandonment into a defined process with a
  clear return date.

### Game Theory: Cooperation Under Repeated Interaction

Robert Axelrod's *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984) demonstrated through tournament
experiments that in iterated games — where the same parties interact repeatedly over time —
cooperative strategies outperform aggressive ones. The optimal strategy, Tit-for-Tat,
cooperates on the first move and then mirrors the other player's previous move. It never
defects first but always responds to defection, and it immediately returns to cooperation
when the other player cooperates.

The settlement context provides the ideal environment for cooperation to emerge, because
members interact daily for years or decades. This represents exactly the iterated game
structure where cooperation should flourish — IF the right conditions hold. Axelrod
identified the necessary conditions:

1. Interactions must continue indefinitely, with no "end game" where defection pays
2. Defection must be detected reliably
3. The cost of defection must exceed the benefit
4. There must exist a mechanism for returning to cooperation after a defection event

The conflict resolution protocol provides conditions (2), (3), and (4):

- **Condition 2 — Detection**: Transparent mediation ensures that defection (harming another
  community member, free-riding, hoarding) becomes visible to the community.
- **Condition 3 — Cost**: Community accountability through the mediation circle ensures that
  the social costs of defection exceed the benefits.
- **Condition 4 — Return path**: The restorative process provides the return-to-cooperation
  mechanism — it offers a structured path back from conflict to functional relationship,
  which Tit-for-Tat requires.

Without these mechanisms, game theory predicts that cooperation will gradually erode as
individuals discover that free-riding goes unpunished and that there exists no reliable path
back from conflict to collaboration.

## Core Principles

1. **The Cooling Principle**: No mediation begins until all participants' heart rates and cortisol levels have returned to baseline. The 24-hour minimum waiting period represents neurological infrastructure, not social convention. Attempting resolution while sympathetic activation persists will produce escalation. When in doubt, wait longer. No mediator has ever regretted waiting an extra day. Many have regretted proceeding too early.

2. **The Restoration Principle**: The goal of conflict resolution is to REPAIR HARM, not to assign blame or punish wrongdoers. Every mediation asks Zehr's three questions: Who was harmed? What are their needs? Whose obligation is it to address those needs? A mediation that identifies a "winner" and a "loser" has failed, regardless of the outcome.

3. **The E-Prime Principle**: All formal mediation uses strict E-Prime. Identity-based language ("You ARE...") does not get permitted. The mediator pauses any speaker who uses "to be" constructions and asks them to reformulate. This represents neurological intervention, not linguistic pedantry — it prevents the activation of identity-defense circuits that derail productive dialogue.

4. **The Mirroring Principle**: Before any party can respond to a grievance, they must first repeat the grievance back to the speaker's satisfaction. This prevents arguments based on misunderstanding and forces genuine listening. If mirroring cannot be achieved, the mediation pauses — the parties have not yet reached readiness. Proceeding without successful mirroring builds resolution on a foundation of misunderstanding.

5. **The Anti-Hierarchy Principle**: No individual accumulates permanent authority over conflict resolution. Mediators rotate. The Apprenticeship Mandate ensures no individual holds irreplaceable power. Any community member who attempts to leverage skill monopoly into social dominance gets identified through the community's rotation and apprenticeship systems and has their leverage structurally dismantled — not through confrontation but through skill distribution.

## Practical Implementation

### The Cooling-Off Protocol

The 24-Hour Rule from earlier iterations of this protocol remains the foundation, but the full implementation requires more specificity to function reliably under stress.

1. **Trigger**: Any of the following activates the cooling-off protocol:
   - Voices raised above conversational volume
   - Physical posturing (standing over someone, blocking a path, clenching fists)
   - Any participant verbally requests a pause

   The threshold stays deliberately low — it activates far before violence becomes likely,
   because the goal is to interrupt the neurochemical cascade early.

2. **Immediate separation**: All parties physically separate. Each goes to a different area
   of the settlement. No one follows or continues the discussion. No one sends a message
   through a third party. The separation must involve genuine physical distance — not two
   people in the same building seething in different rooms.

3. **Minimum duration**: 24 hours for disputes between two people. Extended to 48 hours if
   the dispute involves more than two people (faction dynamics add complexity) or if physical
   posturing occurred (indicating deeper sympathetic activation that requires longer clearance
   time).

4. **During cooling-off**: Each party writes a brief E-Prime account of the conflict from
   their perspective. This written account serves two purposes:

   - It forces reflection and organization of thought, converting raw emotional experience
     into structured narrative
   - It provides a reference document for mediation that prevents memory distortion — under
     stress, memory becomes unreliable within hours, and after 24 hours each party will have
     unconsciously edited their recollection to support their position

   The written account, produced closer to the event, provides an anchor.

5. **Resumption**: After the cooling period, a designated mediator contacts both parties
   separately to confirm willingness to proceed. If either party declines, the cooling period
   extends another 24 hours. Maximum three extensions (total cooling period: 96 hours) before
   the community council intervenes. If a party consistently refuses to engage after the
   maximum cooling period, the community council meets with that individual separately to
   understand the underlying resistance — which often signals a deeper problem (fear of the
   other party, shame, or a structural issue that mediation alone cannot address).

### The Mediation Circle Protocol

The Tripartite Mediation Circle constitutes the core conflict resolution mechanism. Its structure draws from Navajo Peacemaking circles, Quaker clearness committees, and modern restorative justice conferencing.

**Composition**:

- The two (or more) disputing parties
- Three neutral facilitators selected by rotating roster (never fewer than three — a single
  mediator can attract perceived bias; two can deadlock; three provides reliable neutrality
  and allows a tiebreaker if facilitators disagree on process)
- Any directly affected third parties (e.g., if the dispute involves shared resources, others
  who share those resources may attend as observers with speaking rights limited to the harm
  identification phase)

**The Space**:

Circular seating in the meditation/meeting room (see Module 03, Restorative Architecture).
No one sits at a "head" position. All participants remain equidistant from center. Natural
light enters from two sides. No audience beyond the participants listed above. The space must
feel contained and private — the knowledge that others can overhear inhibits honesty.

**The Seven-Step Process**:

1. **Opening**: The lead facilitator states the purpose: "We gather to understand what
   happened, who was affected, and how to repair the harm." The facilitator reminds all
   participants of the E-Prime requirement and the mirroring protocol. Ground rules stated:
   one speaker at a time, no interruptions, each person will have full opportunity to speak,
   and the goal centers on repair rather than punishment.

2. **Party A speaks**: Party A describes the conflict from their perspective, using E-Prime.
   No interruptions. The facilitators listen and take notes. Time limit: 10 minutes. If Party
   A needs more time, the facilitator may grant a 5-minute extension, but longer than 15
   minutes typically indicates that the speaker has moved from describing the conflict to
   relitigating it, which the facilitator gently redirects.

3. **Party B mirrors**: Before responding, Party B must restate Party A's grievance to Party
   A's satisfaction. ("What I hear you saying is...") If Party A says "No, that does not
   capture what I said," Party B tries again. The facilitator assists by identifying which
   element Party B missed or distorted. This step continues until Party A confirms the mirror
   reflects their experience accurately.

   This step alone resolves approximately **30-40% of disputes** — many conflicts rest on
   genuine misunderstanding that becomes visible only through mirroring. When Party B
   successfully mirrors a grievance they did not previously understand, the conflict often
   dissolves without further intervention.

4. **Party B speaks**: Party B describes the conflict from their perspective, using E-Prime.
   Same rules as Step 2.

5. **Party A mirrors**: Same mirroring protocol in reverse. Party A must demonstrate
   understanding of Party B's perspective before proceeding.

6. **Harm identification**: The facilitators summarize: "The harms we have heard described
   include: [specific list]." Both parties confirm the list captures all harms completely.
   If either party identifies additional harms at this stage, those receive addition to the
   list. The facilitators then read back the complete list one final time. This step matters
   because it transforms an amorphous sense of grievance into a concrete, bounded list —
   which makes repair design tractable.

7. **Repair design**: All participants — both parties AND the facilitators — discuss concrete
   actions to repair the identified harms. The repair must satisfy four criteria:

   - **(a) Specific**: "I will check the gate latch after passing through it" rather than
     "I will try harder"
   - **(b) Time-bounded**: "for the next 30 days"
   - **(c) Observable**: others can verify the action was taken without the actor needing to
     self-report
   - **(d) Agreed to by the harmed party**: not imposed by the facilitators

   If the parties cannot agree on a repair, the facilitators propose options and both parties
   can accept or modify them. A mediation that cannot produce a repair agreement after
   good-faith effort gets escalated to the community council, which represents a last resort
   rather than a routine step.

**Follow-up**: The facilitators check in with both parties at two intervals:

- **7-day check-in**: Confirm the repair agreement has begun and functions as intended. Make
  minor adjustments if practical obstacles have emerged.
- **30-day check-in**: Confirm the repair agreement has been fully fulfilled. If both parties
  report satisfaction, the matter closes.

If the repair has not been fulfilled at either check-in, the facilitators convene a brief
follow-up mediation to understand why and adjust the agreement. Upon closure, the
facilitators destroy their notes — no permanent record of resolved disputes exists, because
a permanent record transforms mediation from a healing process into a surveillance mechanism.

### Dismantling Hierarchy: The Apprenticeship Mandate

The Strongman represents one of the most dangerous threats to a small settlement's
stability. A Strongman emerges not necessarily from malice but from structural conditions:
when one person holds a critical skill that no one else possesses, that person holds de facto
power over the community regardless of their intentions. If only one person can repair the
water system, maintain the forge, suture a wound, or navigate by stars, that person's
cooperation becomes non-negotiable — and non-negotiable dependency breeds resentment,
deference, and eventually either tyranny or sabotage.

- **The Principle**: No critical settlement skill remains held by only one person. Every
  skill-holder must have at least one active apprentice who trains toward full competence.
  This represents not just knowledge preservation but structural power distribution.

- **Implementation**: The apprenticeship timeline varies by skill complexity:

  | Skill Category | Examples | Time to Competence |
  |---|---|---|
  | Basic maintenance | Water filter cleaning, fence repair, food preservation | 6 months |
  | Intermediate | Animal husbandry, medicinal herb identification, blacksmithing | 12-18 months |
  | Complex | Midwifery, advanced metalwork, structural engineering, trauma medicine | 2-3 years |

  The apprentice does not need to match the master's expertise — they need sufficient
  competence that the community can survive the master's absence.

- **The Power Audit**: Quarterly, the community reviews its skill distribution in a
  structured assessment. For each critical skill, the audit identifies:

  - (a) The primary practitioner
  - (b) The apprentice or apprentices
  - (c) The apprentice's estimated time to competence
  - (d) Any skills with no apprentice, flagged as single points of failure

  The Power Audit results get posted publicly — transparency ensures that skill monopolies
  cannot persist unnoticed.

- **Strongman Prevention**: If an individual begins leveraging their unique skill for social
  influence ("Without me, you cannot..."), the community's first response targets the
  structural condition rather than the individual. Accelerate apprenticeship in that skill.
  Recruit a second apprentice. Bring in knowledge from trade with neighboring settlements if
  available. Removing the skill monopoly removes the leverage. Confrontation without removing
  the monopoly merely creates resentment and confirms to the would-be Strongman that they
  hold real power. The structural approach succeeds where the interpersonal approach fails
  because it changes the CONDITIONS that enable dominance rather than asking the dominant
  person to voluntarily relinquish their advantage.

### Community Maintenance Practices

These practices function as PREVENTIVE infrastructure — they reduce conflict frequency and
intensity before disputes escalate to the mediation level. A community that relies solely on
mediation to manage conflict has already failed at the primary level. Mediation handles the
conflicts that slip through; these practices prevent most conflicts from forming.

- **Shared meals**: At least one communal meal per day (see Module 01, Daily Rhythm). Shared
  eating activates oxytocin release and maintains the personal relationships that prevent
  minor irritations from escalating. A person with whom you share daily bread remains a
  person to you, not an abstraction. When communal meals decline, social fragmentation
  follows reliably — this pattern has been documented in kibbutzim, monasteries, and military
  units.

- **Role rotation**: No permanent leadership positions. Coordination roles (water manager,
  food storage manager, watch coordinator, meeting facilitator) rotate on a monthly or
  seasonal schedule. Rotation prevents power accumulation, builds empathy (everyone
  experiences every role's challenges and constraints), and generates distributed competence.
  The person who complains about the water manager's decisions will manage water next month
  and discover the constraints firsthand.

- **Community check-in**: Weekly, 30-minute all-hands meeting. Each member shares one thing
  that went well during the week and one thing that needs attention. E-Prime enforced
  throughout. This practice surfaces minor problems before they fester — the dripping faucet
  that annoys someone, the shared tool that keeps disappearing, the schedule conflict that
  two people have been silently resenting. Small problems addressed early never become large
  conflicts.

- **Seasonal celebration**: Equinoxes and solstices marked with community celebration —
  shared food, music, storytelling, recognition of accomplishments. Celebration builds social
  bonds that buffer against conflict during stressful periods (pre-winter preparation,
  drought, illness, resource scarcity). The social capital accumulated during celebration
  provides a reservoir of goodwill that the community draws on during hardship.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Premature Mediation**: Mediation attempted while participants remain in sympathetic
   activation. Heart rate elevated, cortisol high, amygdala hijack active. Result: escalation
   instead of resolution. The mediator often becomes a target for displaced aggression,
   damaging future mediation credibility. Prevention: strict 24-hour minimum. When in doubt,
   wait longer. No resolution has ever been made worse by an extra day of cooling.

2. **Mediator Bias**: A facilitator has a pre-existing relationship with one party and
   unconsciously favors them — through body language, question framing, or repair design that
   favors the ally. Prevention: three facilitators selected by rotating roster with mandatory
   recusal for close relationships. Either party can object to a facilitator selection without
   providing justification. The objection itself suffices.

3. **Mirroring Refusal**: A party refuses to or cannot mirror the other's grievance. This
   serves as a diagnostic signal: it means the party remains in defensive mode (ego
   protecting identity) or genuinely does not understand the other's position. Prevention:
   pause mediation, extend cooling-off. Do not force mirroring — forced compliance without
   genuine understanding produces false resolution that collapses within days and damages
   faith in the entire mediation process. Return when the person can mirror willingly.

4. **Repair Without Specificity**: The mediation produces a vague agreement ("I will try to
   do better") that cannot be observed or verified. Result: the harmed party sees no change,
   resentment deepens, and faith in mediation erodes. This failure mode occurs most often
   when facilitators feel pressure to reach closure and accept a vague agreement rather than
   extending the mediation to design a concrete one. Prevention: the repair must meet all
   four criteria — specific, time-bounded, observable, and agreed to by the harmed party.
   The facilitators bear responsibility for ensuring these criteria before closing the
   mediation.

5. **Faction Crystallization**: A dispute between two individuals expands to involve their
   respective social circles. The community splits into two camps that support their
   respective friend or ally. Side conversations, gossip, and alliance-building replace
   direct engagement. Prevention: mediation occurs between the individuals directly involved.
   Supporters may attend as observers but do not speak during the process. The facilitators
   actively redirect any discussion of the dispute that occurs outside the mediation circle.
   The community check-in (weekly meeting) provides a space to address the faction dynamic
   openly if it has begun to form.

6. **The Apprenticeship Gap**: A critical skill-holder dies or becomes incapacitated before
   the apprentice achieves competence. The skill monopoly that the Apprenticeship Mandate
   aimed to eliminate instead gets eliminated by circumstance, leaving a dangerous gap.
   Prevention: apprenticeship begins the moment a skill-holder achieves competence, not when
   it seems convenient. The quarterly Power Audit tracks apprenticeship progress and flags
   skills where the apprentice remains far from competence. For the most critical skills
   (water, medicine, food preservation), consider training two apprentices simultaneously.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Restorative justice** — a paradigm of justice focused on repairing harm rather than punishing offenders (Zehr, 2002)
- **Retributive justice** — the conventional paradigm focused on identifying wrongdoers and assigning proportional punishment
- **Amygdala hijack** — the brain's threat-detection system overriding rational processing before the prefrontal cortex can evaluate the situation (Goleman, 1995)
- **Cooling-off period** — the mandatory 24-48 hour separation following a conflict trigger, grounded in HPA axis clearance timing
- **Tripartite Mediation Circle** — the three-facilitator mediation structure that provides neutral, balanced conflict processing
- **Active Mirroring** — the requirement that each party restate the other's grievance to their satisfaction before responding
- **E-Prime enforcement** — the prohibition of "to be" verb forms during mediation, preventing identity-based attacks
- **Four Horsemen** — Gottman's four communication patterns (criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling) that predict relationship failure
- **Tit-for-Tat** — Axelrod's optimal cooperation strategy in iterated interactions: cooperate first, then mirror the other's behavior
- **Apprenticeship Mandate** — the requirement that every critical skill-holder train at least one apprentice to prevent skill monopolies
- **Power Audit** — the quarterly community review of skill distribution and apprenticeship progress
- **Harm identification** — Step 6 of the mediation process, converting amorphous grievance into a concrete, bounded list of specific harms
- **Repair design** — Step 7 of the mediation process, producing specific, time-bounded, observable, and mutually agreed repair actions
- **Role rotation** — the practice of cycling coordination roles through community members to prevent power accumulation
