# Mental Illness and Compassionate Care: Zero-Tech Psychiatric Protocols

**Alignment:** Outcome 4: Psychological Centeredness

In any population of 50-150 people, severe mental illness WILL occur. Schizophrenia affects approximately 1% of all populations worldwide regardless of culture, nutrition, or environment (WHO data). Bipolar disorder affects approximately 2-3%. Major depressive episodes affect 5-15% of people at some point in their lives. Dementia will affect a proportion of those who survive past 65. These are not pathologies of industrial civilization — they are features of human neurobiology that manifest in every known culture across all of recorded history. A settlement that has no protocol for mental illness will respond with fear: exile, abuse, restraint, or neglect. A settlement WITH a protocol responds with understanding: the afflicted person has a brain that functions differently, not a moral failing, not a spiritual curse, and not a choice.

This module provides the neuroscience of major mental illnesses, the botanical and behavioral interventions available without pharmaceutical infrastructure, the structured care protocols, and the community education that makes compassionate management possible. The goal has never been cure — modern psychiatry, with its full pharmaceutical arsenal, does not cure schizophrenia or bipolar disorder either. The goal is management: reducing symptom severity, maintaining the person's dignity, preserving their place in the community, and keeping everyone safe during acute episodes.

The evidence base for these protocols draws from three converging sources: the neuroscience of psychiatric illness (which explains WHY certain interventions work), the ethnobotanical record (which documents WHAT plant-based interventions have been used across cultures for millennia), and the historical models of community-based care (which demonstrate HOW non-pharmaceutical management can succeed). None of these sources alone suffices. Together, they form a coherent framework for zero-tech psychiatric care.

> **CAUTION**: This module addresses the management of severe mental illness in a zero-pharmaceutical environment. The botanical interventions described here are SUPPORTIVE, not curative. They reduce symptom severity and enable management, but they do not treat the underlying neurological condition. No claim of cure is made or implied. Persons in acute psychotic crisis may pose a danger to themselves or others — the containment protocols described here are safety measures, not punishments.

## Theoretical Foundation

### The Neuroscience of Major Mental Illness

**Schizophrenia.**
The dopamine hypothesis — excess dopaminergic activity in the mesolimbic pathway produces positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, thought disorder). Hypodopaminergia in the mesocortical pathway produces negative symptoms (flat affect, anhedonia, social withdrawal). Affects approximately 1% of all populations. Onset typically 18-25 in males, 25-35 in females.

The hallucinations are REAL to the person experiencing them — they arise from genuine neural activity, not imagination. The brain cannot distinguish internally generated sensory signals from externally generated ones. When a person with schizophrenia reports hearing voices, the auditory cortex activates identically to how it activates when processing real external sound. Telling the person the voices are "not real" is neurologically meaningless — the experience IS real, only its source is internal rather than external.

The negative symptoms (withdrawal, flat affect, reduced speech) are often more disabling than the positive symptoms, because they erode the person's ability to participate in social life and perform daily tasks. In a settlement context, these negative symptoms may be mistaken for laziness or deliberate social refusal.

**Bipolar disorder.**
Dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis and monoamine neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine).

Manic episodes produce elevated dopamine and norepinephrine, manifesting as grandiosity, decreased sleep need (the person may go 3-5 days without sleep and feel energized), impulsive behavior, pressured speech, and potentially psychotic features.

Depressive episodes produce reduced serotonin and norepinephrine, manifesting as profound sadness, anhedonia, psychomotor retardation, and suicidal ideation. Affects approximately 2-3% of populations.

The manic phase presents the greatest danger in a settlement context because the individual may make impulsive decisions that endanger themselves or others, and their elevated energy and confidence may initially appear to be productive leadership rather than illness. A manic person may volunteer for dangerous tasks, pick fights with authority figures, begin grandiose construction projects, or give away essential supplies — all while appearing vigorous and convinced of their own correctness. The depressive phase that follows mania is often profoundly deep and carries high suicide risk.

**Major depression.**
The monoamine hypothesis — reduced serotonergic and noradrenergic transmission. HPA axis hyperactivity producing chronic cortisol elevation. Neuroinflammation contributing to neural circuit dysfunction. Affects 5-15% lifetime prevalence across all studied populations.

In a settlement, depression manifests as withdrawal from communal activities, reduced work output, social isolation, disturbed sleep (either insomnia or hypersomnia), appetite changes, and — in severe cases — suicidal ideation.

The risk: depression often remains invisible because the person withdraws rather than disrupts, and the community may interpret withdrawal as laziness, antisocial choice, or ingratitude rather than illness.

Chronic cortisol elevation from untreated depression also produces downstream physical effects — immune suppression, cardiovascular stress, muscle wasting — making the person physically ill as well as psychologically incapacitated.

**Dementia.**
Progressive neurodegeneration through several distinct pathways:
- Alzheimer's disease (amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tau tangles)
- Vascular dementia (cerebrovascular damage from repeated small strokes)
- Lewy body dementia (alpha-synuclein protein deposits)

Affects approximately 5-8% of those over 60 (WHO). Symptoms include progressive memory loss, confusion about time and place, personality changes (aggression, paranoia, disinhibition), and eventual loss of self-care ability.

In a settlement without modern diagnostic tools, early dementia may be confused with normal aging or depression. The critical difference: depression responds to social engagement and botanical support; dementia does not improve, only progresses. Early identification matters because it allows the community to plan for increasing care needs and to begin training the person's family and Anchor team while the person can still participate in their own care planning.

### The Anti-Stigma Imperative

Cross-cultural psychiatric research provides the strongest evidence for community-based care. The WHO International Pilot Study of Schizophrenia (1973) and the WHO Determinants of Outcome of Severe Mental Disorders study (1992) found that people with schizophrenia in developing countries with LESS psychiatric infrastructure had BETTER long-term outcomes than those in developed countries with full pharmaceutical treatment.

Patients in Nigeria, India, and Colombia showed higher rates of complete remission and lower rates of chronic disability than patients in the United States, United Kingdom, and Denmark. The leading hypothesis: community integration, social role preservation, and reduced stigma are therapeutic factors that outweigh pharmaceutical intervention.

The mechanism operates through social neuroscience. Social isolation amplifies every mental illness:
- Schizophrenia worsens in isolation because the person has no reality-checking social contact — without external sensory input and social feedback, internally generated signals dominate unchallenged
- Depression deepens in isolation because the withdrawal-loneliness cycle feeds itself — withdrawal reduces social reward, which deepens anhedonia, which increases withdrawal
- Even dementia progresses faster in socially isolated individuals, likely because reduced cognitive stimulation accelerates neural degeneration

Community integration — keeping the person within the social fabric, with a role, with human contact, with daily structure — functions as treatment in its own right.

### Historical Models of Community-Based Care

**The Geel Model (Belgium, 700+ years).**
Since at least the 13th century, the town of Geel in the Flemish region of Belgium has hosted people with severe mental illness as "boarders" in ordinary family homes. No locked wards, no restraints, no institutional separation. The boarders participate in family life, perform household work within their capacity, and are treated as eccentric family members rather than patients.

The system arose organically around the legend of St. Dymphna, patron saint of the mentally ill, whose shrine drew pilgrims seeking healing. Those who were not "healed" stayed — and the families of Geel took them in.

Modern psychiatric evaluation of the Geel system (Roosens, 1979; Goldstein & Godemont, 2003) found:
- Lower rates of violent episodes
- Higher patient satisfaction
- Lower symptom severity scores
- Longer periods of functional stability compared to institutional care

The mechanism: normalization and social role preservation reduce the chronic stress that exacerbates psychiatric symptoms. When the community treats mental illness as a manageable condition rather than a terrifying aberration, the afflicted person's stress response diminishes, and with it, symptom severity.

**Soteria House (Loren Mosher, 1971-1983).**
A residential treatment program for acute schizophrenia that used minimal or no antipsychotic medication and relied instead on interpersonal support, a calm non-institutional environment, and staff trained to "be with" rather than "treat" residents.

The Soteria model employed non-professional staff selected for empathy and calm temperament — a deliberate choice that parallels this module's Anchor protocol. Outcomes matched or exceeded conventional hospitalization with antipsychotic medication at 6-week, 2-year, and 6-year follow-up. Published in the *Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease* (1999).

The mechanism: reduced environmental stress (no institutional setting, no forced medication, no mechanical restraint) allows the brain's own regulatory systems to function more effectively. The implications for settlement care are direct: a calm, supportive environment with trained companions can manage acute psychotic episodes without pharmaceuticals.

**Moral treatment movement (Philippe Pinel, 1793; William Tuke, 1796).**
Pinel famously ordered the chains removed from the mentally ill at the Bicetre Hospital in Paris during the French Revolution — an act of both clinical insight and political courage. Tuke, a Quaker merchant, founded the York Retreat in England after learning of the death of a Quaker patient in the York Asylum under brutal conditions.

The Retreat treated mental illness with rest, meaningful work, fresh air, nutritious food, and kindness rather than restraint and punishment. Both Pinel and Tuke achieved dramatic improvements in patient outcomes compared to the prevailing asylum model, which relied on chains, beatings, cold water immersion as punishment, and permanent confinement.

The principle established by the moral treatment movement remains the foundation of all ethical psychiatric care: humane treatment is more effective than coercive treatment, even for severe illness.

## Core Principles

1. **The Biological Understanding Principle.**
Mental illness is a neurological condition, not a moral failing, spiritual affliction, or personal choice. The community must understand this BEFORE a crisis occurs. Education about the biological basis of mental illness constitutes preventive infrastructure — it determines whether the community responds with compassion or with fear. The time to teach the dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia is at a calm equinox gathering, not during a psychotic episode.

2. **The Integration Principle.**
The afflicted person remains within the community to the maximum extent that safety allows. Isolation worsens every mental illness. Social role preservation (even a simplified role) maintains dignity and provides the social contact that moderates symptoms. The Geel model demonstrates this principle across 700 years of continuous practice. Exile is not a treatment — it is an abandonment that guarantees deterioration.

3. **The Anchor Principle.**
During acute episodes, one designated person (the Anchor) provides calm, consistent presence. The Anchor does not argue with delusions, does not attempt to "fix" the person, and does not express fear or frustration. The Anchor validates the EMOTION behind the symptom ("I can see you feel frightened") without validating the content of the delusion. This approach works because the emotional experience is genuine even when the perceptual content is false — the person truly feels terror, even if the threat they perceive does not exist.

4. **The Body-First Principle.**
When the mind is in crisis, stabilize the BODY first. Sleep, food, water, warmth, physical safety. Manic and psychotic episodes are massively worsened by sleep deprivation — restoring sleep is the single most important intervention available. Botanical sedatives, rhythmic physical activity, and environmental calm all target the body to indirectly stabilize the mind. The body-first approach works because the brain is a biological organ embedded in a biological system — its chemistry responds to sleep, nutrition, temperature, and physical state.

5. **The Rotation Principle.**
Caring for a person in mental health crisis is profoundly exhausting — emotionally, physically, and cognitively. No single caretaker can sustain this for more than 4-6 hours without degradation of empathy and judgment. The community MUST rotate caretakers on a strict schedule. Caretaker burnout is itself a mental health crisis that can cascade through the community if unmanaged.

## Practical Implementation

### Community Education Protocol (preventive — implement BEFORE any crisis)

At least twice per year (equinox gatherings), the community holds a structured discussion on the biological basis of mental illness. This education is not optional enrichment — it is critical infrastructure that determines the community's response to its first psychiatric emergency.

Content covers:
- The dopamine and serotonin mechanisms described in the Theoretical Foundation above
- The difference between brain illness and character flaw
- The Geel model as historical proof that community integration works
- The Anchor protocol so that every adult understands the framework before a crisis demands its use

Format: E-Prime language, observational tone, no stigmatizing vocabulary. The community builds a shared language of compassionate care into its daily speech:
- "She went crazy" becomes "She experienced a psychotic episode"
- "He is lazy and refuses to work" becomes "He appears to have entered a depressive episode and has withdrawn from activity"

Language shapes perception — stigmatizing language produces stigmatizing responses.

### The Anchor Protocol

**Selection.**
The Anchor should be a person with high empathy, calm temperament, and the ability to remain non-reactive under distress. Not everyone possesses this capacity — it is a specific skill set that combines emotional intelligence with emotional regulation. Identify 3-4 potential Anchors in the community and train them in advance of any crisis.

Selection criteria:
- The person remains calm when others are shouting
- The person listens without interrupting
- The person does not take others' distress personally
- The person can sit in silence without discomfort

**Training.**
The Anchor practices validating emotional content without engaging delusional content. Core technique: identify the emotion, name it, and offer presence.

Example: if the person says "There are people outside trying to kill me," the Anchor responds "I can see you feel very frightened. I will sit here with you and we will keep watch together."

The Anchor does NOT say "There is nobody outside, you are imagining things" — the person's brain cannot accept this correction, and the disagreement escalates panic because the person now perceives the Anchor as either deceived by the threat or complicit in it.

Additional training includes:
- Recognizing escalation signs (increased volume, pacing, clenched fists, rapid breathing)
- De-escalation through voice modulation (slow, low, steady)
- Physical positioning (never blocking the exit, maintaining arm's-length distance, keeping hands visible and open)

**Duration.**
An Anchor shift lasts 4 hours maximum. At shift change, the outgoing Anchor briefs the incoming Anchor on the person's current state, what has helped, and what has escalated distress. The handoff occurs in the person's presence with a calm introduction: "This is [name], they will be sitting with you now. I have told them that you feel [emotion]."

**Documentation.**
After each shift, the Anchor records (in E-Prime language) what occurred:
- Behavioral observations
- Triggers identified
- Interventions attempted
- Sleep achieved
- Food and water accepted

This log builds the community's understanding of the individual's pattern over time and becomes the primary diagnostic tool in the absence of clinical assessment.

### Biological Grounding Protocols

**Sleep enforcement.**
The single most important intervention for mania and psychosis. Administer valerian (3-5 mL tincture) combined with passionflower (1-2 mL tincture or one cup strong tea) 1-2 hours before desired sleep.

Procedure:
- Darken the room completely — cover windows, extinguish all light sources
- Remove all stimulation: no conversation, no tasks, no noise
- The Anchor remains present but silent, seated rather than standing
- The Anchor breathes slowly and audibly (the person's respiratory rate will entrain to the Anchor's — a documented neurological phenomenon called respiratory entrainment)

Target: 7-9 hours of sleep. Even partial sleep restoration (4-5 hours) significantly reduces manic and psychotic symptom severity. For a person who has been awake for 48+ hours in a manic state, achieving ANY sleep is the priority — do not insist on a full night initially. Four hours of sleep can break a manic escalation cycle.

**Rhythmic bilateral activity.**
Repetitive physical tasks that engage both sides of the body:
- Spinning fiber on a drop spindle
- Grinding grain with a hand quern
- Weeding a garden bed
- Kneading dough
- Walking a set circular path

The bilateral motion produces a metronome effect on neural oscillation patterns, promoting hemispheric coherence. This is the same mechanism underlying EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) — bilateral stimulation appears to facilitate neural processing and reduce emotional arousal.

The activity must be genuinely productive (not busywork) to preserve the person's dignity, low-stakes (failure does not matter), and rhythmic (the repetition is the therapeutic element). Assign the activity at a consistent time each day to build circadian structure.

**Cold water exposure.**
Brief (30-60 second) immersion of hands and forearms in cold water activates the mammalian dive reflex — vagal nerve stimulation that shifts the autonomic nervous system from sympathetic (fight-or-flight) toward parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) dominance. Heart rate drops, breathing slows, blood pressure stabilizes.

This intervention can interrupt acute panic attacks, dissociative states, and escalating agitation within 60-90 seconds.

Technique:
- Fill a basin with the coldest available water
- Guide the person to immerse both hands to the wrists
- Instruct them to hold the immersion while breathing slowly
- Not suitable for hypothermic patients or during cold weather exposure

**Dietary stabilization.**
Regular meals at consistent times — circadian meal structure supports circadian sleep-wake regulation.

Nutritional priorities during acute episodes:
- High-protein diet (protein provides tryptophan, the precursor amino acid for serotonin synthesis)
- Adequate dietary fat (fat provides substrate for neural membrane repair and myelination)
- Avoid all stimulants (caffeine from tea or coffee if available in the settlement)
- Ensure adequate hydration — dehydration exacerbates confusion and agitation
- During manic episodes, the person may refuse food — offer small, calorie-dense portions rather than full meals

### The Containment Protocol (safety, not punishment)

**The Calm Room.**
A designated space in the settlement — NOT a prison cell, NOT a storage room repurposed in an emergency.

Requirements:
- No sharp edges, no fire, no tools, no rope or cordage
- Soft bedding (straw mattress, blankets)
- Natural light through a window with a secure covering that cannot be removed from inside
- Adequate ventilation (minimum 15x15cm shaft or equivalent)
- A door that can be secured from outside but is not airtight

The room should feel like a quiet bedroom, not a cell. Ideally located near the center of the settlement rather than at the periphery — proximity to community life reduces the isolation effect even during containment.

**Trigger for containment.**
The person has become a physical danger to themselves or others:
- Active violence toward others
- Self-harm attempts (cutting, head-banging, attempting to run into hazards)
- Complete inability to recognize their surroundings (severe psychotic disorientation where the person cannot identify where they are, who is with them, or what is happening)

Containment is NOT used for disruptive but non-dangerous behavior such as shouting, agitation, wandering, bizarre speech, or refusal to work. Disruptive non-dangerous behavior is managed through the Anchor protocol and biological grounding, not containment.

**During containment.**
The Anchor remains with or immediately outside the person at all times — the person is never left alone in the Calm Room.

Monitoring schedule:
- Check every 15 minutes for breathing rate, consciousness level, and self-harm risk
- Offer water every hour
- Offer food every 2 hours
- Offer botanical sedatives (but never force them — forcing medication, even botanical, erodes trust and escalates resistance)

If the person refuses sedatives, the Anchor continues with presence, calm voice, and environmental control (darkness, quiet) as the primary interventions.

**Duration.**
Containment lasts only as long as the acute danger persists. As soon as the person can communicate coherently and is no longer a physical danger to themselves or others, containment ends.

Expected duration: 2-24 hours for most acute episodes. Extended containment beyond 24 hours requires community review — at least three adults unrelated to the situation must agree that continued containment remains necessary for safety. The review is documented. No containment exceeds 72 hours without a full community meeting.

**Caretaker rotation during containment.**
4-hour shifts maximum. Each incoming caretaker receives a verbal briefing from the outgoing one covering:
- Current behavioral state
- What has helped
- What has escalated distress
- Food and water intake
- Sleep achieved
- Sedatives administered

After a containment event concludes, ALL caretakers who participated receive 24 hours of rest before returning to any caregiving duties.

### Ongoing Management (between crises)

The person maintains a role in the community — adjusted to their capacity but real, not patronizing. A person with chronic schizophrenia might tend a garden plot, sort harvested materials, or assist with childcare under supervision. A person with recurrent depression maintains their regular role during remission and shifts to lighter duties during depressive episodes. The role provides structure, social contact, purpose, and the dignity of contribution.

A primary Anchor maintains regular contact through a daily check-in of 10-15 minutes. The check-in is structured:
- How did you sleep?
- How is your appetite?
- Are you hearing or seeing anything unusual?
- How are you feeling about your work?

The check-in serves dual purposes — it provides social contact that moderates symptoms, and it monitors for early warning signs of relapse.

Early warning signs are documented for each individual. What preceded their last episode? Sleep disruption is nearly universal as a prodromal sign. The individual's warning profile includes:
- Social withdrawal
- Increased irritability
- Changes in speech pattern (pressured speech before mania, reduced speech before depression)
- Neglect of hygiene
- Specific idiosyncratic triggers (interpersonal conflict, seasonal changes, anniversary dates)

The community watches for these signs and intervenes early with increased Anchor contact, sleep support, and stress reduction — early intervention can prevent full episodes.

Botanical support between crises — low-dose ongoing herbal support to maintain baseline calm without heavy sedation:
- Chamomile tea daily (1-2 cups)
- Skullcap tincture as needed for anxiety (1 mL)
- Lemon balm tea in the evening to support sleep onset

These maintenance doses are far below the acute crisis doses listed in the Botanical Sedative Kit and are safe for long-term daily use.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Stigma Override.**
Despite education, the community reverts to fear and blames the person for their illness. "If they just tried harder they could control it." "They choose to behave this way." "We cannot afford to carry someone who does not contribute."

Prevention: education is not one-time — repeat the biological framework at every equinox gathering. Introduce case discussions of how the community has managed episodes successfully. The community's response to mental illness reveals its character and predicts its resilience under all forms of stress.

2. **Anchor Burnout.**
The designated Anchor takes on too much emotional weight and develops anxiety, insomnia, or depression themselves. Burnout in mental health caregivers is not a risk — it is a near-certainty without structural prevention. The emotional labor of sitting with a person in psychosis for hours, absorbing their fear without reacting, maintaining calm while being shouted at or accused — this depletes the caregiver's own neurological resources.

Prevention: strict 4-hour rotation with no exceptions. The Anchor role distributes across 3-4 trained people. After any containment event, all participating Anchors receive mandatory rest. The community monitors Anchors for signs of secondary traumatic stress.

3. **Containment as Punishment.**
The Calm Room begins to be used punitively — for disagreeable behavior, for disobedience, for "attitude," for social deviance that the community finds uncomfortable but that poses no physical danger. This is the most insidious failure mode because it can develop gradually and be rationalized as "for their own good."

Prevention: containment triggers are explicit and narrow — physical danger only. Any use of containment for non-dangerous behavior constitutes abuse that the community addresses through the conflict resolution protocol (Module 04). The rule is absolute: if the person is not an immediate physical danger to themselves or others, containment is not an option.

4. **Botanical Overdose.**
Excessive sedative administration, particularly combining multiple high-dose preparations. Valerian and passionflower combined at full individual doses can produce dangerous oversedation — shallow breathing, unrousable sleep, and in rare cases respiratory depression.

Prevention: one designated person (the community herbalist) manages the botanical sedative kit. Dosage records are maintained for every administration. No preparation exceeds the dosages listed in this module without documented justification and a second person's agreement. The herbalist monitors for cumulative sedation when multiple preparations are used across a 24-hour period.

5. **Isolation Drift.**
The community gradually reduces contact with the afflicted person — not through malice but through avoidance of discomfort. Conversations become shorter. Invitations to communal meals stop. The person drifts to the social periphery. This drift is self-reinforcing: as the person becomes more isolated, their symptoms worsen, which makes them more difficult to be around, which deepens the isolation.

Prevention: the primary Anchor's daily check-in is a structural safeguard against drift. The regular community meeting (Module 04) includes a standing agenda item on the wellbeing of any member receiving ongoing care. The community explicitly assigns social contact responsibilities — not just the Anchor, but rotating companions for meals and work.

6. **Family Burden.**
A family attempts to manage a severely ill member alone out of shame, protectiveness, or cultural expectations. The family burns out — sleep-deprived, emotionally exhausted, unable to maintain their own work contributions, and increasingly resentful. The illness worsens from inadequate and inconsistent care.

Prevention: the community explicitly frames mental illness care as COMMUNAL responsibility, not family responsibility. The rotation system distributes the burden across the community. The family remains central to the person's care but is not expected to be the sole provider. The Anchor team relieves the family for defined periods every day.

## Vocabulary of the Foundation

- **Anchor** — designated caretaker trained to provide calm, non-reactive presence during psychiatric episodes
- **Biological grounding** — interventions targeting the body (sleep, food, rhythmic activity, cold exposure) to indirectly stabilize the mind
- **Calm Room** — designated safe space for containment during physically dangerous episodes; designed as a quiet room, not a cell
- **Containment protocol** — structured safety procedure for persons who pose immediate physical danger to themselves or others
- **Caretaker rotation** — strict 4-hour shift system preventing burnout among those providing psychiatric care
- **Botanical sedative** — plant-based preparations (valerian, passionflower, skullcap, chamomile, lemon balm) that reduce neural excitability through GABA modulation
- **GABA modulation** — the mechanism by which botanical sedatives reduce neural excitability by enhancing gamma-aminobutyric acid activity at inhibitory synapses
- **Dopamine hypothesis** — the neurological model explaining schizophrenia symptoms as arising from dopaminergic dysregulation in mesolimbic and mesocortical pathways
- **Monoamine hypothesis** — the neurological model explaining depression as arising from reduced serotonergic and noradrenergic transmission
- **Stigma override** — the failure mode in which community fear overrides biological understanding, producing punitive responses to mental illness
- **Rhythmic bilateral activity** — repetitive physical tasks engaging both body sides, producing hemispheric coherence and reduced emotional arousal
- **Early warning signs** — individualized prodromal indicators (sleep disruption, withdrawal, speech changes) that precede psychiatric episodes
- **Geel model** — the 700-year Belgian tradition of integrating persons with severe mental illness into family homes as community members
- **Soteria model** — Loren Mosher's demonstrated approach to managing acute schizophrenia through interpersonal support and calm environment rather than pharmaceuticals
